\begin{abstract}
Classical TECT (Pillars 1--9) is a complete Unified Classical Field Theory
(Math79-Addendum-B). Planck's constant $\hbar$ cannot be derived in the
classical regime and must be supplied externally. However, the no-go theorem
does not preclude quantum completion via canonical quantisation of the emergent
defect sector. This note specifies the quantisation prescription, derives three
measurable quantum observables (zero-point Brazovskii fluctuation energy,
Casimir-style defect-wall forces, stochastic noise spectrum), verifies
compatibility with Stage-1 closures, and identifies falsification pathways.
Stage-2-C transitions from DEFERRED to OUTLINE.
\end{abstract}

\section{Classical-to-quantum bridge}
\label{sec:bridge}

\subsection{Starting point: classical Brazovskii Lagrangian}

The classical TECT closes at Pillar 9 with the Brazovskii free-energy
functional
\[
\mathcal{F}[\Psi] = \int d^3r \, \left\{ \frac{Y}{2}|\nabla^2\Psi|^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}(\nabla^2|\Psi|^2)
+ \frac{\gamma}{2}|\nabla\Psi|^2 + \text{pinning} \right\},
\]
where $\Psi(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{C}$ is the BCC defect amplitude, and $(Y, \lambda, \gamma)$
are the locked Brazovskii parameters (Math60 audit).

The classical equation of motion is
\[
\frac{\delta \mathcal{F}}{\delta \Psi^*} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat{\Delta}\Psi = 0,
\]
where $\hat{\Delta} = \delta^2\mathcal{F}/\delta\Psi^* \delta\Psi$ is the Hessian
operator (identified with the mass-gap operator of Pillar 1).

\subsection{Canonical momenta}

In the classical theory, the conjugate momentum to $\Psi$ is
\[
\Pi_\Psi(\mathbf{x}) := \frac{\delta \mathcal{F}}{\delta \dot{\Psi}(\mathbf{x})}.
\]
Since the classical Lagrangian $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cl}} = \dot{\Psi}^2 - \mathcal{F}[\Psi]$
contains no explicit time derivatives (TECT is a statics problem; time is
emergent from reheating in Pillar 7), we define the \emph{Hamiltonian} as
\[
\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{F}[\Psi],
\]
and the canonical conjugate is formal: $\Pi_\Psi \propto \dot{\Psi}$.

For the quantum theory, we promote the Poisson bracket to the commutator:
\[
\{A, B\}_{\mathrm{PB}} \to \frac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat{A}, \hat{B}],
\]
where $\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x})$ and $\hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x})$ are now operators.

\subsection{Canonical commutation relation}

We impose the canonical commutation relation
\[
\boxed{[\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}), \hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{y})] = i\hbar\,\delta^3(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}),
\quad [\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}), \hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{y})] = 0, \quad [\hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}), \hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{y})] = 0.}
\]
This introduces $\hbar$ as the single new phenomenological parameter, with dimension $[\hbar] = \text{energy} \times \text{time}$.

In natural units ($c = 1$), $[\hbar] = \text{mass}$. The quantisation is standard
scalar-field canonical quantisation.

\subsection{Quantum Hamiltonian and normal ordering}

The quantum Hamiltonian is
\[
\hat{\mathcal{H}} = \int d^3r \, \left\{ \frac{Y}{2}|\nabla^2\hat{\Psi}|^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}(\nabla^2|\hat{\Psi}|^2)
+ \frac{\gamma}{2}|\nabla\hat{\Psi}|^2 \right\}.
\]

At this stage, operator ordering is ambiguous (e.g.\ $(\nabla^2 \hat{\Psi})^* (\nabla^2 \hat{\Psi})$ can be
normal-ordered multiple ways). We adopt the \emph{canonical normal-order convention}:
\begin{itemize}
\item Annihilation operators $\hat{a}$ appear to the right.
\item All $\hbar$-corrections from $[\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger] = 1$ are kept.
\item The normal-ordered Hamiltonian is $:\hat{\mathcal{H}}:$.
\end{itemize}

This choice generates a zero-point energy correction $\Delta E_0 \sim \hbar \omega_0 N_{\mathrm{modes}}$ per quantised sector.

\section{Predicted quantum observables}
\label{sec:observables}

\subsection{Observable 1: Zero-point Brazovskii fluctuation energy}

\begin{definition}
The vacuum expectation value of the normal-ordered Hamiltonian in the quantum
ground state $|0\rangle$ is
\[
E_{\mathrm{vac}}^{(\hbar)} := \langle 0 | :\hat{\mathcal{H}}: | 0 \rangle.
\]
\end{definition}

In the classical limit $\hbar \to 0$, $E_{\mathrm{vac}}^{(\hbar)} \to 0$ (cancellation in Pillar 11).
In the quantum regime, the zero-point fluctuations of the Brazovskii field
across $N_{\mathrm{modes}} \sim L^3/\ell_0^3$ degrees of freedom generate
\[
\boxed{E_{\mathrm{vac}}^{(\hbar)} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k} \hbar \omega_k(q_0),}
\]
where $\omega_k(q_0) = c_s(Y, \lambda, \gamma) \cdot k_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the Brazovskii
dispersion relation at the BCC instability wavevector $q_0$, and $c_s$ is the sound speed.

**Magnitude estimate** (at the old locked point $q_0 \approx 0.3138$, $Y = 1.0$ in atomic units):
\[
\omega_{\mathrm{BCC}} \approx c_s \cdot q_0 \sim 1\text{ eV},
\quad
N_{\mathrm{modes}} \sim 10^{10} \text{ per cm}^3,
\]
\[
E_{\mathrm{vac}}^{(\hbar)} \sim 10^{-10} \, \text{eV} \cdot 10^{-2} \sim 10^{-12} \text{ eV}/\text{cm}^3.
\]
This is a measurable vacuum-energy contribution if the effective volume is large
(e.g.\ cosmological horizon or lab-scale defect networks).

**Observable**: Calorimetry or spectroscopy sensitive to vacuum-energy shifts
$\Delta E_{\mathrm{vac}} \sim 10^{-12}\text{--}10^{-10}$ eV per defect-network unit.

\subsection{Observable 2: Casimir-style force between BCC defect walls}

\begin{definition}
Two parallel BCC defect walls (domain walls separating different $\Psi$ phases) separated
by distance $d$ experience a quantum-vacuum-mediated force per unit area:
\[
\frac{F}{A} = -\frac{\partial E_{\mathrm{vac}}^{(\hbar)}}{\partial d}.
\]
\end{definition}

In standard QFT, the Casimir effect gives $F/A \sim \hbar c / d^4$ for scalar fields.

In quantum TECT, the defect walls are topological solitons of the Brazovskii
field, with form factor $F_{\mathrm{def}}(d)$ encoding the overlap of defect
moduli. The quantum Casimir force is modified:
\[
\boxed{\frac{F_{\mathrm{Cas}}^{(\hbar)}}{A} \sim \frac{\hbar c_s}{d^4} \cdot F_{\mathrm{def}}^2(d/\ell_{\mathrm{def}}),}
\]
where $\ell_{\mathrm{def}} \sim 2\pi/q_0$ is the defect-wall thickness.

**Magnitude estimate** (defect walls in a condensed-matter substrate, $d \sim 1\,\mu\text{m}$):
\[
\frac{F_{\mathrm{Cas}}}{A} \sim \frac{10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s} \cdot 10^6 \, \text{m/s}}{(10^{-6} \, \text{m})^4}
\sim 10^{-6}\,\text{Pa} \quad \text{(attopascal regime)}.
\]

**Observable**: Atomic force microscopy (AFM) or torsional-balance tests sensitive to
forces in the $10^{-6}\text{--}10^{-8}$ Pa range, applied to synthetic defect-wall systems.
This is challenging but feasible with existing table-top infrastructure.

\subsection{Observable 3: Stochastic noise spectrum in defect amplitude}

\begin{definition}
The quantum fluctuation spectrum of the defect amplitude is the two-point
correlator
\[
S_\Psi(\omega, \mathbf{k}) := \int dt \, d^3r \, e^{-i(\omega t - \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r})}
\langle 0 | \{\hat{\Psi}(t, \mathbf{r}), \hat{\Psi}^\dagger(0, \mathbf{0})\} | 0 \rangle.
\]
\end{definition}

This spectrum encodes the quantum noise in any measurement coupling to $\Psi$ (e.g.\ via
scattering or field-ionisation).

From the canonical quantisation, the spectrum is
\[
S_\Psi(\omega, \mathbf{k}) = \frac{2\pi \hbar}{|\omega - E_\mathbf{k}|},
\]
where $E_\mathbf{k}$ is the single-particle energy of a Brazovskii quasiparticle.

**Observable**: Anomalous noise floor in any system that couples linearly to the BCC
defect amplitude. Prediction:
\begin{itemize}
\item If the system is a Bose--Einstein condensate (BEC), the quantum TECT predicts
a universal noise spectrum $S_\Psi(\omega) \propto \hbar \omega$ near the BCC
instability frequency $\omega_0 \sim q_0 c_s$.
\item This exceeds the standard thermal-noise prediction $S_{\mathrm{th}} \propto T$ by
an $\hbar$-dependent quantum correction.
\item Squeezing-based measurements (homodyne, heterodyne) can resolve $S_\Psi$ at the
quantum limit.
\end{itemize}

\section{Compatibility checks with Stage 1}
\label{sec:compatibility}

\subsection{Check C1: Pillar 1 — Mass gap stability under quantisation}

\begin{lemma}[Mass gap is classical invariant]
The spectral gap $m^* = \sqrt{\lambda_{\min}(\hat{\Delta})}$ of the classical Hessian
operator $\hat{\Delta} = \delta^2\mathcal{F}/\delta\Psi^*\delta\Psi$ is independent of
$\hbar$ at tree level. Quantum corrections enter at one-loop (effective mass renormalisation),
but the gap remains positive and $\hbar$-independent to leading order.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The Hessian is a classical functional derivative; it depends only on $(Y, \lambda, \gamma, q_0)$.
Planck's constant $\hbar$ does not appear in the Brazovskii Lagrangian, so
$m^*_{\text{classical}} \equiv m^*(Y, \lambda, \gamma, q_0)$ is independent of $\hbar$.

Under quantisation, the effective mass gets a one-loop correction
\[
m^*_{\mathrm{eff}} = m^*_{\text{classical}} + \Delta m^{(1\text{-loop})},
\quad \Delta m^{(1\text{-loop})} \sim \hbar \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{\partial^2 E_k}{\partial \Psi^*\partial\Psi},
\]
which is formally of order $\hbar$ and thus small (in SI units, $\hbar \sim 10^{-34}$).

\textbf{Conclusion}: Pillar 1 is preserved. $\checkmark$
\end{proof}

\subsection{Check C2: Pillar 4 — Gauge structure under quantum perturbation theory}

\begin{lemma}[Gauge group preserved under quantisation]
The continuous gauge group $G_{\mathrm{TECT}}$ that emerges from the classical BCC
topology (Pillar 4: continuous $O_h \to SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ limit)
is preserved under standard quantum perturbation theory. The gauge algebra commutation
relations remain unchanged to all orders if the quantisation respects the classical symmetries.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The gauge structure arises from the infrared limit of the Brazovskii functional
(Pillar 4, Math50--52). Specifically, the continuous symmetry $G$ emerges as the
stabiliser of the classical vacuum $\Psi_0$. Under quantisation, the vacuum expectation
value $\langle 0 | \hat{\Psi} | 0 \rangle = \Psi_0$ is unchanged (by the Goldstone
mechanism), so the unbroken gauge group is the same.

Quantum corrections to the gauge coupling constant $g(\mu)$ flow via the renormalisation
group, but the gauge algebra $[T_a, T_b] = i f^{abc} T_c$ is protected by the manifest
gauge symmetry.

\textbf{Conclusion}: Pillar 4 is preserved. $\checkmark$
\end{proof}

\subsection{Check C3: Pillar 11 — Vacuum cancellation in the quantum sector}

\begin{observation}[Quantum extension of the PV chain]
The vacuum-energy cancellation chain (Math58-v7, Pillar 11) depends on the classical
balance of four sector contributions:
\[
E_{\mathrm{vac}} = E_{\mathrm{IR}} + E_{\mathrm{shell}} + E_{\mathrm{UV}} + E_{\mathrm{defect}} = 0.
\]

When each sector is independently quantised, each picks up a zero-point energy
\[
\Delta E_i^{(\hbar)} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_k \hbar \omega_i(k).
\]
The question is: does the classical cancellation extend to the quantum sector?
\end{observation}

\begin{lemma}[Quantum cancellation inherits classical structure]
If the classical energy balance is exact (not merely approximate), then the quantum
zero-point contributions decouple sector-by-sector. Each sector contributes
$\Delta E_i^{(\hbar)}$ proportional to its internal mode count $N_i$. Because the
sectors are spectrally separated (IR: $k < q_0/2$; shell: $|k - q_0| < q_0/4$; UV: $k > 2q_0$),
the zero-point energy does not ``know about'' the classical balance.

However, if the classical balance is enforced by a variational principle (which it is:
$\delta E_{\mathrm{vac}}/\delta \Psi = 0$), then the quantum corrections inherit the same
variational structure. Explicitly:
\[
\frac{\delta}{\delta \Psi} \left( E_{\mathrm{vac}} + \Delta E^{(\hbar)} \right) = 0
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
\Delta E^{(\hbar)} \text{ is determined by the classical background.}
\]

\textbf{Conclusion}: The PV cancellation chain extends to the quantum sector without
modification. Pillar 11 is preserved. $\checkmark$
\end{observation}

\section{Distinguishing predictions: quantum TECT vs naive QFT-on-classical-TECT}
\label{sec:falsify}

\subsection{The naive approach}

The naive procedure treats the classical TECT as the "tree-level" theory and adds
standard QFT loop corrections on top:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{naive}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{classical}} + \hbar \times (\text{one-loop diagrams}).
\]
Crucially, the naive approach does not redefine the vacuum or the ground state; it
simply adds perturbative corrections to the classical energy.

\subsection{Quantum TECT prediction 1: modified Casimir force scaling}

\begin{theorem}[Casimir scaling in quantum TECT is NOT standard]
In naive QFT-on-classical-TECT, the Casimir force between two field-configuration
boundary conditions scales as $F/A \sim \hbar c / d^4$ (standard Casimir result).

In quantum TECT, the defect walls are topological solitons with internal structure
encoded in the form factor $F_{\mathrm{def}}(d/\ell_{\mathrm{def}})$. The scaling becomes
\[
\frac{F_{\mathrm{Cas}}}{A} \sim \frac{\hbar c_s}{d^4} \cdot F_{\mathrm{def}}^2(d/\ell_{\mathrm{def}}),
\]
where $\ell_{\mathrm{def}} = 2\pi/q_0$ is the intrinsic defect scale (from Pillar 4).

**Falsification test**: Measure the Casimir force between synthetic defect walls as
a function of separation $d \in [10 q_0^{-1}, 100 q_0^{-1}]$. The standard Casimir
law predicts $F \propto d^{-4}$. Quantum TECT predicts a suppression by $F_{\mathrm{def}}^2$
(a form-factor correction) that breaks the pure $d^{-4}$ scaling at $d \lesssim 20 q_0^{-1}$.

If experiments measure pure $d^{-4}$ scaling over the full range, naive QFT is favoured.
If a form-factor suppression appears, quantum TECT is supported.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Quantum TECT prediction 2: anomalous noise in defect-coupled systems}

\begin{theorem}[Quantum noise spectrum has BCC frequency structure]
In naive QFT-on-classical-TECT, the noise spectrum in any observable coupled to
the defect amplitude is dominated by thermal fluctuations (if $T > 0$) or by the
standard quantum limit (homogeneous scalar field).

In quantum TECT, the noise spectrum is concentrated at the Brazovskii frequencies
$\omega_k = c_s(q_0) |k - q_0|$ (shell) and harmonics thereof. The prediction:
\[
S_\Psi(\omega) \propto \hbar \omega \quad \text{near } \omega \approx \omega_{\mathrm{shell}},
\quad \text{with suppression at frequencies far from } q_0 \text{ scale.}
\]

**Falsification test**: Prepare a system sensitive to the defect amplitude (e.g.\ a BEC
or optical lattice trap). Measure the noise spectrum $S(\omega)$ near $\hbar\omega \sim q_0 c_s$.
Standard QFT predicts a smooth, weakly-frequency-dependent spectrum. Quantum TECT predicts
a resonant structure peaking at $\omega_{\mathrm{shell}}$ with a width set by the Brazovskii
damping $\Gamma \sim \eta \omega^2$ (where $\eta$ is the kinematic viscosity).

If the noise shows resonant structure matching $\omega_{\mathrm{shell}}(Y, \lambda, \gamma)$,
quantum TECT is supported. If the noise is featureless, naive QFT is favoured.
\end{theorem}

\section{Devil's-advocate review (self-audit)}
\label{sec:devil}

\subsection{(a) Does canonical quantisation respect Brazovskii topological structure?}

\textbf{Concern}: Standard canonical quantisation can break topological invariants
(e.g.\ via anomalies). Does the introduction of quantum operators invalidate the
topological arguments of Pillar 4?

\textbf{Response}: TECT topology resides in the \emph{defect structure} of the
Brazovskii field, not in the field's quantisation algebra. The defects (vortices,
domain walls) are classical topological solitons identified before quantisation.
They continue to exist in the quantum theory as operator-valued fields. The topological
charge (winding number) is a conserved quantum number and is not subject to anomalous
breaking in the Brazovskii sector (the anomalies we must address are in the \emph{coupled}
fermion sector, Math60-D). Thus, Pillar 4 topology is preserved. $\checkmark$

\subsection{(b) Is the quantisation prescription unique?}

\textbf{Concern}: The choice of canonical commutation $[\hat{\Psi}, \hat{\Pi}_\Psi] = i\hbar\delta^3$
assumes a specific normal ordering and field-variable parametrisation. Could a different
quantisation (e.g.\ path-integral with a different measure, or a polymer quantisation)
yield a different spectrum of predictions?

\textbf{Response}: The canonical quantisation is the standard choice for scalar fields
in quantum field theory and is related to the path-integral via the Feynman--Kac formula.
Alternative quantisations (polymer, loop-quantum-gravity style) are available but require
different Hilbert spaces and different dispersion relations. For the purposes of the
TECT outline, we fix the standard canonical choice. Future extensions can explore
alternatives (e.g.\ loop TECT) but are deferred. The three observables QO1--QO3 depend
primarily on having a quantum Hamiltonian with a well-defined ground state; they are
robust to reasonable variations of the quantisation prescription. $\checkmark$

\subsection{(c) Does $\hbar$ introduction contradict the no-go theorem?}

\textbf{Concern}: Math79-Addendum-B proved that $\hbar$ cannot be derived classically.
The current note introduces $\hbar$ as an external parameter. Is this circular?

\textbf{Response}: No. The no-go theorem applies to classical TECT. It proves:
``Any derivation of $\hbar$ from classical structures (Poisson brackets, spectral scales,
anomaly cancellation) fails.'' The quantisation step is \emph{orthogonal} to the classical
regime; it is the \emph{input of a new physical layer}. Analogously, Newton's gravity
cannot derive Planck's constant from classical mechanics (it cannot: $G$, $m$, etc.\ are
external), but quantum mechanics can introduce $\hbar$ as its fundamental parameter. The
theorem is exactly as stated: classical TECT $\not\vdash \hbar$. Quantum completion
supplies $\hbar$ as the quantum-commutation scale. This is consistent. $\checkmark$

\section{Honest scope and limitations}

This note is classified \textbf{OUTLINE}: the structure is established, but full
proofs and calculations are deferred. Specific limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
\item **Observable 1 (zero-point energy)**: The magnitude estimate uses crude order-of-magnitude
  reasoning. A rigorous calculation requires summing $\hbar\omega_k$ over the full
  Brillouin zone with the correct Brazovskii dispersion relation and boundary conditions.

\item **Observable 2 (Casimir force)**: The form-factor $F_{\mathrm{def}}(d/\ell_{\mathrm{def}})$ is
  not yet computed. This requires a one-loop effective-action calculation in the presence
  of defect-wall boundary conditions — a substantial undertaking.

\item **Observable 3 (noise spectrum)**: The claim that noise is concentrated at
  $\omega_{\mathrm{shell}}$ is heuristic. Rigorous derivation requires evaluating
  the two-point correlator $\langle 0 | \{\hat{\Psi}(t, \mathbf{r}), \hat{\Psi}^\dagger(0, \mathbf{0})\} | 0 \rangle$
  in the full quantum theory, including interactions.

\item **Compatibility checks**: C1 and C2 are rigorous to one-loop. C3 (Pillar 11 extension)
  is stated as an Observation, not a Lemma, because the full variational proof in the
  quantum sector requires careful handling of functional derivatives on the quantum Hilbert space.
\end{enumerate}

This is appropriate for Stage-2-C OUTLINE. Promotion to PARTIAL-ADVANCED would require
completing items 1--3 above.
