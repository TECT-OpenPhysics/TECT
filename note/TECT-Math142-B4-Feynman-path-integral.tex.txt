\section*{Math142 (Round B4): TECT-Internal Feynman Path Integral}

\subsection*{0. Executive Summary}

This note constructs the \emph{Feynman path integral} formulation of TECT quantum field theory.
Starting from the Fock-space Hamiltonian (Math140), we perform a Wick rotation to imaginary time
(Euclidean formalism), derive the partition function, and extract correlation functions via functional derivatives.

The path integral provides an alternative, \emph{functional} description of TECT quantization,
complementing the Fock-space picture. It also naturally incorporates interactions (beyond Hartree)
and is essential for computing anomaly contributions (Math143).

\subsection*{1. Wick Rotation and Euclidean Formalism}

\subsubsection*{§1.1 Real-time to imaginary-time transformation}

In the Schrödinger picture, the evolution operator is:
$$\hat{U}(t) = e^{-i \hat{H} t / \hbar}.$$

For a \emph{partition function} (thermal or quantum), we define:
$$Z(\beta) = \text{Tr}[e^{-\beta \hat{H}}],$$
where $\beta = 1/(k_B T)$ is the inverse temperature. Setting $\beta = \hbar/E_0$ (Euclidean time extent),
we identify:
$$\tau := i t \quad \text{(imaginary time)},$$
and the evolution operator becomes:
$$e^{-i \hat{H} t / \hbar} \to e^{-\hat{H} \tau / \hbar}.$$

\subsubsection*{§1.2 Euclidean action}

In imaginary time, the classical Lagrangian density becomes a \emph{Euclidean Hamiltonian density}:
$$\mathcal{L}[\Psi, \partial_t \Psi] \to -\mathcal{H}_E[\Psi, \partial_\tau \Psi],$$
where:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{H}_E &= \frac{\rho_{\rm cond}}{2} |\partial_\tau \Psi|^2 + \frac{Y}{2} |\nabla^2 \Psi|^2
    + \text{(potential terms)}. \label{eq:eucl_ham}
\end{align}

The \emph{Euclidean action} is:
\begin{align}
  S_E[\Psi] &= \int_0^\beta d\tau \int d^3 x \, \mathcal{H}_E[\Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau)]. \label{eq:eucl_action}
\end{align}

Note: The action is now \emph{real} and positive-definite (imaginary-time formulation is "well-defined" in
a probabilistic sense, unlike real-time QFT which has oscillating exponentials).

\subsubsection*{§1.3 Partition function in imaginary time}

The quantum partition function is:
\begin{align}
  Z &= \int \mathcal{D}\Psi \, e^{-S_E[\Psi] / \hbar}, \label{eq:path_integral_Z}
\end{align}
where the functional integral is over all field configurations $\Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau)$ with $\tau \in [0, \beta]$,
subject to periodic boundary conditions:
$$\Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau=0) = \Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau=\beta).$$

\subsection*{2. Correlation Functions and Generating Functional}

\subsubsection*{§2.1 Green's functions}

Define the \emph{connected $n$-point Green's function}:
\begin{align}
  G^{(n)}_{\rm con}(\mathbf{x}_1, \tau_1; \ldots; \mathbf{x}_n, \tau_n)
  &:= \frac{1}{Z} \int \mathcal{D}\Psi \, e^{-S_E[\Psi]/\hbar} \, \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \tau_1) \cdots \Psi(\mathbf{x}_n, \tau_n)
    - \text{(disconnected)}.
\end{align}

The \emph{two-point function} (Feynman propagator in imaginary time) is:
\begin{align}
  G^{(2)}(\mathbf{x}, \tau; \mathbf{x}', \tau')
  &:= \frac{1}{Z} \int \mathcal{D}\Psi \, e^{-S_E[\Psi]/\hbar} \, \Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau) \Psi(\mathbf{x}', \tau'). \label{eq:2pt_fn}
\end{align}

For the free theory (Hartree), this is:
\begin{align}
  G_0^{(2)}(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}', \tau - \tau')
  &= \int \frac{d^3 k}{(2\pi)^3} e^{i\mathbf{k} \cdot (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}')}
    \times \frac{e^{-\omega_\mathbf{k} |\tau - \tau'|}}{2 \omega_\mathbf{k}}, \quad \omega_\mathbf{k} > 0. \label{eq:free_2pt}
\end{align}

\subsubsection*{§2.2 Generating functional}

Introduce a \emph{source field} $J(\mathbf{x}, \tau)$ and define the \emph{generating functional}:
\begin{align}
  Z[J] &:= \int \mathcal{D}\Psi \, \exp\left( -\frac{S_E[\Psi]}{\hbar}
    + \frac{1}{\hbar} \int d^3 x \, d\tau \, J(\mathbf{x}, \tau) \Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau) \right). \label{eq:gen_func}
\end{align}

Then:
\begin{align}
  G^{(n)}(\mathbf{x}_1, \tau_1; \ldots; \mathbf{x}_n, \tau_n)
  &= \frac{\hbar^n}{Z[0]} \frac{\delta^n Z[J]}{\delta J(\mathbf{x}_1, \tau_1) \cdots \delta J(\mathbf{x}_n, \tau_n)} \bigg|_{J=0}. \label{eq:deriv_gen_func}
\end{align}

\subsection*{3. Loop Expansion and Perturbation Theory}

\subsubsection*{§3.1 Saddle-point expansion (tree level)}

At tree level, the path integral is dominated by the saddle points of the action (classical field configurations
satisfying the Euler-Lagrange equations):
$$\frac{\delta S_E[\Psi]}{\delta \Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau)} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{(Hartree equations)}.$$

The Hartree ground state corresponds to a constant saddle point:
$$\Psi_{\rm cl}(\mathbf{x}, \tau) = \phi_0 \quad \text{(time-independent condensate).}$$

The tree-level contribution to the partition function is:
$$Z_{\rm tree} = e^{-S_E[\phi_0] / \hbar}.$$

\subsubsection*{§3.2 One-loop Gaussian fluctuations}

Expand around the Hartree saddle point:
$$\Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau) = \phi_0 + \delta \Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau),$$
where $\delta \Psi$ is a small fluctuation.

To one-loop, the action becomes:
\begin{align}
  S_E[\phi_0 + \delta \Psi] &\approx S_E[\phi_0] + \frac{1}{2} \int d^3 x \, d\tau \, d^3 x' \, d\tau'
    \, \delta \Psi(\mathbf{x}, \tau) K^{(2)}(\mathbf{x}, \tau; \mathbf{x}', \tau') \delta \Psi(\mathbf{x}', \tau'),
\end{align}
where $K^{(2)}$ is the \emph{Hessian} (second functional derivative of the action at the saddle point).

The one-loop contribution is:
\begin{align}
  Z_{\rm 1-loop} = \frac{e^{-S_E[\phi_0] / \hbar}}{(\det K^{(2)})^{1/2}}.
\end{align}

The determinant can be computed via Feynman diagrams (loops of virtual shell-mode excitations).

\subsubsection*{§3.3 Feynman diagrams}

For the two-body interaction in TECT (cubic terms, related to the Brazovskii coupling constants),
the one-loop Feynman diagrams are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item **Bubble diagram** (two propagators, one vertex): contributes to the effective Hamiltonian.
\item **Self-energy diagram** (mode renormalization): contributes to $\omega_\mathbf{k}^{\rm ren} = \omega_\mathbf{k} + \Delta \omega_\mathbf{k}$.
\end{enumerate}

These diagrams are identical to those computed in Math119–Math122 (one-loop RG analysis), confirming
consistency between the Fock-space and path-integral formulations.

\subsection*{4. Saddle-Point Approximation and Onsager-Machlup Theory}

\subsubsection*{§4.1 Classical equations of motion}

The stationary phase of the path integral yields the classical equations of motion:
$$\frac{\delta S_E}{\delta \Psi} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \partial_\tau \Psi = \frac{\delta \mathcal{H}_E}{\delta \Psi^*}.$$

In real time (inverse Wick rotation $\tau = it$), this becomes:
$$\partial_t \Psi = i \frac{\delta \mathcal{H}_E}{\delta \Psi^*},$$
which is the \emph{time-dependent Hartree equation}, matching Math98 (Onsager-Machlup formulation).

\subsubsection*{§4.2 Hamiltonian trajectory and phase space}

The classical Hamiltonian gives a trajectory in phase space $(\Psi, \dot{\Psi})$. The Hartree ground state
is a \emph{stationary point} of this trajectory (zero velocity).

Quantum fluctuations around the ground state are described by the one-loop saddle-point expansion.

\subsection*{5. Coupling to External Fields and Functional Derivatives}

\subsubsection*{§5.1 Electromagnetic coupling}

To extract the gauge-theory structure (Pillars 4, gauge groups), couple TECT to external gauge fields:
$$S_E[\Psi, A_\mu] := S_E[\Psi] + S_{\rm gauge}[A_\mu, \Psi],$$
where $S_{\rm gauge}$ contains the covariant derivatives and field-strength tensors.

Functional derivatives with respect to $A_\mu$ yield the current expectation values:
$$\langle J^\mu(\mathbf{x}, \tau) \rangle = \frac{1}{Z} \frac{\delta Z[A_\mu]}{\delta A_\mu(\mathbf{x}, \tau)}.$$

\subsubsection*{§5.2 Stress-energy tensor}

Similarly, couple to the gravitational metric $g_{\mu\nu}$:
$$S_E[\Psi, g_{\mu\nu}] := S_E[\Psi] + S_{\rm gravity}[g_{\mu\nu}, \Psi].$$

The stress-energy tensor is:
$$\langle T_{\mu\nu}(\mathbf{x}, \tau) \rangle = \frac{2}{\sqrt{-g}} \frac{\delta S_E}{\delta g^{\mu\nu}(\mathbf{x}, \tau)}.$$

This connection to gravity is formalized in Pillar 3 / Pillar 10 (Math45, Math110, Math128).

\subsection*{6. Connection between Path Integral and Fock Space}

\subsubsection*{§6.1 LSZ reduction formula}

The LSZ (Lehmann-Symanzik-Zimmermann) reduction formula connects \emph{correlation functions} in the path
integral to \emph{S-matrix elements} in the Fock space:
\begin{align}
  \langle \text{out}, p_1 \cdots p_n | \text{in}, q_1 \cdots q_m \rangle
  &\propto \text{(product of on-shell residues)} \times \text{(amputated Green's function)}.
\end{align}

In the elastic-scattering limit (no particle production), this reduces the computation of Fock-space matrix
elements to functional derivatives of the generating functional.

\subsubsection*{§6.2 Equivalence of formalisms}

The Fock-space quantization (Math139–141) and the path-integral quantization (Math142) are equivalent
representations of the same quantum theory. They differ in computational convenience:
\begin{enumerate}
\item **Fock space**: Natural for low-energy excitations; easy to compute matrix elements.
\item **Path integral**: Natural for non-perturbative effects; manifestly covariant; essential for anomaly calculations.
\end{enumerate}

We use both formulations throughout the branch-A programme.

\subsection*{7. Continuum Limit and Renormalization Group}

\subsubsection*{§7.1 Lattice path integral}

On the lattice, the path integral is:
\begin{align}
  Z_{\rm lat}(h) = \int \mathcal{D}\Psi^{(h)} \, \exp\left( -\frac{S_E^{(h)}[\Psi^{(h)}]}{\hbar} \right),
\end{align}
where the action has explicit lattice spacing dependence (finite-difference approximations to derivatives).

\subsubsection*{§7.2 Continuum limit}

As $h \to 0$, correlation functions obey scaling laws (renormalization group):
\begin{align}
  G^{(2)}(h, \mathbf{k}, \omega) &= G^{(2)}_\infty(\mathbf{k}, \omega) + O(h^2),
\end{align}
where $G^{(2)}_\infty$ is the continuum limit.

The Richardson extrapolation (Math127, Math131) provides a systematic procedure to extract continuum results
from lattice data at multiple $h$ values.

\subsection*{8. Devil's-Advocate Self-Test (CLAUDE.md §6.3.1)}

\subsubsection*{Objection α: Wick rotation legitimacy — is the imaginary-time formalism justified for non-equilibrium dynamics?}

\textbf{Claim}: The Wick rotation is standard for \emph{equilibrium statistical mechanics} (thermal partition function).
But TECT is describing a \emph{real physical system} undergoing quantum fluctuations (non-equilibrium).
Can we rotate to imaginary time and trust the result?

\textbf{Counter-argument (DISMISSED)}:
The Wick rotation is a \emph{mathematical transformation} that converts the quantum time evolution operator
$e^{-i \hat{H} t}$ into the thermal operator $e^{-\beta \hat{H}}$. Formally, we compute the path integral in
imaginary time, and then analytically continue back to real time (Schwinger functions $\to$ Wightman functions).

For \emph{correlation functions} (two-point functions, propagators, etc.), this analytic continuation is
rigorous (Osterwalder-Schrader axioms in constructive QFT). The Feynman rules derived from the imaginary-time
path integral reproduce the correct real-time dynamics.

\textbf{Closure}: DISMISSED. The Wick rotation and back-rotation are standard and rigorous (Peskin-Schroeder, §9).

\subsubsection*{Objection β: Non-linear dispersion and large-field behavior — does the path integral converge?}

\textbf{Claim}: The Euclidean action (Eq. \ref{eq:eucl_action}) must be positive-definite for the path integral
to converge (no oscillations, standard probabilistic measure). But at large fields $|\Psi|$, the quartic and higher
potential terms may change sign, making the action unbounded below.

\textbf{Counter-argument (VALID, with bounds)}:
The Brazovskii potential is:
$$V(\Psi) = \frac{\mu^2}{2} |\Psi|^2 + \frac{\lambda}{4} |\Psi|^4 + \frac{\gamma}{6} |\Psi|^6.$$

At the operating point ($\mu^2 = 0.26 > 0$, $\lambda = -0.43 < 0$, $\gamma = 1.62 > 0$), the potential is
\emph{stabilized by the $\gamma$ term} at large $|\Psi|$:
$$V(|\Psi|) \to \frac{\gamma}{6} |\Psi|^6 \quad \text{as} \quad |\Psi| \to \infty.$$

Thus, the action is positive-definite and the path integral converges (Gaussian measure in the functional-integral sense).

\textbf{Verification}: The numerical simulations (Math55, Math82-H) show a stable BCC ground state with no runaway solutions,
confirming that the potential is bounded below.

\textbf{Closure}: VALID objection, with documented stability bounds. The sixth-order term ensures convergence.

\subsubsection*{Objection γ: Gauge fixing and Faddeev-Popov ghosts — are we handling gauge symmetry correctly?}

\textbf{Claim}: If TECT has emergent gauge symmetries (Pillars 4, 5, etc.), the path integral has overcounting
of gauge-equivalent configurations. This requires \emph{gauge fixing} and introduction of \emph{ghost fields}
(Faddeev-Popov). The current formulation doesn't explicitly include ghosts.

\textbf{Counter-argument (VALID, deferred to later rounds)}:
Yes, gauge-symmetry treatment is a \emph{crucial aspect} of quantum field theory. However, at the stage of
Math142 (basic path-integral formulation), we are working with the \emph{scalar field} $\Psi$ in the
\emph{Coulomb gauge} (or other fixed gauge). The gauge structure emerges later via the \emph{topological} reduction
(Pillars 4–5) and the \emph{emergent gauge fields} (Berry connection, etc.).

Gauge fixing and ghost fields will be incorporated in the \emph{full TECT path integral} (when we couple to gravity
and gauge fields in Pillar 10 / Stage 2). For the current round (B1–B6), we focus on the \emph{scalar field path integral}.

\textbf{Closure}: VALID and DEFERRED. Gauge fixing is an advanced topic, handled in subsequent rounds (Math143–144)
and in the full quantum programme (Stage 2–3).
