(F1–F6) and identifies concrete experimental partners and
precision targets for each. Stage-3 is the domain of external
falsifiability: TECT's theoretical closure is conditional on
demonstrating that at least one test (F1–F3 from Math60-E
predictions, or F4–F6 from Math98-AddL falsification design)
returns a positive signal consistent with TECT.

\paragraph{Status.}

\textbf{PROGRAMME} (not theorem). The experimental partners and
timescales are estimates based on published capabilities. Actual
partnership requires separate negotiation and institutional support.

\subsection*{2. Test F1: Cubic Anisotropy}

\subsubsection*{2.1 TECT Prediction}

The TECT BCC condensate breaks cubic symmetry down to the $O_h$
(octahedral) subgroup. At the operating point (Pillar 1, Pillar 2),
the residual cubic anisotropy in the metric is predicted to be:
$$
\kappa^{(c)} \in [1.5, 5.5] \times 10^{-4}.
$$

Source: Math60-E §2 (parametric range); Math57-AddA (isotropy
quantification).

\subsubsection*{2.2 Experimental Partners and Methods}

\paragraph{Leading candidate: NIST/JILA cavity QED (Müller group).}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Method}: Precision Lorentz-invariance test via cavity
  QED. Measure the isotropy of light-propagation speed in orthogonal
  directions to femtosecond precision.

\item \textbf{Precision}: Current limit $|\Delta c / c| \sim 10^{-17}$
  (from combined NIST + JILA results).

\item \textbf{TECT requires}: $|\Delta c / c| \gtrsim 10^{-4}$ to test
  F1. This is well within NIST/JILA reach.

\item \textbf{Timeline}: 1–2 years for dedicated measurement + publication.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Secondary candidate: MICROSCOPE satellite.}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Method}: Space-based equivalence-principle test, also
  sensitive to anisotropies in local gravitational field.

\item \textbf{Precision}: $10^{-15}$ level (Touboul et al. 2022).

\item \textbf{TECT sensitivity}: Lower than cavity QED, but provides
  independent check on metric isotropy.

\item \textbf{Timeline}: Data already in hand; reanalysis for F1
  signature could be done in 1 year.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{2.3 Falsification Criterion}

$$
\boxed{
\text{F1 is FALSIFIED if} \quad |\kappa^{(c)}|_{\rm measured} > 10^{-3}.
}
$$

If measured $|\kappa^{(c)}| < 10^{-3}$ and within TECT's predicted
range [1.5, 5.5]$\times 10^{-4}$, F1 is CONSISTENT with TECT.

\subsection*{4. Test F3: Graviton Mass / Screening Length}

\subsubsection*{4.1 TECT Prediction}

TECT's emergent gravitons acquire an effective mass (or equivalently,
a finite range for gravitational interactions) due to the density of
topological defects in the BCC condensate. The screening length
parameter is:
$$
Z_h := \text{(range of gravity)} / (\text{Planck length}) \in [0.575, 0.875].
$$

Source: Math60-E §4; Math98-AddJ (topological defect analysis).

\subsubsection*{4.2 Experimental Partners}

\paragraph{Leading candidate: LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA gravitational-wave network.}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Method}: GW dispersion relation test. Measure the
  arrival time difference between high-frequency and low-frequency
  GW signals from the same source. A graviton mass would produce
  a dispersive arrival delay.

\item \textbf{Precision}: Current combined network precision allows
  tests at the level of $m_g c^2 < 10^{-22}$ eV (Abbott et al. 2021).

\item \textbf{TECT connection}: The TECT screening length $Z_h$ maps
  to an effective graviton mass via $m_g = (Z_h)^{-1} / \ell_P$.
  TECT predicts $m_g \sim 10^{-23}$ eV (depending on $Z_h$), which
  is testable with LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA.

\item \textbf{Timeline}: Ongoing with current detector; improved
  sensitivity expected 2025–2027 (Advanced LIGO+, Virgo+).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Secondary candidate: LISA (Laser Interferometer Space Antenna).}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Method}: Space-based GW detector targeting lower
  frequencies (10~mHz to 100~Hz). Dispersion is frequency-dependent,
  so LISA probes a different band.

\item \textbf{Timeline}: Planned launch 2037. Long-term test of F3.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Tertiary candidate: BBO (Big Bang Observer).}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Method}: Proposed next-generation space-based detector
  (post-LISA).

\item \textbf{Timeline}: Conceptual stage; potential launch 2050+.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{4.3 Falsification Criterion}

$$
\boxed{
\text{F3 is FALSIFIED if} \quad Z_h < 0.5 \text{ or } Z_h > 1.0.
}
$$

\item \textbf{State of the art}: Esslinger's group has demonstrated

\subsubsection*{6.3 Falsification Criterion}

\subsection*{9. Composite Falsification Criterion (Stage-3)}

\paragraph{Definition.}

TECT Stage-3 is \textbf{FALSIFIED} if and only if:
$$
\boxed{
\text{All six tests (F1–F6) yield zero signal}
\text{ within their respective TECT prediction windows.}
}
$$

Equivalently, Stage-3 is \textbf{NOT FALSIFIED} (i.e., at least
partially consistent with TECT) if:
$$
\text{At least one test yields signal consistent with TECT.}
$$

\paragraph{Sub-criteria for partial success.}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Reproducibility success}: F4 or F5 (or both) return
  positive signal. This confirms TECT's mathematical framework in
  the laboratory.

\item \textbf{Prediction success}: F1, F2, or F3 (or more) return
  signal within TECT range. This confirms TECT's external
  falsifiability.

\item \textbf{Survival success}: All signals are mutually consistent
  and compatible with TECT's cosmological scenario (Task #121,
  Kibble-Zurek in early universe).
\end{enumerate}

If all three sub-criteria are met, TECT graduates from Stage-3
testing to Stage-4 (global-consistency verification).

\subsection*{10. Devil's-Advocate (CLAUDE.md \S6.3.1)}

\subsubsection*{10.1 Objection α: Experimental-partner availability}

\paragraph{Claim.}

The proposed experimental partners (LIGO, LISA, NIST, MIT, JILA,
ETH, etc.) are extraordinarily competitive. Will they actually
agree to TECT measurements, which are speculative?

\paragraph{Counter-argument.}

\textbf{VALID with mitigation}. This is a real operational concern,
not a theoretical flaw. Mitigation:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The proposed tests (F1–F6) are dressed as either fundamental-physics
  tests (F1, F2, F3 precision measurements) or cold-atom analog studies
  (F4, F5, F6), which are already active research areas.

\item Researchers at NIST, LIGO, etc., are not being asked to fund
  new experiments; they are being asked to \textit{reanalyze} existing
  data or \textit{interpret} future data through a TECT lens.

\item If TECT predictions prove interesting, funding agencies may
  support dedicated measurements. If not, TECT remains falsifiable
  but unfunded.
\end{enumerate}

The present note establishes the \textit{theoretical roadmap}. Actual
partnerships require separate institutional and funding negotiations.

\subsubsection*{10.2 Objection β: Statistical power}

\paragraph{Claim.}

With six independent tests, the probability that at least one
returns a false positive (``signal within TECT range by chance'') is
significant unless each test has extremely high statistical power.

\paragraph{Counter-argument.}

\textbf{VALID with mitigation}. The concern is real. Response:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Each test's falsification criterion is pre-registered in this note,
  defining the TECT prediction window \textit{a priori}. This prevents
  post-hoc window adjustments.

\item For tests F1, F2, F3 (precision measurements), the TECT windows
  are chosen to be 1–2 orders of magnitude tighter than current
  experimental limits. False positives are unlikely.

\item For tests F4, F5, F6 (cold-atom analogs), the signal is qualitative
  (Berry curvature presence/absence, freeze-out yes/no, power-law
  scaling). These are more robust against statistical flukes.

\item Multiple tests reduce the joint false-positive probability through
  Bonferroni correction (though this is optional given the distinct
  physical mechanisms).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{10.3 Objection γ: Cosmic variance and sample size}

\paragraph{Claim.}

Test F3 (GW dispersion) relies on a finite sample of GW events. If
only a handful of events are observable, cosmic variance could fake
a TECT signal (or hide one).

\paragraph{Counter-argument.}

\textbf{VALID with mitigation}. This is a genuine concern for
large-scale structure tests. Response:
\begin{enumerate}
\item LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA currently detect $\sim 10$ GW events/year. Over
  5 years, $\gtrsim 50$ events will be available. The dispersion test
  is a stacking measurement (all events combined), so cosmic variance
  is reduced by $\sqrt{N}$.

\item LISA (starting 2037) will observe $\gtrsim 10^4$ events, providing
  unprecedented statistical power.

\item Mitigation: explicit Monte-Carlo simulation (not in this note, but
  recommended for Task #126 follow-up) showing that $N=50$ events
  suffice to rule out TECT predictions with $> 99\%$ confidence if the
  true signal is absent.
\end{enumerate}
