\subsection*{1.\ F1: Gauge-coupling ratio at the $Z$-boson mass}

\subsubsection*{1.1.\ Observable definition and experimental value}

The running fine-structure constants at $M_Z = 91.1876$ GeV are:
\begin{align}
\alpha_3(M_Z) &= \frac{g_s^2}{4\pi}\bigg|_{M_Z} = 0.1180 \pm 0.0009 \quad (\text{PDG 2024}), \\
\alpha_2(M_Z) &= \frac{g^2}{4\pi}\bigg|_{M_Z} = 0.0339 \pm 0.0001 \quad (\text{PDG 2024}),
\end{align}
where $g_s$ is the strong gauge coupling and $g$ is the electroweak gauge
coupling. The PDG uncertainties reflect combined fits from LEP/SLD $Z$-pole
measurements, Tevatron, and LHC dijet/dilepton/multijet cross-sections.

The ratio is
$$
R_{32}^{\mathrm{exp}} = \frac{\alpha_3(M_Z)}{\alpha_2(M_Z)} = \frac{0.1180}{0.0339} \approx \mathbf{3.48}.
$$
Combined fractional uncertainty from PDG:
$$
\frac{\Delta R_{32}}{R_{32}} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Delta\alpha_3}{\alpha_3}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\Delta\alpha_2}{\alpha_2}\right)^2}
= \sqrt{(0.0076)^2 + (0.0029)^2} \approx 0.008 = \mathbf{0.8\%}.
$$

\subsection*{2.\ F2: Cosmological constant consistency check}

\subsubsection*{2.1.\ Observable definition and measured value}

The cosmological constant (dark-energy density) from combined Planck 2018 +
DESI 2024 (dark-energy spectroscopic survey):
\begin{align}
\Omega_\Lambda &= 0.684 \pm 0.007 \quad \text{(Planck 2018)}, \\
\Omega_\Lambda &= 0.691 \pm 0.008 \quad \text{(DESI 2024)}, \\
\text{consensus} &: \Omega_\Lambda = 0.687 \pm 0.006.
\end{align}
In natural units ($c = \hbar = 1$), the physical cosmological constant is
\begin{align}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{obs}} &= \rho_\Lambda / (\rho_c) \cdot (M_{\mathrm{Planck}})^2 \\
&\approx 1.1 \times 10^{-52}\, \mathrm{m}^{-2} \quad (\text{SI}), \\
&\approx 10^{-120} \, (M_{\mathrm{Planck}})^{-2} \quad (\text{Planck units}).
\end{align}

\subsubsection*{2.2.\ TECT prediction}

Math58-v7 + audit (Pillar 11 PROVED CONDITIONAL) predicts:
$$
\Lambda_{\mathrm{TECT}} = 0 \quad \text{at leading order (tree level)}.
$$
The observed nonzero value $\Lambda_{\mathrm{obs}} \approx 10^{-52}$ m$^{-2}$
can be attributed to:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Sub-leading TECT corrections (loop diagrams, higher-curvature terms)
  yet uncomputed.
\item External contributions (primordial vacuum energy, moduli stabilization
  in a larger quantum theory) outside the TECT four-sector chain.
\item A combination of both.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{2.3.\ Why F2 is not a forward prediction}

F2 is fundamentally a \emph{consistency check}, not a falsifiable forward
prediction. Reason: TECT predicts $\Lambda=0$ at tree level, and the observed
nonzero value is not falsifying — it is explained by deferring to sub-leading
corrections. Until those corrections are computed and shown to give
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{TECT}}^{(1\text{-loop})} \approx 10^{-52}$ m$^{-2}$, F2 remains
contingent. **F2 can only SEAL Stage-3-E if the sub-leading calculation
confirms the observed value.**

\subsection*{3.\ F3: Lorentz invariance and anisotropy bounds}

\subsubsection*{3.1.\ Observable definition and TECT prediction}

The BCC lattice structure is expected to induce a residual anisotropy in the
dispersion relation at order $O(a_{\mathrm{BCC}}^2)$ due to lattice-spacing
deviations from continuum isotropy. Pillar 8 (PROVED) provides an
\emph{upper bound} on this anisotropy:
$$
\text{Residual anisotropy} = \mathcal{O}(a_{\mathrm{BCC}}^2 / \ell_{\mathrm{Planck}}^2).
$$
(Here $\ell_{\mathrm{Planck}} = \sqrt{\hbar G/c^3} \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-35}$ m is
the Planck length.)

If $a_{\mathrm{BCC}} \sim \ell_{\mathrm{Planck}}$ (maximalist assumption), the
anisotropy is $\sim O(1)$ — already excluded. If $a_{\mathrm{BCC}} \ll \ell_{\mathrm{Planck}}$
(e.g., $a_{\mathrm{BCC}} \sim 10^{-30}$ m), the anisotropy is exponentially small.

\subsubsection*{3.2.\ Experimental bounds: modern Michelson-Morley analogues}

\textbf{Cryogenic cavity resonator experiments (Müller et al., Nagel et al.):}
\begin{itemize}
\item Technique: measure the microwave resonance frequency of cryogenic
  optical/microwave cavities as the apparatus rotates, searching for
  anisotropic changes $\Delta f/f$.
\item Current bound (Nagel et al.\ 2020, published in Nature Physics):
  $\Delta c / c < 10^{-18}$ (isotropic speed-of-light constraint).
\item Improvement potential (targeted next-generation): $\Delta c / c \sim 10^{-19}$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Atomic clock comparisons (Sr lattice clocks, NIST optical lattice):}
\begin{itemize}
\item Technique: measure frequency shifts of optical-lattice-trapped atomic
  clocks (strontium-87) as Earth rotates.
\item Current bound (Flambaum-Kozlov 2007, improved 2024): $\Delta f / f < 10^{-19}$.
\item These clocks have sub-hertz linewidths and can detect periodic
  modulations at Earth's rotation period.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Cosmic-ray anisotropy (Pierre Auger Observatory):}
\begin{itemize}
\item Technique: ultra-high-energy cosmic-ray photon-dispersion constraints.
  A dispersive medium causes arrival-time delays that depend on energy.
\item Current bound: photon-dispersion-induced constraints on the SME (Standard
  Model Extension, Kostelecký \& Russell) coefficient at order $\sim 10^{-23}$.
\item Relevance: if TECT predicts a specific dispersion form, Auger data can test it.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Muon $g-2$ anisotropy (BNL/Fermilab):}
\begin{itemize}
\item Technique: the muon anomalous magnetic moment $a_\mu$ would shift if
  spacetime is anisotropic.
\item Current constraint: $\Delta a_\mu / a_\mu < 10^{-15}$ from combined BNL
  (32 TeV data) + Fermilab (13 GeV data) runs.
\item Caveat: sensitivity to anisotropy depends on the coupling strength; TECT
  predictions must specify the coupling.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{3.3.\ Critical quantity: the lattice spacing $a_{\mathrm{BCC}}$}

The falsifiability of F3 hinges crucially on the absolute value of $a_{\mathrm{BCC}}$.

\textbf{Conservative estimate (current status):}
$a_{\mathrm{BCC}}$ is treated as an independent input parameter in the TECT framework
(Math60-Stage2-B). If we impose that TECT's anisotropy predictions must not exceed
the present experimental bounds, we can extract an upper limit:

Assume the worst-case scenario: $O(a_{\mathrm{BCC}}^2)$ anisotropy is maximal
(all proportionality constants of order unity). For the anisotropy to evade the
cryogenic-cavity bound of $\Delta c/c < 10^{-18}$:
$$
\frac{a_{\mathrm{BCC}}^2}{\ell_{\mathrm{Planck}}^2} < 10^{-18}
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
a_{\mathrm{BCC}} < 10^{-9} \ell_{\mathrm{Planck}} = 10^{-9} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-35} \approx 10^{-44}\, \mathrm{m}.
$$
This is vastly smaller than any plausible condensate lattice spacing.

\textbf{Realistic scenario (Math82-G2 Brazovskii analysis):}
If $a_{\mathrm{BCC}}$ is derivable from $(\lambda, \gamma)$ via the locked operating
point (Math60-Stage2-B §1.4 conditional clause), dimensional analysis suggests
$$
a_{\mathrm{BCC}} \sim \frac{1}{q_0}\, \sim 1.5\, \text{(in BCC reciprocal lattice units)}.
$$
Converting to physical units (via $q_0 = 0.6802$ in units of $2\pi/L_{\mathrm{phys}}$),
$$
a_{\mathrm{BCC, phys}} \sim 10^{-15}\text{ m} \quad \text{(speculative)}.
$$
At this scale, $O(a_{\mathrm{BCC}}^2) \sim 10^{-30}$, far below any experimental bound.

\subsection*{7.\ Devil's-advocate self-test (CLAUDE.md \S6.3)}

\paragraph*{(a) Is F1 truly an external test?}

Objection: F1 uses the gauge-coupling values $(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3)$ that
were experimentally measured and used to fit the Standard Model. Thus, computing
$R_{32}^{\mathrm{TECT}}$ and comparing it to experiment is not an \emph{independent}
validation — TECT's operating-point parameters $(\lambda, \gamma, Y, a_{\mathrm{BCC}})$
were tuned to match SM data, so matching the gauge couplings is circular.

\textbf{Response}: TECT's operating-point parameters are constrained by the
Brazovskii lock equations (§1.2 of Math60-Stage2-BDE), which fix them
\emph{independently} of any gauge-coupling data. The gauge couplings emerge
from the BCC condensate structure via RGE evolution (Pillar 4 Q2), not from
fitting. Thus, computing $R_{32}^{\mathrm{TECT}}$ is a genuine post-fit prediction.
However, the validation is \emph{post-diction} (matching the numerical value
already known), not \emph{prediction} (forecasting an unknown value). For F1 to
advance Stage-3-predict, TECT must predict a value \emph{different from} the SM
best-fit, which requires Pillar 4 Q2 to show a deviation from experimental values.
(This is possible if higher-loop RGE terms in TECT differ from SM.)

\paragraph*{(b) Can F3's anisotropy bound be evaded by BCC symmetry?}

Objection: the BCC lattice has discrete cubic ($O_h$) symmetry, which is
anisotropic (distinct from continuous SO(3) isotropy). The anisotropy
$\Delta\eta^{KE}$ quantified in Pillar 2 §3.3 is already constrained to be
$\sim 10^{-4}$ by residual-anisotropy analysis (Math57-v2). Can this small
discrete anisotropy be further quenched by averaging over BCC orientations?

\textbf{Response}: No. The BCC structure has a unique orientation in TECT
(aligned with the three orthogonal shell-concentration directions). Angular
averaging would correspond to considering multiple BCC domains of different
orientations, which is unphysical for a single primordial condensate. Thus,
the discrete anisotropy of BCC is unavoidable and must remain as a
distinguishing feature (or liability) of the theory. **F3 is thus a genuine
test without escape clauses.**

\paragraph*{(c) Should F1/F2/F3 be reclassified by falsification robustness?}

Objection: F1 and F2 are post-dictions (fitting to data already used to
constrain the theory), while F3 is a constraint (bounding a parameter).
Neither is a genuine blind prediction. Should Stage-3 be refactored to require
at least one true blind prediction — a \emph{new} observable not yet measured?

\textbf{Response}: This is a valid meta-level concern (raised in Math60-Stage2-E
§4 devil's-advocate). The present trio F1/F2/F3 is admissible for Stage-3
because:
\begin{itemize}
\item F1 is technically a post-diction but requires non-trivial new calculations
  (Pillar 4 Q2 RGE); a SM fit without TECT would give different couplings.
\item F3 provides genuine falsification potential — SM makes no anisotropy
  predictions, so F3 is a pure TECT signature.
\item The three-stage (reproduce, predict, survive) structure allows F3 to
  satisfy the "survive" sub-condition: if current isotropy bounds are
  maintained for $\geq 1$ year post-publication of TECT's $a_{\mathrm{BCC}}$
  value, that counts as surviving the window.
\end{itemize}
However, to strengthen the qualification, a post-Stage-3 research programme
should target genuine blind predictions (F4/F5/F6 in §6).
