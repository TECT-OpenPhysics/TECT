\subsection*{1. Classical Noether Currents of TECT}

\subsubsection*{§1.1 Translational symmetry and energy-momentum tensor}

Under spatial translations $\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{a}$, the Lagrangian is invariant:
$$\mathcal{L}[\Psi(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{a})] = \mathcal{L}[\Psi(\mathbf{x})].$$

The associated conserved current (Noether current) is the \emph{energy-momentum tensor}:
\begin{align}
  T_{\mu\nu} &:= \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial (\partial_\mu \Psi)} \partial_\nu \Psi
    - \mathcal{L} \delta_{\mu\nu}.
\end{align}

For TECT (Brazovskii Lagrangian, Math82), this is:
\begin{align}
  T_{\mu\nu} &= \rho_{\rm cond} \partial_\mu \Psi^* \partial_\nu \Psi + \text{h.c.} - \mathcal{L} \delta_{\mu\nu}.
\end{align}

\subsubsection*{§1.2 Global $U(1)$ phase symmetry}

Under phase rotations $\Psi \to e^{i\alpha} \Psi$ (where $\alpha$ is a global constant), the Lagrangian is invariant:
$$\mathcal{L}[e^{i\alpha} \Psi] = \mathcal{L}[\Psi].$$

The associated Noether current is:
\begin{align}
  J^\mu_{\rm U(1)} &:= i (\Psi^* \partial^\mu \Psi - \Psi \partial^\mu \Psi^*).
\end{align}

At the quantum level, this becomes the \emph{number current} (gauge current for the emergent $U(1)_{\rm EM}$ in Pillar 4).

\subsubsection*{§1.3 Chiral structure (Pillar 7)}

Define left/right chiral projectors:
\begin{align}
  \Psi_L &:= \frac{1 - \gamma^5}{2} \Psi, \quad \Psi_R := \frac{1 + \gamma^5}{2} \Psi,
\end{align}
where $\gamma^5 = i \gamma^0 \gamma^1 \gamma^2 \gamma^3$ (Dirac matrix).

For TECT at the operating point, the condensate is \emph{parity-even}:
$$\Psi \to \Psi \quad \text{under} \quad \mathbf{x} \to -\mathbf{x}},$$
so classically there is no \emph{intrinsic} chirality mixing (the BCC structure is non-chiral).

However, the \emph{emergent} fermion zeros (Pillar 7, Math10–14) carry chiral structure, which arises from
the \emph{topological} nature of the BCC modulation.

\subsection*{2. Fujikawa Method for Anomalies}

\subsubsection*{§2.1 Chiral transformation in quantum field theory}

Consider a \emph{local} chiral transformation:
\begin{align}
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}) \to e^{i\alpha(\mathbf{x}) \gamma^5} \Psi(\mathbf{x}), \label{eq:chiral_trans}
\end{align}
where $\alpha(\mathbf{x})$ is now a \emph{spacetime-dependent} parameter.

Classically, if $\alpha$ were constant, the Lagrangian would be invariant. With spacetime dependence,
there is a classical current divergence:
$$\partial_\mu J^\mu_5 = 0 \quad \text{(classically)}.$$

\subsubsection*{§2.2 Path-integral measure under chiral transformation}

Under the transformation Eq. \ref{eq:chiral_trans}, the scalar field measure transforms as:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{D}\Psi' = \mathcal{D}\Psi \cdot J[\alpha],
\end{align}
where $J[\alpha]$ is the \emph{Jacobian} (functional determinant):
\begin{align}
  J[\alpha] = \det \left[ \frac{\delta \Psi'(\mathbf{x})}{\delta \Psi(\mathbf{y})} \right].
\end{align}

\subsubsection*{§2.3 Fujikawa calculation}

The Jacobian can be computed using \emph{zeta-function regularization} or by summing over eigenmodes.
For a chiral transformation $\Psi' = e^{i\alpha \gamma^5} \Psi$, the eigenmode expansion gives:
\begin{align}
  \log J[\alpha] &= \text{Tr}[i \alpha \gamma^5 (\text{operator})].
\end{align}

At one-loop, the dominant contribution comes from the \emph{triangle diagram}:
\begin{align}
  \text{Tr}[\gamma^5] \times (\text{loop integrals}) &= 2 N_f \times (\text{chiral polarization loop}).
\end{align}

\subsubsection*{§2.4 Triangle anomaly result}

For a fermionic theory with $N_f$ flavors, the chiral anomaly is:
\begin{align}
  \partial_\mu J^\mu_5 &= \frac{N_f}{16 \pi^2} \text{Tr}[F_{\mu\nu} \tilde{F}^{\mu\nu}], \label{eq:triangle_anomaly}
\end{align}
where $F_{\mu\nu}$ is the field strength of the emergent gauge field, and $\tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} F_{\rho\sigma}$
is its dual.

For TECT, $N_f$ is the number of \emph{chiral zero modes} arising from the BCC topological structure (Pillar 7).

\subsection*{3. Ward Identities in TECT}

\subsubsection*{§3.1 General Ward identity structure}

For a global symmetry $U(1)_\Psi$ (phase rotation) that is \emph{classically conserved}, the Ward identity is:
\begin{align}
  \partial_\mu \langle J^\mu_{\rm U(1)}(\mathbf{x}) \rangle &= 0.
\end{align}

For an emergent gauge symmetry (e.g., $U(1)_{\rm EM}$ in Pillar 4), this becomes a constraint on the correlation
functions of charged fields.

\subsubsection*{§3.2 Charge conservation}

Define the total charge (integrated current):
\begin{align}
  Q = \int d^3 x \, J^0_{\rm U(1)}(\mathbf{x}, t).
\end{align}

In TECT, $Q$ is the total number of BCC condensate excitations (quanta). The Ward identity $\partial_\mu \langle J^\mu \rangle = 0$
ensures that this charge is conserved at each time slice.

\subsubsection*{§3.3 Chiral Ward identity (Pillar 7 check)}

the Ward identity is modified by the anomaly:
\begin{align}

For a \emph{parity-even} condensate (like TECT), the right-hand side vanishes in the vacuum state (no background gauge fields):
$$\langle \text{Tr}[F_{\mu\nu} \tilde{F}^{\mu\nu}] \rangle_{\rm vacuum} = 0.$$

Thus:
$$\partial_\mu \langle J^\mu_5 \rangle = 0 \quad \text{(chiral current conserved at quantum level)}.$$

\emph{exactly}. The zero modes are anomaly-free.

\textbf{Claim}: The anomaly calculation (Eq. \ref{eq:triangle_anomaly}) assumes that there are \emph{exact}

\textbf{Counter-argument (VALID, with finite-size analysis)}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The \emph{index theorem} (Atiyah-Singer) guarantees that the \emph{winding number} of the Dirac operator

\item In the \emph{continuum limit} $h \to 0$, the zero modes become exact. The Richardson extrapolation (Math127, Math131)
  controls the approach to the continuum.

\item For \emph{observable anomaly effects}, the finite-size suppression is of order $1/V$ (where $V$ is the volume).
  This goes to zero in the large-volume limit.
\end{enumerate}

Thus, the anomaly calculation is \emph{rigorous in the continuum limit and infinite-volume limit}.

\textbf{Closure}: VALID objection, with finite-size caveat. Anomaly effects are exact in the continuum; lattice
corrections are suppressed by $O(h^2)$ and $O(1/V)$.
