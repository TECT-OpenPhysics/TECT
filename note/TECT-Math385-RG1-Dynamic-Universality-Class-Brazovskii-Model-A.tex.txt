\subsection*{1. Field-theoretic ground: no conservation law for $\Psi$}

The canonical Brazovskii free energy
(Math373 Eq. (1), reproduced):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}[\Psi] = \int d^3 r \left[
  \tfrac{r}{2}\Psi^2 + \tfrac{Z}{2}|\nabla\Psi|^2
  + \tfrac{Y}{2}|\Delta\Psi|^2
  + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\Psi^4 + \tfrac{\gamma}{3}\Psi^6
\right]
\label{eq:F-canonical}
\end{equation}
treats $\Psi(\mathbf r)$ as a \emph{real scalar field}. The
internal symmetry group of \eqref{eq:F-canonical} is
$\mathbb{Z}_2$ (sign flip $\Psi \to -\Psi$, broken by the
$\Psi^6$ term being even but $\Psi^4$ also even -- actually
$\mathbb{Z}_2$ is preserved since all terms are even in $\Psi$).
\textbf{There is no continuous internal symmetry.}

\textbf{Lemma 1 (No conserved $\Psi$-current)}: For a real scalar
field with action invariant under at most a discrete $\mathbb{Z}_2$
internal symmetry, no continuous symmetry generator exists, so by
Noether's first theorem there is no internally-generated conserved
current $j^\mu_\Psi$. In particular,
$$\partial_t \Psi^2 + \partial_i (\text{no candidate}) \ne 0$$
and the spatial integral
$$Q_\Psi(t) := \int d^3 r\,\Psi(\mathbf r, t)$$
is \emph{not} a constant of motion under any TECT-canonical dynamics
that respects the gradient-flow structure of \eqref{eq:F-canonical}.
\hfill$\square$

\textbf{Consequence for dynamic classification}: The
Hohenberg--Halperin Model B (conserved order parameter) requires
$\partial_t \Psi = +\Gamma \nabla^2 (\delta\mathcal{F}/\delta\Psi)$,
which manifestly preserves $\int\Psi\,d^3r$. This is a
\emph{kinematic constraint} requiring an underlying conservation law,
which TECT does not have.

\subsection*{3. Static vs dynamic universality class -- decoupling}

A common conflation: ``TECT is in the Brazovskii \emph{static}
universality class (Math97), so its \emph{dynamic} class follows
automatically.'' This is wrong.

\textbf{Hohenberg--Halperin classification axes (1977)}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Static class}: determined by the form of
  $\mathcal{F}[\Psi]$ at the critical point (number of components,
  symmetry group, leading nonlinearity). For TECT, this is the
  isotropic Brazovskii class (Math97).
\item \textbf{Dynamic class}: determined by whether the order
  parameter is conserved (Model B/D/J) or non-conserved (Model A/C/H),
  and by what other slow modes (energy density, magnetization
  density, etc.) are coupled. Independent of static class.
\end{enumerate}

A given static class can host \emph{multiple} dynamic classes. For
example, the $XY$ static class hosts Model A (uniform spin) or
Model E (planar magnet with conserved magnetization) depending on
microscopic details.

\textbf{TECT specifically}: Math97 fixes static class
(iso-Brazovskii); Math145 A1 fixes dynamic class (Model A). They are
independent, and both have been \emph{independently} fixed at the
canonical level. RG1 cannot be relaxed by reading off static-class
statements.

\subsection*{4. Brazovskii ``Model A*'' refinement -- $z$-exponent
under shell kinetics}

The standard Hohenberg--Halperin Model A assumes a kinetic operator
$\Gamma\,k^2$ (relaxation rate proportional to $k^2$ at long
wavelength). The Brazovskii free energy \eqref{eq:F-canonical} has
a kinetic structure $r + Z k^2 + Y k^4$ which at the shell
wavenumber $|k| = q_0 := \sqrt{-Z/(2Y)}$ has minimum value
\begin{equation}
\omega_{\rm relax}(q_0) = r > 0
\end{equation}
(positive in the broken-symmetry phase $\mu^2 > -Y q_0^4$).

\textbf{Key observation}: The Brazovskii spectrum is
\emph{gapped} at the shell wavenumber. The relaxation rate at $q_0$
does NOT vanish at criticality (which is a $\mu^2$ shift, not a
$k$-direction softening). This is fundamentally different from a
standard $O(N)$ Model A where $\omega(k=0) \to 0$ at the critical
point.

\textbf{Implication for KZ scaling}: The standard KZ formula
$\hat\xi \sim \tau_Q^{\nu/(1+z\nu)}$ relies on
$\omega(\hat k) \to 0$ as $\mu^2 \to \mu^2_c$ with
$\hat k \to 0$. For Brazovskii, this fails: instead, the freezing
is governed by the \emph{shell-mode relaxation} at finite $q_0$,
with rate $r(\mu^2)$ that vanishes only at the spinodal endpoint
$\mu^2 = -Y q_0^4$, not at the first-order transition $\mu^2_c$.

\textbf{Consequence}: The Brazovskii KZ exponent is \emph{non-standard}
and lies in a class that has been studied in the soft-matter
literature (Hohenberg--Swift 1995; Fredrickson--Binder 1989) but is
NOT one of the standard Hohenberg--Halperin classes. The relevant
exponent depends on the first-order vs second-order character of the
transition (TECT is weakly first-order per Brazovskii 1975).

\textbf{Quantitative estimate (Brazovskii first-order regime)}:
For a weakly first-order transition with thin nucleation barrier,
the dominant defect-production mechanism is \emph{nucleation from
critical bubbles} rather than KZ continuous freezing. The relevant
timescale is the bubble nucleation time
$\tau_{\rm nuc} \sim \exp(\Delta F^*/T)$ with $\Delta F^*$ the
nucleation barrier. For TECT operating-point parameters, Math122-124
estimate $\Delta F^* \sim 10\,k_B T$, giving
$\tau_{\rm nuc} \sim e^{10} \sim 2 \times 10^4$ in code units.

\textbf{Reframing}: Math361's KZ analysis assumed continuous
second-order-like freezing. For a first-order Brazovskii transition,
the defect-density formula is different:
\begin{equation}
n_d \sim \tau_{\rm nuc}^{-1} \cdot \text{(quench duration)}
\end{equation}
which can be \emph{much smaller} than the standard KZ estimate if
$\Delta F^* \gg T$ (exponentially suppressed nucleation). However,
the Math122 estimate $\Delta F^* \sim 10\,k_B T$ gives
$\tau_{\rm nuc}^{-1} \sim e^{-10} \sim 4.5 \times 10^{-5}$,
producing only $\sim 10^4$-fold suppression of defect density --
not the $10^{36}$-fold suppression Math361 estimated would be needed.

\subsection*{6. Devil's-advocate self-test (CLAUDE.md \S 6.3.1)}

double-well-with-shell-modulation), but the field equation
$\partial_t \Psi = -\Gamma \delta\mathcal{F}/\delta\Psi^* + \xi$

The mitigation: post-transition, the relevant fluctuations are

the universality sense.

\textbf{Objection $\beta$}: The Brazovskii spectrum has a minimum

q_0$. This gives an \emph{effective} dynamic exponent

\emph{Response: DISMISSED with explicit counter.} The $z = 4$ in

Brazovskii Model A*, the $z = 4$-like spectrum near $q_0$ arises
from the \emph{static} $(\nabla^2 + q_0^2)^2$ shell kinetic

different mechanism. Crucially, Brazovskii Model A* does \emph{not}
preserve $\int\Psi\,d^3r$ (which Model B does). The $z = 4$
matching is coincidental in the static-spectrum sense and does not
imply universal-class equivalence in the dynamic-correlation
sense. KZ defect density depends on the freeze-out
\emph{relaxation rate} (linear-time pole), not just the
