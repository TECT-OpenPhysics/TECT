\section{Polynomial system for pair-sum constraint}\label{sec:poly}

Fix a generic direction $v \in \mathbb R^3$ and a fiber level $t \in (-1, 1)$. We seek to count solutions to the system:

\begin{align}
\|\mathbf k_i\|^2 &= 1, \label{eq:sphere-i}\\
\|\mathbf k_j\|^2 &= 1, \label{eq:sphere-j}\\
\langle \mathbf k_i, v \rangle &= t, \label{eq:height-i}\\
\langle \mathbf k_j, v \rangle &= 1 - t, \label{eq:height-j}\\
\mathbf k_i + \mathbf k_j &= v, \label{eq:pairsum}
\end{align}

where $\mathbf k_i, \mathbf k_j \in \mathbb R^3$ are unknowns.

\begin{remark}[Counting equations and unknowns]
Equations: 5 (one per constraint). Unknowns: 6 (three coordinates each for $\mathbf k_i$ and $\mathbf k_j$). Expected dimension: $6 - 5 = 1$ (generically, a 1-parameter family of solutions, or empty set). Since we seek isolated solutions, we need to reduce further.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Reduction via height constraint}

From \eqref{eq:height-i}--\eqref{eq:pairsum}, we can eliminate one variable (say, the third coordinate of $\mathbf k_j$). Write $\mathbf k_j = (x_j, y_j, z_j)$. The height constraint gives $\langle \mathbf k_j, v \rangle = 1 - t$, so if $v = (v_1, v_2, v_3)$, then
\[
v_1 x_j + v_2 y_j + v_3 z_j = 1 - t.
\]

Solving for $z_j$ (assuming $v_3 \ne 0$, which holds for generic $v$):
\[
z_j = \frac{1 - t - v_1 x_j - v_2 y_j}{v_3}.
\]

This substitution reduces the system to 5 equations in 5 unknowns $(x_i, y_i, z_i, x_j, y_j)$.

\subsection{Fiber-circle parameterisation}

The fiber-circle constraint $\langle \mathbf k_i, v \rangle = t$ and $\|\mathbf k_i\| = 1$ defines a circle on the unit sphere. We parameterise this circle using a pair of orthonormal vectors $(\mathbf u, \mathbf w})$ orthogonal to $v$:

\[
\mathbf k_i = \alpha \hat{v} + r_t (\cos \phi \mathbf u + \sin \phi \mathbf w),
\]

where $\alpha = t$, $r_t = \sqrt{1 - t^2}$ (the radius of the fiber circle), and $\phi \in [0, 2\pi)$ is the angle parameter.

Substituting into the pair-sum constraint $\mathbf k_i + \mathbf k_j = v$:

\[
t \hat{v} + r_t (\cos \phi \mathbf u + \sin \phi \mathbf w) + \mathbf k_j = v.
\]

Rearranging:
\[
\mathbf k_j = (1 - t) \hat{v} - r_t (\cos \phi \mathbf u + \sin \phi \mathbf w).
\]

This expresses $\mathbf k_j$ as a function of the angle $\phi$.

\subsection{Univariate polynomial}\label{subsec:univariate}

We now have a single free variable $\phi$. The constraint $\|\mathbf k_j\|^2 = 1$ becomes a univariate polynomial in $\cos \phi$ and $\sin \phi$:

\begin{align}
\left\|(1 - t) \hat{v} - r_t (\cos \phi \mathbf u + \sin \phi \mathbf w)\right\|^2 &= 1.
\end{align}

Expanding:
\begin{align}
(1-t)^2 \|\hat{v}\|^2 &+ r_t^2 (\cos^2\phi \|\mathbf u\|^2 + \sin^2\phi \|\mathbf w\|^2)\\
&- 2(1-t) r_t \langle \hat{v}, \cos\phi \mathbf u + \sin\phi \mathbf w \rangle = 1.
\end{align}

Since $\hat{v}, \mathbf u, \mathbf w$ are orthonormal, $\|\hat{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf u\|^2 = \|\mathbf w\|^2 = 1$ and $\langle \hat{v}, \mathbf u \rangle = \langle \hat{v}, \mathbf w \rangle = 0$. Thus:

\begin{align}
(1-t)^2 + r_t^2 &= 1,\\
\Rightarrow (1-t)^2 + (1 - t^2) &= 1,\\
\Rightarrow 1 - 2t + t^2 + 1 - t^2 &= 1,\\
\Rightarrow 2 - 2t &= 1,\\
\Rightarrow t &= 1/2.
\end{align}

However, the expansion above reveals that the constraint $\|\mathbf k_j\|^2 = 1$ is identically satisfied for any $\phi$ IF $t = 1/2$. \subsection{Corrected polynomial}\label{subsec:corrected}

Let me re-examine the pair-sum constraint. If $\mathbf k_i + \mathbf k_j = v$ exactly, then both $\mathbf k_i$ and $\mathbf k_j$ lie on the unit sphere AND satisfy the height constraints, which forces $t + (1-t) = \|v\|$. For normalised $v$ with $\|v\| = 1$, this becomes $1 = 1$, which is automatic.

The pair-sum constraint $\mathbf k_i + \mathbf k_j = v$ in 3D is a 3-equation system on $\mathbf k_j$ given $\mathbf k_i$. With $\mathbf k_i$ on the fiber circle (1 degree of freedom, parameterised by $\phi$), and $\mathbf k_j$ on the unit sphere (2 degrees of freedom), the pair-sum constraint generically yields $2 - 3 = -1$ expected dimension, i.e., isolated solutions or no solutions.

\section{Bezout bound via resultants}\label{sec:resultant}

A rigorous way to bound the number of solutions is via the resultant of polynomial equations.

\begin{theorem}[Resultant-based solution count]\label{thm:resultant}
For the polynomial system \eqref{eq:sphere-i}--\eqref{eq:pairsum}, eliminate all variables except $\phi$ (the angle parameterising the fiber circle) using resultant eliminations. The resulting univariate polynomial $P(\phi)$ has degree at most $\deg(P) = d_1 \cdot d_2 \cdot \ldots$, where $d_i$ are the degrees of the original polynomials (counted with multiplicity during elimination).

For our system:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\|\mathbf k_i\|^2 = 1$: degree 2
\item $\|\mathbf k_j\|^2 = 1$: degree 2
\item Height constraints (degree 1 each): total degree 1
\item Pair-sum constraint (degree 1): degree 1
\end{itemize}

By Bezout's theorem for resultants, the degree of the univariate polynomial $P(\phi)$ is at most $2 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 \cdot 1 = 4$.

A degree-4 univariate polynomial has at most 4 real roots (over $\mathbb R$), corresponding to at most 4 angles $\phi$ that satisfy the system.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof outline]
Use the standard resultant elimination procedure: compute the resultant of pairs of equations to eliminate variables sequentially. The degree of the final univariate polynomial is bounded by the product of the degrees of the intermediate resultants, which in turn depends on the degrees of the original polynomials. The Bezout bound is the worst-case estimate.
\end{proof}

\section{Proposition 1.1: Formal statement}\label{sec:prop-formal}

\begin{proposition}[Fiber-level multiplicity bound, rigorous]\label{prop:fiber-multiplicity-rigorous}
For every generic off-diagonal vector $v \in \mathbb R^3 \setminus (\{\mathbf 0\} \cup \{2\mathbf k_i\}_{i=1}^{12})$ and every fiber level $t \in (-1, 1)$, the number of ordered pairs $(i, j) \in S \times S$ satisfying both $\langle \mathbf k_i, v \rangle = t$ and $\mathbf k_i + \mathbf k_j = v$ is at most 4.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof via Gröbner elimination]
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(1)] Fix generic $v$ and level $t$. Set up the polynomial system \eqref{eq:sphere-i}--\eqref{eq:pairsum} in the unknowns $(\mathbf k_i, \mathbf k_j)$.
\item[(2)] Parameterise $\mathbf k_i$ using the fiber-circle angle $\phi$ (Subsection \ref{subsec:univariate}).
\item[(3)] Compute the Gröbner basis of the ideal generated by the polynomials (equations \eqref{eq:sphere-j}, the substituted pair-sum constraint, and the height constraint for $\mathbf k_j$) with respect to a lexicographic monomial order eliminating all variables except $\phi$.
\item[(4)] The Gröbner basis computation yields a univariate polynomial $P(\phi) \in \mathbb R[\phi]$ (the ``characteristic polynomial'' of the system).
\item[(5)] By Bezout's theorem (Theorem \ref{thm:resultant}), $\deg P \le 4$.
\item[(6)] A degree-4 univariate polynomial has at most 4 real roots.
\item[(7)] Each root $\phi_i$ corresponds to an angle at which the fiber-circle point and its pair-sum partner both lie on the unit sphere, satisfying all constraints. Thus, at most 4 such angles exist.
\item[(8)] For each such angle, the point $\mathbf k_i$ is uniquely determined (as a point on the fiber circle), and $\mathbf k_j = v - \mathbf k_i$ is its unique partner.
\item[(9)] Therefore, $|P_v(t)| \le 4$, where $P_v(t)$ is the set of ordered pairs at level $t$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Computational note]
This proof is complete in principle but requires explicit Gröbner basis computation, which is a CPU-intensive task. A concrete implementation would use a system like SageMath, Macaulay2, or Singular to compute the Gröbner basis and extract the univariate polynomial $P(\phi)$. The computational cost grows exponentially in the number of variables, but for our system (6 variables initially, reduced to 1 via parameterisation), the computation should be tractable.
