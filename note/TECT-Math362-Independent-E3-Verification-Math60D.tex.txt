§ 1. MATH60-D FRAMEWORK: TOPOLOGICAL STATE ENUMERATION
==================================================================

**From Math60-D**: The TECT phase space is partitioned into topological
sectors $\pi_k(\mathcal{M}_{\rm BCC}/H)$, where $\mathcal{M}_{\rm BCC}$ is
the BCC order-parameter manifold and $H$ is the unbroken symmetry.

Each sector $\pi_k$ corresponds to a **distinct topological class** of
configurations, characterized by integer winding numbers.

**Classification of ground states**:

Within the ZERO-winding sector ($\pi_0$, trivial topology):
- Ground state: Uniform BCC with fixed phase and lattice orientation
- Degeneracy: 12 (different lattice orientations in cubic space)
- Energy density: $\rho_0 = -0.314$ (Brazovskii units, from Math82)

Within the ONE-winding sector ($\pi_1$, single defect):
- Contains BCC + one topological defect (monopole, vortex, domain wall)
- Defect carries topological charge $Q = 1$
- Energy cost relative to $\pi_0$: $\Delta E_{\rm defect} \sim 4$ GeV
  (from Math218-AddA §1.2.1)

Within the TWO-winding sector ($\pi_2$):
- Contains BCC + two topological defects (or one double-charge object)
- Energy cost: $\Delta E \sim 8$ GeV (approximately additive)

And so forth.

---

**Critical observation** (Math60-D §3):

At temperature $T \sim 10^{16}$ GeV (cosmological transition), the free
energy of each topological sector is:

$$\mathcal{F}_k(T) = E_k - T \Delta S_k,$$

where $E_k$ is the ground-state energy in sector $\pi_k$ and $\Delta S_k$
is the entropy (number of microscopic configurations per sector).

For the zero-winding sector:
$$\mathcal{F}_0(T) = E_0 - T S_0.$$

For the one-defect sector:
$$\mathcal{F}_1(T) = E_0 + \Delta E_{\rm defect} - T (S_0 + \Delta S_{\rm defect}).$$

**Comparison**:

$$\mathcal{F}_1(T) - \mathcal{F}_0(T) = \Delta E_{\rm defect}
  - T \Delta S_{\rm defect}.$$

**Surprising result** (per Math60-D §4):

At high temperature, the entropy term can DOMINATE, making $\mathcal{F}_1 <
\mathcal{F}_0$. The one-defect sector is ENERGETICALLY PREFERRED!

$$\frac{\mathcal{F}_1}{\mathcal{F}_0} = 1 + \frac{\Delta E_{\rm defect} - T
\Delta S_{\rm defect}}{E_0} < 1 \quad \text{(for $T$ large enough)}.$$

**Implication for E_3'**:

If the one-defect sector is lower free energy than the zero-defect sector
at temperature $T_c \sim 10^{16}$ GeV, then the BCC vacuum (zero-winding)
is NOT the unique global minimum. The LOWEST free energy state is the
ONE-DEFECT state (or higher-defect states).

**E_3' prediction**: "BCC vacuum is unique" requires:
$$\mathcal{F}_0(T_c) \leq \mathcal{F}_k(T_c) \quad \forall k \geq 1.$$

**Math60-D test**: Compute $\mathcal{F}_k$ for k=0,1,2,... and compare.

§ 2. ENTROPY CALCULATION FOR DEFECT SECTORS
==================================================================

**Entropy of the zero-winding sector** (BCC ground state):

Counting microscopic configurations:
- Each condensate orientation: 12 choices
- Lattice phase modulations within the correlation-length volume: $\exp(S_{\rm bulk})$
- At $T = T_c$, the bulk entropy density is: $s_{\rm bulk} \sim (T_c / M_{\rm Pl})^{1/2} \times
  k_B \sim 10^8 k_B$ (extensive in bulk volume)

Total entropy: $S_0 = s_{\rm bulk} \times V_{\rm corr}$, where $V_{\rm corr} \sim
\xi^3 \sim 1$ nm$^3$ is the correlation-length volume.

**Entropy of the one-defect sector**:

The defect adds entropy because:
(a) Defect position is MACROSCOPIC: can be anywhere in volume V (not fixed)
(b) Defect orientation: internal DOF (e.g., monopole spin, vortex winding axis)
(c) Defect distortion: surrounding BCC can be locally deformed

Additional entropy from defect:
$$\Delta S_{\rm defect} \sim k_B \log(\text{# microstates per defect})
  \sim k_B \log(V/\xi^3) + k_B \log(\text{internal DOF}).$$

For a cosmological volume $V \sim \xi^3 H^{-3}(T_c) \sim (10^{13})^3$ (in
Hubble units), this is:

$$\Delta S_{\rm defect} \sim k_B \log(10^{39}) \sim 90\, k_B.$$

**Free-energy comparison**:

At $T_c \sim 10^{16}$ GeV:

$$\mathcal{F}_1 - \mathcal{F}_0 = \Delta E_{\rm defect} - T_c \Delta S_{\rm
defect}$$

$$= 4\,\mathrm{GeV} - 10^{16}\,\mathrm{GeV} \times 90\, k_B.$$

In natural units, $k_B = 8.6 \times 10^{-5}$ GeV/K. At $T_c$:

$$T_c k_B \sim 10^{16} \times 10^{-5} \sim 10^{11}\,\mathrm{dimensionless}.$$

Thus:

$$\mathcal{F}_1 - \mathcal{F}_0 \sim 4 - 90 \times 10^{11}
  \sim -9 \times 10^{12}\,\mathrm{GeV}.$$

**The ONE-DEFECT SECTOR IS LOWER in free energy by ~10^{12} GeV!**

Conclusion: At $T = T_c$, the universe is MORE LIKELY to be in the one-
defect state than the zero-defect BCC state.

§ 4. DEVIL'S-ADVOCATE SELF-TEST (CLAUDE.md §6.3.1)
==================================================================

**Objection α: "Your entropy calculation assumed the defect can move
FREELY throughout the correlation-length volume. But if defects are
HEAVY (high energy cost), they will be LOCALIZED to the region where
they were created (Kibble-Zurek, which forms them at specific locations).
Free motion is unphysical."**

**Response (DISMISSED)**: The entropy calculation is about the PARTITION
FUNCTION at temperature T, not about DYNAMICS. At equilibrium (thermal
equilibrium), a defect created at one location WILL diffuse throughout
the volume over the timescale ~ L²/D, where D is the diffusion constant.
For the cosmological timescale (age of universe), defects have ample time
to equilibrate spatially (unless Hubble friction freezes them in, which
is the dynamical argument from Math360, orthogonal to the thermodynamic
argument here).

---

**Objection β: "The entropy gain ΔS_defect ~ 90 k_B seems arbitrary. How
sensitive is the conclusion to this number? If ΔS is actually smaller,
the free-energy gap might vanish, and BCC could be competitive with the
defect sector."**

**Response (VALID-with-mitigation)**: Fair point. The entropy calculation
depends on (i) the Hubble-scale volume, (ii) the internal DOF per defect,
(iii) the defect size. A more rigorous calculation would use the FULL
partition function of the defected BCC configuration.

Mitigation: This Turn (Math362) is a CROSS-CHECK, not a primary proof.
Even if the entropy calculation is off by a factor of 2-3, the order of
magnitude is clear: cosmological-volume entropy dominates over GeV-scale
energy costs.

---

**Objection γ: "You compare F_0 vs. F_1 at a SINGLE temperature T_c.
But as the universe COOLS, the entropy term (−T ΔS) becomes SMALLER in
magnitude. Perhaps at T < T_c, the zero-defect sector becomes competitive
again, and the system TRANSITIONS back to BCC."**

**Response (VALID-with-mitigation)**: Correct observation. At T → 0:

$$\mathcal{F}_k(T) \to E_k$$

(entropy term vanishes). So at very low T, the lowest-energy state (zero-
defect BCC, by definition) is favoured.

However, this does NOT rescue E_3'. Here's why:

If the system is in the HIGH-ENTROPY one-defect sector at T = T_c and
COOLS to T = 0, it will remain in the one-defect sector (defects do NOT
annihilate, as Math360 showed). Even at T → 0, the one-defect configuration
survives as a TOPOLOGICAL RELIC.

So the END-STATE (T → 0) is: **one-defect BCC, not zero-defect BCC**.

E_3' requires the zero-defect state, which is violated.
