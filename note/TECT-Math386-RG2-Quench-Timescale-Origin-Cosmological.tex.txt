\subsection*{1. Math196 canonical derivation: $\tau_Q$ from FRW}

The Math196 derivation chain (recapitulated):

\textbf{Step 1 (cosmological cooling)}: In radiation-dominated FRW,
$\rho_{\rm rad} \sim T^4$, $H^2 = 8\pi G \rho/3$, giving $H \propto
T^2$ and the cooling rate
\begin{equation}
\dot T = -HT.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 2 (Hartree-driven $\mu^2(T)$)}: One-loop thermal
correction to the Brazovskii mass:
\begin{equation}
\mu^2(T) = \mu^2_0 + \lambda T^2 \cdot (\text{order-1 factor}),
\quad \frac{\partial\mu^2}{\partial T}\bigg|_{T_c} =: a = \lambda T_c.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 3 (chain rule)}:
\begin{equation}
\dot\mu^2 = \frac{\partial\mu^2}{\partial T}\,\dot T = a \cdot (-HT)
= -a H T_c \quad (\text{near criticality}).
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 4 (quench rate)}: At the operating point
$|\mu^2_c| = R_C^{\rm global} \approx 0.0114$:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{|\dot\mu^2|}{|\mu^2_c|} = \frac{a H T_c}{|\mu^2_c|}
= \frac{0.43 \cdot 1 \cdot H}{0.0114} \approx 38\,H.}
\label{eq:quench-rate-canonical}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 5 (quench timescale)}: $\tau_Q := |\dot\mu^2|^{-1}
\cdot |\mu^2_c| = 1/(38 H)$. So
\begin{equation}
\tau_Q \sim H^{-1} / 38 \sim H^{-1}.
\end{equation}
The quench timescale is parametrically the Hubble time, with a
small numerical prefactor.

\textbf{Where does the internal TECT scale enter?}: Only through
$|\mu^2_c| = R_C^{\rm global}$ (which is set by $q_0, \lambda,
\gamma$) and through $a = \lambda T_c$ (which is set by
$\lambda$). These are \emph{constants} in the cosmological evolution,
not dynamical timescales. The dynamical timescale -- $\tau_Q$ -- is
governed by $H$ alone.

\subsection*{2. Sign-direction analysis: shorter $\tau_Q$ would
hurt, not help}

Suppose for argument's sake that $\tau_Q$ were misidentified and the
correct value is the internal-mode oscillation period
\begin{equation}
\tau_Q^{\rm int} \sim 1/q_0 \approx 1.47 \quad (\text{code units})
\sim 10^{-29} \text{s} \quad (\text{at TECT scale}\sim 10^{16}\text{GeV}^{-1}).
\end{equation}
Compare to cosmological:
\begin{equation}
\tau_Q^{\rm cosm} \sim H^{-1} \sim 10^{-24} \text{s}
\quad (\text{at GUT-scale }H\sim10^{-3}\,M_{\rm Pl}).
\end{equation}
The internal timescale is $\sim 10^5$ times \emph{shorter} than the
cosmological timescale.

\textbf{KZ scaling consequence}: $\hat\xi \sim \tau_Q^{1/(1+z\nu)}
= \tau_Q^{1/4}$ (for Brazovskii Model A* with $\nu=1/2, z=2$). A
$10^5$-fold shorter $\tau_Q$ produces a $10^{5/4} \approx 18$-fold
shorter $\hat\xi$, hence $\sim 18^3 \approx 5800$-fold LARGER
$n_d$. This makes $E_3'$ overproduction WORSE by 4 orders of
magnitude, not better.

\textbf{Conclusion of sign-direction analysis}: Even if RG2 were
correct (which Math196 says it is not), the sign of the effect is
opposite to relief. RG2-as-internal-clock cannot help close $E_3'$.

\subsection*{3. Operational analysis: which $\tau_Q$ enters
$n_d$?}

Math195 \S 3 (canonical) showed that the KZ defect-density formula
\begin{equation}
n_d \sim \tau_Q^{-d\nu/(1+z\nu)}
\end{equation}
depends on $\tau_Q$ but \emph{not} on the absolute timescale of the
field theory (the $1/q_0$ does not appear). This is the universal
KZ statement: the defect density depends only on the rate at which
the order parameter is driven through the transition, not on the
microscopic relaxation rate.

\textbf{However}: this universal statement holds only if the
microscopic relaxation rate $\tau_{\rm relax}^{-1}$ is FASTER than
the quench rate $\tau_Q^{-1}$. If $\tau_{\rm relax} > \tau_Q$, the
system cannot equilibrate during the quench and a \emph{different}
non-adiabatic mechanism governs.

\textbf{For TECT}: $\tau_{\rm relax} \sim 1/(\Gamma r) \sim
1/(\Gamma \mu^2_{\rm eq})$. For $\Gamma \sim O(1)$ (canonical) and
$\mu^2_{\rm eq} \sim 1$ (post-transition deep BCC), $\tau_{\rm relax}
\sim O(1)$ in code units, i.e., $\sim 10^{-29}$ s at TECT scales. This
is $\sim 10^5$ times faster than $\tau_Q^{\rm cosm} \sim 10^{-24}$ s.
The condition $\tau_{\rm relax} \ll \tau_Q$ holds, KZ analysis is
valid, and $\tau_Q^{\rm cosm}$ is the relevant rate.

\subsection*{4. Domain-of-validity boundary: rapid-quench limit}

The KZ analysis assumes $\tau_Q \gg \tau_{\rm relax}$, i.e., the
field has time to track the quasi-equilibrium during the quench.
The rapid-quench limit $\tau_Q \lesssim \tau_{\rm relax}$ would
produce a different mechanism (Glauber-like sudden freezing), where
the defect density is set by initial-state fluctuation amplitude
rather than by KZ scaling.

For TECT, $\tau_Q^{\rm cosm}/\tau_{\rm relax} \sim 10^5$, deep in the
slow-quench regime. The KZ analysis is canonical.

\textbf{Hypothetical}: If TECT were in a different cosmological
era where $H$ were much larger (e.g., during inflation,
$H_{\rm infl} \sim 10^{-5}\,M_{\rm Pl}$, comparable to TECT scales),
the KZ assumption could break down. However:

\item TECT condensation is canonically POST-inflationary
  (Math145 A2: ``ultra-high-energy isotropic fluid'' = post-reheating).
\item Pre-reheating BCC condensation would be inconsistent with

\end{itemize}
Therefore the canonical $H \lesssim 10^{-3}\,M_{\rm Pl}$ regime
applies and KZ is valid.

\subsection*{7. Self-adversarial review (CLAUDE.md \S 6.3.5(a))}

\begin{enumerate}

Brazovskii), $z \approx 2$ (Model A leading order). Standard
