§ 2. HESSIAN OPERATOR DEFINITION
==================================================================

**Definition**: The Hessian (second functional derivative) at the ground
state $\Psi_{\rm min}$ is:

$$\hat{\Delta}_{\rm Hess} := \frac{\delta^2 \mathcal{F}}{\delta\Psi\delta\Psi^*}
\bigg|_{\Psi_{\rm min}}.$$

**Explicit form** (Einstein summation over Cartesian indices):

Let $\Psi(x) = |\Psi_{\rm min}| e^{i\phi(x)}$ be a perturbation of the
ground state (decomposed into radial $|\Psi|$ and angular $\phi(x)$ parts).
The BCC ground state $\Psi_{\rm min}$ is a real-valued 12-component
strain field living in $(q_0, q_1, q_2, \ldots)$ space (reciprocal lattice).

For simplicity, work in the effective 2D projection (major BCC channels):
$$\Psi(x) = \Psi_0 + \delta\Psi(x),$$
where $\Psi_0 = |\Psi_{\rm min}| e^{i\phi_0}$ (static, uniform) and
$\delta\Psi(x)$ is a small fluctuation.

**Functional Taylor expansion**:

$$\mathcal{F}[\Psi_0 + \delta\Psi] = \mathcal{F}[\Psi_0] +
\int d^3x\, \left(\frac{\delta\mathcal{F}}{\delta\Psi^*}\bigg|_0 \delta\Psi^*
+ \text{c.c.}\right) +
\int d^3x\,d^3y\, \delta\Psi^*(x)\,K(x,y)\,\delta\Psi(y) + O((\delta\Psi)^3).$$

At the minimum, the first-order term vanishes. The second-order kernel
is the Hessian operator:

$$K(x,y) = \frac{\delta^2\mathcal{F}}{\delta\Psi^*(x)\delta\Psi(y)}
\bigg|_{\Psi_{\rm min}}.$$

**Explicit Hessian** (Brazovskii free energy):

$$K(x,y) = \left[\, \mu^2 \delta^3(x-y) - Z\nabla_x^2\delta^3(x-y)
+ Y\nabla_x^4\delta^3(x-y) + \text{(quartic + sextic)}\,\right].$$

Quartic and sextic terms contribute only at points where $|\Psi_{\rm min}|
\neq 0$ (i.e., inside the condensate core). For spatially homogeneous
perturbations (Fourier modes), the Hessian becomes:

$$K(k) := \mu^2 + Z k^2 + Y k^4 + \lambda_{\rm eff} + \gamma_{\rm eff},$$

where $\lambda_{\rm eff}$ and $\gamma_{\rm eff}$ are effective coefficients
determined by the ground-state configuration.

**For the BCC ground state** (12-mode, multi-component order parameter):

The Hessian is a $12 \times 12$ matrix in mode space:

$$H_{\alpha\beta}(k) = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\left(\mu^2 + Z k^2 + Y k^4\right)
+ \lambda_{\alpha\beta}\,|\Psi_{\rm min}|^2 + \gamma_{\alpha\beta}^{(4)}
+ \text{(off-diagonal coupling terms from symmetry breaking)}.$$

§ 3. GOLDSTONE AND ROTATIONAL ZERO-MODES
==================================================================

**Symmetry considerations**:

The BCC condensate breaks the original $O(3)$ spatial rotation symmetry.
The unbroken symmetry is a subgroup $G_{\rm unbroken}$ depending on the
condensate orientation.

**Goldstone modes** (zero-energy cost):

For each broken generator, there is one zero-mode. By Goldstone's theorem:

1. **Phase rotation** $\Psi \to e^{i\theta} \Psi$ is unbroken (axial
   symmetry). No Goldstone mode.

2. **Spatial translation** $\Psi(x) \to \Psi(x - a)$ is unbroken. The
   derivative $\partial_i \Psi$ is a zero-mode. This gives **3 Goldstone
   modes** (one per spatial direction).

3. **Rotation of the BCC lattice** about an axis aligned with one of the
   condensate stripes: this breaking depends on the orientation. For the
   "standard" BCC (12-mode, N=8 cubic unit cell), there are **N_{\rm rot}**
   rotational zero-modes, where $N_{\rm rot}$ is the number of internal
   rotational degrees of freedom not fixed by lattice symmetry.

   For a 12-mode BCC: $N_{\rm rot} \sim 3$ (rotation about the principal axes
   that don't align with lattice directions).

**Total zero-modes expected**: $3 + 3 = 6$ (translational + rotational).

(This is a consistency check: the number of zero-eigenvalues of the
Hessian must equal the codimension of the symmetry-breaking.)

§ 4. CLASSIFICATION OF THE MASSIVE SPECTRUM
==================================================================

**Definition**: Massive modes are Hessian eigenmodes with eigenvalue
$\lambda_i > 0$.

**Classification scheme**:

| Mode type | Energy range | Physical meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Goldstone** | $\lambda = 0$ | Long-wavelength translational/rotational freedom |
| **Soft mode** | $0 < \lambda \lesssim 0.01$ (Brazovskii units) | Near-critical fluctuation; may couple to Kibble-Zurek defect production |
| **Elastic/phonon** | $\lambda \sim 0.01 - 0.1$ | Sound-wave-like excitations (propagating strain waves) |
| **Gapped** | $\lambda > 0.1$ | Fully gapped, short-range excitations; stable |

**Critical question for stability**:

Are ALL massive modes positive ($\lambda_i > 0$ for $i > 6$)?

If YES: BCC is a **local minimum** → move to Turn 5 (eigenvalue computation).
If NO: BCC is a **saddle point** → Lemma E_3' FALSIFIED by vacuum instability.

§ 5. STRUCTURE OF THE HESSIAN MATRIX (FINITE-VOLUME APPROXIMATION)
==================================================================

**Discretisation**: On a lattice of size $L \times L \times L$ with
lattice constant $a = 2\pi q_0^{-1} \approx 0.3$ nm, the Hessian in
real space is:

$$H_{\mathbf{n},\alpha; \mathbf{m},\beta} := \frac{\delta^2\mathcal{F}}{
\delta\Psi(\mathbf{n},\alpha) \delta\Psi^*(\mathbf{m},\beta)}
\bigg|_{\Psi_{\rm min}},$$

where $\mathbf{n}, \mathbf{m}$ are lattice site indices and
$\alpha, \beta \in \{1, \ldots, 12\}$ are the BCC order-parameter
components (reciprocal lattice basis).

**Matrix dimension**:
- Number of lattice sites: $N_{\rm sites} = (L/a)^3$
- For $L = 10$ nm (typical simulation volume), $N_{\rm sites} \sim 40^3 \sim 64000$
- With 12 order-parameter components: total Hessian size ~ $768000 \times 768000$
- **Computational cost**: Full eigendecomposition is intractable.

**Approximation strategy** (Lanczos + Lowe-Parrinello):

Use Krylov-subspace iterative methods to extract the lowest $k \sim 30$ eigenvalues
and eigenvectors. The Lanczos algorithm does NOT require forming the full matrix
explicitly; instead, it uses matrix-vector products $H \cdot v$.

**Matrix-vector product** ($O(N)$ operation):

Given a fluctuation vector $v = (\delta\Psi(\mathbf{n}_1, 1), \ldots,
\delta\Psi(\mathbf{n}_{N_{\rm sites}}, 12))$, compute $H \cdot v$ by:

(a) Fourier transform $v \to \hat{v}(k)$ (FFT, cost $O(N \log N)$)
(b) Multiply in Fourier space by $\mu^2 + Z k^2 + Y k^4$ (cost $O(N)$)
(c) Transform back (IFFT, cost $O(N \log N)$)
(d) Add nonlinear correction from $\lambda_{\alpha\beta}|\Psi_{\rm min}|^2$ term (cost $O(N)$)

**Total per Lanczos step**: $O(N \log N)$, feasible for $N \sim 10^5 - 10^6$.

§ 6. BOUNDARY CONDITIONS AND PERIODICITY
==================================================================

**Imposed structure** (to match Math82 numerical setup):

- **Periodic boundary conditions**: $\Psi(\mathbf{x} + L\mathbf{e}_i) = \Psi(\mathbf{x})$
  for each direction $\mathbf{e}_i \in \{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z}\}$.

- **BCC unit cell**: Supercell of size $2L \times 2L \times 2L$ containing
  the full 12-mode pattern (or multiples thereof for larger systems).

- **Ground state $\Psi_{\rm min}$**: Read from Math82 numerical solution
  (either $N=16, 32, 64$ system sizes; details in Turn 5).

**Consequence for Goldstone modes**:

Under periodic BC, the three translational Goldstone modes are:

$$\psi_i(x) = \partial_i \Psi_{\rm min}(x), \quad i \in \{x, y, z\}.$$

These are eigenfunctions with $\lambda = 0$ (by construction, since
$\partial_i \Psi_{\rm min}$ satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equation
automatically).

**Practical check**:

When we run Lanczos in Turn 5, we must verify that the lowest 3-6 eigenvalues
are indeed $\lambda \approx 0$ (within numerical tolerance $\sim 10^{-10}$ in
Brazovskii units), and the corresponding eigenvectors are proportional to
$\nabla \Psi_{\rm min}$ and $\mathbf{R} \Psi_{\rm min}$ (rotations).

---

**Objection β: "The 12-mode BCC is not the ONLY vacuum of Brazovskii theory.
There are also body-centered-cubic variants (BCC', BCC'', etc.) with different
symmetry breaking. How do we know BCC is the ground state, not a saddle or
higher-energy minimum?"**

**Response (VALID-with-mitigation)**: Math82 and Math220-AddB already establish
that BCC has the LOWEST free energy among all candidates in the Brazovskii
phase space. The Hessian analysis here ASSUMES BCC is the putative global
minimum and tests whether it's locally stable. If Hessian is negative,
the assumption is contradicted (Math82 must be re-audited). If Hessian
is positive, it's consistent with Math82's claim. The cross-check in
Turn 7 (alternative proofs) will harden this.

---

**Objection γ: "Perturbations around $\Psi_{\rm min}$ are assumed small.
But Kibble-Zurek defect production during phase transition might involve
LARGE field fluctuations that exit the quadratic regime. The Hessian is
irrelevant for large-amplitude dynamics."**

**Response (VALID-with-mitigation)**: True. The Hessian stability tells us
the vacuum is stable against SMALL perturbations. During the cosmological
phase transition (rapid cooling), the order parameter evolves via:

$$\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = -\Gamma \frac{\delta\mathcal{F}}{\delta\Psi^*}$$

(Langevin TDGL dynamics). If $\Psi_{\rm min}$ is a global minimum AND all
wavy perturbations decay (Hessian positive), then the system will eventually
settle to $\Psi_{\rm min}$ even after large transient excursions. However,
the TIME to reach $\Psi_{\rm min}$ depends on the barrier heights and
damping $\Gamma$ — not just the Hessian. Turn 6-8 will address the
Kibble-Zurek quench more directly via explicit defect-production estimates.
