\section{Minimal Theorem/Lemma Checklist for an SM+GR-Type Infrared Limit in TECT}

\section{1. Main infrared target}

The correct infrared goal is not to assume the Standard Model plus General Relativity as a fundamental axiom, but to show that TECT admits an effective long-distance description of SM+GR type.

Accordingly, the appropriate main target theorem is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\textbf{Main IR Theorem (target form).}
\quad
\text{If conditions }(C1)\text{--}(C8)\text{ hold, then the infrared effective theory of TECT is of SM+GR type, up to suppressed higher-order corrections.}
}
\end{equation}

Here the conditions \((C1)\text{--}(C8)\) should be separated into theorem/lemma-level subproblems.

\section{2. Minimal checklist}

\subsection{Lemma C1: Continuum infrared sector}
A controlled long-wavelength continuum limit must exist, with sufficiently suppressed residual anisotropy and an effectively Lorentzian propagation structure.

\subsection{Lemma C2: Einstein-type gravitational sector}
The infrared theory must contain a metric or massless spin-2 sector with universal coupling, and non-Einstein extra gravitational polarizations must decouple or be sufficiently suppressed.

\subsection{Lemma C3: Gauge sector emergence}
The infrared theory must contain the observed Standard Model gauge content, or an infrared-equivalent gauge structure reproducing the same tested gauge phenomenology.

\subsection{Lemma C4: Chiral matter and family sector}
The infrared theory must contain a chiral matter sector with the observed family structure, while unwanted mirror sectors must be removed, lifted, or screened.

\subsection{Lemma C5: Canonical kinetic normalization}
After projection to the infrared degrees of freedom, the kinetic terms must be reducible to canonical form so that masses and mixings are physically well-defined.

\subsection{Lemma C6: Decoupling of extra states}
All non-Standard-Model light modes must either become sufficiently heavy, sufficiently weakly coupled, or otherwise invisible in tested infrared regimes.

\subsection{Lemma C7: Suppression of dangerous higher operators}
Potentially dangerous Lorentz-violating, flavor-violating, baryon/lepton-violating, or non-Einstein operators must be sufficiently suppressed in the infrared effective action.

\subsection{Lemma C8: Consistency conditions}
The infrared theory must satisfy anomaly consistency, stability, unitarity, and positivity requirements.

\section{3. Status logic}

The present goal is not yet to prove the full main theorem, but to organize the proof structure:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{(i) isolate the minimal conditions,}
\quad
\text{(ii) tag each as closed / conditional / open,}
\quad
\text{(iii) determine the dependency order for future proofs.}
}
\end{equation}

This converts an otherwise overwhelming infrared-emergence problem into a structured research program.

\section{2. Logical dependency order}

The logical dependency structure is

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
K1 \;\longrightarrow\; K2 \;\longrightarrow\; (K3,\ K4).
}
\end{equation}

\subsection{2.1. Why \(K1\) comes first}

The CKM matrix is defined as the relative misalignment of the up-sector and down-sector operators acting on the same left-handed family space. Therefore, without a common three-dimensional family subspace
\begin{equation}
\mathcal H_{\rm fam}=\mathrm{span}\{\phi_A\}_{A=1}^3,
\end{equation}
the CKM problem is not yet even well-defined.

Hence:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
K1 \text{ is the first logical prerequisite.}
}
\end{equation}

\subsection{2.2. Why \(K2\) comes before the physical interpretation of \(K3\) and \(K4\)}

After projection to the family sector, the kinetic form is generically
\begin{equation}
Z_{AB}\neq \delta_{AB}.
\end{equation}
Then the projected Hamiltonian entries
\begin{equation}
d_A,\qquad h_{AB}
\end{equation}
are not yet physical observables, since they still depend on a non-canonical family metric. Only after canonical normalization,
\begin{equation}
Z\to I,
\end{equation}
does one obtain the physically meaningful normalized family Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
H_f^{\rm phys}=Z^{-1/2}H_f^{\rm fam}Z^{-1/2}.
\end{equation}

Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
K2 \text{ must precede the physical reading of both }K3\text{ and }K4.
}
\end{equation}

\subsection{2.3. Why \(K3\) and \(K4\) come only after \(K1\) and \(K2\)}

The nondegenerate splittings
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{21},\ \Delta_{32},\ \Delta_{31}
\end{equation}
and the hierarchy seed
\begin{equation}
|h_{12}|>|h_{23}|>|h_{13}|
\end{equation}
must both be read from the normalized physical family Hamiltonian. Otherwise they remain basis-dependent artifacts.

Hence the logical order is exactly:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
K1 \to K2 \to (K3,K4).
}
\end{equation}

\section{Schur-Complement Mechanism for an Induced Carrier-Lowering Well}

\section{1. Goal}

\section{2. Minimal two-sector single-defect Hessian}

Consider a light carrier field \(\psi\) and a heavy auxiliary/longitudinal sector \(X\), expanded around a single-defect background \(P_{X_0}\). The quadratic Hessian takes the block form
\begin{equation}
\boxed{

=
\begin{pmatrix}

\end{pmatrix},

\end{equation}
where:
\begin{equation}
L_\ell = -Z_\ell \nabla^2 + U_\ell(x),
\qquad
L_h = -Z_h \nabla^2 + M_h^2 + U_h(x),
\end{equation}
and \(C\) is the light-heavy mixing operator induced by the defect background.

We assume:
\begin{equation}
Z_\ell>0,\qquad Z_h>0,\qquad M_h^2>0,
\end{equation}
and we study the low-energy light-sector problem near an energy \(E\) below the heavy-sector threshold.

\section{3. Schur-complement reduction}

The block eigenvalue equation
\begin{equation}
\mathbb H
\begin{pmatrix}
\psi\\ X
\end{pmatrix}
=
E
\begin{pmatrix}
\psi\\ X
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
gives
\begin{equation}
(L_\ell-E)\psi + C X = 0,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
C^\dagger \psi + (L_h-E)X = 0.
\end{equation}
Assuming \(L_h-E\) is invertible on the relevant domain, we solve for \(X\):
\begin{equation}
X=-(L_h-E)^{-1}C^\dagger \psi.
\end{equation}
Substituting back into the light equation yields the exact reduced light-sector problem
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
L_{\rm eff}(E)\psi
=
\Big(
L_\ell - C(L_h-E)^{-1}C^\dagger
\Big)\psi
=
E\psi.
}
\end{equation}

\section{4. Sign theorem for the induced term}

Assume that
\begin{equation}
L_h-E > 0
\end{equation}
as an operator on the heavy sector. Then \((L_h-E)^{-1}\) is positive definite, and for every light-sector test state \(\varphi\),
\begin{equation}
\left\langle \varphi,\,
C(L_h-E)^{-1}C^\dagger \varphi
\right\rangle
=
\left\langle
(L_h-E)^{-1/2}C^\dagger\varphi,\,
(L_h-E)^{-1/2}C^\dagger\varphi
\right\rangle
\ge 0.
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
-\,C(L_h-E)^{-1}C^\dagger
\ \text{is negative semidefinite.}
}
\end{equation}

This is the key structural fact:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{integrating out a positive heavy sector lowers the effective light-sector energy.}
}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}

\qquad

\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
