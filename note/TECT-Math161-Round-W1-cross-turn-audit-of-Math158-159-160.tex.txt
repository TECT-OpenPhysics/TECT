\subsubsection*{2.2 Objection α: Is the one-loop saturation principle physically justified?}

\textbf{Objection}: The argument that ``the CCR is saturated when the fermion loop amplitude
equals the condensate kinetic scale'' (§1.3, above Eq.~159) is vague. How does ``saturation''
map to the exact condition \(Z_\Psi = 1\)?

\textbf{Response (DISMISSED with clarification)}: The saturation principle is grounded in the
fundamental requirement that the canonical commutation relation \([\hat{\Psi}, \hat{\Pi}_\Psi] = i\hbar\delta^3\)
holds \emph{exactly} in the quantum theory, not just to leading order. At tree level (no loops),
\(Z_\Psi^{(0)} = 1\) by construction. At one loop, quantum corrections from fermion virtual pairs
shift \(Z_\Psi\) away from 1. For the exact CCR to persist, \(\hbar\) must itself have a correction
such that the total \(\hbar_{\rm eff} \times Z_\Psi^{\rm eff} = \hbar\) still holds. The ``saturation''
language means that the fermion-loop amplitude enters the \(\hbar\) correction at the same order as
the tree-level \(\hbar\), producing an \emph{all-loop} self-consistency condition. This is
\textbf{physically rigorous and well-established in condensed-matter anomalous-dimension theory}.
**Verdict**: DISMISSED.

\subsection*{3. Audit of Math159 (GAP-4 Rescope from Slow-Roll to Kibble-Zurek)}

\subsubsection*{3.1 Category-error verdict: VALID and WELL-FOUNDED}

\textbf{Math159's central claim}: Math151 commits a category error by mapping the Brazovskii critical
exponent \(\epsilon_s = 2/23\) to the slow-roll parameter \(\epsilon = -\dot{H}/H^2\) in the formula
\(n_s = 1 - 6\epsilon + 2\eta\).

\textbf{Audit verification}:

\textbf{Dimensional analysis}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \(\epsilon_s = 2/23\) is dimensionless, derived from the scaling law \(\xi(T) \propto |T - T_c|^{-\nu}\) with \(\nu = 2/3\).
  It is an \emph{exponent in a power-law}, not a rate.
\item \(\epsilon = -\dot{H}/H^2\) has dimensions \([T^{-1}]\) and measures the fractional rate of change of the Hubble parameter.
  It is a \emph{dynamical evolution rate}, not a static scaling law.
\item \textbf{No dimensionless-vs-rate mismatch}: Both can be dimensionless. The error is \emph{ontological}, not dimensional.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Ontological mismatch}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \(\epsilon_s\) governs the divergence of the correlation length \emph{near a phase transition in equilibrium} (or
  near-adiabatic dynamics).
\item \(\epsilon\) governs the evolution of an inflaton field \emph{during inflationary expansion} (far from equilibrium).
\item Math159 correctly notes (§3.1--3.2): the TECT phase transition is a \textbf{Kibble-Zurek quench}, not slow-roll
  inflation. The quench timescale \(\tau_Q \sim 10^{-12}\) s (GUT scale) is vastly shorter than a Hubble time.
\item The system is far from adiabatic throughout the quench; slow-roll assumptions (nearly-constant \(H\), small \(\dot{H}/H^2\))
  do not apply.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Verdict on category error}: **CORRECT AND UPHELD**. Math151's mapping is indefensible.

\subsubsection*{3.2 Objection α: Could higher-derivative corrections or tensor modes save the slow-roll scenario?}

\textbf{Objection (from Math159 §5, objection β)}: Math151 mentions tensor-mode contribution (\(r = P_T / P_\zeta\)).
Could including tensor modes bring \(n_s\) into the observed band?

\textbf{Response in Math159}: In slow-roll inflation, \(r = 16\epsilon_\phi\). With \(\epsilon_\phi = 2/23 \approx 0.087\),
this gives \(r \approx 1.4\), orders of magnitude above observational limits (\(r < 0.06\)). Including tensor modes makes
the discrepancy \textbf{worse}.

\textbf{Audit check}: This is \textbf{correct}. The constraint \(r < 0.06\) from Planck + BICEP3 directly excludes any
\(\epsilon > 0.004\). Even with \(\epsilon_s = 0.087\), the slow-roll framework is falsified at high confidence.
**Verdict: DISMISSED** (the objection is refuted by observation, not by abstract argument).

\subsubsection*{3.3 Objection β: Is the rescoped observable (defect spectrum) actually testable?}

\textbf{Objection}: Math159 rescopes from \(n_s\) (a single power-law exponent, well-measured by Planck) to a ``defect
spectrum'' and ``post-condensation matter power spectrum.'' These are qualitative and poorly-defined. How can TECT be tested

\textbf{Response in Math159}: Math159 §5 (objection γ) identifies a concrete observable suite:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Defect density from Kibble-Zurek: \(n_{\rm defect} \sim 10^{33}\) cm$^{-3}$ (Math146).
\item Gravitational-wave background from defect annihilation (Math151 §II, status F2: PASS, \(\Omega_{\rm GW} \ll 10^{-8}\)).
\item CMB power-law suppression at small scales from defect-induced asymmetry.
\item Matter power spectrum \(P_m(k)\) distinct from \(\Lambda\)CDM.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Audit assessment}: These \textbf{are} concrete and testable:
\begin{itemize}
\item Defect density is a falsifiable prediction (either cosmic strings/domain walls form at the predicted scale, or they don't).
\item GW background is directly observable by LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA and PTA observations.
\item Matter power spectrum can be measured by galaxy surveys (SDSS, DESI, Euclid).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{However}, the \emph{quantitative predictions} (peak location in \(P_m(k)\), exact GW spectrum) require:
\begin{itemize}
\item Numerical simulation of Kibble-Zurek dynamics on a cosmological lattice.
\item Solution of the Einstein--BCC coupled system from transition to recombination.
\item Comparison to observational surveys.
\end{itemize}

Math159 correctly labels this as beyond the current scope (§8, scope limitations 1--3).

\textbf{Verdict: VALID, SCOPE CLARIFIED}. The rescoped observables are testable in principle. The quantitative predictions
require extended numerical work (future task). The present note correctly identifies the observable suite and acknowledges
the scope limitation.

\textbf{Objection}: Rescoping from \(n_s\) to defect spectrum is a \emph{strategic retreat}, not a solution. If TECT is a

a TECT prediction; (2) identify an alternative observable that \emph{is} a TECT prediction; (3) leave the full numerical

\textbf{Verdict: UPHELD as a scope clarification, not refuted as an evasion}. If the user's goal is a TOE that predicts \(n_s\)

\textbf{Honest assessment}: TECT can no longer claim to be a full inflationary TOE (explanans of \(n_s\)). It is a Unified Classical Field

\subsection*{4. Audit of Math160 (GAP-2 BRST Faddeev-Popov Determinant)}

\subsubsection*{4.1 Key result verification}

\textbf{Math160's main theorem}: The one-loop Faddeev--Popov determinant on BCC-emergent gauge orbits is:
\begin{equation}
\det \mathcal{M}_{\rm FP} = \exp\left[\frac{1}{(4\pi)^2} \sum_i n_i \ln\frac{\mu^2}{\lambda_i^2} + \mathrm{i}\Gamma_{\rm Berry}[\Psi]\right] \times (\text{finite}, \neq 0).
\end{equation}

\textbf{Audit check}:

(1) \textbf{Non-vanishing}: Theorem 1 (§II.4) proves \(\lambda_{\min} > 0\) via elliptic operator theory. The argument is standard
and sound. The BCC background field \(A^{\mathrm{bg}}_\mu\) is strictly localized on the first shell (finite set of lattice sites),
making the potential \(V_{\rm bg} > 0\) everywhere. Hence \(\det \mathcal{M}_{\rm FP} > 0\). **CORRECT**.

(2) \textbf{UV finiteness}: The lattice provides a natural cutoff \(\Lambda_{\rm UV} = \pi/a_{\rm BCC}\). The zeta-function integral
is manifestly finite when cut off at \(\Lambda_{\rm UV}\). After subtraction of the dimensional-regularisation pole via the standard
QFT counterterm, the finite part is scheme-dependent but well-defined. **CORRECT**.

(3) \textbf{TECT-specific Berry-phase signature}: The claim that the ghost determinant gains a factor \(\exp[\mathrm{i}\Gamma_{\rm Berry}[\Psi]]\)
from the topological defect structure of the BCC order manifold is \textbf{non-trivial and novel}. Let me audit this claim separately (§4.2).

\subsubsection*{4.2 Objection α: Is the Berry-phase identification mathematically rigorous, or heuristic?}

\textbf{Objection}: Math160 §III.2 defines the Berry phase as \(\Gamma_{\rm Berry} = \sum_{j=1}^{12} \oint \theta_j \, \mathrm{d}\rho_j\),
where \(\rho_j\) is a ``parameter path on the moduli torus.'' This is vague. What is the domain of the path integral, and when is
it actually non-trivial?

\textbf{Response in Math160}: §III.2 states that for a closed loop in \(M_{\rm BCC}\) (e.g., a cyclic permutation of the 12 reciprocal
vectors under \(O_h\)), the Berry phase is quantized: \(\Gamma_{\rm Berry} = 2\pi \times (\text{integer winding number}).\)

\textbf{Audit assessment}:

The Berry phase for a closed loop in a fiber bundle is a well-established concept (Pancharatnam-Berry, Wilczek-Zee). For the BCC
condensate with \(O_h\) cubic symmetry, the moduli space is indeed a torus \(T^{12}\) (12 phases of the reciprocal-space Fourier
amplitudes). A non-trivial loop (e.g., a braiding of the 12 defect topologies) does accumulate a quantized Berry phase.

However, Math160 does not explicitly prove that such a non-trivial loop is \emph{unavoidable} in the path integral over \(\Psi\).
The claim is that the ghost determinant \emph{depends on} the topological winding. But if all physical paths in \(\Psi\)-space have
zero winding (i.e., all loops are contractible), then the Berry-phase factor is always 1 and the TECT-specific signature vanishes.

\textbf{Verdict: VALID, SCOPE CLARIFIED}. The Berry-phase concept is rigorous. But the claim that it is a ``TECT-specific signature''
depends on the path integral exploring non-contractible loops in \(M_{\rm BCC}\). This is plausible (the BCC defect network has non-trivial
topology) but requires explicit verification that non-zero winding occurs in the dominant path-integral contributions. Currently deferred.

\textbf{Recommended mitigation}: Add a follow-up task (open in OPEN-QUESTIONS): ``Q-2026-04-26-Math160-Berry-phase-non-triviality:
Verify that the BCC Kibble-Zurek defect configuration samples non-contractible loops in \(M_{\rm BCC}\) with non-zero probability.''

\subsubsection*{4.3 Objection β: Is the conditional on Pillar-4 SO(10) redundant?}

\textbf{Objection}: Math160 claims status ``PROVED CONDITIONAL on Pillar-4 SO(10) emergence.'' But Math157 already
has this exact same conditional (GAP-3 anomaly closure is also conditional on Pillar-4). Are we just duplicating the condition?

\textbf{Response in Math160}: §VI.1 states: ``The conditional clause is identical to the dependency in Math157 (anomaly closure),

\textbf{Audit assessment}: **CORRECT**. The two conditions are genuinely the same: both GAP-2 (BRST gauge-fixing) and GAP-3

appropriately acknowledged.

\textbf{Verdict: DISMISSED}. The conditional is not redundant; it is correctly stated.

\subsubsection*{4.4 Objection γ: Does the non-linear-realisation vs.\ gauge-symmetry tension invalidate the BRST proof?}

\textbf{Objection (from Math160 §V, objection γ)}: At tree level, the \(G_{\rm SM}\) action on the BCC condensate is non-linear
(Goldstone-Nambu mechanism). BRST gauge-fixing assumes a linear local gauge symmetry acting on matter fields. The two frameworks
seem incompatible.

\textbf{Response in Math160}: §V, objection γ verdict is ``VALID, SCOPE CLARIFIED''. Math160 resolves the tension by noting that
the Faddeev-Popov procedure applies to the \emph{coupled} \((\Psi, A, \psi)\) system, where \(\psi\) is the fermionic matter field
(BCC quasiparticle excitations) that couples minimally to \(A_\mu^a\). The gauge redundancy is in the \(\psi\)-sector, not in \(\Psi\)
alone. The ghost determinant acquires \(\Psi\)-dependence (via the Berry phase) because the ghosts ``know'' that the gauge field is
emergent from the condensate.

\textbf{Audit assessment}: This is a \textbf{thoughtful resolution}. However, the honest scope limitation (explicitly stated in Math160)
is that the \emph{full} path-integral quantization of \(\Psi\) itself is deferred to Pillar-1 (Newton-Krylov solver). The present theorem
applies only to \(\Psi\) held fixed at the BCC ground state.

\textbf{Verdict: VALID, SCOPE CLARIFIED}. The theorem is correct under the stated restriction (fixed \(\Psi_0\)). The full quantum-corrected
\(\Psi\) dynamics is a separate calculation (open task).

\subsubsection*{Audit objection α: Did the audit miss circular dependencies?}

\textbf{Concern}: The audit accepted Math158's route-independence claim based on the argument that inputs (Yukawa coupling, fermion
mass) are not directly input from gravity. But could there be a more subtle circularity via the Brazovskii order-parameter VEV
\(\phi_0\)? If \(\phi_0\) is determined by the BCC free energy, which in turn depends on \(\rho_{\rm cond}\) via elastic properties,
then the fermionic saturation might be indirectly circular.

\textbf{Counter-argument (AUDIT SELF-VERDICT: DISMISSED)}:

The Brazovskii free energy is:
\[
F[\Psi] = \int d^3r \left[\frac{1}{2}(\nabla\Psi)^2 + \mu^2(T) |\Psi|^2 + \lambda |\Psi|^4 + \cdots \right].
\]

The VEV \(\phi_0 = \langle \Psi \rangle\) is determined by minimizing \(F\) with respect to \(\Psi\):
\[
\frac{\delta F}{\delta \Psi}\bigg|_{\Psi = \phi_0} = 0 \implies 2\mu^2(T) \phi_0 + 4\lambda \phi_0^3 = 0 \implies \phi_0 = \sqrt{-\mu^2(T)/2\lambda}.
\]

This depends on the \emph{temperature}, not on the elastic modulus \(\rho_{\rm cond}\). The elastic properties (sound speed \(c_T\),
bulk modulus) come from the \emph{fluctuations} of \(\Psi\) around \(\phi_0\), not from the VEV itself. Hence \(\phi_0\) is independent
of \(\rho_{\rm cond} \leftrightarrow G\).

\textbf{Verdict}: Audit objection α is DISMISSED. No hidden circularity detected.
