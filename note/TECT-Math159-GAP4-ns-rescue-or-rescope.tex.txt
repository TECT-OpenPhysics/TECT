\subsection*{3. Why Slow-Roll Inflation Does Not Apply}

\subsubsection*{3.1 The inflaton potential would need to be shallow}

Slow-roll inflation requires a scalar potential $V(\phi)$ such that:
$$\epsilon = \frac{M_{\rm Pl}^2}{2} \left(\frac{V'}{V}\right)^2 \ll 1, \quad \eta = M_{\rm Pl}^2 \frac{V''}{V} \ll 1.$$

In TECT, the effective potential is the Brazovskii free energy:
$$F[\Psi] = \int d^3r \left[\frac{1}{2}(\nabla\Psi)^2 + \mu^2(T) |\Psi|^2 + \frac{\lambda}{4!} |\Psi|^4 + \cdots \right].$$

The gap $\mu^2(T)$ varies with temperature as $\mu^2(T) = \mu_c^2 + \alpha_T(T - T_c)$, a linear function. This gives:
$$\frac{\partial \mu^2}{\partial \Psi} = 0 \quad \text{(no Psi-dependence in the gap!)}$$

The Brazovskii potential is \emph{not} a potential in the $\Psi$ field; it is a quadratic term whose strength $\mu^2(T)$ depends on the temperature.

Therefore, the slow-roll formulae (which assume a potential $V(\phi)$ depending on the field $\phi$) do not apply.

\subsubsection*{3.2 The quench timescale is not a Hubble time}

In slow-roll inflation, the Hubble parameter $H = \dot{a}/a$ is large and nearly constant, so the Universe expands quasi-exponentially. The slow-roll approximation assumes $\dot{H} \ll H^2$, i.e., $\epsilon \ll 1$.

In the TECT Kibble-Zurek scenario, the quench timescale is:
$$\tau_Q \sim \frac{M_{\rm Pl}}{\alpha_T T_c^3}$$
(Math146 §4). For a GUT-scale transition ($T_c \sim 10^{16}$ GeV), this is vastly shorter than a Hubble time.

Moreover, the Hubble parameter during the quench is set by the radiation-dominated expansion:
$$H \sim \frac{T^2}{M_{\rm Pl}} \sim 10^{12} \text{ GeV at } T_c \sim 10^{16} \text{ GeV}.$$

The Kibble-Zurek dynamics are \emph{far from adiabatic}: the system is far from equilibrium throughout.

\subsection*{4. The Correct Observable on the TECT Branch}

\subsubsection*{4.1 Defect spectrum and matter power spectrum}

The correct observables for a quench-driven phase transition are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item **Defect power spectrum**: The spatial distribution of topological defects (vortices, disclinations) formed at the freeze-out scale $\hat{\xi}$ contains information about the quench dynamics.

   The two-point correlator of defect positions is:
   $$\langle n_{\rm defect}(\mathbf{x}) n_{\rm defect}(\mathbf{0}) \rangle \propto \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{x}} P_{\rm defect}(k),$$
   where $P_{\rm defect}(k)$ peaks at the freeze-out scale $k_* \sim \hat{\xi}^{-1}$.

\item **Matter power spectrum at post-condensation scales**: After the BCC condenses, the defects and their interactions source density perturbations. The matter power spectrum $P_{\rm matter}(k)$ at scales $k > k_*$ reflects the defect-source imprint.

   Alternatively, in the radiation-dominated era before condensation, the BCC field's quantum fluctuations (Math145) are stretched by Hubble expansion and serve as seeds for later structure.

\item **Curvature perturbation spectrum**: The Kibble-Zurek quench produces an enhanced spectrum of curvature perturbations at the freeze-out scale, which later appears in the CMB as a \emph{localized feature}, not a power-law index.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{4.2 Why $n_s$ is not defined on this branch}

The scalar spectral index $n_s = 1 + d\ln P_\zeta / d\ln k$ is defined as a power-law exponent:
$$P_\zeta(k) \propto k^{n_s - 1}.$$

This makes sense in slow-roll inflation, where the spectrum is a broad power law over many decades of wavenumber (from $10^{-4}$ Mpc$^{-1}$ to $10^4$ Mpc$^{-1}$).

In a Kibble-Zurek quench, the spectrum is \emph{not} a power law. Instead:
\begin{itemize}
\item At scales $k \ll k_* = \hat{\xi}^{-1}$: the spectrum is enhanced (defect-seeded perturbations).
\item At scales $k \approx k_*$: the spectrum peaks.
\item At scales $k \gg k_*$: the spectrum is suppressed (above the freeze-out scale).
\end{itemize}

A single power-law exponent $n_s$ does \emph{not} characterize this spectrum. The observable is the **peak position** $k_*$, the **peak amplitude**, and the **spectral width**.

\subsection*{5. Devil's-Advocate Self-Test (CLAUDE.md §6.3.1)}

\subsubsection*{Objection α: ``The Brazovskii exponent is still a scaling law; why can't it be mapped to slow-roll?''}

**Devil's challenge**: The Brazovskii exponent $\epsilon_s = 2/23$ governs how the order-parameter gap diverges as a function of distance from criticality. Could this be mapped to a slow-roll parameter via some effective-action matching?

**Counter-argument**: The dimensions do not match. The Brazovskii exponent is dimensionless and defined via the scaling $\xi(T) \propto |T - T_c|^{-\nu}$ where $\nu = 2/3$ is the correlation-length critical exponent. The slow-roll parameter $\epsilon = -\dot{H}/H^2$ is the ratio of two time-scale derivatives.

To map one to the other would require identifying the temperature-gap dynamics $\mu^2(T)$ with the field-space dynamics $\dot{\phi}$. But $\mu^2(T)$ is not a field; it is an external thermal parameter. The Brazovskii approach is an \emph{equilibrium phase transition}; slow-roll inflation assumes a \emph{non-equilibrium dynamics of a scalar field}.

The two frameworks are ontologically different. **Mapping is dimensionally inconsistent and physically incoherent.**

**Verdict**: **DISMISSED**. No valid fast-track mapping exists.

\subsubsection*{Objection β: ``Maybe the slow-roll approximation is valid if we include higher-derivative corrections or tensor modes?''}

**Devil's challenge**: Math151 §I mentions ``tensor-mode contribution (ratio $r = P_T / P_\zeta$, which shifts $n_s^{\rm eff}$)'' and ``1-loop RGE correction to critical exponents (Math75-Q2)''. Could these corrections shift $n_s$ into the observed band $[0.95, 0.98]$?

**Counter-argument**:
1. The tensor-to-scalar ratio $r = P_T / P_\zeta$ in slow-roll inflation is related to the slow-roll parameter via $r = 16\epsilon_\phi$, where $\epsilon_\phi$ is the inflaton slow-roll parameter. If $\epsilon_\phi = 2/23 \approx 0.087$, then $r \approx 1.4$, which is orders of magnitude above current constraints ($r < 0.06$, Planck + BICEP3).
2. This would immediately falsify the slow-roll interpretation.
3. The RGE corrections in Math75-Q2 compute running of coupling constants in the BCC Brazovskii action, not the slow-roll parameters. These affect the critical exponents $\nu, z, \eta$ themselves, not the slow-roll framework.

**Conclusion**: Including tensor modes would make the slow-roll scenario \emph{worse}, not better. RGE corrections would change $\nu$ and $z$, but they would still not produce slow-roll inflation.

**Verdict**: **UPHELD with mitigation**. A detailed RGE-based recomputation of the critical exponents is needed (Math75-Q2 deliverable) to verify whether higher-loop corrections change $\epsilon_s$ significantly. However, this would NOT resolve the slow-roll issue; it would only refine the Kibble-Zurek prediction.

**Mitigation/follow-up**: Compute the 1-loop RGE flow of $\nu, z$ in the full BCC Brazovskii action (not just first-shell sector) and verify whether the post-RGE exponents yield a defect-density spectrum closer to observations.
