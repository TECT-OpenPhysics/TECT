\subsection*{1. Math361 Mechanism (I) ``suppression insufficient'' — assumptions}

Math361 \S 2 claim: ``Soft modes might suppress KZ defect production,
but even maximally helpful, suppression is not large enough to remove
relic defects below the cosmological threshold.''

\textbf{Implicit assumptions in Math361}:

\textbf{A1 (KZ universality class)}: TECT defect production follows
standard Kibble--Zurek scaling $\xi \sim \tau_Q^{0.26}$,
$n_{\rm defect} \sim \hat\xi^{-3} \sim \tau_Q^{-0.78}$. This is the
isotropic-Brazovskii universality class prediction, valid ONLY if
TECT $\in \mathcal{U}_{\rm iso-Brazovskii}$ in the dynamic-critical
sense (not just the static sense of Math97). Math97 establishes static
universality; dynamic universality is a separate claim.

\textbf{Reasoning gap RG1}: Could TECT belong to a different dynamic
universality class with different KZ exponents? In particular, if
TECT has a conserved order parameter (Model B in Hohenberg-Halperin
1977 classification) instead of the assumed non-conserved (Model A),
the dynamic exponent $z$ changes from $\sim 2$ to $\sim 4$, and KZ
scaling becomes $\xi \sim \tau_Q^{1/z\cdot\nu}$ with much faster
$\xi$ growth. Larger $\xi$ at the freeze-out time means SMALLER
$n_{\rm defect}$, potentially crossing the cosmological threshold.

\textbf{Action item Math385}: Determine TECT's dynamic universality
class. If Model B (conserved $\Psi$), Math361 KZ exponent is wrong
and Mechanism (I) suppression may be sufficient.

\textbf{A2 (Quench timescale $\tau_Q$ origin)}: Math361 takes $\tau_Q$
from cosmological Hubble timescale. But the BCC condensation
transition might not be governed by the cosmological clock — it
could be governed by an internal TECT timescale (e.g., the shell
mode period $1/q_0$ or the condensate amplitude relaxation).

\textbf{Reasoning gap RG2}: If $\tau_Q$ is the internal timescale
$\sim 1/q_0$ (much shorter than Hubble), then $\hat\xi$ at freeze-out
is much smaller, $n_{\rm defect}$ much LARGER. Math361's choice of
$\tau_Q = H^{-1}$ could be too generous (slow quench → small defect
density). However, if $\tau_Q$ is even shorter, the freeze-out
mechanism might fundamentally differ from KZ (rapid quench limit).

\textbf{Action item Math386}: Determine the correct $\tau_Q$ for BCC
condensation under TECT cosmological dynamics. Might require coupling
the condensation field to FRW background.

\subsection*{2. Math362 Mechanism (II) ``Hubble friction ACCELERATES'' —
counter-intuitive verdict deserves re-audit}

Math362 \S (Mechanism (II) verdict): ``Hubble friction actually
ACCELERATES relic production by preventing annihilation.''

This is a counter-intuitive result. Standard expectation: friction
SUPPRESSES nonequilibrium structures by damping them. The Math362
argument: at the freezing timescale $\tau_f \sim H^{-1}$, defects
cannot encounter each other (relative velocity damped); annihilation
rate $\to 0$; relic abundance frozen at the production rate.

\textbf{Implicit assumptions in Math362}:

\textbf{A3 (Defect velocity scaling)}: Math362 assumes defects move
with thermal velocity $v_{\rm th} \sim T^{1/2}$ damped by Hubble
$H$. The damping equation $\dot v = -3 H v + F_{\rm thermal}/m$
gives terminal $v_{\rm term} \sim F_{\rm thermal}/(3 H m)$.
Annihilation rate $\Gamma_{\rm ann} \sim n \sigma v_{\rm term}$
which $\to 0$ as $H \to 0$ (late times).

\textbf{Reasoning gap RG3}: This assumes \emph{thermal} velocity
distribution. In a freezing-out condensate, defects are
\emph{topologically pinned} to the BCC lattice; their motion is
governed by lattice dynamics (phonon-like), not thermal. Lattice
defect velocity could be MUCH higher than $v_{\rm term}$, especially
near the freeze-out time when the BCC is still soft. Math362's
$v_{\rm term} \to 0$ as $H \to 0$ may be wrong; lattice defects can
have $v$ independent of $H$ at the relevant scales.

\textbf{Action item Math387}: Compute the lattice-pinned defect
velocity distribution at freeze-out. If $v$ is driven by BCC
dynamics (not thermal), $\Gamma_{\rm ann}$ does not vanish in the
$H \to 0$ limit, and Math362's verdict ``Hubble friction
ACCELERATES'' could be inverted to ``Hubble friction has minimal
effect; standard equilibrium $\Gamma_{\rm ann}$ governs.''

\textbf{A4 (Boundary conditions: cosmological horizon vs periodic
lattice)}: Math362 implicitly assumes the cosmological horizon
$\sim H^{-1}$ provides a boundary that separates causally
disconnected patches. Defect density per Hubble volume becomes the
relevant quantity. But the Math376/378-380 numerical lattice uses
periodic boundary conditions, which corresponds to an INFINITE
universe with no horizon-induced patch separation.

\textbf{Reasoning gap RG4}: The Math360/361/362 cosmological argument
operates in FRW spacetime; the Math376-380 BCC stability verification
operates on a periodic lattice. These two are physically
disconnected. The relic-defect counting in Math362 may not directly
apply to the canonical Brazovskii partition function on a periodic
domain, and vice versa: BCC stability on a periodic lattice does
not automatically imply cosmological uniqueness in FRW.

\textbf{Action item Math388}: Reconcile the two boundary-condition
frameworks. If Math362's FRW-horizon argument has a periodic-lattice
analog (i.e., applies to TECT's canonical formulation), the verdict
stands. If not, $E_3'$ may be a false constraint imported from FRW
that doesn't apply to the canonical TECT free energy.

as the failure criterion, a $10^{-22}$ suppression would EASILY
satisfy the bound. RG1 (Model B reclassification) could be sufficient
on its own.

\textbf{Sign-direction}: All four RG corrections push toward LOWER
defect density / WEAKER constraint, which would make $E_3'$ EASIER
to satisfy. If any single RG resolves in favor of TECT, Pillar 4
could close.

\textbf{Order of priority}: Math388 (boundary conditions) has the
