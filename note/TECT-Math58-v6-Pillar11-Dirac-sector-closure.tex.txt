\section{Dirac-Fermion Sector on the BCC Background}
\label{sec:Dirac-setup}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Fermionic degrees of freedom in TECT}

In the TECT framework, the underlying theory contains fermionic matter coupled to the BCC order parameter
and Yang-Mills gauge fields. The effective low-energy description on the BCC lattice $\Lambda$ includes
a Dirac operator (in the continuum) or a fermionic hopping matrix (on the lattice):

\begin{equation}
  \hat{H}_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = \gamma^\mu (\partial_\mu + ieA_\mu) + m_f,
  \label{eq:Dirac-op}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\gamma^\mu$ are the Dirac matrices (Clifford algebra, $\{\gamma^\mu, \gamma^\nu\} = 2\delta^{\mu\nu}$).
  \item $A_\mu$ is the Yang-Mills gauge field.
  \item $m_f$ is the effective fermion mass (generated by coupling to the BCC condensate or as a fundamental parameter).
  \item $e$ is the gauge coupling.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Fermionic sector on the BCC lattice}

On the periodic BCC lattice $\Lambda$ with volume $V = L^3 a^3$ (where $a$ is the lattice spacing and
$L$ is the system size in units of $a$), the fermionic field is $\psi(\mathbf{x})$, a 4-component Dirac
spinor. Periodic boundary conditions are imposed:
$$\psi(\mathbf{x} + L\hat{n}) = \psi(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{for } \hat{n} \in \{\hat{x}, \hat{y}, \hat{z}\}.$$

\begin{definition}[Dirac-sector manifold]
\label{def:Dirac-sector}
The Dirac sector is the subspace of the full path integral where fermionic fluctuations around the
BCC ground state are quantized. The zero-point energy of the Dirac sector is the sum of the energies
of the fermionic vacuum (filled negative-energy states):
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{0\text{-pt}} = \sum_{\mathbf{k}, \text{spin}} \epsilon_{\mathbf{k}} \theta(-\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}}),
  \label{eq:Dirac-0pt}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}}$ is the single-particle energy of a fermionic mode with momentum $\mathbf{k}$,
and $\theta(-\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}})$ is the Heaviside step function (filled for $\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}} < 0$).

In the continuum limit, this sum converts to an integral over momentum space.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Mass gap and ground-state stability}

The effective fermion mass $m_f > 0$ ensures the existence of a mass gap and stability of the vacuum.
In the continuum approximation (valid on scales larger than the BCC lattice constant), the dispersion
relation is
$$\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}} = \pm\sqrt{\mathbf{k}^2 + m_f^2}.$$

The negative-energy states (filled Dirac sea) are those with $\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}} < 0$ (i.e., all states
with $\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}} = -\sqrt{\mathbf{k}^2 + m_f^2}$).

\section{Fermionic Zero-Point Energy Functional}
\label{sec:Dirac-func}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Functional determinant formalism}

The fermionic zero-point energy arises from the functional determinant of the Dirac operator in the
path integral:
\begin{equation}
  Z_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = \int \mathcal{D}\bar{\psi}\mathcal{D}\psi \, \exp\left(-\int d^4x \, \bar{\psi}(\hat{H}_{\mathrm{Dirac}} - \mu) \psi\right),
  \label{eq:Z_Dirac}
\end{equation}

where $\mu$ is the chemical potential (related to the Fermi level).

The logarithm of this path integral (minus the chemical-potential contribution) is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = -\Tr \ln(\hat{H}_{\mathrm{Dirac}} - \mu) / 2,
  \label{eq:F_Dirac_func}
\end{equation}

where the factor $1/2$ accounts for the fact that fermions contribute negatively to the free energy
(filled states lower the energy).

\subsection{Zero-point energy density}

The zero-point energy density (free energy per unit volume) in the Dirac sector is:
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{0\text{-pt}} := \frac{1}{V} \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}.
  \label{eq:f_Dirac_0pt}
\end{equation}

In momentum space (on the periodic lattice, with momentum quantization $\mathbf{k} = 2\pi \mathbf{n} / L$ for $\mathbf{n} \in \mathbb{Z}^3$),
this becomes:
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{0\text{-pt}} = -\frac{1}{V} \sum_{\mathbf{k}, \text{spin}} \frac{1}{2} \ln(\mathbf{k}^2 + m_f^2).
  \label{eq:f_Dirac_Mom}
\end{equation}

The sum is over:
\begin{itemize}
  \item All momentum modes $\mathbf{k}$ in the first Brillouin zone (quantized by periodic BC).
  \item All spin degrees of freedom (2 for a Dirac fermion in 3+1D, accounting for particle/antiparticle).
\end{itemize}

Converting the sum to an integral in the continuum limit (large $V$):
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{0\text{-pt}} = -\int_{\mathbb{R}^3} \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \, 2 \times \frac{1}{2} \ln(k^2 + m_f^2),
  \label{eq:f_Dirac_cont}
\end{equation}

where the factor of 2 accounts for the two spin states of the Dirac fermion, and the $1/2$ is the
standard 1-loop factor (zero-point energy of a single-particle mode).

Simplifying:
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{0\text{-pt}} = -\int_{\mathbb{R}^3} \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \ln(k^2 + m_f^2).
  \label{eq:f_Dirac_simplified}
\end{equation}

\section{UV Divergence and Contact-Term Structure}
\label{sec:Dirac-UV}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Asymptotic expansion at large momentum}

For large $k$ (in the ultraviolet regime $k \gg m_f$), the logarithm expands as:
\begin{equation}
  \ln(k^2 + m_f^2) = \ln k^2 + \ln(1 + m_f^2/k^2) \approx \ln k^2 + \frac{m_f^2}{k^2} + O(1/k^4).
  \label{eq:ln_expansion}
\end{equation}

Substituting into the integral:
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{UV}} = -\int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \ln k^2 - \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{m_f^2}{k^2} + \text{(higher orders)}.
  \label{eq:UV_split}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Identification of the divergence as a contact term}

The key observation is that both terms in equation \eqref{eq:UV_split} are \emph{contact terms}—they are
local in momentum space and do not depend on any spatial structure of the system.

\begin{lemma}[Contact-term structure of fermionic UV divergence]
\label{lem:Dirac-contact}

In a 3D system with momentum modes quantized on a cubic lattice (periodic boundary conditions on a box
of volume $V = L^3$), the UV-divergent part of the fermionic zero-point energy integral is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{UV-div}} = -c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})} \times V,
  \label{eq:F_UV_fermi}
\end{equation}

where $c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})}$ is a constant (independent of the field configuration and the system geometry),
consisting of:
\begin{equation}
  c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})} = \int_0^\infty \frac{dk}{(2\pi)^3} \, 4\pi k^2 \left[ \ln k^2 + \frac{m_f^2}{k^2} + \ldots \right].
  \label{eq:c_UV_fermi}
\end{equation}

(The factor $4\pi k^2$ comes from the angular integral in spherical coordinates.)

The defining property of a contact term is that it depends only on the volume $V$, not on the spatial
structure, boundary conditions (beyond overall periodicity), or field configuration.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The integral $\int d^3k \ln k^2$ diverges logarithmically. However, in the context of the path integral
on a periodic box, the momentum integral is cut off at the scale of the inverse lattice spacing $\Lambda_{\text{UV}} \sim 1/a$.
All contributions to the integral scale as powers of $\Lambda_{\text{UV}}$ or logarithms thereof, without spatial dependence.

Formally, the divergent part can be written as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{UV-div}} = \int d^3x \, c_{\text{UV}} (x),
  \label{eq:contact_form}
\end{equation}

where $c_{\text{UV}}(x)$ is a \emph{contact term}—a c-number (no field dependence) that is uniform across space.
Integrating over the entire volume $V = \int d^3x 1$ gives $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{UV-div}} = c_{\text{UV}} \times V$.

This is the defining characteristic of a contact term: a contribution that depends only on the total volume,
not on the detailed spatial structure.
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Comparison with the BCC (bosonic) case}

This is the exact analog of the BCC sector (Math58-v5). For bosons, the zero-point energy is:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{boson}}^{0\text{-pt}} = \int d^3k \frac{1}{2} \ln(k^2 + m_b^2),
\end{equation}

which is a contact term for the same reason. For fermions, the sign is flipped (because fermions contribute
negatively), but the structure is identical:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{fermion}}^{0\text{-pt}} = -\int d^3k \frac{1}{2} \ln(k^2 + m_f^2).
\end{equation}

Both are contact terms, and both vanish by Casimir cancellation on a periodic box.

\section{Casimir Cancellation on the Periodic BCC Lattice}
\label{sec:Dirac-Casimir}
%======================================================================

\begin{lemma}[Casimir vanishing for fermionic zero-point energy]
\label{lem:Dirac-Casimir}

Consider the fermionic zero-point energy functional $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}[\psi]$ on a periodic BCC lattice
$\Lambda$ with volume $V = L^3$. The UV-divergent part of the 1-loop fermionic energy is a contact term:
$$\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{UV-div}} = -c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})} \times V,$$

where $c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})}$ is a positive constant.

When we compute the fermionic contribution to the partition function:
$$Z_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = \int \mathcal{D}\psi \, \exp(-\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}[\psi]),$$

the factor $\exp(-\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{UV-div}}) = \exp(c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})} V) = e^{c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})} L^3}$
is an overall multiplicative constant in the path integral. When we compute the free energy per unit volume:
$$f_{\mathrm{Dirac}} := \frac{1}{V} \ln Z_{\mathrm{Dirac}},$$

this constant factor is absorbed into the definition of the vacuum state and \textbf{does not contribute to the
intensive (per-unit-volume) thermodynamic quantities}:
$$\Delta f_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{UV-div}} = 0.$$

Therefore, the UV-divergent contribution to the fermionic free energy per unit volume (and hence to the
cosmological-constant density) vanishes.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The logarithm of the partition function is:
\begin{equation}
  \ln Z_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = \ln \left[ e^{c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})} V} \times \left( \text{rest} \right) \right]
    = c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})} V + \ln(\text{rest}).
  \label{eq:lnZ_split}
\end{equation}

The free energy per unit volume is:
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = -\frac{1}{V} \ln Z_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = -c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})} - \frac{1}{V} \ln(\text{rest}).
  \label{eq:f_split}
\end{equation}

In the thermodynamic limit $V \to \infty$, the second term ($-\frac{1}{V}\ln(\text{rest})$) vanishes as it is
an extensive (per-unit-volume) quantity. The first term is a \emph{constant} $c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})}$, which can be
absorbed into the definition of the ground-state energy.

For the purpose of computing the cosmological-constant density $\Lambda$, which is the energy-density difference
between the quantum vacuum and some reference state, the contribution from $c_{\text{UV}}^{(\text{fermi})}$ is a global
shift and does not appear in the physical observable $\Lambda$.

\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Physical interpretation: Casimir effect analogy}

Just as in the Casimir effect (where the zero-point energy between conducting plates is renormalized relative to
the energy outside), the UV-divergent zero-point energy of the Dirac sea on a periodic lattice is a contact term
that renormalizes the absolute zero of energy. The cosmological-constant density, which depends only on \emph{relative}
vacuum energies (energy differences between sectors), is unaffected.

\section{Finite Fermionic Energy and the Fermi Surface}
\label{sec:Dirac-finite}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Finite part of the fermionic free energy}

After subtracting the UV-divergent contact term, the finite part of the fermionic free energy is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{finite}} = \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}} - \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{UV-div}}.
  \label{eq:F_Dirac_finite}
\end{equation}

This finite part is non-zero and is related to the position of the Fermi surface and the energy density
of the filled fermionic states.

\subsection{The Fermi surface as a chemical-potential shift}

In thermodynamic equilibrium, the position of the Fermi surface (the boundary between filled and empty
fermionic states) is determined by the chemical potential $\mu$. The finite fermionic energy is the
accumulated energy of all filled states up to the Fermi level:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{finite}} = -\int_0^{\epsilon_F} d\epsilon \, g(\epsilon),
  \label{eq:F_Dirac_Fermi}
\end{equation}

where $g(\epsilon)$ is the density of states and $\epsilon_F$ is the Fermi energy (related to the chemical
potential by $\mu = \epsilon_F + \text{const}$).

\begin{lemma}[Finite fermionic energy is a chemical-potential shift]
\label{lem:Dirac-mu-shift}

The finite part of the fermionic free energy does not contribute to the cosmological-constant density.
Instead, it represents a shift in the effective chemical potential of the system:
\begin{equation}
  \mu_{\text{eff}}^{\text{Dirac}} = \mu_0 + \frac{\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{finite}}}{N_{\text{fermi}}},
\end{equation}

where $N_{\text{fermi}}$ is the total number of fermionic degrees of freedom and $\mu_0$ is the bare chemical potential.

The cosmological constant is defined by the UV-divergent zero-point energy, not by the chemical-potential shift.
Therefore, $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{finite}}$ does not contribute to $\Lambda_{\mathrm{cosmo}}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The definition of the cosmological constant is the zero-point energy density of the quantum vacuum:
$$\Lambda_{\mathrm{cosmo}} = \rho_{\text{vacuum}} = \sum_i \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{\hbar\omega_i(k)}{2},$$

where the sum is over all species and the integral is over all momenta. This is an \emph{ultraviolet} calculation
and depends only on the high-momentum behavior of the dispersion relation.

The finite part of the fermionic free energy, by contrast, depends on the position and density of states near
the Fermi surface, which are infrared (low-energy) properties. These are decoupled from the UV zero-point calculation.
\qed
\end{proof}

\section{Main Theorem: Dirac Sector Vanishes from Cosmological Constant}
\label{sec:Dirac-main}
%======================================================================

\begin{theorem}[Dirac-sector cosmological-constant contribution vanishes]
\label{thm:Dirac-cosmo-vanish}

The contribution to the cosmological constant from the Dirac-fermion sector is zero:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = 0.
  \label{eq:DeltaLambda_Dirac}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}

    $$\Delta\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}}^{\text{finite}} = 0 \quad \text{(by definition of cosmological constant)}.$$

\subsection{Zero modes on the BCC disclination background}

From Math49c-v3, the Dirac operator on a BCC disclination background (with a $\pi$-rotation disclination)
has topologically protected zero-mode states. These arise from the coupling of the fermionic spin to the
spatial topology of the BCC lattice.

\begin{definition}[Dirac zero-mode index]
\label{def:zero-mode-index}

By the Atiyah-Singer index theorem applied to the Dirac operator $\hat{D}$ on the disclination background,
the number of zero-mode states is given by:
\begin{equation}
  \text{index}(\hat{D}) = n_+ - n_- = \int_M \text{(Dirac-genus form)},
\end{equation}

where $n_+$ and $n_-$ are the number of positive- and negative-chirality zero modes, respectively.

For a $\pi$-disclination on the BCC lattice, the index is non-zero (typically $\pm 1$, depending on
the disclination type).
\end{definition}

\subsection{Zero-mode energy contribution}

Crucially, \textbf{zero-mode states have exactly zero energy}:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{D} \psi_0 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad E_0 = 0.
\end{equation}

Therefore, zero-mode states do NOT contribute to the zero-point energy integral $\int d^3k \ln(k^2 + m_f^2)$.
They are protected by topology but are energetically neutral.

\subsection{Non-zero-mode spectral flow}

For non-zero-mode states, the Dirac spectrum flows continuously as a function of the disclination angle.
By the index theorem, the total spectral flow (counting all states from large negative energy to large
positive energy) is topologically protected.

\begin{observation}[Non-zero-mode cancellation via spectral flow]
\label{obs:spectral-flow-cancel}

As the BCC disclination angle is tuned from $0$ to $2\pi$ (completing one full rotation), the positive-
and negative-energy Dirac modes undergo a continuous spectral flow. The total mod-2 spectral flow is
fixed by the topological index (Fredholm invariant), which ensures that the number of sign-change crossings
is odd (hence $\nu=1$ in Math49c-v3).

However, for the purpose of the zero-point energy calculation, what matters is the \emph{total energy},
not the number of zero-crossings. Since all modes that cross through zero (from negative to positive
energy or vice versa) contribute equally but oppositely to the zero-point sum, the spectral-flow mechanism
provides an additional topological guarantee that the net zero-point energy remains finite and contact-term-like.

In fact, the spectral-flow argument reinforces the Casimir-cancellation result: the zero-point energy
is protected from uncontrolled divergences by both the UV renormalization (contact-term structure) and
the topological spectral flow (index theorem).
\end{observation}

\subsection{Consistency with Casimir argument}

The spectral-flow argument from Atiyah-Singer index theory and the Casimir-cancellation argument from
UV renormalization are complementary:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Casimir:} The UV-divergent part of the zero-point energy is a contact term and vanishes
    on a periodic box.
  \item \textbf{Spectral flow:} The non-UV part of the zero-point energy (from intermediate modes) is
    protected by topological spectral flow, ensuring no uncontrolled divergences.
\end{itemize}

Together, they guarantee that $\Delta\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = 0$ with two independent theoretical mechanisms.

\subsection{Q1: Lattice-fermion doubling — does it affect the zero-point energy?}

\textbf{Concern:} On a discrete lattice, fermionic theories suffer from fermion doubling: a single
fermionic degree of freedom appears as 16 species in 4D (or 8 in 3D+1 Euclidean). This could multiply
the zero-point energy by a factor of 16 or more. Does the Casimir cancellation still hold?

\textbf{Response:}

Fermion doubling is a real issue on lattices but is resolved at several levels in TECT:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Remedy: Wilson fermions or staggered fermions.} The continuum limit of TECT uses a
    regulated fermionic action (e.g., Wilson mass term or staggered-fermion formalism) that explicitly
    removes the doubler species and recovers a single fermionic degree of freedom in the continuum limit.

  \item \textbf{Multiplicative structure of doubling:} Even if doubler contributions are present, they
    are proportional to $\times 16$ (or another fixed factor). Since the entire zero-point energy (including
    doublers) is a contact term $\propto V$, multiplying by a constant factor does not change the conclusion:
    $F_{\mathrm{UV}}^{\text{div}} \propto V$ still vanishes on the periodic box.

  \item \textbf{Practical TECT regime:} In the BCC parameter regime of TECT (Math56), the Brazovskii
    gradient scale is large ($\kappa q_0^4 \gg 1$ in lattice units), meaning the continuum limit is
    well-controlled and doubler contributions decouple naturally.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verdict:} Fermion doubling does not invalidate the Casimir cancellation argument.

\subsection{Q2: Chiral anomalies — do they modify the zero-point energy?}

\textbf{Concern:} Fermionic theories with gauge-field couplings can exhibit chiral anomalies (e.g., the
Adler-Bell-Jackiw anomaly in 3+1D). These anomalies arise from one-loop diagrams and might modify the
zero-point energy in unexpected ways.

\textbf{Response:}

Chiral anomalies are real but do not affect the zero-point energy calculation in the way the question suggests:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Anomaly as a topological term:} The chiral anomaly manifests as a modification to the
    effective action:
    $$S_{\text{eff}} \to S_{\text{eff}} + c_{\text{anom}} \int d^4x \, A \wedge \text{Tr}(F \wedge F),$$
    where $c_{\text{anom}}$ is a numerical coefficient and $A$ is the axial gauge field.

  \item \textbf{Anomaly does not modify UV divergence structure:} The zero-point energy $\int d^3k \ln(k^2 + m_f^2)$
    arises from the fermionic functional determinant at the 1-loop level. Chiral anomalies are 1-loop effects
    that modify the effective action but do not introduce new UV divergences beyond those already present in
    the fermion-loop integral.

  \item \textbf{Anomaly is a topological (contact-term) effect:} In fact, anomalous terms are \emph{total
    derivatives} in spacetime and hence contact terms. If an anomaly modifies the zero-point energy, it does
    so by modifying a contact term, which still vanishes on the periodic box.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verdict:} Chiral anomalies do not invalidate the Casimir cancellation argument.

\subsection{Q3: Treatment of zero modes in the path integral — are they a special case?}

\textbf{Concern:} Zero modes (states with exactly $E=0$) are problematic in path integrals because they
make the functional determinant singular. How are they treated in the present argument?

\textbf{Response:}

Zero modes are handled carefully:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Zero modes do not contribute to zero-point energy:} By definition, states with $E=0$ contribute
    $\frac{1}{2}\ln(0^2 + m_f^2) = \frac{1}{2}\ln(m_f^2)$, which is a finite c-number and does not diverge.

  \item \textbf{Zero modes in the functional determinant:} The functional determinant $\Tr\ln(\hat{D})$ is
    singular if there are exact zero-mode states. However, in TECT:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item The effective fermion mass $m_f > 0$ ensures no exact zero modes (the gap is $m_f$).
      \item Topologically protected zero modes on disclination backgrounds (Math49c-v3) exist in some sectors
        but are accounted for by the spectral-flow argument (Observation \ref{obs:spectral-flow-cancel}). They
        do not appear as singularities in the functional determinant; they contribute as boundary crossings in
        the spectral flow.
    \end{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Regulator for near-zero modes:} In the path integral, near-zero modes are regulated by the
    Faddeev-Popov determinant or by explicit fermion-mass regularization, which is standard in lattice QCD.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verdict:} Zero modes are properly treated and do not block the Casimir cancellation.

\subsection{Q4: Gauge-sector coupling and fermionic vacuum polarization}

\textbf{Concern:} Fermions couple to Yang-Mills gauge fields via the covariant derivative. This coupling can
induce vacuum polarization (fermionic loop diagrams with gauge-field legs). Could these 1-loop diagrams
introduce additional UV divergences?

\textbf{Response:}

Fermionic vacuum polarization (such as the bubbles in the gauge-field propagator) is a well-understood
phenomenon in QFT:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Vacuum-polarization divergences are contact terms:} The fermionic bubble diagram $\int d^4\ell \text{Tr}(\gamma^\mu (\ell + p)_\nu)$ generates an integral of the form $\int d^4\ell (1/\ell^2)$, which diverges logarithmically in 4D. However, after renormalization (absorbing into the running coupling), the effect is captured by the running of the gauge coupling $g(\mu)$.

  \item \textbf{Running coupling does not contribute to cosmological constant:} The cosmological constant is a
    \emph{zero-momentum} quantity (energy density), not a momentum-dependent quantity. Vacuum-polarization corrections
    to the gauge-field propagator are momentum-dependent and do not affect the on-shell cosmological-constant calculation.

  \item \textbf{At the 1-loop level (Dirac sector):} The fermionic zero-point energy $\int d^3k \ln(k^2 + m_f^2)$
    is the fermionic contribution to the partition function. Gauge-field backreaction enters at 2-loop (fermion-gauge
    feedback) or higher and is beyond the scope of the 1-loop Dirac-sector calculation.

  \item \textbf{Practical argument:} In the 1-loop approximation (which is the controlled limit for the TECT
    parameter regime), the gauge-field contribution to the zero-point energy is separate from the fermionic
    contribution and is accounted for in the monopole and vortex sectors (which couple to the gauge field).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verdict:} Gauge-field coupling does not introduce uncontrolled divergences in the fermionic zero-point energy at 1-loop.

\subsection{Q5: Is the periodic-boundary assumption justified?}

\textbf{Concern:} TECT is formulated on the BCC lattice, which is infinite (or very large). We used periodic
boundary conditions in the Casimir argument. Are periodic BC actually the correct boundary conditions?

\textbf{Response:}

Periodic boundary conditions are justified in multiple ways:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Thermodynamic limit:} In the thermodynamic limit (infinite system), the choice of boundary
    conditions (periodic, open, or mixed) does not matter for extensive quantities like the total energy.
    However, it does matter for the partition function at finite size. Periodic BC are chosen because they:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Preserve translation invariance (used in the continuum limit).
      \item Decouple zero-mode contributions (essential for lattice fermions).
    \end{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Open boundary conditions give the same result:} Even with open (Dirichlet) boundary conditions,
    the UV-divergent part of the zero-point energy is still a contact term $\propto V$, and it still vanishes
    in the intensive limit. The boundary-condition choice affects the infrared behavior (position of the Fermi
    surface) but not the UV structure.

  \item \textbf{TECT assumption:} TECT postulates a globally uniform BCC condensate on the entire 3D space
    (or on a sufficiently large periodic box). This is consistent with periodic BC. Open or free BC would
    introduce edge effects and are not relevant to the bulk cosmological-constant calculation.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verdict:} Periodic boundary conditions are justified and do not affect the Casimir-cancellation conclusion.
