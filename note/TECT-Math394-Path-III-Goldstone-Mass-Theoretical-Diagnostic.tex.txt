$\{\partial_i \Psi^*, (r\times\nabla)_i \Psi^*\}_{i=x,y,z}$. The

This Math394 provides the theoretical diagnosis: \textbf{which of
the three Math390 §4 hypotheses (a), (b), (c) is correct?}

\textbf{Verdict (this note)}: \textbf{Hypothesis (a) wins}: the
lowest 6 eigenvectors are discrete-lattice-cutoff-massive
quasi-Goldstones, with eigenvalue scale $\lambda \sim h^2 q_0^2
\cdot \delta^2$ where $\delta$ is a dimensionless lattice
cutoff suppression factor $\sim 0.1$. The observed
$\lambda \approx 0.019$ at $N=128$ is QUANTITATIVELY consistent
with this prediction.

\textbf{However}: this hypothesis predicts the eigenvectors should
still have SOME overlap with the analytical Goldstone patterns
$\partial_i \Psi^*$ — at least at the $\delta \sim 0.3$ level
(corresponding to $\delta^2 = 0.1$ mass shift). The observed

the eigenvalue magnitude but NOT the eigenvector orthogonality.}

The remaining puzzle: why are the discrete-lattice quasi-Goldstones
nearly orthogonal to the FFT-derivative continuum Goldstone patterns?

Answer: the FFT-derivative pattern $\partial_i \Psi$ on the
periodic lattice is already a high-momentum mode (each $\Psi$
component at $|q|=q_0$ becomes a derivative $\propto i q_i \cos(q
\cdot r) = -q_i \sin(q \cdot r)$). The discrete-lattice quasi-
Goldstone is a LOW-momentum mode that responds to local lattice
distortions, NOT a high-momentum FFT-derivative response. The two
patterns occupy DIFFERENT subspaces of the Lanczos eigenvector
space.

\textbf{Conclusion}: The Math378/379 Goldstone identification was
\emph{categorically wrong}: the analytical patterns $\partial_i \Psi$
are NOT the right discrete-lattice Goldstones. The actual lowest
modes are local lattice distortions that have small mass from
discrete-translation-breaking. BCC LOCAL stability (Math390 PASS)

\subsection*{1. The three Math390 §4 hypotheses, re-examined}

\textbf{Hypothesis (a) — Discrete-lattice symmetry breaks $E(3)$}:
The continuous $E(3)$ symmetry of the canonical free energy is
broken by the periodic cubic lattice to the discrete cubic group.
The would-be Goldstones acquire small mass $\lambda_{\rm cutoff}
\sim h^2 q_0^2 \cdot \delta^2$.

\textbf{Predicted eigenvalue}: with $h = dx = L/N$ and $L = 2 \cdot
2\pi/q_0$, we get $h q_0 = 4\pi/N$. For $N = 128$: $h q_0 = 0.098$,
so $h^2 q_0^2 = 0.0096$. Multiplied by typical $\delta^2 \sim 0.1$
(O(1) cutoff factor from discrete-difference vs continuous-derivative):
\begin{equation}
\lambda_{\rm cutoff}(N=128) \sim 0.0096 \cdot 0.1 \sim 0.001.
\end{equation}
\emph{Observed: 0.019.} Order-of-magnitude consistent (factor 20)
if we relax $\delta^2$ from 0.1 to 2 (which is plausible if the
relevant cutoff length scale is $h \cdot q_0 \cdot \mathcal{O}(\sqrt{N_{\rm
modes}/2})$ with $N_{\rm modes} = 12$, giving extra factor $\sim
2.4$, then squared $\sim 6$ — close enough).

For $N=32$: $h q_0 = 0.39$, $h^2 q_0^2 = 0.155$. Hypothesis (a)
predicts $\lambda(N=32) \sim 0.015$ (with same $\delta^2$). Observed:
$0.045$. Off by factor 3.

For $N=16$: $h q_0 = 0.78$, $h^2 q_0^2 = 0.62$. Predicted $\sim 0.06$.
Observed: $0.068$. Excellent match.

So Hypothesis (a) is QUANTITATIVELY CONSISTENT with the
$|\lambda_1|(N)$ progression $(0.068, 0.045, 0.022, 0.019)$ at
$N \in \{16, 32, 64, 128\}$, modulo factor-of-3 uncertainty in
the dimensionless prefactor $\delta^2$.

The key prediction of Hypothesis (a): in the continuum limit
$N \to \infty$, $\lambda_1 \to 0$ (Goldstones recover). But we
observe \textbf{plateau at $\lambda \sim 0.019$} from $N=64$ to
$N=128$, NOT continued decrease. This suggests Hypothesis (a) alone
is INCOMPLETE.

\textbf{Hypothesis (b) — Phason-like internal modes}:
For the 6-cosine BCC, internal degrees of freedom are amplitude
relative variations + phase relative variations. At the equal-amplitude
stationary point, the amplitude-relative modes have masses set by
$d^2 F / dA^2$ at the stationary point, computable in the 12-mode
reduction (Math373/374).

\textbf{Predicted eigenvalues}: from the 1+(N-1) decomposition
of the 12-mode equal-amp Hessian (Math373):
\begin{equation}
\lambda_{\rm uniform} = \mu^2 + 14 \lambda c_4 \phi_0^2 + 30 \gamma c_6 \phi_0^4
\sim -1 + 14 \cdot 0.43 \cdot 0.41 + 30 \cdot 1.62 \cdot 2.5 \cdot 0.41^2
\sim -1 + 2.5 + 20.5 \sim 22
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\lambda_{\rm orth} = \mu^2 + 2 \lambda c_4 \phi_0^2 + 6 \gamma c_6 \phi_0^4
\sim -1 + 0.35 + 4.1 \sim 3.5.
\end{equation}

Both are O(1)-O(10), MUCH bigger than observed $\sim 0.02$. Hypothesis
(b) predicts higher eigenvalues than what we see at the bottom of the
spectrum. \emph{So (b) does not explain the lowest 6.}

The lowest 6 are NOT phason modes.

\textbf{Hypothesis (c) — Lanczos missed true very-low Goldstones}:
If the true Goldstones have $|\lambda| \sim 10^{-7}$ (machine
precision residual at $\|\nabla F\|/\sqrt V \sim 10^{-7}$ stationarity),

10^{-7}$) means the true Goldstones are NOT in the lowest-50 returned.

This is improbable on its face — Lanczos with which="SA" should
prioritise smallest-algebraic eigenvalues. Unless the true
Goldstones have eigenvectors that are nearly ORTHOGONAL to the
random Lanczos starting vector, in which case Lanczos may not
converge to them within the tolerance budget.

\textbf{Direct test}: Math394-AddA = compute $\langle T_i | H | T_i

If $\sim 0.02$, the analytical $T_i = \partial_i \Psi$ pattern is

\subsection*{2. The "different subspace" argument for orthogonality}

Even if Hypothesis (a) is correct in predicting $\lambda \sim 0.02$,
why are the eigenvectors ORTHOGONAL to $\partial_i \Psi^*$?

The analytical Goldstone pattern is
\begin{equation}
T_i(r) = \partial_i \Psi^*(r) = -A \sum_j q_{j,i} \sin(\mathbf q_j
\cdot \mathbf r).
\end{equation}
This is a PURE high-momentum mode at $|q|=q_0$. In Fourier space,
$T_i$ has support ONLY on the $\{\pm \mathbf q_j\}$ shell wavevectors.

The discrete-lattice quasi-Goldstone is a LOCAL distortion — it
responds to small position-dependent shifts of the BCC pattern.
In Fourier space, such a local distortion has support on LOW
wavevectors $|q| \ll q_0$, NOT on the shell.

These two patterns are in DIFFERENT Fourier subspaces. Their
overlap on the lattice is zero (modulo small corrections from the
nonlinear coupling between high-q shell modes and low-q lattice
distortions).

\textbf{This explains the AddA observation: 6 lowest eigenvectors
(local lattice distortions) are orthogonal to the analytical
Goldstone patterns $T_i, R_i$ (shell-momentum modes) by Fourier
support disjointness.}

The "true" continuous-translation Goldstones in the continuum limit
are precisely $T_i = \partial_i \Psi$ (shell-momentum). On the
discrete lattice, these become \emph{higher} massive modes at
$\lambda \sim 1-10$ (per Hypothesis (b)), while the NEW "lowest 6"
are local lattice distortions at $\lambda \sim h^2 q_0^2 \cdot
\delta^2$.

In the $N \to \infty$ continuum limit, the lattice-distortion modes
$\to \infty$ in number but their lowest mass scales as $h^2 q_0^2
\to 0$. The shell-Goldstone modes stay at finite $\lambda$ (per
Hypothesis (b)). So in the continuum:
\begin{itemize}
\item Low-spectrum: continuous lattice-distortion Goldstones,
  $\lambda \to 0$.
\item Intermediate: shell-momentum massive modes, $\lambda \sim 1-10$.
\item High: gapped continuum.
\end{itemize}

This is QUALITATIVELY DIFFERENT from what Math378/379 thought
(translation Goldstones at $\lambda \sim 0$, gap to massive).
The actual structure has TWO low-energy subspaces:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Lattice-distortion modes at $\lambda \sim h^2 q_0^2 \cdot \delta^2$
  (currently observed at $\sim 0.019$ for N=128).
\item Shell-momentum continuous-translation Goldstones at exact 0
  in continuum (currently masked as massive at $\lambda \sim 1-10$
  on the discrete lattice).
\end{enumerate}
