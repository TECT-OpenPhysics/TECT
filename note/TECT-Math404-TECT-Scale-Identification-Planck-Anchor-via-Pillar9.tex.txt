\section*{TECT-Math404. TECT canonical scale identification via Pillar 9 (Newton-$G$) anchoring: TECT scale = Planck scale within $\mathcal{O}(1)$ factor}

\subsection*{0. Headline}

Using the Pillar 9 / Math110-AddI canonical relation
\begin{equation}
  \hbar = \frac{c^3 a_{\rm condensate}^2}{16 \pi G}
\end{equation}
with observed CODATA values $(\hbar, c, G)$, the TECT condensation length is fixed at
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\;
    a_{\rm condensate} = \sqrt{16\pi G \hbar / c^3} = 7.09\,\ell_{\rm Pl} = 1.146 \times 10^{-34}\,\text{m}
  \;}
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{\rm Pl} = \sqrt{\hbar G/c^3} = 1.616 \times 10^{-35}$ m is the Planck length. The factor $\sqrt{16\pi} \approx 7.09$ is purely combinatorial (it arose in the elastic-modulus derivation Math110-AddG).

In TECT canonical units where $a_{\rm condensate} = 2\pi/q_0 = 9.24$ TECT length units:
\begin{align}
  \text{1 TECT length unit} &= 1.240 \times 10^{-35}\,\text{m} = 0.77\,\ell_{\rm Pl}, \\
  \text{1 TECT energy unit} &= \hbar c / (1.240 \times 10^{-35}\,\text{m}) = 1.591 \times 10^{19}\,\text{GeV} = 1.30\,M_{\rm Pl}.
\end{align}

\textbf{TECT canonical scale = Planck scale within $\mathcal{O}(1)$ factor.}

\subsection*{1. Derivation}

The Pillar 9 elastic-modulus relation (Math110-AddG, Math110-AddH, Math110-AddI):
\begin{equation}
  \rho_{\rm condensate} = \frac{c^4}{16\pi G\, a_{\rm condensate}^2}.
\end{equation}
Rearranging for $a_{\rm condensate}$:
\begin{equation}
  a_{\rm condensate}^2 = \frac{c^4}{16\pi G\,\rho_{\rm condensate}}.
\end{equation}
Identifying $\rho_{\rm condensate}$ with the TECT vacuum-energy density and using $\hbar = (\rho_{\rm condensate} \cdot a_{\rm condensate}^3)/c$ (TECT canonical Math110 derivation):
\begin{equation}
  \rho_{\rm condensate} = \frac{\hbar c}{a_{\rm condensate}^3}, \quad \Rightarrow \quad
  a_{\rm condensate}^2 = \frac{c^4}{16\pi G \cdot (\hbar c/a_{\rm condensate}^3)} = \frac{c^3 a_{\rm condensate}^3}{16\pi G \hbar}.
\end{equation}
Solving for $a_{\rm condensate}$:
\begin{equation}
  a_{\rm condensate} = \sqrt{16\pi G\hbar/c^3} = \sqrt{16\pi}\cdot\ell_{\rm Pl}.
\end{equation}
Numerically: $\sqrt{16\pi} = 7.0898$, so $a_{\rm condensate} = 7.09\,\ell_{\rm Pl} = 1.146 \times 10^{-34}$ m.

Inversely, $\rho_{\rm condensate} = c^4/(16\pi G \cdot 7.09^2 \ell_{\rm Pl}^2) = c^4/(16\pi G \cdot 50.3 \ell_{\rm Pl}^2)$. With $c^4/G = c^4/G = 1.21 \times 10^{44}$ N (Planck force), and $1/\ell_{\rm Pl}^2 = 3.83 \times 10^{69}$ m$^{-2}$:
\begin{equation}
  \rho_{\rm condensate} = \frac{c^4}{16\pi G \cdot 50.3 \ell_{\rm Pl}^2} = \frac{1.21 \times 10^{44}}{16\pi \cdot 50.3} \cdot 3.83 \times 10^{69} = 1.83 \times 10^{109}\,\text{Pa} = 1.83 \times 10^{109}\,\text{J/m}^3.
\end{equation}
This is the Planck energy density divided by $\sim 16\pi \cdot 50.3 = 2528$, i.e., $\rho_{\rm condensate} \approx \rho_{\rm Pl}/2528$. Sub-Planckian by factor $\sim 2500$.
