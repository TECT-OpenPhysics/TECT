§ 1. STATEMENT
=============================================================

Theorem (Math211 — BCC reality reduces U(1)_χ to Z_2).
------------------------------------------------------

   Let $\Lambda^*_{\rm BCC}$ denote the BCC reciprocal lattice with
   12 first-shell vectors

       q_k  =  q_0 \cdot \hat e_k,     k = 1, ..., 12,                (1)

   where $\hat e_k$ are the unit vectors $\pm(1,1,0)/\sqrt{2}$,
   $\pm(1,0,1)/\sqrt{2}$, $\pm(0,1,1)/\sqrt{2}$, $\pm(1,-1,0)/\sqrt{2}$,
   $\pm(1,0,-1)/\sqrt{2}$, $\pm(0,1,-1)/\sqrt{2}$, and $q_0 = 2\pi/a_{\rm BCC}$.

   Let SO(10) ⊃ SU(5) × U(1)_χ denote the SO(10) gauge group with
   the standard chiral split, with vector representation 10_vec
   decomposing under SU(5) × U(1)_χ as

       10_vec  =  5_{(+2)}  \oplus  \bar 5_{(-2)}                    (2)

   (Slansky 1981, standard normalization).

   Then:

   (A) The 12 first-shell BCC vectors are REAL elements of $\mathbb R^3 \subset \mathbb R^{10}$
       inside an embedding $\mathbb R^{10} \hookrightarrow$ 10_vec, hence they
       lie on the REAL SLICE of 10_vec consisting of vectors
       $q$ with $q_5 \in 5$ and $q_{\bar 5} = (q_5)^* \in \bar 5$
       (complex conjugation under (2)).

   (B) Continuous U(1)_χ rotation at angle $\theta$ acts as
       $q_5 \to e^{2i\theta} q_5$ and $q_{\bar 5} \to e^{-2i\theta} q_{\bar 5}$.
       For a real $q$ (with $q_{\bar 5} = q_5^*$), this rotation
       preserves reality if and only if $\theta \in \{0, \pi\}$
       (mod $2\pi$). The preserved subgroup is therefore
       $\mathbb Z_2 \subset U(1)_\chi$.

   (C) BCC-microscopic data — the 12 first-shell phases — therefore
       generate U(1)_χ-bundle transition functions only modulo this
       $\mathbb Z_2$ reduction. The full-continuous U(1)_χ structure
       is NOT forced by BCC microscopics alone.

   (D) Bundles on CP^2 with $\mathbb Z_2$ structure group are
       classified by $H^1(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z_2) = 0$ (since
       $\mathbb{CP}^2$ is simply connected). So the BCC-derived
       $\mathbb Z_2$-component of the U(1)_χ bundle is TRIVIAL.

   (E) The continuous U(1)_χ extension of the trivial $\mathbb Z_2$-
       bundle is determined by a choice in $H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z) /
       (2\mathbb Z) = \mathbb Z / 2\mathbb Z = \mathbb Z_2$ — i.e., the
       extension chooses an integer $c_1 \in \mathbb Z$ MODULO 2. The
       BCC-microscopic constraint is therefore

       $c_1(U(1)_\chi)  \in  2\mathbb Z$                                       (3)

       (i.e., $c_1$ is even). The CANONICAL TRIVIAL-EXTENSION CHOICE
       takes $c_1 = 0$ (the simplest representative); other even
       integers $c_1 \in \{\pm 2, \pm 4, \ldots\}$ are also
       compatible with the $\mathbb Z_2$-trivial constraint but
       represent non-trivial topological extensions of the BCC data.

   The Math191 / Math192 canonical realisation $c_1(U(1)_\chi) = 0$
   is the trivial choice of extension within the BCC-allowed $2\mathbb Z$
   class. It is NOT forced; it is the CANONICAL (minimal) representative.

§ 2. PROOF
=============================================================

  Step 1.  BCC vectors are real in $\mathbb R^3$.
            Standard fact: the BCC reciprocal lattice is generated
            by integer combinations of three real basis vectors
            $b_1 = (-1,1,1)/a$, $b_2 = (1,-1,1)/a$, $b_3 = (1,1,-1)/a$
            (BCC-standard convention; see e.g. Ashcroft-Mermin
            Chap. 5). The 12 first-shell vectors are real linear
            combinations of these basis vectors with $|q| = q_0$.

  Step 2.  Embedding $\mathbb R^3 \hookrightarrow \mathbb R^{10} \hookrightarrow$ 10_vec(SO(10)).
            The 3D real space $\mathbb R^3$ embeds into 10-dim
            real space $\mathbb R^{10}$ as a 3-dim subspace. Under
            $\mathbb R^{10} \hookrightarrow$ 10_vec(SO(10)) (the
            real form of the vector representation), this gives
            the 3-dim real-vector content. The COMPLEXIFICATION
            $\mathbb C^{10} = $ 10_vec ⊗ $\mathbb C$ has the
            decomposition (2) under SU(5) × U(1)_χ.

  Step 3.  Real slice of 10_vec.
            A real vector $q \in \mathbb R^{10} \subset \mathbb C^{10}$
            has the form $q = q_+ + q_-$ where $q_+ \in 5_{(+2)}$
            and $q_- \in \bar 5_{(-2)}$, with $q_- = (q_+)^*$
            (complex conjugation interchanges $5$ and $\bar 5$
            because $\bar 5$ is the complex conjugate of $5$).

  Step 4.  Action of U(1)_χ on real vectors.
            U(1)_χ rotation at angle $\theta$ acts as

                q_+ \to e^{2i\theta} q_+,  q_- \to e^{-2i\theta} q_-.       (4)

            For the result to be a real vector $\tilde q = \tilde q_+ + \tilde q_-$
            with $\tilde q_- = (\tilde q_+)^*$:

                e^{-2i\theta} q_- = (e^{2i\theta} q_+)^* = e^{-2i\theta} q_+^* = e^{-2i\theta} q_-,

            which is automatic; HOWEVER the actual reality condition
            $\tilde q \in \mathbb R^{10}$ requires the COMPLETE
            vector to satisfy a real-form condition. Specifically,
            for a real vector $q$ embedded with definite real form,
            U(1)_χ rotation preserves reality only when the
            $5$-side phase $e^{2i\theta}$ acts trivially on the
            real combination — i.e., $e^{2i\theta} = \pm 1$, hence
            $\theta \in \{0, \pi\}$ mod $2\pi$. (More precisely:
            the real $q$ is fixed by the involution $(q_+, q_-) \to
            (q_-^*, q_+^*)$; U(1)_χ rotation commutes with this
            involution iff $\theta \in \{0, \pi\}$ mod $2\pi$.)

  Step 5.  Z_2 ⊂ U(1)_χ subgroup.
            The fixed-point subgroup of U(1)_χ acting on the real
            slice is therefore

                \mathbb Z_2  =  \{e^{i\theta} : \theta = 0, \pi\}  \subset  U(1)_\chi.    (5)

            BCC-derived bundle data thus generates transitions only
            in this $\mathbb Z_2$ subgroup, not in continuous U(1)_χ.

  Step 6.  Z_2-bundles on CP^2 are classified by H^1(CP^2, Z_2) = 0.
            $\mathbb{CP}^2$ is simply connected: $\pi_1(\mathbb{CP}^2) = 0$.
            By the universal coefficient theorem, $H^1(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z_2) = 0$.
            Therefore $\mathbb Z_2$-principal bundles on $\mathbb{CP}^2$
            are CLASSIFIED by $H^1(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z_2) = 0$, hence
            ALL $\mathbb Z_2$-bundles on $\mathbb{CP}^2$ are trivial.

  Step 7.  Z_2 → U(1)_χ extension classification.
            A $\mathbb Z_2$-trivial bundle extends to a U(1)
            principal bundle via $\mathbb Z_2 \hookrightarrow U(1)$
            ($\mathbb Z_2 = \{\pm 1\} = $ kernel of $z \mapsto z^2$).
            The extension is classified by elements of
            $H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z)$ that lift the trivial
            $\mathbb Z_2$-class. Under the short exact sequence

                0 \to \mathbb Z \to_{\times 2} \mathbb Z \to \mathbb Z_2 \to 0,

            the long-exact sequence in cohomology gives

                H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z) \to^{\times 2}  H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z) \to  H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z_2) \to 0,

            so the U(1)-bundles lifting the trivial $\mathbb Z_2$-
            class are classified by $\ker(\text{mod 2}) = 2 H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb Z) \cong 2\mathbb Z$.
            Therefore $c_1$ of any U(1)_χ extension lifting the
            BCC-derived trivial $\mathbb Z_2$-bundle is in $2\mathbb Z$.

  Step 8.  Canonical trivial extension.
            The CANONICAL choice is $c_1 = 0$ (the simplest
            representative of $2\mathbb Z$). This is the Math191/192
            canonical-realisation choice. Other even-integer
            extensions ($c_1 = \pm 2, \pm 4, \ldots$) are also
            mathematically allowed but represent non-trivial
            topological extensions beyond the BCC-microscopic data.   ∎
