§ 2. HUBBLE FRICTION AND DEFECT VELOCITY
==================================================================

**Dynamics of a defect** in the cosmological background:

The equation of motion for a defect position $\mathbf{x}_{\rm def}(t)$ in
an expanding universe is:

$$\frac{d^2\mathbf{x}_{\rm def}}{dt^2} + 2H(t)\frac{d\mathbf{x}_{\rm def}}{dt}
  = \mathbf{F}_{\rm int} + \text{(thermal noise)},$$

where:
- $2H(t)$ is the Hubble-friction coefficient (derived from the
  Friedmann metric)
- $\mathbf{F}_{\rm int}$ is the force from defect-defect interactions
  (or elastic strain relaxation)

**Hubble friction dominance**:

At $T \sim T_c \sim 10^{16}$ GeV, we have $H(T_c) \sim 7 \times 10^{13}$ GeV
(from Math218-AddA).

The mechanical inertial term $d^2\mathbf{x}/dt^2$ is negligible compared to
friction if:

$$2H(t) \gg \Gamma_{\rm mech},$$

where $\Gamma_{\rm mech} \sim (\tau_{\rm relax})^{-1}$ is the mechanical
dissipation rate in the condensate.

For a typical condensed-matter system, $\tau_{\rm relax} \sim 10^{-10}$ s
(nanosecond), giving $\Gamma_{\rm mech} \sim 10^{10}$ s$^{-1} \sim 10^{10}$ GeV.

Comparing:
$$\frac{2H(T_c)}{\Gamma_{\rm mech}} \sim \frac{7 \times 10^{13}}{10^{10}}
  \sim 10^3 \gg 1.$$

**Conclusion**: Hubble friction DOMINATES mechanical dissipation by
~1000×. Defects are much more strongly damped by cosmological expansion
than by the condensate's intrinsic viscosity.

---

**In the overdamped limit**:

$$2H(t)\frac{d\mathbf{x}_{\rm def}}{dt} \approx \mathbf{F}_{\rm int},$$

the velocity is set by the balance between Hubble friction and inter-defect force:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{x}_{\rm def}}{dt} \sim \frac{\mathbf{F}_{\rm int}}{2H(t)}.$$

For an attractive force between antidefects (magnitude $F_{\rm int} \sim 1$ GeV
at separation $r \sim 10$ nm), the approach velocity is:

$$v_{\rm approach} \sim \frac{1\,\mathrm{GeV}}{2 \times 7 \times 10^{13}\,\mathrm{GeV}}
  \sim \frac{1}{1.4 \times 10^{14}} \sim 10^{-14}\,c.$$

This is an extremely SLOW approach velocity — much slower than the
Hubble expansion rate.

**Defect separation growth due to expansion**:

In a universe expanding with scale factor $a(t)$, the comoving separation
$r_{\rm com}(t)$ between two defects satisfies:

$$\frac{dr_{\rm com}}{dt} = v_{\rm approach} - H(t)\, r_{\rm com}.$$

The second term (Hubble expansion) represents the RECESSION velocity of
comoving coordinates.

For our parameters:
$$H(t) \times r_{\rm com} \sim 7 \times 10^{13}\,\mathrm{GeV} \times 10^{-13}\,\mathrm{GeV}^{-1}
  \sim 1 \quad \text{(dimensionless growth rate).}$$

Compared to the approach velocity $v_{\rm approach} \sim 10^{-14} c$, Hubble
expansion **OUTPACES** the attraction, and defects SEPARATE.

§ 3. INTERACTION RANGE AND DEFECT-PAIRING PROBABILITY
==================================================================

**Question**: For defects to annihilate, an antidefect pair must come
within the "annihilation radius" $r_{\rm ann} \sim \xi$ (healing length
~1 nm).

Given the Kibble-Zurek density $n_{\rm KZ} \sim 10^{15}$ cm$^{-3}$ (from
Math359), what is the AVERAGE distance between a defect and its nearest
antidefect?

**Random Poisson process estimate**:

For a uniform density $n_{\rm KZ}$ of randomly distributed particles in 3D,
the average nearest-neighbor distance is:

$$\bar{r}_{\rm NN} \sim (n_{\rm KZ})^{-1/3} \sim (10^{15})^{-1/3}
  \sim 10^{-5}\,\mathrm{cm} = 100\,\mu\mathrm{m}.$$

**Time to approach** (if approach velocity $v_{\rm approach}$ were constant):

$$t_{\rm approach} \sim \frac{\bar{r}_{\rm NN}}{v_{\rm approach}}
  \sim \frac{100\,\mu\mathrm{m}}{10^{-14} c}
  \sim \frac{10^{-3}\,\mathrm{cm}}{10^{-14} \times 3 \times 10^{10}\,\mathrm{cm/s}}
  \sim \frac{10^{-3}}{3 \times 10^{-4}}\,\mathrm{s}
  \sim 3\,\mathrm{s}.$$

**But Hubble expansion timescale**:

$$t_{\rm Hubble} \sim H^{-1} \sim 10^{-24}\,\mathrm{s}.$$

The **ratio** is:

$$\frac{t_{\rm approach}}{t_{\rm Hubble}} \sim \frac{3}{10^{-24}} \sim 3 \times 10^{24}.$$

In just ONE Hubble time, the Hubble expansion would increase the separation
by:

$$\Delta r_{\rm Hubble} \sim H(t) \cdot \bar{r}_{\rm NN} \cdot H^{-1}
  = \bar{r}_{\rm NN} \sim 100\,\mu\mathrm{m}.$$

Since defects start at separation ~100 μm and Hubble expansion increases it
by ~100 μm per Hubble time, **defects drift APART, not together**.

**Conclusion**: Hubble friction prevents antidefects from finding each
other. Even in a particle-physics collision, the interaction would occur
at the incredibly low rate:

$$\Gamma_{\rm pair}^{\rm Hubble-limited} \sim \frac{v_{\rm approach}}{L^3}
  \sim \frac{10^{-14} c}{(100\,\mu\mathrm{m})^3}
  \sim 10^{-35}\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}.$$

Over the age of the universe (13 billion years ~ 10^{17} s), the probability
of a SINGLE pair finding each other is:

$$P \sim \Gamma \times t_{\rm age} \sim 10^{-35} \times 10^{17} \sim 10^{-18}.$$

This is negligible: **defects do NOT annihilate in an expanding universe**.

§ 5. CAN MECHANISM (I) SAVE E_3'? QUANTITATIVE CHECK

**Recall from Math359**: Soft-mode enhancement of KZ susceptibility might
suppress defect formation by a factor ~10^{-4}.

If defect density is reduced from $n_{\rm KZ} \sim 10^{45}$ GeV$^3$ to:

$$n_{\rm suppressed} \sim 10^{-4} \times 10^{45} = 10^{41}\,\mathrm{GeV}^3
  \sim 10^{11}\,\mathrm{cm}^{-3}.$$

The nearest-neighbor spacing becomes:

$$\bar{r}_{\rm NN} \sim (10^{11})^{-1/3} \sim 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{cm} = 1\,\mu\mathrm{m}.$$

The approach velocity is still $v_{\rm approach} \sim 10^{-14} c$ (unchanged
from Hubble-friction analysis), so the time to encounter is:

$$t_{\rm encounter} \sim \frac{1\,\mu\mathrm{m}}{10^{-14} c}
  \sim \frac{10^{-4}\,\mathrm{cm}}{3 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{cm/s}}
  \sim 10^{-1}\,\mathrm{s} = 0.1\,\mathrm{s}.$$

But the Hubble time is STILL $H^{-1} \sim 10^{-24}$ s, which is **18 orders
shorter** than 0.1 s. Defects still FREEZE IN.

**More aggressive suppression?**

To have $\bar{r}_{\rm NN} < 10^{-27}$ cm (so that encounter time is < Hubble
time), we would need:

$$n \sim 10^{81}\,\mathrm{cm}^{-3},$$

and the original KZ density would need to be suppressed by a factor:

$$\frac{10^{45}}{10^{81}} \sim 10^{-36}.$$

This is an **unimaginably large suppression**—far beyond what soft modes or
any known physics can achieve. Physics does not provide a 36-order suppression.

**Conclusion**: Mechanism (I) CANNOT save E_3', even with aggressive
suppression factors. Defects are a persistent cosmological nuisance.

§ 7. DEVIL'S-ADVOCATE SELF-TEST (CLAUDE.md §6.3.1)
==================================================================

**Objection α: "Your calculation assumes defects are POINTLIKE particles
moving freely in the condensate. But in reality, defects might be COMPOSITE
structures (networks of vortices, monopole-antimonopole pairs) that decay
into each other via quantum tunneling. Have you accounted for quantum
tunneling rates?"**

**Response (VALID-with-mitigation)**: Fair point. Quantum tunneling through
potential barriers is not included in the Hubble-friction analysis, which
treats defects classically. If the tunneling timescale $\tau_{\rm tunnel}$
is shorter than the Hubble time, defects could decay.

However, typical tunneling timescales in particle physics are EXPONENTIALLY
suppressed: $\tau_{\rm tunnel} \sim \tau_0 e^{A/\hbar}$, where A is the action.
For a cosmological defect of size ~1 nm and barrier height ~GeV, the exponent
is typically ~10-100, giving $\tau_{\rm tunnel} \sim 10^{-10}\,\mathrm{s}$
(much longer than Hubble time ~10^{-24} s).

So quantum tunneling also does NOT save defects from freezing in.

---

**Objection β: "You computed the interaction force $F_{\rm int} \sim 1$ GeV
between defects at separation 10 nm. But if defects are formed at much
SMALLER separations (e.g., within 1 nm as KZ correlated pairs), they might
annihilate immediately before Hubble friction can separate them."**

**Response (DISMISSED)**: Kibble-Zurek defects form with UNCORRELATED
phases (winding numbers are frozen in randomly across the correlation
volume ~$\xi^3$). A defect and antidefect don't form as a correlated pair;
they emerge at random locations ~$\xi$ apart (mean separation). By the time
they're formed, they're already separated by ~nm scales, subject to Hubble
friction.

---

**Objection γ: "The defect density $n_{\rm KZ} \sim 10^{15}$ cm$^{-3}$ is
your estimate from Math359. But this is a CLASSICAL estimate. In the
quantum field theory, defect production might be exponentially suppressed
or enhanced. Have you checked the QUANTUM computation?"**

**Response (VALID-with-mitigation)**: The KZ density formula
$n_{\rm KZ} \sim \xi^{-3}$ is a classical scaling law derived from correlation-
length growth during a quench. Quantum effects (ZPE, quantum tunneling,
Casimir forces) could modify this, but the leading-order scaling is
expected to hold.

A rigorous quantum computation would involve lattice QFT simulations of
the TDGL quench in a expanding Friedmann background. This is beyond the
scope of the present Turn, but should be flagged as a necessary follow-up
for a FINAL closure of E_3'.
