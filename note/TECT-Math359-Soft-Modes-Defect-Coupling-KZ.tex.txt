§ 1. INDEPENDENT HESSIAN VERIFICATION (Orthogonal Method)
==================================================================

**Motivation**: Math358 used Lanczos eigenvalue decomposition. To rule out
numerical errors, we verify stability using a DIFFERENT method: QR
factorization of the discretised Hessian matrix.

**Method**: Direct factorisation $H = QR$ where Q is orthogonal and R is
upper triangular. The diagonal of R gives accurate estimates of eigenvalues
(all positive → stable).

**Expected result** (assuming correct Math358 computation):
All diagonal entries of R positive → confirms local minimum.

**Pseudocode** [supplementary Math359_qr_verify.py]:

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.linalg import qr

# Load Hessian matrix H (dense, ~5000 x 5000 for smallest system)
# from Math358 checkpoint

H_dense = load_hessian_matrix()  # Shape: (N_total, N_total)

# QR factorisation
Q, R = qr(H_dense, mode='economic')

# Extract diagonal (proxy for eigenvalues in upper-triangular form)
diag_R = np.diag(R)

print("QR diagonal entries (proxy for eigenvalues):")
print(f"  Minimum (excluding ~0): {np.min(diag_R[np.abs(diag_R) > 1e-10]):.6e}")
print(f"  Maximum: {np.max(diag_R):.6e}")
print(f"  Number of negative entries: {np.sum(diag_R < -1e-6)}")

# Cross-check with Lanczos eigenvalues
lanczos_evals = load_lanczos_eigenvalues()  # From Math358
qr_evals = diag_R[diag_R != 0]

print(f"\nComparison with Lanczos:")
print(f"  Lanczos λ_min (nonzero): {np.min(lanczos_evals[np.abs(lanczos_evals)>1e-10]):.6e}")
print(f"  QR      λ_min (nonzero): {np.min(qr_evals[np.abs(qr_evals)>1e-10]):.6e}")
print(f"  Discrepancy: {abs(np.min(lanczos_evals[np.abs(lanczos_evals)>1e-10]) - np.min(qr_evals[np.abs(qr_evals)>1e-10])) / np.min(np.abs(lanczos_evals[np.abs(lanczos_evals)>1e-10])) * 100:.2f}%")
```

**Expected verdict**: QR and Lanczos should agree to within ~0.1%, confirming
stability is robust.

§ 2. SOFT-MODE CHARACTERISATION: COUPLING TO DEFECTS
==================================================================

**Definition**: Soft modes are eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_{\rm soft}$ with
eigenvalues λ_soft ~ 10^{-4} from Math358.

**Question**: What is the physical meaning of these eigenvectors? Do they
correspond to displacements that favour topological defect formation?

**Analysis via topological-charge correlation**:

The topological charge (winding number) at a point $\mathbf{r}$ is defined by
the circulation of the phase around a small loop:

$$Q(\mathbf{r}) := \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint \nabla \phi \cdot d\mathbf{l},$$

where $\phi = \arg(\Psi)$ is the order-parameter phase.

A soft-mode eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_{\rm soft}$ induces a perturbation:

$$\Psi(\mathbf{r}) \to \Psi_{\rm min}(\mathbf{r}) + \epsilon \mathbf{v}_{\rm soft}(\mathbf{r}).$$

The resulting change in topological charge is:

$$\Delta Q \sim \epsilon \int d^3r\, \left\langle \mathbf{v}_{\rm soft}(\mathbf{r})
\,\big|\, \text{(winding-number operator)} \,\big| \Psi_{\rm min}(\mathbf{r}) \right\rangle.$$

**Calculation** (heuristic):

For the BCC ground state with 12 modes arranged in reciprocal-lattice
geometry, soft modes typically correspond to:

(a) **Phase modulations** (modulation of $\arg(\Psi)$ along soft-mode
    direction): Can create long-range phase winding → **topological defect seeds**.

(b) **Amplitude modulations** (modulation of $|\Psi|$ without phase change):
    Typically do NOT create topological charge; correspond to elastic waves.

(c) **Chirality oscillations** (rotation of the order-parameter pattern in
    complex space): Mix of (a) and (b).

**Empirical observation from Math358 eigenvectors**:

Among the 4-5 soft modes (λ ~ 10^{-4} - 10^{-3}), approximately **2-3 are
phase-modulating** (defect-prone) and **1-2 are purely amplitude-modulating**
(elastic).

**Estimate of defect-production coupling strength**:

For a soft mode $\mathbf{v}_{\rm soft}$ with eigenvalue λ_soft ~ 10^{-4},
the susceptibility to external perturbations is:

$$\chi_{\rm soft} \sim \frac{1}{\lambda_{\rm soft}} \sim \frac{1}{10^{-4}} = 10^4\,\text{(Brazovskii units)}.$$

Compared to a typical gapped mode (λ ~ 0.1):

$$\chi_{\rm gapped} \sim \frac{1}{0.1} = 10,$$

the soft mode is **1000 times more susceptible** to fluctuations.

**Implication for Kibble-Zurek**: During rapid cooling (quench),
thermal fluctuations in the soft-mode degrees of freedom are amplified
by the large susceptibility χ_soft. This drives the system to explore
configurations with non-zero winding, leading to defect creation.

§ 3. KIBBLE-ZUREK DEFECT DENSITY ESTIMATE
==================================================================

**Standard Kibble-Zurek formula** (from Math97, Math218-AddA):

When the order parameter undergoes a phase transition with cooling rate
$\dot{T} = -\beta$ (temperature decreasing), the defect density is:

$$n_{\rm defect} \sim \xi_{\rm KZ}^{-3},$$

where the Kibble-Zurek length is:

$$\xi_{\rm KZ} \sim \left(\frac{\dot{\xi}}{\dot{T}}\right)_{\rm crit}
  \sim \sqrt{\frac{1}{|t_c| \dot{T}}},$$

with $|t_c|$ being the critical exponent (typically $|t_c| \sim 0.5$ for
second-order transitions).

**For the BCC cosmological transition** ($T_c \sim 10^{16}$ GeV):

(1) Correlation-length velocity at transition:
$$\dot{\xi}|_{T_c} \sim O(1)\,\mathrm{(dimensionless in natural units)}.$$

(2) Cooling rate in expanding universe:
$$\dot{T} \sim -H(T_c) \times T_c,$$
where $H(T_c) \sim 7 \times 10^{13}\,\mathrm{GeV}$ (from Math218-AddA §1.2.3).

Thus:
$$\dot{T} \sim -7 \times 10^{13}\,\mathrm{GeV} \times 10^{16}\,\mathrm{GeV}
  \sim -7 \times 10^{29}\,\mathrm{GeV}^2/\mathrm{time}.$$

(3) Kibble-Zurek length:
$$\xi_{\rm KZ} \sim \sqrt{\frac{0.5 \times 1}{7 \times 10^{29}}}
  \sim \sqrt{10^{-30}} \sim 10^{-15}\,\mathrm{GeV}^{-1}.$$

(4) Defect density:
$$n_{\rm defect} \sim (10^{-15})^{-3} \sim 10^{45}\,\mathrm{GeV}^3.$$

**But with soft-mode enhancement**:

The large susceptibility χ_soft ~ 10^4 amplifies thermal fluctuations,
potentially suppressing defect formation (fluctuations randomize the
phase more, preventing coherent winding) or enhancing it (soft modes
provide low-energy pathways to defect configurations).

**Mechanism (I) — suppression scenario**:

If soft modes SUPPRESS defect formation (by providing a dissipative
channel that damps winding-number growth), then:

$$n_{\rm defect}^{\rm actual} \sim \chi_{\rm soft}^{-1} \times n_{\rm KZ}
  \sim 10^{-4} \times 10^{45} = 10^{41}\,\mathrm{GeV}^3.$$

Even suppressed by 4 orders, defects still remain abundantly produced (per
Math218-AddA §2.3 calculation).

**Mechanism (I) — enhancement scenario**:

If soft modes ENHANCE defect formation (by making it energetically cheap to
explore defect configurations), then defect density increases even further.

**Provisional conclusion**: Mechanism (I) (direct suppression of Kibble-Zurek
production by soft-mode dynamics) appears UNLIKELY to reduce the defect
density below observable levels (needed for E_3' uniqueness). Soft modes
either maintain or increase the KZ defect yield.

**Objection α: "You argue that soft modes make defect formation CHEAP

**Response (DISMISSED)**: True, but complementary. The soft-mode analysis
addresses the PRODUCTION side (during transition). The Hubble-friction

Soft modes affect WHERE (in configuration space) the system explores during
the quench; Hubble friction affects WHETHER (in time) defects can annihilate
before freezing in.

---

**Objection β: "Your estimate that soft modes are 1000x more susceptible
(χ_soft ~ 10^4 vs. χ_gapped ~ 10) assumes the 'gapped mode' has λ ~ 0.1.
But what if the gapped modes are MUCH stiffer (λ ~ 1)? Then the relative
enhancement would be smaller. You're not being quantitative about the
full spectrum."**

**Response (VALID-with-mitigation)**: Fair point. The 1000x figure assumes
the gapped-mode spectrum is dominated by λ ~ 0.1. From Math358 (simulated
results), the spectrum has modes ranging from λ ~ 0.02 to λ ~ 0.3, so the
geometric mean is closer to λ ~ 0.15. This would give:

$$\chi_{\rm gapped} \sim 1/0.15 \approx 6.7,$$
$$\chi_{\rm soft}/\chi_{\rm gapped} \sim 10^4 / 6.7 \sim 1500.$$

So the "thousand-fold enhancement" is actually ~1500-fold. This doesn't
change the conclusion (soft modes are still VERY susceptible), but the
precision is important. Future work should include a full spectral analysis
of the susceptibility landscape.

---

**Objection γ: "The soft modes might be ARTEFACTS of the finite-volume
discretisation. In the infinite-volume limit, the soft modes might
DISAPPEAR, leaving only truly gapped modes. Your defect analysis would then
be based on spurious modes."**

**Response (VALID-with-mitigation)**: The soft modes are observed consistently
across N=16, 32, 64 (Math358 results). If they were discretisation artefacts,
they should VANISH as N → ∞. Instead, they PERSIST, indicating they are
genuine bulk modes, not finite-size effects.

Mitigation: Extrapolate to N → ∞ limit and verify soft-mode eigenvalues
converge. Also, check if soft modes correspond to known physical excitations
(e.g., phonons, Goldstone descendants) in the continuum limit.
