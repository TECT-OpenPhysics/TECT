\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Defect classification (\S 1)}: TECT defects are
  Brazovskii-condensate dislocations / disclinations of the BCC

  2\pi/q_0$, giving $w/b \sim 1/(2\pi)$, $\Delta E_{\rm PN} \sim

\subsection*{2. Peierls-Nabarro lattice-pinning physics}

For a dislocation of width $w$, Burgers vector $b$, in an elastic
medium with shear modulus $G$, the Peierls-Nabarro pinning energy
per unit length (Hirth \& Lothe 1982 \S 8.7) is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta E_{\rm PN}}{L} = \frac{G b^2}{2\pi(1-\nu_P)}
\cdot \exp\left(-\frac{2\pi w}{b}\right),
\label{eq:PN-energy}
\end{equation}
where $\nu_P \sim 0.3$ is the Poisson ratio of the medium (a
TECT-condensate property; not directly computed but typical
solid-state value).

\textbf{For TECT}: $w \sim R_c \sim 1/q_0$, $b \sim 2\pi/q_0$,
giving $w/b \sim 1/(2\pi) \approx 0.16$. The exponential factor
\begin{equation}
\exp(-2\pi w/b) \sim \exp(-1) \approx 0.37.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Shear modulus estimate}: For the Brazovskii BCC condensate
near the operating point, the shear modulus is dimensionally
$G \sim |\mu^2|^{?} \cdot q_0^{?}$. From Math82-AddH amplitude
$\phi_0 \sim 0.27$, free-energy density $\sim -0.314$, and
characteristic length $\sim 1/q_0$:
\begin{equation}
G \sim \phi_0^2 \cdot |\mu^2_c| \sim 0.073 \cdot 0.0114 \sim
8.3 \times 10^{-4} \quad (\text{code units}).
\end{equation}

\textbf{Peierls-Nabarro barrier per unit length (TECT-canonical)}:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta E_{\rm PN}}{L} \sim \frac{8.3\times 10^{-4} \cdot
(2\pi/q_0)^2}{2\pi \cdot 0.7} \cdot e^{-1}
\sim \frac{8.3\times 10^{-4} \cdot 86}{4.4} \cdot 0.37
\sim 6 \times 10^{-3} \quad (\text{code units}).
\end{equation}
For a defect segment of length $\sim 1/q_0$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{\rm PN}^{\rm segment} \sim 6 \times 10^{-3}/q_0
\sim 9 \times 10^{-3} \quad (\text{code units}).
\end{equation}
At $T \sim T_c \sim 1$ in TECT code units, $\Delta E_{\rm PN}/T
\sim 0.009$. This is SMALL -- the Peierls barrier is essentially
negligible compared to thermal energy at $T_c$.

\textbf{Implication for defect velocity}:
\begin{equation}
v_{\rm PN}(T) = a_{\rm latt} \cdot \nu_0 \cdot
\exp(-\Delta E_{\rm PN}/T) \sim (2\pi/q_0) \cdot q_0 \cdot
\exp(-0.009) \sim 2\pi \cdot 0.99 \approx 6.2,
\end{equation}
in code units (length per time). Converting: at TECT scale
$10^{16}$ GeV, length unit $\sim 10^{-16}$ GeV$^{-1}$, time
unit $\sim 10^{-16}$ GeV$^{-1}$, so $v_{\rm PN} \sim 6.2 c$.

\textbf{Wait}: $v_{\rm PN} > c$ is unphysical. The Peierls model
gives the \emph{escape rate} from one lattice well to the next, not
the velocity. The correct interpretation:

\begin{equation}
v_{\rm PN} \le \min(v_{\rm sound\,in\,condensate}, c).
\end{equation}

\textbf{Sound speed in BCC condensate}: For a Brazovskii medium,
the longitudinal sound speed is $c_s \sim \sqrt{G/\rho}$ where
$\rho$ is the inertial density. For a relativistic order parameter,
$\rho \sim \phi_0^2$ and $c_s \sim \sqrt{G/\phi_0^2} =
\sqrt{|\mu^2_c|} \sim \sqrt{0.0114} \sim 0.107$ in code units of
$c$. So $v_{\rm PN} \sim \min(c_s, ...) \sim 0.1\,c$.

\textbf{Refined defect velocity at freeze-out}:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{v_{\rm def}^{\rm PN} \sim 0.1\,c \quad (\text{TECT canonical})}.
\end{equation}

This is $\sim 10^{13}$ times the Math360 thermal-Hubble-damped
estimate $v_{\rm therm} \sim 10^{-14} c$.

\subsection*{3. Quantitative annihilation timescale with PN
correction}

Math360 annihilation timescale (with thermal velocity):
$t_{\rm approach} \sim r_{\rm NN}/v_{\rm therm} \sim 100\,\mu\text{m}
/10^{-14} c \sim 3$ s.

With PN-corrected velocity $v_{\rm PN} \sim 0.1\,c$:
$t_{\rm approach}^{\rm PN} \sim 100\,\mu\text{m}/0.1 c \sim
3.3 \times 10^{-12}$ s.

Compare to Hubble timescale $t_{\rm Hubble} \sim 10^{-24}$ s:
\begin{equation}
\frac{t_{\rm approach}^{\rm PN}}{t_{\rm Hubble}} \sim
\frac{3.3 \times 10^{-12}}{10^{-24}} \sim 3.3 \times 10^{12}.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Verdict}: Defects still cannot annihilate within one Hubble
time, by 12 orders of magnitude. Math361's required suppression of
$\sim 10^{36}$ is met by combination (Math360's 24 orders + RG3's
12 orders correction = 36 orders, marginal closure!) -- BUT this
is just the right order; closer examination shows...

Wait, let me reconsider. Math360 said $t_{\rm approach}/t_{\rm Hubble}
\sim 10^{24}$ ratio, i.e., needed correction factor $10^{24}$. RG3
provides correction factor $v_{\rm PN}/v_{\rm therm} \sim 10^{13}$.
Net residual ratio $\sim 10^{24}/10^{13} = 10^{11}$. \textbf{Defects
still don't annihilate in time, by 11 orders of magnitude.}

\textbf{Math361 ``required suppression $> 10^{36}$''}: This refers
to the suppression of $n_{\rm defect}$ relative to KZ value, NOT
to the time-ratio. Different quantity. Annihilation can suppress
$n_{\rm defect}$ by a factor that depends on annihilation efficiency.
Even if defects can annihilate (i.e., $t_{\rm approach} < t_{\rm
Hubble}$), the suppression depends on how many annihilation events
happen.

\textbf{Most charitable interpretation of RG3}: With $v_{\rm PN}$
correction, defects are mobile enough to potentially annihilate
within $\sim 10^{12}$ Hubble times -- but cosmological evolution
goes through many orders of magnitude of $H$, so over $10^{12}$
Hubble times, defects could undergo significant annihilation.

\textbf{Refined estimate}: Standard cosmological-defect annihilation
gives a network density that scales as $n_d \sim 1/(L_{\rm corr}^2 \cdot
t)$ where $L_{\rm corr}$ is the correlation length. For a scaling
defect network (Vilenkin-Shellard 1994 \S 9.2), the residual
density at present epoch is $n_d \sim H_0^2/v_{\rm def}^2$. With
$v_{\rm def} = v_{\rm PN} \sim 0.1\,c$ (vs Math360's $v_{\rm therm}
\sim 10^{-14} c$), the residual density is suppressed by
$(v_{\rm PN}/v_{\rm therm})^2 = 10^{26}$.

\textbf{Refined RG3 conclusion}: With the PN-corrected velocity
$v_{\rm PN}$, the cosmological residual defect density via standard
network-scaling argument is suppressed by $\sim 10^{26}$. This is
\emph{less than} the Math361 required $\sim 10^{36}$ but still
substantial. The gap is now $\sim 10^{10}$, a much smaller deficit
than the original Math361 conclusion (which assumed essentially
zero annihilation).

\textbf{Status of Mechanism (II) verdict}: REFINED but not
overturned. ``Hubble friction prevents annihilation'' should be
replaced by ``Hubble friction allows network-scaling annihilation
with residual relic density still $\sim 10^{10}$ above the
cosmological bound.'' The qualitative verdict (insufficient closure)

\subsection*{5. Devil's-advocate self-test (CLAUDE.md \S 6.3.1)}

\textbf{Objection $\alpha$}: The Peierls-Nabarro formula
\eqref{eq:PN-energy} is for crystalline solids (atomic lattices).
Does it apply to a Brazovskii BCC \emph{condensate} (a continuous
field with periodic modulation)?\\
\emph{Response: VALID-with-mitigation.} The PN model is physically
derived from the periodic potential a defect feels as it moves
through a medium with discrete translational symmetry. A BCC
Brazovskii condensate has exactly such a periodic potential
(period $2\pi/q_0$). The PN formula's structural form is therefore
applicable; the precise coefficients (shear modulus $G$, Poisson
$\nu_P$) need TECT-specific values, but the leading
$\exp(-2\pi w/b)$ scaling is universal. Mitigation: refining the
exact $G, \nu_P$ values is queued as Math387-AddA but does not
change the order-of-magnitude estimate.

\textbf{Objection $\beta$}: The estimate $\Delta E_{\rm PN}/T \sim
0.009$ at $T_c$ is small, but \emph{below} $T_c$ the BCC condensate
hardens and $G$ grows. At freeze-out, $\Delta E_{\rm PN}$ might be
much larger, suppressing $v_{\rm PN}$ exponentially.\\
\emph{Response: VALID-with-mitigation, refines bound.} For
$T \ll T_c$, $G$ scales as $\phi_0^2(T) \sim |\mu^2(T)|/\lambda$
which grows from 0 at $T_c$ to $O(1)$ at $T \to 0$. So
$\Delta E_{\rm PN} \sim G \sim \phi_0^2$. At $T = T_c/2$,
$\phi_0^2 \sim 0.5$ and $\Delta E_{\rm PN}/T \sim 0.5/0.5 = 1$,
giving $\exp(-1)$ Boltzmann suppression -- still mild, $v_{\rm PN}
\sim c_s/3$. Even at $T = T_c/10$, $\Delta E_{\rm PN}/T \sim 10$,
$v_{\rm PN} \sim c_s \cdot e^{-10} \sim 5 \times 10^{-5}\,c$.
Mitigation: the relevant freeze-out temperature where $v_{\rm PN}$
becomes Hubble-limited is $T_{\rm freeze}$ such that $v_{\rm PN}
\cdot t_{\rm Hubble} \sim r_{\rm NN}$, i.e., $v_{\rm PN} \sim
r_{\rm NN} \cdot H \sim 100\,\mu m \cdot 10^{14} \text{GeV} \sim
10^{-15}\,c$. From $v_{\rm PN}(T) = c_s \exp(-\Delta E_{\rm PN}(T)/T)$,
this gives $\Delta E_{\rm PN}/T \sim 30$, so freeze-out at
$T \sim T_c/30 \sim 0.03$, with $\phi_0^2 \sim 0.03$. Detailed
network-scaling formula would integrate from $T_c$ to $T_{\rm freeze}$,
giving residual density estimate $n_d^{\rm relic}/n_d^{\rm KZ} \sim
\exp(-N_{\rm anneal})$ where $N_{\rm anneal}$ is the number of
e-folds of annealing. Estimate: $N_{\rm anneal} \sim \log(t_{\rm
freeze}/t_c) \sim 10$, giving $\exp(-10) \sim 4 \times 10^{-5}$
suppression. Combined with the network scaling $10^{26}$, total
suppression $\sim 10^{30}$. Still 6 orders short of the $10^{36}$
bound. \textbf{The refined estimate brings the gap closer but does
not close it.}

\textbf{Objection $\gamma$}: The BCC condensate is a
\emph{relativistic} order parameter, not a non-relativistic crystal.
The Peierls-Nabarro analysis is rooted in non-relativistic elasticity.
Is the relativistic generalization valid?\\
\emph{Response: VALID-with-mitigation.} Relativistic corrections
modify the dispersion relation but preserve the periodic-potential
structure that gives PN. The leading $\exp(-2\pi w/b)$ factor is
geometric, set by the ratio of defect width to lattice period; this
is unchanged by relativity. Velocity ceilings change from sound
speed to $c$, but for $c_s < c$ (which is generic for weakly
condensed Brazovskii media), the ceiling is still $c_s$.
Relativistic refinements are subleading. Status: DISMISSED with the
caveat that a full relativistic Brazovskii-defect dynamics has not
been worked out in the TECT literature.

\subsection*{6. Self-adversarial review (CLAUDE.md \S 6.3.5(a))}
