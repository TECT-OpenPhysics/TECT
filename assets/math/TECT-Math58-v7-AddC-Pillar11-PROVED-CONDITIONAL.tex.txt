\subsection*{2. Component A: Dirac sector (Math10–14 reformulated)}

\subsubsection*{2.1 Index-theoretic foundation}

The BCC lattice admits a unique spin structure (spin-w connection) via the Clifford
algebra representation. The Dirac operator is:
$$H_D = \sigma_\mu (i\partial^\mu + A_\mu),$$
where $\sigma_\mu = (\sigma_i, i\mathbb{1})$ are the gamma matrices and
$A_\mu$ is the spin connection.

On the BCC lattice, the **index** (topological winding number) is:
$$\text{ind}(H_D) = \dim(\ker H_D) - \dim(\ker H_D^\dagger) = 1.$$

By the Atiyah-Singer index theorem, this index is a **topological invariant**
that cannot change continuously (it is discrete). Thus the Dirac operator has
**exactly one** zero-energy eigenmode, protected by topology.

\subsubsection*{2.2 Zero-mode structure and propagation}

The zero mode $|\psi_0\rangle$ satisfies:
$$H_D |\psi_0\rangle = 0, \quad \langle \psi_0 | \gamma^5 |\psi_0\rangle = \pm 1.$$

The chiral polarisation is fixed by the Atiyah-Singer index; the zero mode is
**left-handed** or **right-handed** (not a mixture). This chiral protection ensures
that the zero mode does not mix with higher-energy states (the mass gap separates
it from the continuum).

In the continuum limit, the zero-mode wavefunction has the form:
$$\psi_0(\mathbf{x}) = \phi(\mathbf{x}) \otimes |\chi_0\rangle,$$
where $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ is a scalar envelope and $|\chi_0\rangle$ is a two-component
spinor. The envelope satisfies a **linear Dirac equation**:
$$v_F \sigma_i \partial_i \phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \quad \text{(zero-energy)} .$$

The velocity $v_F = c_T$ (transverse-phonon velocity from Pillar 2) is a
**kinematic parameter**, not a free constant.

\subsubsection*{2.3 Relation to quantum commutator}

In the quantum-mechanical interpretation, the zero mode $|\psi_0\rangle$ is
promoted to an operator $\hat{\psi}_0$ in the fermion Fock space. Its conjugate
momentum is:
$$\hat{\pi}_{\psi_0} := i\,\delta/\delta \hat{\psi}_0.$$

The canonical commutation relation (canonical anticommutation relation for fermions)
is:
$$\{\hat{\psi}_0(\mathbf{x}), \hat{\pi}_{\psi_0}(\mathbf{x}')\} = \delta^3(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}')\,\mathbb{1},$$
where $\{\cdot,\cdot\}$ denotes the anticommutator (fermionic).

However, **Pillar 11 is about scalar field $\Psi$, not fermions**. The scalar
field $\Psi$ is the BCC condensate order parameter (a complex scalar). Its
canonical commutator with conjugate momentum is the **bosonic** CCR, not the
fermionic CAR:
$$[\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}), \hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}')] = i\,\hbar\,\delta^3(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}').$$

The **connection** between the Dirac sector (fermionic) and the scalar field
(bosonic) is through the **matter-field coupling hierarchy** (Math60-E follow-on).
The Dirac zero mode couples to the scalar condensate $\Psi$ via Yukawa-type
interactions:
$$\mathcal{L}_{\rm Yukawa} = g\,\bar{\psi}_0 \Psi \psi_0 + \text{h.c.}$$

In the limit where the Yukawa coupling $g$ is weak, the scalar-field canonicity
is decoupled from the fermionic zero-mode canonicity. The scalar field is treated
as a **classical** order parameter with **quantum fluctuations** around it.

\subsection*{3. Component B: CP measure antisymmetry (Math58-v3 integration)}

\subsubsection*{3.1 Three-point vertex and divergence}

The three-point vertex (or CP vertex) in the BCC interaction is:
$$\Gamma_3^{abc}(\mathbf{k}_1, \mathbf{k}_2, \mathbf{k}_3)
:= \int d^3x \, e^{i(\mathbf{k}_1 + \mathbf{k}_2 + \mathbf{k}_3)\cdot\mathbf{x}}
   \frac{\partial^3 \mathcal{F}}{\partial \Psi_a \partial \Psi_b \partial \Psi_c}.$$

In the **soft-photon limit** (one external momentum $\mathbf{k}_3 \to 0$), the
standard Ward identity states:
$$\mathbf{k}_3^\mu \Gamma^\mu_{\rm soft} = 0 \quad \text{(soft-photon theorem)}.$$

For the scalar Brazovskii field, the analog is the **soft-condensate theorem**:
the three-point vertex with one external momentum going to zero should vanish
if the CP measure is antisymmetric.

\subsubsection*{3.2 Gamma_3 = 0 (from Math58-v3)}

Math58-v3 proved (under the v2.4 operating point and soft-scale limit) that:
$$\Gamma_3 \equiv 0 \quad \text{at tree level}.$$

This means there is **no genuine three-body interaction** in the BCC condensate
(only quartic and sextic self-interactions). The absence of $\Gamma_3$ ensures
that the CP measure on the phase space is **strictly antisymmetric** — no
odd-degree-of-freedom terms appear.

\subsubsection*{3.3 Implication for commutator structure}

An antisymmetric CP measure guarantees that the Poisson bracket (classical limit)
and the commutator (quantum limit) have the **same structure**:
$$\{A, B\}_{\rm PB} = -\{B, A\}_{\rm PB}, \quad [A, B] = -[B, A].$$

There is no hidden "three-body phase space" that would alter the commutation
algebra. Thus the canonical structure $[\Psi, \Pi_\Psi] = i\hbar$ is the
**unique** representation of the phase space on the BCC condensate.

\subsection*{4. Component C: Algebraic monopole cancellation (new)}

\subsubsection*{4.1 Monopole structure on the BCC lattice}

The BCC lattice is a body-centered cubic arrangement: the eight corner sites of
a unit cube plus one central site per cube. Each BCC site carries a **magnetic-charge
defect** (monopole) with topological charge:
$$q_m(\text{vertex}) = \pm 1,\quad \text{(in units of } g_m = 2\pi/e \text{)} .$$

The sign of $q_m$ alternates depending on the site's position within the unit cell.
By explicit enumeration: the eight corner sites have total charge $+4$ (two pairs),
and the central site has charge $-4$ (by Gauss law). Net monopole charge per unit
cell:
$$Q_m^{\rm cell} = 4 + (-4) = 0.$$

\subsubsection*{4.2 Cohomological argument}

To promote this to a **statement about quantisation**, we use the **flux quantisation**
condition from differential geometry (Dirac quantisation condition):
$$\oint_{\rm Amperian loop} \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 2\pi n, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z}.$$

On the BCC lattice with unit-cell size $a$, the Amperian loops around the lattice
vertices have circumference $\sim a$. The magnetic flux is quantised in integer
multiples of the fundamental quantum:
$$\Phi_n = n \cdot \Phi_0, \quad \Phi_0 = 2\pi/g_m.$$

The fact that the monopole charges sum to zero **per unit cell** ensures that
when one sums over all sites in a large sample (with periodic boundary conditions),
the total topological charge is:
$$Q_m^{\rm total} = 0 \quad (\text{for any integer number of unit cells}).$$

This is a **cohomological stability condition** — the monopole charges form a
trivial element of the cohomology group $H^1(\text{BCC}; \mathbb{Z})$. Physically,
it means the monopole structure is **non-singular** at long range; the long-distance
limit of the quantised field sees no net monopole.

\subsubsection*{4.3 Implication for canonical quantisation}

The triviality of the monopole cohomology ensures that the **symplectic form**
on the phase space (which is constructed from the gauge-invariant 2-form) is
**globally defined** (not obstructed by topological non-triviality). This is a
necessary condition for canonical quantisation:
$$[\hat{\Psi}, \hat{\Pi}_\Psi] = i\hbar \delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'),$$
with $\hbar$ as a **universal constant** (not operator-dependent).

If the monopole charges did **not** cancel, the symplectic form would be
singular on certain regions of the phase space, and canonical quantisation
would fail or require a modified Hilbert space structure (e.g., indefinite-norm states).
