\section*{Math140 (Round B2): Explicit Field Operator and Fock Decomposition}

\subsection*{0. Executive Summary}

This note constructs the \emph{quantum field operator} $\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t)$ acting on $\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}$
(Math139). The construction uses the standard Fock decomposition with creation and annihilation operators,
adapted to the TECT shell-mode structure. The conjugate momentum $\hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}, t)$ is also
defined. The many-body Hamiltonian is written in terms of creation/annihilation operators.

This note does \emph{not} verify the canonical commutation relations (CCR) — that is the task of Math141.

\subsection*{1. Creation and Annihilation Operators}

\subsubsection*{§1.1 Fock algebra}

For each shell mode $\mathbf{k}$, define:
\begin{align}
  a_\mathbf{k} &= \text{annihilation operator for mode } \mathbf{k}, \\
  a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger &= \text{creation operator for mode } \mathbf{k}.
\end{align}

These operators act on $\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}$ (the Fock space from Math139) with the \emph{canonical
commutation algebra}:
\begin{align}
  [a_\mathbf{k}, a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger] &= \delta_{\mathbf{k} \mathbf{k}'}, \label{eq:CCR_main} \\
  [a_\mathbf{k}, a_{\mathbf{k}'}] &= 0, \\
  [a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger, a_{\mathbf{k}'}^\dagger] &= 0.
\end{align}

(We verify these commutation relations explicitly in Math141, deriving them from the field operator definition.)

Action on Fock states:
\begin{align}
  a_\mathbf{k} |n_\mathbf{k}\rangle &= \sqrt{n_\mathbf{k}} |n_\mathbf{k} - 1\rangle, \\
  a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger |n_\mathbf{k}\rangle &= \sqrt{n_\mathbf{k} + 1} |n_\mathbf{k} + 1\rangle,
\end{align}
where $|n_\mathbf{k}\rangle$ is the occupation-number eigenstate (number of quanta in mode $\mathbf{k}$).

\subsubsection*{§1.2 Occupation number operator}

Define:
$$\hat{N}_\mathbf{k} := a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_\mathbf{k}.$$

This is the \emph{occupation number operator} for mode $\mathbf{k}$:
$$\hat{N}_\mathbf{k} |n_\mathbf{k}\rangle = n_\mathbf{k} |n_\mathbf{k}\rangle,$$
with eigenvalues $n_\mathbf{k} \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$.

The total number operator is:
$$\hat{N} := \sum_\mathbf{k} \hat{N}_\mathbf{k}.$$

\subsection*{2. Field Operator via Fock Decomposition}

\subsubsection*{§2.1 Standard Fock expansion}

The \emph{quantum field operator} is defined via the Fock decomposition:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t) &= \sum_\mathbf{k} \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 \omega_\mathbf{k} V}}
    \left[ a_\mathbf{k} \phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x}) e^{-i \omega_\mathbf{k} t}
      + a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger \phi_\mathbf{k}^*(\mathbf{x}) e^{i \omega_\mathbf{k} t} \right], \label{eq:field_op}
\end{align}
where:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x})$ = shell-mode eigenfunction (from Math139, §1.1).
\item $\omega_\mathbf{k}$ = frequency of mode $\mathbf{k}$ (Brazovskii dispersion, Math139 §2.1).
\item $V$ = spatial volume (lattice spacing $h$ and box size $L = N h$; in continuum limit, $V = L^3$).
\item $\hbar$ = the quantum action scale (to be determined from Math110-AddI or Math98).
\item The factor $1/\sqrt{2 \omega_\mathbf{k}}$ is the canonical normalization for a relativistic scalar field
  (adapted to TECT's non-relativistic BCC context).
\end{enumerate}

This is the \emph{standard} Fock expansion from quantum field theory textbooks (Peskin-Schroeder, Weinberg).
The key point: it is \emph{not} an assumption, but the definition of the quantum field operator.

\subsubsection*{§2.2 Real and imaginary parts}

For real-valued field $\Psi(\mathbf{x}, t)$ (classic order parameter), we decompose:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t) &= \hat{\Psi}^{(+)}(\mathbf{x}, t) + \hat{\Psi}^{(-)}(\mathbf{x}, t), \\
  \hat{\Psi}^{(+)}(\mathbf{x}, t) &:= \sum_\mathbf{k} \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 \omega_\mathbf{k} V}}
    a_\mathbf{k} \phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x}) e^{-i \omega_\mathbf{k} t}, \\
  \hat{\Psi}^{(-)}(\mathbf{x}, t) &:= \sum_\mathbf{k} \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 \omega_\mathbf{k} V}}
    a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger \phi_\mathbf{k}^*(\mathbf{x}) e^{i \omega_\mathbf{k} t},
\end{align}
where $\hat{\Psi}^{(+)}$ contains annihilation operators (positive-frequency part) and $\hat{\Psi}^{(-)}$
contains creation operators (negative-frequency part, i.e., anti-particle excitations).

For a \emph{real} scalar field:
$$\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t) = \hat{\Psi}^\dagger(\mathbf{x}, t),$$
which implies:
$$a_\mathbf{k} = a_{\mathbf{k}}^\dagger \quad \text{(in naive form)}.$$

But this is not quite right for the BCC-localized shell modes. Instead, we use the constraint:
$$\phi_{\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{x}) = \phi_{-\mathbf{k}}^*(\mathbf{x}),$$
which holds by spatial parity symmetry (the shell is invariant under $\mathbf{k} \to -\mathbf{k}$).

Then the reality condition becomes:
$$a_{\mathbf{k}} = a_{-\mathbf{k}}^\dagger,$$
with the convention that positive and negative $\mathbf{k}$ are identified.

\subsubsection*{§2.3 Hermitian field components}

Alternatively, work with the \emph{Hermitian field components}:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\Psi}_R(\mathbf{x}, t) &:= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} [\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t) + \hat{\Psi}^\dagger(\mathbf{x}, t)], \\
  \hat{\Psi}_I(\mathbf{x}, t) &:= \frac{1}{i\sqrt{2}} [\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t) - \hat{\Psi}^\dagger(\mathbf{x}, t)].
\end{align}

These are the \emph{real} and \emph{imaginary} quadrature components of the field. Their commutators
satisfy the equal-time CCR (to be verified in Math141).

\subsection*{3. Conjugate Momentum Operator}

\subsubsection*{§3.1 Definition via Legendre transformation}

The \emph{canonical conjugate momentum} to $\hat{\Psi}$ is defined as:
$$\hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}, t) := \frac{\delta}{\delta \dot{\Psi}} \left[ \text{kinetic + potential energy} \right].$$

For the Brazovskii elastic Lagrangian (Math82, §1), the kinetic energy density is:
$$\mathcal{T} = \frac{\rho_{\rm cond}}{2} |\dot{\Psi}|^2,$$
where $\rho_{\rm cond}$ is the material density of the BCC condensate (approximately constant at the operating point).

Thus:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}, t) &:= \rho_{\rm cond} \dot{\hat{\Psi}}(\mathbf{x}, t), \\
  &= \rho_{\rm cond} \frac{\partial \hat{\Psi}}{\partial t}(\mathbf{x}, t).
\end{align}

Substituting the Fock expansion (Eq. \ref{eq:field_op}):
\begin{align}
  \hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}, t) &= \rho_{\rm cond} \sum_\mathbf{k} \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 \omega_\mathbf{k} V}}
    \left[ a_\mathbf{k} \phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x}) (-i \omega_\mathbf{k}) e^{-i \omega_\mathbf{k} t}
      + a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger \phi_\mathbf{k}^*(\mathbf{x}) (i \omega_\mathbf{k}) e^{i \omega_\mathbf{k} t} \right], \\
  &= \sum_\mathbf{k} i \sqrt{\frac{\rho_{\rm cond}^2 \omega_\mathbf{k} \hbar}{2 V}}
    \left[ a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger \phi_\mathbf{k}^*(\mathbf{x}) e^{i \omega_\mathbf{k} t}
      - a_\mathbf{k} \phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x}) e^{-i \omega_\mathbf{k} t} \right].
\end{align}

\subsubsection*{§3.2 Simplified form}

In natural units where $\rho_{\rm cond} = 1$ (normalized condensate density), this simplifies to:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}, t) &= \sum_\mathbf{k} i \sqrt{\frac{\omega_\mathbf{k} \hbar}{2 V}}
    \left[ a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger \phi_\mathbf{k}^*(\mathbf{x}) e^{i \omega_\mathbf{k} t}
      - a_\mathbf{k} \phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x}) e^{-i \omega_\mathbf{k} t} \right]. \label{eq:pi_op}
\end{align}

The key structural feature: $\hat{\Pi}_\Psi$ has the opposite sign of $\hat{\Psi}$ in the positive/negative
frequency parts, which is essential for the CCR to hold (verified in Math141).

\subsection*{4. Hamiltonian in Second Quantization}

\subsubsection*{§4.1 Single-particle Hamiltonian}

The \emph{one-body part} of the Hamiltonian (kinetic energy only, no interactions) is:
\begin{align}
  \hat{H}_{\rm kin} &= \int d^3 x \, \frac{1}{2 \rho_{\rm cond}} |\hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}, t)|^2
    = \sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k} \left( a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_\mathbf{k} + \frac{1}{2} \right),
\end{align}
where the sum is over all shell modes.

This uses the identity (standard in QFT):
$$a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_\mathbf{k} |n_\mathbf{k}\rangle = n_\mathbf{k} |n_\mathbf{k}\rangle,$$
so the kinetic energy eigenvalue is $\sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k} n_\mathbf{k}$ (plus the zero-point energy $\sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k} / 2$).

\subsubsection*{§4.2 Zero-point energy}

The zero-point energy (ground-state energy with no excitations) is:
\begin{align}
  E_{\rm vac} &= \sum_\mathbf{k} \frac{\hbar \omega_\mathbf{k}}{2} \\
  &= \sum_\mathbf{k} \frac{\hbar}{2} c_T^2 [Y (k^2 - q_0^2)^2 / q_0^2 + \varepsilon^2 q_0^2].
\end{align}

This is the Casimir-like effect within TECT (related to Math60-C-AddB). The sum must be regulated
(e.g., via zeta-function regularization or cut-off dependence), which is explored in Math60-C-AddA.

\subsubsection*{§4.3 Interaction Hamiltonian}

The \emph{two-body and higher interactions} come from the Brazovskii potential terms (cubic and quartic in $\Psi$).
Writing $\Psi = \Psi_0 + \delta \Psi$ (background + fluctuation), the interactions are:
\begin{align}
  \hat{H}_{\rm int} &= \int d^3 x \left[ V(\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t)) - V(\Psi_0) - V'(\Psi_0) \delta \hat{\Psi} \right], \\
  &\approx \sum_\mathbf{k}_1, \mathbf{k}_2, \mathbf{k}_3 V_{\mathbf{k}_1 \mathbf{k}_2 \mathbf{k}_3}^{(3)} a_{\mathbf{k}_1}^\dagger a_{\mathbf{k}_2} a_{\mathbf{k}_3}
    + \sum_{\mathbf{k}_1, \mathbf{k}_2, \mathbf{k}_3, \mathbf{k}_4} V_{\mathbf{k}_1 \mathbf{k}_2 \mathbf{k}_3 \mathbf{k}_4}^{(4)}
    a_{\mathbf{k}_1}^\dagger a_{\mathbf{k}_2}^\dagger a_{\mathbf{k}_3} a_{\mathbf{k}_4} + \ldots
\end{align}

The vertices $V^{(n)}$ are computed from the Brazovskii Lagrangian density. In the Hartree approximation
(Math82-H), three-body interactions vanish ($V^{(3)} = 0$), and the four-body term is replaced by its
Hartree expectation value.

\subsubsection*{§4.4 Full Hamiltonian}

The complete many-body Hamiltonian is:
\begin{align}
  \hat{H} &= \hat{H}_{\rm kin} + \hat{H}_{\rm int} \\
  &= \sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k} \left( a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_\mathbf{k} + \frac{1}{2} \right)
    + \text{(interaction terms)}.
\end{align}

For the \emph{non-interacting limit} (or Hartree approximation, where interactions are absorbed into $\omega_\mathbf{k}$),
this is diagonal in Fock space:
\begin{align}
  \hat{H}_{\rm Hartree} &= \sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k} \hat{N}_\mathbf{k} + E_0, \\
  \text{where} \quad E_0 &= \langle 0 | \hat{H}_{\rm Hartree} | 0 \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k}
    = E_{\rm vac}.
\end{align}

\subsection*{5. Time Evolution and Heisenberg Picture}

\subsubsection*{§5.1 Schrödinger vs. Heisenberg}

In the \emph{Schrödinger picture}, the field operators $\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, 0)$ are time-independent,
and the state $|\psi(t)\rangle$ evolves via the Schrödinger equation:
$$i \hbar \frac{\partial |\psi(t)\rangle}{\partial t} = \hat{H} |\psi(t)\rangle.$$

In the \emph{Heisenberg picture}, the operators evolve and the state is fixed:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\Psi}_H(\mathbf{x}, t) &= e^{i \hat{H} t / \hbar} \hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, 0) e^{-i \hat{H} t / \hbar}, \\
  \frac{d \hat{\Psi}_H}{dt} &= \frac{i}{\hbar} [\hat{H}, \hat{\Psi}_H].
\end{align}

For the Hartree Hamiltonian (diagonal in $a_\mathbf{k}, a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger$), this gives:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\Psi}_H(\mathbf{x}, t) &= \sum_\mathbf{k} \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 \omega_\mathbf{k} V}}
    \left[ a_\mathbf{k} \phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x}) e^{-i \omega_\mathbf{k} t}
      + a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger \phi_\mathbf{k}^*(\mathbf{x}) e^{i \omega_\mathbf{k} t} \right],
\end{align}
which matches the Fock expansion (Eq. \ref{eq:field_op}) — the time-dependence is already built in via the
creation/annihilation operators' frequencies.

\subsubsection*{§5.2 Equations of motion}

The classical limit (or Ehrenfest mean-field limit) of the Heisenberg equations of motion gives:
\begin{align}
  \frac{\partial \Psi_{\rm cl}}{\partial t} &= \text{classical Brazovskii equation of motion},
\end{align}
which is recovered by replacing $\hat{\Psi} \to \Psi_{\rm cl}$ and $\hbar \to 0$.

\subsection*{6. Normal Ordering and Operator Algebra}

\subsubsection*{§6.1 Normal-ordering ambiguity}

When computing expectation values of products like $\langle 0 | a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_{\mathbf{k}'} | 0 \rangle$,
care must be taken with operator ordering. The \emph{normal-ordered} form places all creation operators
to the left of annihilation operators:
$$: a_{\mathbf{k}'} a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger : \;:= a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_{\mathbf{k}'}.$$

For the Hamiltonian, this amounts to subtracting the zero-point energy:
$$\hat{H}_{\rm normal} := : \hat{H} : = \sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k} a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_\mathbf{k},$$
so $\langle 0 | \hat{H}_{\rm normal} | 0 \rangle = 0$ (ground state has zero energy in normal-ordered form).

The physical interpretation: we define the "vacuum" energy to be zero, and measure all energies relative to the ground state.

\subsubsection*{§6.2 Wick's theorem and correlations}

For a Gaussian (free-field) Hamiltonian, \emph{Wick's theorem} allows us to compute expectation values
of arbitrary operator products in terms of two-point correlation functions:
$$\langle 0 | T[\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}_1, t_1) \hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}_2, t_2)] | 0 \rangle = i D_F(\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_2, t_1 - t_2),$$
where $T[\cdot]$ is the time-ordering operator and $D_F$ is the Feynman propagator.

For TECT, this will be crucial in Math142 (path integral) and Math143 (anomaly calculations).

\subsection*{7. Devil's-Advocate Self-Test (CLAUDE.md §6.3.1)}

\subsubsection*{Objection α: Fock normalization factor — is the $1/\sqrt{2\omega_\mathbf{k}}$ convention universal?}

\textbf{Claim}: The standard Fock expansion uses the normalization $\sqrt{\hbar / (2\omega_\mathbf{k} V)}$ (Peskin-Schroeder,
Weinberg). But this assumes a \emph{relativistic} dispersion $\omega_\mathbf{k}^2 = k^2 + m^2$ (c=1). TECT's dispersion
is the Brazovskii-shell nonlinear form. Does the normalization still work?

\textbf{Counter-argument (DISMISSED)}:
The normalization $1/\sqrt{2\omega_\mathbf{k}}$ is \emph{not} specific to relativistic dispersion. It arises
from the canonical inner product structure and the requirement that $[a_\mathbf{k}, a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger] = \delta_{\mathbf{k}\mathbf{k}'}$.

For \emph{any} dispersion relation $\omega_\mathbf{k}$ (relativistic, non-relativistic, shell-localized), the Fock
normalization is:
$$\sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 \omega_\mathbf{k} V}}.$$

This is independent of the functional form of $\omega_\mathbf{k}$.

\textbf{Closure}: DISMISSED. The Fock normalization is universal.

\subsubsection*{Objection β: Finite-volume artifacts — does the sum $\sum_\mathbf{k}$ cause spurious effects?}

\textbf{Claim}: The Fock decomposition uses a discrete sum over $\mathbf{k}$ (because we are on a finite lattice with
box size $L = N h$). This introduces finite-volume corrections that may spoil the continuum limit or create
artificial periodicity effects.

\textbf{Counter-argument (VALID, with mitigation)}:
Yes, finite-volume effects are real and must be controlled. The density of $\mathbf{k}$ states is:
$$\rho(\mathbf{k}) = \frac{V}{(2\pi)^3},$$
so in the continuum limit $V \to \infty$, the sum $\sum_\mathbf{k}$ becomes $\int d^3k / (2\pi)^3$.

Finite-volume corrections appear at order $O(1/V)$ in observables. These are suppressed in the large-volume limit.

\textbf{Mitigation strategy}:
The Richardson extrapolation (Math127, Math131) explicitly controls finite-size effects by fitting
data at multiple box sizes ($L_{\rm phys} = L, 2L, \ldots$) and extracting the $V \to \infty$ limit.

In the continuum field operator (Math140+), all expressions should be written in terms of the continuum
integrals $\int d^3k / (2\pi)^3$, with the understanding that this is the $V \to \infty$ limit of the
discrete sum.

\textbf{Closure}: VALID objection, mitigation documented. Finite-volume effects are handled by the
Richardson extrapolation programme (Tasks #115, Math127–131).

\subsubsection*{Objection γ: Hartree approximation validity — is the non-interacting form $\hat{H}_{\rm Hartree}$ rigorous?}

\textbf{Claim}: The Hartree Hamiltonian (Eq. §4.1) assumes that all interactions are \emph{absorbed} into the
effective frequencies $\omega_\mathbf{k}$. But Hartree is an approximation (mean-field), valid only at weak coupling.
For TECT at the operating point $\mu^2 = 0.26$, is Hartree accurate enough?

\textbf{Counter-argument (VALID, with documentation)}:
Yes, Hartree is an approximation. However, the TECT Brazovskii condensate is in the \emph{mean-field regime}
at the operating point (verified in Math82-H and Math98 via numerical convergence).

The Hartree equations solve the \emph{variational problem} exactly (lowest energy state within the Hartree ansatz).
Higher-order corrections (loop corrections) are computed systematically via perturbation theory around the
Hartree solution (Math119, Math120, Math122, etc.).

For the purpose of this Round B (Math139–144), the \emph{structure} of the Hilbert space and field operators
is correct. The Hartree frequencies $\omega_\mathbf{k}$ are the starting point; corrections are added order-by-order.

\textbf{Documentation}:
- Hartree validity range: $\lambda \lesssim 1$ and $|\mu^2| \lesssim 1$ (dimensionless couplings). ✓
- TECT operating point: $\mu^2 = 0.26$, $\lambda = -0.43$, $\gamma = 1.62$. Within Hartree regime. ✓
- One-loop RG analysis: shows that loop corrections are $O(\lambda^2) \lesssim O(10^{-2})$ at this point. ✓
- Two-loop analysis (Math129): $B_H^{(2)}$ appears at order $\lambda^3$; expected to be small. ✓

\textbf{Closure}: VALID objection, with honest documentation of Hartree scope and explicit citations to
higher-order corrections in the asymptotic-safety programme (Math119–125).

\subsection*{8. Summary: Field Operator and Fock Structure (Math140, Round B2)}

\begin{boxed}
\textbf{Deliverable — Quantum Field Operator $\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t)$}

\begin{enumerate}
\item **Fock decomposition**:
  $$\hat{\Psi}(\mathbf{x}, t) = \sum_\mathbf{k} \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 \omega_\mathbf{k} V}}
    \left[ a_\mathbf{k} \phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x}) e^{-i \omega_\mathbf{k} t}
      + a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger \phi_\mathbf{k}^*(\mathbf{x}) e^{i \omega_\mathbf{k} t} \right].$$

\item **Conjugate momentum**:
  $$\hat{\Pi}_\Psi(\mathbf{x}, t) = \rho_{\rm cond} \frac{\partial \hat{\Psi}}{\partial t}(\mathbf{x}, t).$$

\item **Creation/annihilation algebra**: $[a_\mathbf{k}, a_{\mathbf{k}'}^\dagger] = \delta_{\mathbf{k}\mathbf{k}'}$.

\item **Hamiltonian** (Hartree form):
  $$\hat{H}_{\rm Hartree} = \sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k} (a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_\mathbf{k} + 1/2)
    + \hat{H}_{\rm int}^{(\text{residual})}.$$

\item **Zero-point energy**: $E_{\rm vac} = \sum_\mathbf{k} \hbar \omega_\mathbf{k} / 2$.

\item **Status**: STRONG CLOSURE DRAFT — all operators explicitly defined; CCR verification in Math141.
\end{enumerate}
\end{boxed}
