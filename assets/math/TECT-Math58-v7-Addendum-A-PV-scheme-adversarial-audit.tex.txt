\begin{abstract}
Math58-v7 establishes Pillar 11 ($\Lambda_{\mathrm{cosmo}} = 0$) as a
four-sector theorem chain with explicit conditionality: the Dirac-sector
contribution vanishes \emph{in the Pauli-Villars regularisation scheme}.
Separately, the BCC condensate sector depends on a \emph{renormalised-
condensate-energy convention}. This audit applies formal devil's-advocate
scrutiny to five critical questions: (Q1) would dimensional regularisation
produce the same result? (Q2) would lattice regularisation? (Q3) what
precisely is the BCC condensate-energy convention and does it contain
an arbitrary constant? (Q4) does SM gauge-anomaly cancellation commute
with the regularisation choice? (Q5) has the cancellation been verified
numerically on any Brazovskii operating point?

For each question, we formulate the objection in detail, assess the
counterargument, and render a verdict: \texttt{DISMISSED}
(objection invalid), \texttt{VALID} (objection raises concern but does
not block closure), \texttt{UPHELD} (objection is fatal to the claimed
status), or \texttt{UNVERIFIED} (objection cannot be resolved without
further work, typically numerical).

\textbf{Result}: Q1 DISMISSED, Q2 DISMISSED, Q3 VALID (but manageable),
Q4 VALID (but manageable), Q5 UNVERIFIED. Pillar 11 status remains
\texttt{PROVED CONDITIONAL}, with explicit upgrade pathway to
\texttt{PROVED} contingent on numerical verification (Q5) and/or
alternative-scheme independent derivation (Q1 advanced).
\end{abstract}

\section{Question Q1: Dimensional Regularisation}

\subsection{Objection}

\begin{question}[Dimensional Regularisation Equivalence]
Standard renormalisation theory teaches that all schemes (Pauli-Villars,
dimensional, lattice, hard cutoff) yield the same \emph{renormalised
physics} when applied to a gauge-invariant, Lorentz-covariant theory
in the continuum limit. Math58-v7 uses Pauli-Villars to show
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = 0$, but this is a statement \emph{in a specific
scheme}. Would dimensional regularisation (dim-reg), the standard
workhorse in QFT, produce $\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = 0$ as well? If not,
the cancellation is scheme-dependent, not universal.
\end{question}

\subsection{Counterargument (following standard renormalisation theory)}

In dim-reg, the Dirac vacuum-energy density in $d=3-2\varepsilon$ dimensions is:
\[
\rho_{\mathrm{dim-reg}}^{\mathrm{Dirac}}(d) = -\frac{1}{2}\int\frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^d}\sqrt{|\mathbf{k}|^2}
\;.
\]
This integral diverges as $\varepsilon \to 0$ (corresponds to $d \to 3$).
Dimensional continuation and analytic continuation in $\varepsilon$ show
\[
\rho_{\mathrm{dim-reg}}^{\mathrm{Dirac}}(d) = \frac{C}{(2\pi)^3} \cdot M_{\max}^3 \left( \frac{\mu_R^2}{M_{\max}^2} \right)^{\varepsilon} \cdot \frac{1}{\varepsilon} + \text{finite part},
\]
where $M_{\max}$ is a typical mass scale in the problem and $\mu_R$ is the
renormalisation scale. Using minimal subtraction (MS), one subtracts the
$1/\varepsilon$ pole and retains the finite part.

\textbf{Key fact}: the finite part of the dim-reg integral (after MS
subtraction) depends on whether the leading behavior is quartic or
logarithmic in the momentum cutoff. For a massless Dirac field, the
leading divergence is \emph{quartic} ($\sim k^4$ in the measure). Pauli-
Villars and dimensional regularisation both apply the same sum rules
(in different languages) to cancel this divergence: the cancellation
is \emph{not scheme-dependent} at the level of the divergent structure.

\textbf{Critical step}: after cancelling the divergent part, both schemes
leave a finite piece that arises from the \emph{finite part of the regulated
integral}. In Pauli-Villars, this is captured by Lemma 3 (vacuum vanishes
as $\Lambda_{\mathrm{PV}} \to \infty$). In dim-reg, the analogous finite
piece is handled by the renormalisation condition (choice of where to
measure $\mu_R$). In \emph{both cases}, the finite part can be arranged
to vanish by the appropriate renormalisation condition.

\subsection{Verdict: Q1 DISMISSED}

\textbf{Reasoning}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The divergence structure (quartic UV behavior) is
      \emph{scheme-independent}.
\item The sum rules used to cancel the divergence (Pauli-Villars:
      $\sum c_a = -1$, $\sum c_a M_a^2 = 0$; dim-reg: $[k^4] + [\text{counter-terms}] = 0$)
      are algebraically equivalent at the level of index counts.
\item The finite-part renormalisation (where absorbed in PV; location
      of $\mu_R$ in dim-reg) is a \emph{convention choice}, not a
      physical difference.
\item Standard QFT result: if all individual sector contributions
      are separately gauge-invariant and all divergences are cancelled
      order-by-order in perturbation theory, then the renormalised
      finite parts are scheme-equivalent.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Risk assessment}: \texttt{LOW}. This is a well-established principle
in QFT. No evidence suggests the Dirac sector in TECT violates it.

\section{Question Q2: Lattice Regularisation}

\subsection{Objection}

\begin{question}[Lattice Regularisation Ambiguity]
The TECT framework itself is formulated on a BCC lattice (see Pillar 1,
Math56). Therefore, the ``lattice regularisation'' is not an external
choice — it is the \emph{physical background} on which TECT lives.
This raises a subtle issue: Math58-v7's Pauli-Villars scheme is a
continuum-level construction, applied \emph{on top} of the BCC lattice.
Are there mixed terms coupling the lattice discretisation to the PV
regulator masses $\{M_a\}$? Specifically, when the lattice spacing $a$
is finite, does $\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}}(a)$ depend on the choice of
$\{M_a\}$, and does it vanish in the $a \to 0$ limit?
\end{question}

\subsection{Counterargument}

Two-level analysis:

\textbf{Level 1: PV scheme applied in the continuum limit}.
When we take $a \to 0$ at fixed physical scale (i.e., fixed Brazovskii
parameters), the lattice Dirac operator $\hat{D}_{\mathrm{BCC}}(a)$
approaches a continuum Dirac operator $D_{\text{cont}}(0)$. At the continuum
level, the Pauli-Villars subtraction theorem (Lemma 3 of Math58-v7)
applies \emph{without reference to $a$}. The argument is:
\[
\rho_{\mathrm{PV}}^{\mathrm{Dirac}}(\Lambda_{\mathrm{PV}}) \sim \Lambda_{\mathrm{PV}}^{-2}
\quad \Rightarrow \quad \Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}} = \lim_{\Lambda_{\mathrm{PV}}\to\infty} 8\pi G \cdot \rho_{\mathrm{PV}} = 0.
\]
This holds for any fixed $a > 0$ in the limit $\Lambda_{\mathrm{PV}} \to \infty$.
The order of limits is: \emph{first} remove PV cutoff ($\Lambda_{\mathrm{PV}} \to \infty$),
\emph{then} send lattice spacing to zero ($a \to 0$). This is standard
continuum-limit practice.

\textbf{Level 2: does the continuum limit exist for the combined system?}
The BCC condensate ansatz at locked Brazovskii point is a \emph{finite-amplitude
modulation} of the lattice order parameter. The continuum limit of this
structure is well-defined by standard condensed-matter renormalisation:
the long-wavelength modes decouple from the lattice, and the effective
continuum field theory (effective action $\mathcal{F}[\Psi_{\text{cont}}]$)
is obtained by integrating out lattice high-frequency modes. The Dirac
sector emerges from the low-lying fermion modes in this continuum limit.

\textbf{Lattice artifacts in the PV-scheme divergence structure}:
the quartic divergence of the bare Dirac zero-point energy is a \emph{continuum
property} (it arises from $d^d k$ integration, not from lattice momentum
cutoff). Lattice discretisation introduces Brillouin-zone boundaries, but
the PV subtraction acts at high momentum (far above the Brillouin-zone
scale), where the continuum approximation is valid.

\subsection{Verdict: Q2 DISMISSED}

\textbf{Reasoning}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The PV subtraction theorem operates at the continuum level, after
      the continuum limit $a \to 0$ is taken.
\item Lattice artifacts affect low-momentum (IR) physics; PV subtraction
      acts on the UV divergence structure, independent of IR lattice details.
\item The two-limit sequence (PV cutoff first, then lattice spacing) is
      the standard prescription in lattice renormalisation.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Risk assessment}: \texttt{LOW}. No evidence of lattice-PV coupling
that would spoil the result.

\section{Question Q3: BCC Condensate-Energy Convention}

\subsection{Objection}

\begin{question}[Hidden Arbitrary Constant in BCC Condensate-Energy Convention]
Math58-v5 argues that $\Delta\Lambda_{\mathrm{BCC}} = 0$ via a
\emph{renormalised-condensate-energy convention}. The objection is: what
is this convention precisely, and does it contain an arbitrary additive
constant? Specifically, in the thermodynamic free energy
\[
F[\Psi] = \int_V d^3 r \, \mathcal{F}[\Psi(r), \nabla\Psi(r)],
\]
one can always shift $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F} + C$ for any constant $C$
without changing the physics (thermodynamic extrema are preserved). When
TECT extracts the ``BCC condensate energy,'' does it choose a specific
renormalisation point (e.g., $\Psi = 0$ as reference), or does the choice
affect the cosmological constant?
\end{question}

\subsection{Counterargument and clarification}

The condensate energy is defined as follows (standard statistical mechanics):
\[
E_{\mathrm{cond}}[\Psi^*] := \min_{\Psi} F[\Psi] - F[0],
\]
i.e., the energy \emph{difference} between the condensed state $\Psi^*$ and
the reference state $\Psi = 0$. This is unambiguous: the arbitrary
additive constant cancels because we take a difference.

\textbf{In the cosmological context}:
the cosmological constant in GR is defined through the stress-energy tensor
\[
G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G (T_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{matter}} + T_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{vac}}),
\]
where $T^{\mu\nu}_{\mathrm{vac}}$ arises from the vacuum energy density.
In TECT, the vacuum energy density is computed as a field-theory expectation
value in the ground state of the Brazovskii functional. The contribution
to the stress-energy comes from the zero-point energies of the excitation
modes (magnons, phonons, fermions) in the BCC background, not from the
condensation energy $E_{\mathrm{cond}}$ itself.

\textbf{Math58-v5 convention statement}: The argument is that the
\emph{residual vacuum-energy density} (zero-point energies of excitations
in the BCC background) does \emph{not} contribute to $\Lambda_{\mathrm{cosmo}}$
because these energies are absorbed into the definition of the chemical
potential $\mu$ (Fermi-liquid renormalisation). This is a physically
sensible choice: the cosmological constant is reserved for contributions
that do \emph{not} renormalise into single-particle properties.

\textbf{Precise statement}: $\Delta\Lambda_{\mathrm{BCC}} = 0$ means that,
in the renormalisation scheme where excitation zero-point energies shift
the chemical potential, the BCC sector \emph{does not contribute an independent
term to the cosmological constant}. This is not an arbitrary convention but
a physically justified renormalisation choice (analogous to normal ordering
in QFT).

\subsection{Verdict: Q3 VALID (manageable)}

\textbf{Reasoning}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The objection is technically valid: the condensate-energy definition
      \emph{does} rely on a choice of reference state and renormalisation
      scheme.
\item However, the specific choice made in Math58-v5 is \emph{not arbitrary}
      but motivated by standard Fermi-liquid renormalisation: excitation
      energies conventionally shift $\mu$, not $\Lambda$.
\item The cosmological constant is determined by the sector contributions
      that are \emph{not} absorbed into single-particle properties.
\item The scheme choice should be \emph{explicitly stated} in the theorem
      statement (it is, in Math58-v7 Remark 6.3).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Risk assessment}: \texttt{MODERATE}. The convention is physically
reasonable, but a peer reviewer may ask for: (i) explicit comparison with
alternative conventions (e.g., if excitation energies were counted toward
$\Lambda$, what would $\Lambda_{\mathrm{BCC}}$ be?); (ii) a numerical check
that the choice is consistent with the other sectors.

\textbf{Upgrade pathway}: Compute $\Lambda_{\mathrm{BCC}}$ under the
\emph{alternative} convention (excitations contribute to $\Lambda$) and
verify that the total $\Lambda_{\mathrm{total}}$ remains zero when combined
with alternative-scheme Dirac contribution. This would demonstrate
scheme-independence at a meta-level.

\section{Question Q4: SM Anomaly Cancellation and Regularisation Scheme}

\subsection{Objection}

\begin{question}[Scheme Dependence of SM Gauge Anomaly Cancellation]
Pillar 5 establishes that TECT produces a chiral fermion sector. Standard
anomaly-cancellation theory shows that the SM gauge anomalies (QCD
$SU(3)_c^3$, electroweak $SU(2)_L^3 - U(1)^2$, etc.) cancel among the
three generations of quarks and leptons. This cancellation is typically
computed at tree level (index theorem) and verified in one-loop anomaly
calculations. The question is: does the regularisation scheme (PV vs.
dim-reg vs. lattice) affect the anomaly-cancellation verdict?
Specifically, does Math58-v7's use of Pauli-Villars for the Dirac sector
introduce an inconsistency with SM anomaly cancellation?
\end{question}

\subsection{Counterargument}

\textbf{Anomaly-cancellation universality}:
The index theorem (Atiyah-Singer) is a purely topological statement: it
counts zero modes of differential operators without reference to
regularisation. The index is \emph{scheme-independent} by definition.

\textbf{One-loop anomaly calculations}:
In all schemes (PV, dim-reg, lattice, hard cutoff), the one-loop anomaly
is computed by evaluating the triangle diagram and taking the limit
(regulator removed). The result is:
\[
\mathcal{A}^{ABC} = \sum_{\text{fermions}} T^A T^B T^C \, \mathrm{tr}[\text{repr}] + \text{contributions from mirrors}.
\]
This is scheme-independent; different schemes reproduce the same numerical
coefficient. Pauli-Villars is specifically designed so that loop integrals
match dimensional regularisation order-by-order.

\textbf{Relevance to TECT}: The anomaly-cancellation condition in TECT is
a topological/algebraic constraint on the fermion content (Pillars 5, 14,
Math10–Math14). It does not depend on the dynamical regularisation choice
used to compute quantum corrections to the cosmological constant. The two
are \emph{orthogonal concerns}.

\textbf{Explicit coupling check}: Math58-v7 computes $\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}}$
via PV subtraction. This computation is restricted to the zero-point energy
of the Dirac sea, which is a \emph{single-particle} vacuum energy, not a
quantum-correction triangle diagram. The anomaly, by contrast, arises from
\emph{loop diagrams} with gauge-field fluctuations. These are different
computational contexts.

\subsection{Verdict: Q4 VALID (but manageable)}

\textbf{Reasoning}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The objection is technically valid: one can imagine a regularisation
      choice that breaks gauge invariance (e.g., a cutoff scheme that treats
      different helicities differently).
\item However, Pauli-Villars is a gauge-invariant regulator by construction
      (it preserves gauge symmetry explicitly at every step).
\item Anomaly cancellation and vacuum-energy renormalisation are computed
      in different contexts (loop diagrams vs. one-loop zero-point energy).
\item No conflict between PV-regulated $\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}}$ and SM
      anomaly cancellation is known in the literature.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Risk assessment}: \texttt{MODERATE}. The concern is more conceptual
than mathematical: a careful statement that "PV-scheme Dirac closure is
compatible with SM gauge-invariance and anomaly cancellation" should be
added to Math58-v7.

\textbf{Upgrade pathway}: Cite Peskin-Schroeder or Weinberg QFT volume
on Pauli-Villars gauge invariance (standard material), and explicitly
state in Math58-v7 that the anomaly-cancellation computation in Pillars 5
and 14 is independent of the PV choice for $\Lambda_{\mathrm{Dirac}}$.

\section{Question Q5: Numerical Verification}

\subsection{Objection}

\begin{question}[Absence of Numerical Verification]
Questions Q1–Q4 are \emph{analytical} (scheme universality, convention
justification, anomaly compatibility). But Physics also requires
\emph{numerical checks}. The four-sector cancellation $\Lambda_{\mathrm{total}}
= 0$ claimed by Math58-v7 Theorem 5 is an \emph{analytic result}. Has it
been verified numerically on \emph{any} concrete Brazovskii operating point
(e.g., the locked parameters $\mu^2=0.26$, $\lambda=-0.43$, $\gamma=1.62$,
or the updated target $\mu^2=5\times 10^{-3}$)? If not, this is a major
gap: the cancellation could contain a hidden numerical coefficient or
depend on the choice of Brazovskii parameters in a way the analysis misses.
\end{question}

\subsection{Counterargument and honest scope statement}

\textbf{Current status}: Math58-v7 is a \emph{pure-theory note}. It
contains:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Monopole sector: CP conjugation symmetry argument (Math58-v2, v3).
      This is \emph{exact} within the BCC lattice model and does not require
      numerics.
\item Vortex sector: vortex-pair partition and CP conjugation (Math58-v4,
      v4-sublemma-closure). This is \emph{graph-theoretic/combinatorial};
      numerics could verify it but are not necessary.
\item BCC condensate sector: renormalisation-convention statement (Math58-v5).
      This is \emph{scheme-definition}; not a numerical prediction.
\item Dirac sector: Pauli-Villars subtraction theorem (Math58-v7).
      This is \emph{analytic} (Peskin-Schroeder level) and has no tunable
      parameters.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Type of verification needed}:
Numerical closure would consist of extracting the four individual sector
contributions $\{\Lambda_i\}_{i \in \{\mathrm{mon,vor,BCC,Dirac}\}}$ from
a converged Newton solution at a specific Brazovskii point and checking
that they sum to zero (within numerical precision). This requires:

1. A fully converged BCC condensate solution $\Psi^*(\mathbf{r})$ on a
   sufficiently large grid ($N \geq 64^3$ or larger).
2. Methods to extract each sector's vacuum-energy contribution from the
   converged solution (Fock-space decomposition or mode-counting).
3. An explicit accounting of zero-point energies for monopoles, vortices,
   and BCC excitations.

\textbf{Current barrier}: Pillar 1 (mass/spectral gap) is still in
\texttt{SCAFFOLD} status (as of 2026-04-24, per TOE-FACT-SHEET.md). No
fully converged BCC solution is yet available at a regime where $\Lambda_{\min}
> 0$ (stable minimum). Therefore, extracting individual sector contributions
from a run is premature.

\subsection{Verdict: Q5 UNVERIFIED}

\textbf{Reasoning}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The four-sector cancellation is a \emph{symbolic result}: it follows
      from (mostly) symmetry arguments and standard renormalisation theory.
\item Numerical verification is \emph{feasible in principle} but not yet
      executed because the Newton-solver infrastructure does not yet deliver
      a stable BCC minimum in the continuum limit.
\item This is \emph{not a defect in Math58-v7}, but rather an honest scope
      statement: the result is theorem-grade, but external-consistency
      verification awaits Pillar 1 closure.
\item The probability of numerical breakdown (the four sectors not actually
      summing to zero) is low, given the symbolic derivations, but nonzero.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Risk assessment}: \texttt{HIGH} (for complete confidence), but
\texttt{MODERATE} (for internal-consistency purposes) because the symbolic
argument is sound.

\textbf{Upgrade pathway}: Once Pillar 1 delivers a converged BCC solution,
implement a sector-decomposition subroutine that extracts $\Lambda_i$ for
each of $i \in \{\mathrm{monopole, vortex, BCC, Dirac}\}$ and computes
their sum. A single successful run would upgrade Q5 from UNVERIFIED to
VERIFIED.

\textbf{Quantitative target}: $|\Lambda_{\mathrm{total}}| < 10^{-3} \times
\max_i |\Lambda_i|$ (i.e., cancellation to three significant figures).
