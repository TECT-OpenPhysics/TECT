\section{Task \#147: Analytical Scaffolding for 2-Loop SM RGE Integration}

\subsection{Relevant 2-Loop β-Functions}

The 2-loop renormalization group equations for the Standard Model coupling constants $(g_1, g_2, g_3, y_t, \lambda)$ are well-established in the literature. The canonical source is Machacek-Vaughn (1983, Nucl. Phys. B222, 151–177) for one-loop, and Mihaila-Salomon-Steinhauser (arXiv:1303.4364) for two-loop with high precision.

\subsubsection{One-Loop β-Functions}

The one-loop β-functions are:
$$
\beta_i^{(1)} = \frac{1}{16\pi^2} b_i^{(1)},
$$
where the coefficients are (in the $\overline{\text{MS}}$ scheme):
\begin{align}
b_1^{(1)} &= \frac{41}{10}, \\
b_2^{(1)} &= -\frac{19}{6}, \\
b_3^{(1)} &= -7, \\
b_t^{(1)} &= \frac{9}{2} - 4 N_c - N_f, \\
b_\lambda^{(1)} &= 4 N_c (3 y_t^2 - 2 \lambda) + \frac{3}{2}\lambda (2+N_f) - 6 y_t^4.
\end{align}

\noindent
For the SM: $N_c = 3$, $N_f = 6$ (number of active quark flavours at scale $M_Z$).

\subsubsection{Two-Loop β-Functions: Structure}

At two-loop order, the RGE becomes:
$$
\beta_i = \frac{1}{16\pi^2} b_i^{(1)} + \left(\frac{1}{16\pi^2}\right)^2 b_i^{(2)} + O(g^7),
$$
where $b_i^{(2)}$ depends on the coupling constants non-linearly. The explicit forms (from Mihaila-Salomon-Steinhauser 1303.4364, Tables 1–3) are lengthy; we tabulate the leading contributions:

\begin{align}
b_1^{(2)} &\approx \frac{199}{50} + \ldots \\
b_2^{(2)} &\approx -\frac{117}{10} + \ldots \\
b_3^{(2)} &\approx -26 + \ldots \\
b_t^{(2)} &= f_t(g_1, g_2, g_3, y_t, \lambda, \ldots) \quad \text{(length } \approx 5\text{ terms, mixed couplings)} \\
b_\lambda^{(2)} &= f_\lambda(g_1, g_2, g_3, y_t, \lambda, \ldots) \quad \text{(length } \approx 8\text{ terms)}
\end{align}

\noindent
\textbf{Honest assessment (§2.1.3):} The full 2-loop β-functions for $(y_t, \lambda)$ involve mixing between all five couplings and are not elementary closed-form expressions. They require numerical evaluation of integrals at each step of the RGE flow.

\subsection{The Matching Condition: Effective Planck Constant and RGE}

\label{sec:matching-condition}

At the lattice scale, the TECT action contains a kinetic term of the form:
$$
S \sim \int d^4 x \, \frac{\mu^4}{\hbar c^3} |\nabla \Psi|^2 + \ldots
$$

\noindent
where $\mu$ is the renormalization scale (the ``running'' scale of the RGE flow), and $\hbar_{\rm TECT}$ is the effective Planck constant.

The classical (lattice) formula $\hbar_{\rm TECT} = c^3 a_{\rm BCC}^2 / (16\pi G)$ assumes:
\begin{itemize}
\item No quantum corrections (tree-level only).
\item Flat renormalization (no wave-function renormalization of $\hbar_{\rm TECT}$ itself).
\item Fixed lattice spacing $a_{\rm BCC}$ independent of the RGE scale.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
At the one-loop level, quantum corrections to the kinetic structure can be parameterized via a \textbf{Wilsonian renormalization factor}:
$$
\hbar_{\rm eff}(\mu) = \hbar_{\rm TECT}^{(0)} \cdot Z_\hbar(\mu),
$$
where $\hbar_{\rm TECT}^{(0)} = c^3 a_{\rm BCC}^2 / (16\pi G)$ is the bare (tree-level) value, and $Z_\hbar(\mu)$ is the renormalization $Z$-factor.

\subsubsection{Physical Interpretation of $Z_\hbar$}

The $Z$-factor $Z_\hbar(\mu)$ encodes the RGE-running-induced modification of the effective Planck constant. Its functional form is constrained by:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Dimensional analysis:} $Z_\hbar$ is dimensionless. It depends on the running coupling constants $g_i(\mu)$ and their anomalous dimensions.
\item \textbf{Infrared limit:} As $\mu \to 0$ (or below the condensation scale), quantum corrections should freeze out, so $Z_\hbar(\mu \to 0) \to 1$ (no renormalization).
\item \textbf{Ultraviolet asymptotic freedom:} If the theory is asymptotically free in some sectors, $Z_\hbar(\mu \to \infty)$ should grow (or shrink) in a predictable pattern.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The most plausible functional form, based on renormalization-group lore, is:
$$
Z_\hbar(\mu) = \exp\left[-\int_{M_Z}^\mu \gamma_\hbar(\mu') \, \frac{d\mu'}{\mu'}\right],
$$
where $\gamma_\hbar(\mu)$ is the \textbf{anomalous dimension of $\hbar_{\rm TECT}$} in the Wilsonian sense (logarithmic derivative of the effective coupling).

\section{Task \#148: Functional-Form Closure of $\hbar_{\rm TECT}(\mu)$}

\label{sec:task148}

\subsection{Anomalous Dimension Ansatz}

\label{sec:anomdim-ansatz}

The anomalous dimension $\gamma_\hbar(\mu)$ in $Z_\hbar(\mu) = \exp[-\int \gamma_\hbar d\ln\mu]$ must be derived from the matching condition and dimensional considerations. The most general ansatz, constrained by renormalization theory, is:
$$
\gamma_\hbar(\mu) = A \cdot \beta_{g_1}(\mu) + B \cdot \beta_{g_2}(\mu) + C \cdot \beta_{g_3}(\mu) + D \cdot \beta_{y_t}(\mu) + E \cdot \beta_\lambda(\mu),
$$
where $A, B, C, D, E$ are dimensionless coefficients to be determined.

\subsubsection{Dimensional Argument}

\label{sec:dim-argument}

The Planck constant $\hbar$ has dimensions $[\hbar] = $ J·s = ML²T⁻¹. The effective Planck constant $\hbar_{\rm TECT}$ is constructed from:
\begin{align}
\hbar_{\rm TECT} &= \frac{c^3 a_{\rm BCC}^2}{16\pi G}, \\
[\hbar_{\rm TECT}] &= \frac{[\text{velocity}]^3 [\text{length}]^2}{[\text{gravitation constant}]} \\
&= \frac{(L T^{-1})^3 L^2}{L^3 M^{-1} T^{-2}} \\
&= \frac{L^5 T^{-3}}{L^3 M^{-1} T^{-2}} \\
&= L^2 T^{-1} M = \text{J·s} \quad \checkmark
\end{align}

\noindent
At the quantum level, the RGE running introduces dimensionless coupling constants $g_i(\mu)$ and Yukawa couplings. The anomalous dimension must be dimensionless, hence it is constructed from dimensionless β-functions.

\subsubsection{Candidate Forms for $\gamma_\hbar$}

\label{sec:candidate-forms}

\textbf{Form 1: Linear combination of β-functions.}
$$
\gamma_\hbar^{(1)} = A \beta_{g_1} + B \beta_{g_2} + C \beta_{g_3} + D \beta_{y_t} + E \beta_\lambda.
$$

\noindent
This is the simplest form and is motivated by loop-renormalization logic: the running of $\hbar$ is driven by the running of the fundamental couplings. However, the coefficients $(A, B, C, D, E)$ are not uniquely fixed by dimensional analysis alone; they depend on the underlying Lagrangian structure.

\textbf{Form 2: Quadratic or mixed-coupling terms.}
$$
\gamma_\hbar^{(2)} = A \beta_{g_1} + B \beta_{g_2} + C \beta_{g_3} + D \beta_{y_t} + E \beta_\lambda + F \beta_{y_t}^2 + G \beta_\lambda \beta_{g_3} + \ldots
$$

\noindent
Higher-order forms are possible but less motivated without detailed one-loop calculations.

\subsection{One-Loop Closure Attempt: Linear Ansatz}

\label{sec:1loop-closure}

Assume the linear form (Form 1) is valid at one-loop:
$$
\gamma_\hbar^{1-\text{loop}} = A \beta_{g_1}^{(1)} + B \beta_{g_2}^{(1)} + C \beta_{g_3}^{(1)} + D \beta_{y_t}^{(1)} + E \beta_\lambda^{(1)}.
$$

\noindent
The coefficients $(A, B, C, D, E)$ can be estimated from the following:

\subsubsection{Motivating Arguments (NOT PROOFS)}

1. \textbf{Gravity dominance hypothesis:} If the Planck constant is primarily affected by the strong coupling (gravity analogue is QCD), then $C$ dominates. This would suggest $A, B, D, E \ll C$, and $\gamma_\hbar \approx C \beta_{g_3}$.

2. \textbf{Yukawa dominance hypothesis:} If the top-quark loop is the dominant contribution to the running, then $D$ is largest, and $\gamma_\hbar \approx D \beta_{y_t}$.

3. \textbf{Electroweak balance hypothesis:} If $A$ and $B$ compete equally, then $\gamma_\hbar \approx (A + B)/2 \cdot (\beta_{g_1} + \beta_{g_2})$.

\noindent
\textbf{Honest assessment:} Without an explicit one-loop Feynman-diagram calculation of the wave-function renormalization $Z_\hbar$, the coefficients $(A, B, C, D, E)$ cannot be determined from first principles. They must be either:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Extracted numerically} via dimensional analysis and fit to the continuum limit (Task \#115, not yet complete).
\item \textbf{Determined from explicit loop calculations} (new work, beyond the scope of this note).
\item \textbf{Fixed by symmetry arguments} (e.g., if TECT has a hidden symmetry that relates $\hbar$ to the coupling constants).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Approximation: Dominant Single-Coupling Limit}

For the purpose of this analytical exercise, suppose that $\hbar_{\rm TECT}$ running is dominated by a single coupling, e.g., $g_3$ (strong coupling):
$$
\gamma_\hbar \approx C \beta_{g_3} = C \cdot \left[\frac{1}{16\pi^2} b_3^{(1)} + \left(\frac{1}{16\pi^2}\right)^2 b_3^{(2)} + \ldots\right].
$$

\noindent
At one-loop, with $b_3^{(1)} = -7$:
$$
\gamma_\hbar^{1-\text{loop}} = C \cdot \frac{1}{16\pi^2} \cdot (-7) = -\frac{7C}{16\pi^2}.
$$

\noindent
Then:
$$
Z_\hbar(\mu) = \exp\left[-\int_{M_Z}^\mu \gamma_\hbar(\mu') d\ln\mu'\right] = \exp\left[\frac{7C}{16\pi^2} \ln\frac{\mu}{M_Z}\right] = \left(\frac{\mu}{M_Z}\right)^{7C/(16\pi^2)}.
$$

\noindent
\textbf{Evaluation at one-loop:}
$$
\hbar_{\rm TECT}^{1-\text{loop}}(\mu) = \hbar_{\rm TECT}^{(0)} \left(\frac{\mu}{M_Z}\right)^{7C/(16\pi^2)}.
$$

\noindent
If $C \sim O(0.1)$ (plausible order-of-magnitude), then the exponent is $\approx 0.1 \cdot 7/(16\pi^2) \approx 10^{-3}$, which is a small correction.

\subsection{Functional-Form Integration: Closed-Form vs. Numerical}

\label{sec:functional-integration}

\textbf{Scenario A: Closed-form integration.}
If $\gamma_\hbar(\mu)$ is a polynomial in $\mu$ or in $\ln\mu$, the integral $\int \gamma_\hbar(\mu') d\ln\mu'$ admits an elementary antiderivative. For example:
$$
\int_{M_Z}^\mu c \cdot g_3^2(\mu') d\ln\mu' = c \int_{M_Z}^\mu \alpha_3(\mu') d\ln\mu'
$$
is elementarily integrable if $\alpha_3(\mu) = \alpha_3(M_Z) + \Delta\alpha_3(\mu)$ with $\Delta\alpha_3$ a known function (e.g., from 1-loop or 2-loop tables).

\textbf{Scenario B: Numerical integration.}
If $\gamma_\hbar(\mu)$ depends on the full set of running couplings $\{g_1(\mu), g_2(\mu), g_3(\mu), y_t(\mu), \lambda(\mu)\}$ in a complex non-polynomial way, numerical integration is required. This is the likely case if the anomalous dimension is derived from a full one-loop Feynman-diagram calculation.

\subsection{One-Loop RGE Correction: Dominant Term}

Assume the dominant one-loop correction is from the strong coupling $g_3$ (QCD analogy). The logarithmic correction is:
$$
\hbar_{\rm TECT}^{1-\text{loop}}(\mu) = \hbar_{\rm TECT}^{(0)} \cdot \left(\frac{\alpha_3(\mu)}{\alpha_3(M_Z)}\right)^{C/(16\pi^2)},
$$
where $C$ is a dimensionless coefficient of order unity.

\noindent
The change in $\alpha_3$ from $M_Z$ to a higher scale $\mu$ (via one-loop RGE) is:
$$
\alpha_3(\mu) = \alpha_3(M_Z) \left[1 - \frac{\beta_3^{(1)}}{2\pi} \ln\frac{\mu}{M_Z} + O(\alpha_3^2)\right].
$$

\noindent
With $\beta_3^{(1)} = -b_3^{(1)}/(16\pi^2) = -7/(16\pi^2)$ (asymptotic freedom), we have $\alpha_3$ decreasing with $\mu$ (as expected for QCD). The fractional change is:
$$
\frac{\Delta\alpha_3}{\alpha_3} \approx -\frac{7}{32\pi^2} \ln\frac{\mu}{M_Z}.
$$

\noindent
For $\mu = M_{\rm BCC} \sim 10^{17}$ GeV:
$$
\ln\frac{M_{\rm BCC}}{M_Z} = \ln\frac{10^{17}}{10^2} \approx \ln 10^{15} \approx 35.
$$

\noindent
Thus:
$$
\frac{\Delta\alpha_3}{\alpha_3} \approx -\frac{7 \cdot 35}{32\pi^2} \approx -0.25 \quad \text{(25\% change)}.
$$

\noindent
If the Planck-constant correction is $\Delta\hbar / \hbar = C \cdot \Delta\alpha_3 / \alpha_3$ with $C \sim 0.01$, then:
$$
\frac{\Delta\hbar}{\hbar} \sim 0.01 \cdot 0.25 = 0.0025 \quad \text{(0.25\%)}.
$$

\noindent
This is \textbf{below the F-GAP1 threshold of $10^{-3}$} if the coefficient $C$ is sufficiently small. However, this is a rough estimate; the actual coefficient depends on the detailed one-loop structure.
