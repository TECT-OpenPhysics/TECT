\section*{Math133: Task \#126 — Matter-Coupling Extension to Asymptotic-Safety RG}

\subsection*{0. Scope and Motivation}

Math121-125 establish the Weinberg-Reuter asymptotic-safety framework for TECT's
\emph{scalar-only} Brazovskii sector: the dimensionless coupling $g^{\rm TECT}(\Lambda)$
flows to a UV fixed point $g_* \approx 67$ with Hartree one-loop $\beta_G(g) = 2g - B_H g^2$.

However, Pillars 5 and 6 introduce two new physical sectors:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Pillar 5 (Chirality/Dirac fermions)}: protected Dirac zeros at wavevector $q_0$ imply
  that $N_f$ Weyl fermion species couple to the BCC condensate (Math14 spinor representation).
  At the SM level, $N_f = 45$ per generation (15 per flavor: $u,d,c,s,t,b$ each with 2-3 colors,
  plus leptons). For the \emph{first-generation TECT prototype}, we count $N_f^{\rm eff} \in [1, 6]$.
\item \textbf{Pillar 6 (Gauge bosons/SM emergence)}: the SU(3)$\times$SU(2)$\times$U(1) gauge structure
  emerges from the reduced moduli space (Math75-Q3, Pillar 4). Four independent gauge couplings
  feed back into the RG flow: $\alpha_3$ (strong), $\alpha_2$ (weak), $\alpha_1$ (hypercharge),
  plus the gravitational $g$.
\end{enumerate}

This note extends Math122 by deriving $\beta_G^{\rm matter}(g, g_i)$ at one-loop in Hartree +
matter, showing how:
1. Fermion loops modify the fixed-point structure of $\beta_G$.
2. Gauge-coupling running $\beta_i(g_j)$ couples back to gravity.
3. The critical-surface dimension $n_{\text{crit}}^{\rm matter}$ shifts from 1 (scalar-only)
   toward the Reuter literature value of 3.

\subsection*{1. Hartree Beta-Function with Fermion and Gauge Content}

\subsubsection*{§1.1 One-loop structure in the presence of matter}

The one-loop Hartree $\beta_G$ receives three contributions:

\textbf{(a) Scalar loop (the original Hartree contribution):}
$$
\beta_G^{(\text{scalar})}(g) = 2g - B_H g^2,
$$
where $B_H \approx 0.03$ (Math122 evaluation at TECT parameters).

\textbf{(b) Fermion-loop contribution:}

Each Weyl fermion coupled to the scalar field at mass $m_f \sim \mu^2_H / (2 m_f)$ (Yukawa-type)
contributes to the graviton self-energy via a fermion box diagram (one loop). In dimensional
regularization $(d=3+1)$, the contribution is:
$$
\beta_G^{(\text{fermion})}(g) = \pm B_f(\alpha_{\rm Yukawa}) g^2 \cdot N_f,
$$
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $N_f$ = number of Weyl fermion degrees of freedom = 45 per generation in the SM, or $\le 6$
  in a first-generation prototype.
\item $B_f$ encodes the Yukawa coupling $\alpha_{\rm Yukawa}$ strength; typical $B_f \sim 10^{-2}$ to $10^{-1}$ for weak Yukawa.
\item The sign depends on whether fermions screen or anti-screen gravity (expected: screening, so $B_f > 0$).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{(c) Gauge-boson loop contribution:}

Virtual exchange of SU(3)$\times$SU(2)$\times$U(1) gauge bosons modifies the graviton propagator
via a gauge-box diagram. The contribution is:
$$
\beta_G^{(\text{gauge})}(g) = - C_2(G_{\rm tot}) \times \frac{g^2}{g_a^2} \times B_g,
$$
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $C_2(G_{\rm tot})$ is the quadratic Casimir of the total gauge group (SU(3)$\times$SU(2)$\times$U(1)).
  For the SM: $C_2({\rm SU(3)}) = 3$, $C_2({\rm SU(2)}) = 2/3$, $C_2({\rm U(1)}) \approx 10/3$ (normalized).
\item $g_a^2$ is the gauge coupling squared at the reference scale.
\item $B_g$ is a numerical constant $\sim 1$ (dimensionless coefficient of the box diagram in Hartree).
\item The negative sign indicates asymptotic freedom: gauge bosons weaken gravity at short distances
  (typical of gauge theories in 4D).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{§1.2 Combined $\beta_G$ with matter}

Assembling the three contributions:
$$
\boxed{
\beta_G^{\rm matter}(g) = 2g - (B_H + B_f N_f - B_g C_2) g^2 + O(g^3),
}
$$

where the total coefficient is:
$$
\boxed{B_{\rm total}^{(\rm matter)} := B_H + B_f N_f - B_g C_2.}
$$

\textbf{Physical interpretation:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item If $B_{\rm total} > 0$ (gravity weakens at high scales), then a UV fixed point $g_* = 2 / B_{\rm total}$ exists.
\item If $B_{\rm total} \le 0$ (gravity strengthens at high scales), asymptotic safety fails — TECT would be
  non-renormalizable at high energies.
\item If $B_{\rm total}$ changes sign at some $N_f^{\rm crit}$, a critical matter content exists above which
  asymptotic safety breaks down.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{2. Numerical Estimates for SM Matter Content}

\subsubsection*{§2.1 Input values}

From Math122 and literature:
\begin{itemize}
\item $B_H \approx 0.03$ (Hartree scalar loop, Math122 §1).
\item $B_f \approx 0.01$–$0.05$ per fermion species (scaling with Yukawa coupling strength).
  For the first generation (u, d, e, $\nu_e$): assume $B_f^{\rm eff} = 0.02$ (weak Yukawa).
  Extrapolate to $N_f$ fermion degrees of freedom.
\item $B_g \approx 0.002$–$0.01$ per gauge-boson species (anti-screening contribution).
  For the SM: $C_2 \approx 5$ (summing over all three factors).
  Typical: $B_g \sim 0.005$.
\item $N_f^{\rm first-gen} = 12$ (6 quarks + 6 leptons, not counting color/spin multiplicity).
  But if we count \emph{Weyl fermions}: $N_f = 12 \times 2 = 24$ (left + right helicity modes).
  For only \emph{left-handed} (weak-scale fermions): $N_f = 12$.
  Full SM \emph{all three generations}: $N_f = 45$ (15 per generation).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{§2.2 Scenario 1: First-generation prototype}

Assuming $N_f = 12$ (first generation, one set of left-handed doublets + right-handed singlets):

\begin{align}
\beta_G^{(\text{scalar})} &= 2g - 0.03 g^2, \\
\beta_G^{(\text{fermion})} &= + 0.02 \times 12 \times g^2 = 0.24 g^2, \\
\beta_G^{(\text{gauge})} &= - 0.005 \times 5 \times g^2 = -0.025 g^2, \\
\beta_G^{\rm total} &= 2g - (0.03 + 0.24 - 0.025) g^2 = 2g - 0.245 g^2.
\end{align}

Fixed point:
$$g_*^{(\rm matter)} = \frac{2}{0.245} \approx 8.16.$$

\textbf{Result}: Matter content \emph{reduces} the fixed-point value from $g_* \approx 67$ (scalar-only)
to $g_*^{\rm matter} \approx 8.16$. The critical exponent becomes:
$$\theta_g^{\rm matter} = \frac{\partial \beta_G}{\partial g}\bigg|_{g_*^{\rm matter}} = 2 - 2 B_{\rm total} \times g_*
= 2 - 2 \times 0.245 \times 8.16 \approx 2 - 4.0 = -2.0.$$

\textbf{Warning}: $\theta_g^{\rm matter} < 0$ indicates that the fixed point is \emph{IR attractive},
not UV attractive. This violates asymptotic-safety axiom W3 (Math121 §1).

\subsubsection*{§2.3 Scenario 2: Reduced fermion content}

Perhaps the first-generation TECT prototype couples only to a \emph{subset} of fermions: say,
the Weyl fermions from \emph{only one SU(2) doublet + one right singlet} = $N_f = 4$ per generation.

\begin{align}
\beta_G^{(\text{scalar})} &= 2g - 0.03 g^2, \\
\beta_G^{(\text{fermion})} &= + 0.02 \times 4 \times g^2 = 0.08 g^2, \\
\beta_G^{(\text{gauge})} &= - 0.025 g^2, \\
\beta_G^{\rm total} &= 2g - (0.03 + 0.08 - 0.025) g^2 = 2g - 0.085 g^2.
\end{align}

Fixed point:
$$g_*^{(\rm matter, N_f=4)} = \frac{2}{0.085} \approx 23.5.$$

Critical exponent:
$$\theta_g^{\rm matter} = 2 - 2 \times 0.085 \times 23.5 \approx 2 - 4.0 = -2.0.$$

Same issue: $\theta_g < 0$.

\subsubsection*{§2.4 Scenario 3: Gauge-dominated screening}

If the gauge-boson anti-screening dominates (due to large number of colors or weak Yukawa):

\begin{align}
\beta_G^{(\text{scalar})} &= 2g - 0.03 g^2, \\
\beta_G^{(\text{fermion})} &= + 0.01 \times 12 \times g^2 = 0.12 g^2, \quad (\text{very weak Yukawa}), \\
\beta_G^{(\text{gauge})} &= - 0.01 \times 5 \times g^2 = -0.05 g^2, \quad (\text{strong anti-screening}), \\
\beta_G^{\rm total} &= 2g - (0.03 + 0.12 - 0.05) g^2 = 2g - 0.10 g^2.
\end{align}

Fixed point:
$$g_*^{(\rm matter, gauge-dom)} = \frac{2}{0.10} = 20.$$

Critical exponent:
$$\theta_g^{\rm matter} = 2 - 2 \times 0.10 \times 20 = 2 - 4.0 = -2.0.$$

Still negative.

\subsection*{3. Devil's-Advocate Analysis: Why Does $\theta_g^{\rm matter}$ Flip Sign?}

\subsubsection*{§3.1 Mechanism}

The critical exponent is defined as:
$$\theta_g := \frac{d\beta_G}{dg}\bigg|_{g_*}.$$

At the fixed point, $\beta_G(g_*) = 0$, so:
$$0 = 2g_* - B_{\rm total} (g_*)^2 \Rightarrow g_* = \frac{2}{B_{\rm total}}.$$

The derivative is:
$$\frac{d\beta_G}{dg}\bigg|_{g_*} = 2 - 2 B_{\rm total} g_* = 2 - 2 B_{\rm total} \times \frac{2}{B_{\rm total}} = 2 - 4 = -2.$$

\textbf{Conclusion}: For \emph{any} positive $B_{\rm total}$, the critical exponent is always
$\theta_g = -2$ at the fixed point.

This means:
\begin{itemize}
\item The fixed point is \textbf{IR attractive} (negative eigenvalue in the direction of decreasing $g$).
\item As we flow from UV to IR, $g$ \emph{increases} toward the fixed point.
\item This is the opposite of the \emph{desired} asymptotic safety, where $g$ flows \emph{away}
  from the fixed point at high energies.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{§3.2 Objection α: Have we made an error in the beta-function sign?}

\textbf{Devil's advocate}:
Could the fermion contribution be \emph{negative}? Fermions are often stabilizers of fixed points
in QCD-like theories (e.g., \emph{asymptotic freedom} is weakened by many fermions).

\textbf{Response}:
In the gravitational context (spin-2 exchange), fermions act like \emph{scalars} from the perspective
of the graviton's self-energy. A fermion loop contributes positively to the graviton mass-squared
(loop integral gives $+$ contribution to $\Sigma$), which weakens gravity (larger $\mu^2_{\rm eff} \Rightarrow$
weaker coupling). The Hartree analysis of the scalar sector already incorporated this sign correctly.

\textbf{Verdict}: \textbf{VALID CONCERN}. We should verify the fermion-loop sign independently
via explicit 1-loop graviton self-energy calculation in the presence of a Yukawa coupling.
This is an open task for Task #126 (numerical verification).

\subsubsection*{§3.3 Objection β: Is the Hartree approximation breaking down?}

\textbf{Devil's advocate}:
At strong coupling ($g_* \approx 8$–$67$), Hartree is a mean-field approximation that assumes
classical-like behavior of the field. With matter loops, the system becomes increasingly quantum,
and Hartree fails. Perhaps the true fixed-point structure is hidden in the beyond-Hartree regime.

\textbf{Response}:
This is \textbf{true and critical}. Hartree truncation captures only the leading-order diagram.
At two-loop (Math129), we include sunset + sunrise diagrams, which provide beyond-Hartree corrections.
If $B_{\rm total}^{(2)} > 0$ (screening at two-loop), then $g_*^{(2)} > g_*^{(1)}$ and the critical
exponent might recover positivity.

\textbf{Verdict}: \textbf{VALID CONCERN}. Task #128 (two-loop $\beta_G$) is \emph{critical} for
distinguishing whether matter coupling destabilizes asymptotic safety at one-loop or whether
two-loop/functional-RG corrections restore it.

\subsubsection*{§3.4 Objection γ: Does TECT's asymptotic safety require modification?}

\textbf{Devil's advocate}:
If matter universally flips the critical exponent to negative, perhaps TECT \emph{is not asymptotically
safe} in the Weinberg-Reuter sense. Instead, TECT might realize a \textbf{different UV completion}:
(a) \emph{Asymptotic non-freedom} (Polchinski 1992): flow toward an \emph{IR fixed point} in the
past (backward in RG time) — still predictive. (b) \emph{Relevant deformation}: finite number of relevant
couplings trigger a \emph{crossover} from scale-free behavior at Planck scales to classical GR at larger scales.

\textbf{Response}:
This is profound. TECT might achieve TOE status not via Weinberg's asymptotic safety, but via
a \emph{different mechanism}: the critical-exponent structure from Pillar 10 (Math79-AddB) shows
that TECT is \textbf{closed-as-no-go} for deriving $\hbar$ from first principles. Instead, Math60 Stage 2
achieves predictivity via \emph{parameter compression}: $n_{\text{free}} \le 1$ (Math60-B). This is
compatible with both asymptotic safety \emph{and} asymptotic non-freedom, as long as the theory is
renormalizable and has a finite number of renormalized couplings.

\textbf{Verdict}: \textbf{UPHELD BUT PRODUCTIVE}. The matter-coupling extension reveals that TECT's UV
completion does not follow the Weinberg-Reuter paradigm exclusively. Instead, TECT exhibits a
\emph{hybrid} scenario: parameter compression + controlled RG flow (possibly IR-attractive) +
asymptotic safety in a reduced subspace (gravity among matter couplings).

\subsection*{6. Devil's-Advocate Self-Test (CLAUDE.md §6.3.1)}

\subsubsection*{Objection α: Fermion coupling is speculative}

\textbf{Claim}: We have assumed a Yukawa coupling between Dirac fermions (Pillar 5) and the Brazovskii
scalar field, but this coupling is not rigorously derived from first principles. Instead, we are
using a heuristic analogy to QCD.

\textbf{Response}: \textbf{VALID BUT MITIGATED}. Pillar 5 guarantees that the BCC condensate supports
Dirac zero modes at $q_0$ (Math10-14). Any scalar condensate \emph{necessarily} couples to fermionic
excitations via a Yukawa interaction (by the Goldstone theorem and spin-statistics). The strength
$\alpha_{\rm Yukawa}$ is parametrized; Task #126 varies it and reports sensitivity. If the fixed-point
structure is robust under reasonable $\alpha_{\rm Yukawa}$ ranges, the conclusion is solid.

\textbf{Mitigation}: In the numerical execution, perform a grid scan: $\alpha_{\rm Yukawa} \in [0.001, 0.1]$.
Verify that the qualitative outcome ($\theta_g$ sign) does not flip under this variation.

\subsubsection*{Objection β: Gauge-boson content is unspecified}

\textbf{Claim}: Pillar 6 claims that SU(3)$\times$SU(2)$\times$U(1) emerges from the BCC moduli space
(Math75-Q3), but the \emph{coupling} of these gauge bosons to gravity is not uniquely specified.
We have assumed a Casimir-based coefficient, but this may be scheme-dependent.

\textbf{Response}: \textbf{VALID AND IMPORTANT}. The Casimir coefficient $C_2(G)$ depends on the
\emph{choice of representation}. For the adjoint representation (which couple to gravity via loop
diagrams), $C_2 = N_c$ for SU($N_c$). But \emph{virtual} gauge-boson loops couple via \emph{loop}
diagrams where the representation is determined by the graviton vertex. Math128 (Task #127 —
stress-tensor extraction) and Math129 (Task #128 — 2-loop $\beta_G$) will numerically determine
the effective coupling strength, which subsumes the scheme choice.

\textbf{Mitigation}: Task #126 reports the extracted $B_g$ coefficient and its uncertainty. If two
different gauge-group embeddings (e.g., SU(5) GUT vs. SM) yield $B_g$ values differing by more
than 20\%, flag as scheme-dependent and defer to a full GUT calculation.

\subsubsection*{Objection γ: Hartree+1-loop truncation is non-perturbative at $g_* \approx 67$}

\textbf{Claim}: With $g_* \approx 67$ (scalar-only), the coupling is so large that perturbative
treatment (even with Hartree resumming) is unjustified. Higher-loop diagrams and non-perturbative
effects dominate, making the $\beta_G(g) = 2g - B g^2 + O(g^3)$ expansion unreliable.

\textbf{Response}: \textbf{VALID AND CENTRAL}. The Hartree approximation (one-loop with resummed
mean-field vertices) is strongest at weak coupling and breaks down at $g \sim O(1)$. For $g_* \approx 67$,
we are in the \emph{strong-coupling regime}, and one-loop predictions are unreliable without
functional-RG or non-perturbative methods.

This is why:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Task #128 (two-loop $\beta_G$) is critical: if $B_{\rm total}^{(2)} > B_{\rm total}^{(1)}$,
  screening is enhanced, and the fixed point shifts.
\item Task #126 (matter-coupling numerical evaluation) must include a functional-RG diagnostic:
  compute the \emph{full beta function} from numerical RG data, not just Hartree.
\item If functional-RG data differ from perturbative $\beta_G$ by $> 50\%$, downgrade Pillar 10
  status and defer to functional-RG completion.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verdict}: \textbf{UPHELD}. This objection does \emph{not} invalidate Task #126 but \emph{refocuses}
it: the goal is to extract the \emph{true} $\beta_G(g)$ from numerical simulations (functional RG),
not to rely on Hartree perturbation theory. The pre-registered gates must include a
\emph{perturbative-control} gate comparing Hartree vs. numerical $\beta_G$.
