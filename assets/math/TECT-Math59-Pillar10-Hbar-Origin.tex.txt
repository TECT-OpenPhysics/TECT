\begin{abstract}
This note attempts to derive Planck's constant $\hbar$ (or equivalently, the commutation relation
$[\hat{\Psi}, \hat{\Pi}] = i\hbar$) from the classical free-energy functional of the BCC condensate.
We examine four standard routes — canonical quantization of lattice displacements, zero-point
fluctuations, Berry-phase topological quantization, defect-core quantum mechanics — and prove
\emph{individually} that each of them fails to fix the quantization scale from the classical data alone.
All four obstructions share a common structural origin: TECT's classical phase space carries a
\emph{standard symplectic 2-form} $\omega$, and $\omega$ determines its Poisson algebra only up to
an overall multiplicative scale.

\emph{General statement (Conjecture~\ref{conj:higher-form-barrier}, v1.1):}
\emph{No} classical-field-theoretic derivation of $\hbar$ within TECT's current phase-space structure
can succeed without adjoining either (i) a dynamical principle coupling microscopic scales
$(a,q_0)$ to the symplectic scale, or (ii) a higher-form ($k$-plectic with $k\ge 2$ in the Baez-Hoffnung
convention) geometric structure. This statement is held as a \textbf{conjecture}, not a theorem —
it is motivated by the four case studies below and by well-established folklore on symplectic vs.\
quantum algebraic structure, but no no-go theorem over the category of all classical field theories
admitting TECT's BCC condensate as a solution is provided here.

Pillar 10 is therefore recorded as \textbf{OPEN-NEGATIVE}: four specific routes closed by
individual lemmas/theorems; the general impossibility claim open as a conjecture. This is an honest
and productive negative result — it identifies the precise algebraic extension TECT would need to
become a complete TOE without committing to a no-go claim the note does not prove.
\end{abstract}

\begin{center}\footnotesize\textit{Version note (2026-04-21, v1.1).} In v1, the classical phase space was
described as ``2-plectic''. This is non-standard: under the Baez-Hoffnung convention an $n$-plectic
structure is a closed non-degenerate $(n+1)$-form, so a standard symplectic 2-form is ``1-plectic''.
Throughout this note ``symplectic 2-form'' means the standard Hamiltonian symplectic structure (a closed
non-degenerate bilinear form). Where the Baez-Hoffnung convention matters (e.g., in the conjecture that
``higher-form'' structure is required) we write ``$k$-plectic with $k\ge 2$'' explicitly.\end{center}

\section{Prologue: What is Pillar 10?}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{The problem statement}

A Theory of Everything must explain the value of every fundamental constant, including Planck's constant
$\hbar$. In TECT's framework:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \emph{classical} level (Pillars 1–9) derives physics from the Brazovskii free energy
        $\mathcal{F}[\Psi]$, a real-valued functional on the complex order parameter $\Psi(\mathbf{x})$.
  \item The \emph{quantum} level (loop calculations, path integrals, one-loop corrections) introduces
        $\hbar$ externally, typically in the measure $\mathcal{D}\Psi \sim e^{iS/\hbar}$.
\end{itemize}

Pillar 10 asks: \textbf{Can $\hbar$ be derived from $\mathcal{F}[\Psi]$ alone?}

In other words: is there a classical structure in the BCC lattice dynamics such that, when quantized in
a natural way, it yields the observed value $\hbar \approx 1.055 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s?

\subsection{Why this is hard}

In condensed-matter physics, quantum effects (e.g., superfluidity, superconductivity) emerge from
classical lattice interactions. The quantization parameter $\hbar$ is \emph{inserted by hand}; its value
is not derived. To derive $\hbar$ from first principles, one would need to show that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The classical action $S[\Psi]$ has a hidden mathematical symmetry that \emph{forces}
        quantization.
  \item The quantization is not a choice (deformation quantization, geometric quantization) but
        an \emph{inevitable consequence} of the underlying structure.
  \item The value of the quantization parameter (what we call $\hbar$) is determined by the
        microscopic scales of the problem (lattice spacing $a$, shell momentum $q_0$).
\end{enumerate}

No known framework has achieved this. Planck's constant is understood as a \emph{conversion factor}
between classical action (dimensions energy × time) and the dimensionless phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$ in
the quantum path integral. The value $\hbar \approx 10^{-34}$ is \emph{measured}, not derived.

\section{Attempt 1: Canonical quantization of lattice displacements}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Lattice Hamiltonian and Poisson bracket structure}

The BCC condensate classically occupies the first shell of reciprocal-lattice vectors:
\begin{equation}
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}) = A \sum_{\mathbf{G} \in S_{\mathrm{BCC}}} z_0^a e^{i\mathbf{G} \cdot \mathbf{x}},
  \quad a = 1, 2, 3.
  \label{eq:BCC-ansatz}
\end{equation}

Each mode has a classical equation of motion from the free-energy functional:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \Psi_\mathbf{G}}{\partial t} = -\frac{\delta \mathcal{F}}{\delta \Psi_\mathbf{G}^*},
  \label{eq:eom-classical}
\end{equation}

This is a deterministic (real-valued) dynamics. At the classical level, the phase space
consists of pairs $(\Psi_\mathbf{G}, \dot{\Psi}_\mathbf{G})$ for each shell mode $\mathbf{G}$.

The classical phase space is \emph{2-dimensional per mode}: one complex field $\Psi_\mathbf{G}$ has two
real degrees of freedom. The dynamical equations define a flow on this 2D phase space. On any 2D manifold,
there exists a unique 2-form symplectic structure (up to scaling) compatible with the flow, given by
\begin{equation}
  \omega = d\Psi_\mathbf{G} \wedge d\Psi_\mathbf{G}^*,
  \label{eq:2form-symplectic}
\end{equation}

or in terms of real and imaginary parts $\Psi_\mathbf{G} = q + ip$:
\begin{equation}
  \omega = dq \wedge dp.
  \label{eq:2form-real}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Poisson brackets from the 2-form}

From the 2-form $\omega$, the classical Poisson bracket is defined by
\begin{equation}
  \{f, g\} = \omega^{-1}(df, dg),
  \label{eq:poisson-from-2form}
\end{equation}

For the standard 2-form $\omega = dq \wedge dp$, this gives the canonical Poisson bracket:
\begin{equation}
  \{q, p\} = 1, \quad \{q, q\} = 0, \quad \{p, p\} = 0.
  \label{eq:canonical-poisson}
\end{equation}

This is \emph{classical} Hamiltonian mechanics. The Poisson bracket satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Bilinearity}: $\{af + bg, c\} = a\{f, c\} + b\{g, c\}$.
  \item \textbf{Antisymmetry}: $\{f, g\} = -\{g, f\}$.
  \item \textbf{Jacobi identity}: $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0$.
  \item \textbf{Leibniz rule}: $\{f, gh\} = \{f, g\}h + g\{f, h\}$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why canonical quantization does not work}

In the standard canonical quantization program, one \emph{replaces} the Poisson bracket with a
commutator scaled by $i\hbar$:
\begin{equation}
  \{f, g\}_{\text{classical}} \to \frac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat{f}, \hat{g}]_{\text{quantum}}.
  \label{eq:canonical-quantization}
\end{equation}

For the canonical pair $(q, p)$, this gives:
\begin{equation}
  \{q, p\}_{\text{classical}} = 1
  \quad \Rightarrow \quad
  [\hat{q}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar.
  \label{eq:canonical-example}
\end{equation}

\textbf{The problem:} This is a \emph{rule}, not a derivation. The value of $\hbar$ is \emph{input}, not output.
We have simply renamed $\{q, p\} = 1$ as the ``canonical'' value and then inserted $\hbar$ by fiat.

\begin{theorem}[Scale ambiguity of the symplectic-to-quantum map]
\label{thm:symplectic-scale-ambiguity}
Let $M$ be a 2D real symplectic manifold (equivalently, one complex field per mode) with symplectic
2-form $\omega$. Any nonzero rescaling $\omega \to \lambda\,\omega$ with $\lambda>0$ is compatible with
the Hamiltonian flow of any $C^\infty$ energy functional. Under the canonical quantization rule
$\{f,g\}_\omega\mapsto (i\hbar)^{-1}[\hat f,\hat g]$, the commutator scale is $[\hat q,\hat p]=i\lambda$:
the value $\lambda$ is a free input and cannot be fixed from $(M,\omega)$ alone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Not the full ``non-derivability'' claim]
\label{rem:scale-vs-nonderivability}
Theorem~\ref{thm:symplectic-scale-ambiguity} is an elementary and rigorous statement. It proves
exactly the narrow claim that a bare 2D symplectic form does not pin the scale of the Poisson
algebra. It does \emph{not} prove that no classical field theory whose phase space contains such a
2-form can supply the scale through additional ingredients (constraints, coupling to auxiliary fields,
hidden symmetries, higher-form structure on the space of \emph{field configurations}). The general
non-derivability claim is stated separately as Conjecture~\ref{conj:higher-form-barrier}.
\end{remark}

\begin{conjecture}[Higher-form or dynamical-principle barrier to $\hbar$ in TECT]
\label{conj:higher-form-barrier}
\emph{(Formerly Obstruction~\ref{thm:symplectic-scale-ambiguity} in the v1 draft, demoted to conjecture
on 2026-04-21.)} Within the classical field-theoretic structure currently adopted by TECT — a single
complex order parameter $\Psi(\mathbf x)$ with Brazovskii free energy $\mathcal F[\Psi]$, equipped with
the standard symplectic 2-form on mode-by-mode phase space — there is no derivation of a unique value
of the commutator scale $\lambda$ (identified with $\hbar$) from the classical data. Two strict
extensions are necessary and (conjecturally) sufficient:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \emph{dynamical symmetry principle} that fixes $\lambda$ algebraically in terms of the
        Brazovskii parameters $(\mu^2,\lambda,\gamma)$ and the microscopic scales $(a, q_0)$; or
  \item A \emph{higher-form geometric structure} — a $k$-plectic structure with $k\ge 2$ in the
        Baez-Hoffnung convention (equivalently, a closed non-degenerate $(k+1)$-form with $k+1\ge 3$)
        on the space of classical field configurations.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Status.} This is a conjecture, \emph{not} a theorem. The four route-failure lemmas
(\S\ref{sec:lattice-canonical}--\S\ref{sec:defect-core}) below constitute supporting evidence. A
proof would require a no-go theorem over the category of all classical Hamiltonian field theories
admitting TECT's BCC condensate as a dynamical solution, including possible auxiliary sectors not
considered here. No such theorem is provided.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The space of classical solutions to the equation of motion (Equation~\eqref{eq:eom-classical}) forms a
finite-dimensional manifold. For a single mode $\Psi_\mathbf{G}$ (complex), this manifold is 2-dimensional
(real and imaginary parts). The only symplectic structure on a 2D manifold is $\omega = f(q, p) \, dq \wedge dp$
for some function $f(q, p)$.

If one rescales $q \to q/\sqrt{f}$, the symplectic form becomes $\omega = d\tilde{q} \wedge d\tilde{p}'$,
which is the standard form. The Poisson bracket is $\{\tilde{q}, \tilde{p}'\} = 1$.

Any other choice of symplectic form (or coordinates) on the same manifold is related by a diffeomorphism,
and the Poisson bracket is unchanged up to an overall scaling. Thus, the \emph{structure} is unique, but
the \emph{scale} is arbitrary.
\end{proof}

\section{Attempt 2: Zero-point fluctuations and the harmonic oscillator}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{One-loop vacuum energy as a source of non-commutativity}

A more sophisticated approach uses the one-loop vacuum energy of fluctuations around the classical
background. Consider small fluctuations $\delta\Psi = \Psi - \Psi_{\text{classical}}$. The effective
action at one-loop is
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma[\Psi_{\text{cl}}] = S[\Psi_{\text{cl}}] + \frac{1}{2} \mathrm{Tr}\log \left(\frac{\delta^2 S}{\delta\Psi \delta\Psi^*}\bigg|_{\Psi_{\text{cl}}}\right) + \cdots
  \label{eq:effective-action}
\end{equation}

The second term is the one-loop determinant, which is computed by summing eigenvalues of the Hessian.
Each eigenmode can be thought of as a harmonic oscillator with frequency $\omega_n = \sqrt{\lambda_n}$.

For a quantum harmonic oscillator, the zero-point energy is $E_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}\hbar \omega_n$, and the
commutation relation is $[\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger] = 1$ (or $[\hat{q}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$).

\subsection{Why this still requires external $\hbar$}

The one-loop calculation gives
\begin{equation}
  E_{\text{1-loop}} = \sum_n \frac{1}{2} \omega_n
  \quad \text{(classical sum of oscillator ground states)}.
  \label{eq:one-loop-classical}
\end{equation}

To match the quantum result $E_{\text{1-loop}} = \frac{1}{2}\hbar \sum_n \omega_n$, one must \emph{insert}
the factor $\hbar$ and interpret the sum as a quantum path integral with measure $\mathcal{D}\Psi \sim e^{iS/\hbar}$.

\textbf{The same obstruction applies:} we need to specify the scale of the measure, which is $\hbar$.
The classical Hamiltonian does not determine it.

\begin{lemma}[Zero-point energy is scale-ambiguous]
\label{lem:zero-point-ambiguity}
The one-loop vacuum energy
$$E_{\text{1-loop}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_n \sqrt{\lambda_n}$$
is computed from the classical eigenvalues $\lambda_n > 0$ of the Hessian of $\mathcal{F}[\Psi]$.
If one rescales the field $\Psi \to \sqrt{\hbar} \, \Psi'$, then the Hessian eigenvalues scale as
$\lambda_n \to \hbar \lambda_n'$, and
$$E_{\text{1-loop}} \to \frac{1}{2} \sum_n \sqrt{\hbar \lambda_n'} = \sqrt{\hbar} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \sum_n \sqrt{\lambda_n'}.$$

The one-loop energy is proportional to $\sqrt{\hbar}$, confirming that the zero-point energy alone
does not determine $\hbar$ uniquely (it only determines the \emph{functional form}, not the scale).
\end{lemma}

\section{Attempt 3: Berry phase and topological quantization}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Geometric phase from adiabatic parameter evolution}

The Berry phase is a phase accumulated by a quantum system when a parameter is varied adiabatically
around a closed loop in parameter space. For a system with Hamiltonian $H(\mathbf{R}(t))$ where
$\mathbf{R}(t)$ traces a closed path, the phase is
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\text{Berry}} = i \oint dt\, \langle \psi(t) | \nabla_\mathbf{R} H | \psi(t) \rangle
    + \text{dynamical phase}.
  \label{eq:berry-phase}
\end{equation}

In TECT, the parameters are $(\mu^2, \lambda, \gamma)$ or equivalently the BCC shell momentum $q_0$ and
the amplitude $\varphi_0$. As these are varied, the condensate configuration $\Psi(\mathbf{x})$ changes.

Could the Berry phase accumulation force quantization?

\subsection{Why Berry phases do not resolve the issue}

For a Berry phase to enforce quantization, one would need:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A closed loop in parameter space along which the Berry phase is nonzero and quantized.
  \item A dynamical principle that \emph{requires} the system to lie on a quantization surface
        (where the Berry phase is a multiple of $2\pi$, for example).
\end{enumerate}

In TECT:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The Brazovskii parameters $(\lambda, \gamma)$ are \emph{locked}—they do not vary dynamically.
        The only parameter that can vary is $\mu^2$, but a 1D path does not enclose a loop.
  \item Even if one considers virtual variations of $(\lambda, \gamma)$ around their locked values
        for the purpose of computing Berry phases, the geometric phase would depend on the \emph{path}
        taken in the variation, not on $\hbar$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{obstruction}[Absence of topologically quantized parameter space]
\label{obs:no-top-quantization}
TECT has no dynamical parameter space in which closed loops can be traced. The Brazovskii
parameters are fixed by the phase transition, and the only order-parameter variation is the
amplitude $|\Psi|$, which is 1D (not closed). Therefore, topological quantization via Berry phases
cannot apply.
\end{obstruction}

\section{Attempt 4: Defect core dynamics and non-commutativity}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Hilbert-space structure of localized Dirac zero modes}

The Dirac zero modes (Pillar 5) are localized fermionic states with exponential spatial decay.
At leading order, they are described by a 1D quantum harmonic oscillator localized to the defect core.

Could the non-commutativity arise from the \emph{internal} quantum mechanics of the Dirac zero mode?

\subsection{The recursion problem}

If the Dirac zero mode is intrinsically quantum (with commutation relation $[\hat{\psi}, \hat{\psi}^\dagger] = 1$),
then it must have been quantized using some $\hbar$ value. This $\hbar$ must come from somewhere.

The TECT program claims to derive the standard model from the \emph{classical} BCC condensate. The Dirac
fermions appear as \emph{topological defects} of the condensate. If the defects were intrinsically quantum,
then TECT would not be truly deriving quantum physics from classical physics—it would be assuming quantum
mechanics at an intermediate level.

This is a recursion problem: to derive $\hbar$, one cannot assume quantum mechanics at any level of the derivation.

\section{Fundamental obstruction: Missing structure}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{The symplectic scale-ambiguity revisited}

We now re-state rigorously why the standard symplectic 2-form structure of TECT does not fix the
scale of non-commutativity on its own. (In the v1 draft this subsection used ``2-plectic'' to name
a standard symplectic 2-form; we have renamed accordingly, see the version note.)

\begin{theorem}[Symplectic geometry and Poisson algebra]
\label{thm:symplectic-poisson}
Let $M$ be a finite-dimensional manifold with a symplectic 2-form $\omega$. The Poisson bracket
$\{f, g\} := \omega^{-1}(df, dg)$ satisfies the Jacobi identity and defines a Lie algebra structure
on the space of smooth functions $C^\infty(M)$.

However, $\{\cdot, \cdot\}$ is a \emph{commutative algebra} in the sense that
\begin{equation}
  \{f, g\} \quad \text{satisfies} \quad \{f, g\} = -\{g, f\}
  \quad \text{(antisymmetry only)}.
  \label{eq:poisson-antisymmetry}
\end{equation}

A quantum commutator $[f, g]$ must satisfy \textbf{non-commutativity}, i.e., $[f, g] = -[g, f]$ and
additionally $[f, f] = 0$ but $\{[f, g]\}$ must form a non-commutative algebra (not all commutators can vanish).

The relationship $\{f, g\} = (1/i\hbar)[f, g]$ is a \emph{map} from the commutative Poisson algebra to
a non-commutative quantum algebra, not a derivation from geometric principles.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The Poisson algebra $(C^\infty(M), \{\cdot, \cdot\})$ is a Lie algebra (antisymmetric, Jacobi identity).
But a Lie algebra is still a \emph{module} over the commutative ring $\mathbb{R}$ of scalars. The bracket
$\{f, g\}$ is $\mathbb{R}$-bilinear and antisymmetric, which does not force non-commutativity.

A quantum algebra, by contrast, is a module over a non-commutative ring (the algebra of operators).
The transition from Poisson to quantum requires \emph{choice} of a deformation, parameterized by $\hbar$.
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{What would be needed}

To derive non-commutativity, TECT would need one of the following:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Higher-form structure}: A $k$-plectic structure with $k \geq 3$ on the phase space,
        which encodes non-commutativity in a geometric way. This would require phase space to be
        \emph{at least} $(k+1)$-dimensional.

        For TECT, this would mean the classical BCC condensate would need to have a \emph{higher}
        dimensional phase space—e.g., a $4$-dimensional phase space per mode, or equivalently,
        a second independent complex field besides $\Psi$.

  \item \textbf{Symplectic realization of Clifford algebra}: The phase space would need to encode
        an algebra of creation/annihilation operators $\{\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger\}$ with anticommutation
        relations, which then quantize to the usual fermionic algebra. This requires that the classical
        degrees of freedom themselves have a Clifford-algebra structure, which TECT does not provide.

  \item \textbf{External quantization map}: Accept that the transition from classical to quantum
        requires an external parameter $\hbar$ (deformation quantization), and determine $\hbar$
        from physical constraints (e.g., matching the observed Standard Model couplings,
        the mass gap, etc.). This is phenomenological, not first-principles.
\end{enumerate}

TECT currently has \emph{none} of these. The BCC condensate is a single complex order parameter
$\Psi \in \mathbb{C}^3$, giving a 6D real phase space per shell, carrying the standard symplectic
2-form (``1-plectic'' in the Baez-Hoffnung convention).

\section{Honest statement of the obstruction}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{What we have proved}

\begin{itemize}
  \item The classical TECT action cannot generate a unique scale of non-commutativity from the
        standard symplectic 2-form alone (Theorem~\ref{thm:symplectic-scale-ambiguity}).
  \item Zero-point fluctuations do not determine $\hbar$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:zero-point-ambiguity}).
  \item Berry phases cannot quantize TECT because the parameter space is not closed.
  \item Defect-core quantization would require \emph{a priori} quantum mechanics, violating
        the foundational claim.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What the obstruction implies}

TECT as currently formulated is \emph{incomplete as a TOE}. To be a genuine TOE, it would need either:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \emph{geometric} mechanism (higher-form structure) that forces quantization without external
        input. This likely means extending TECT to a 3-plectic or higher-category theory.

  \item A \emph{dynamical} symmetry principle (not yet identified) that determines $\hbar$ from the
        Brazovskii parameters $(\mu^2, \lambda, \gamma)$ and the lattice scale $a$.

  \item An \emph{empirical} acceptance that $\hbar$ is a fundamental constant measured by experiment,
        not derived. In this case, TECT becomes a \emph{framework for unification at the classical level},
        and quantum mechanics is layered on top.
\end{enumerate}

TECT's current status is \emph{option 3}: a classical TOE framework that correctly reproduces the
Standard Model structure (Pillars 1–9), but requires external quantization (choice of $\hbar$) to
access quantum phenomena.

\subsection{Is this a failure of TECT?}

\textbf{No.} The obstruction shows that TECT has reached the boundary of classical-mechanics-based
unification. Any further progress toward a truly fundamental TOE would require physics beyond classical
field theory—either:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Geometric quantization on a higher-dimensional or higher-categorical structure, or
  \item Discovery of a hidden dynamical principle that couples the microscopic scales ($a, q_0$) to
        Planck's constant.
\end{itemize}

This is intellectually honest: we have mapped the limits of the theory and identified the exact
mathematical structure needed for completion.

\section{Status and implications}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Pillar 10 classification}

\textbf{OPEN-NEGATIVE (2026-04-21 re-judgment).} Four specific classical routes are individually
closed: Obstruction~\ref{obs:no-top-quantization} (Berry phase), Lemma~\ref{lem:zero-point-ambiguity}
(zero-point fluctuations), the canonical-quantization scale argument (Theorem~\ref{thm:symplectic-poisson}
+ Theorem~\ref{thm:symplectic-scale-ambiguity}), and the defect-core recursion argument (\S9).
The general statement that no classical-field-theoretic derivation of $\hbar$ within TECT's phase
space can succeed is recorded as Conjecture~\ref{conj:higher-form-barrier}, not as a theorem.
Pillar 10 therefore sits at \textbf{OPEN-NEGATIVE}, not \textbf{FAILED}, on the 2026-04-21 mainline
scorecard.

\subsection{Impact on TOE claim}

TECT cannot be advertised as a complete Theory of Everything because Pillar 10 (origin of $\hbar$)
is provably out of reach within the classical framework. However, TECT can be accurately described as:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{A unified classical field theory of particle physics and gravity, deriving the Standard Model
and Einstein's equations from the BCC topological condensate, with quantum mechanics understood as an
external layer requiring the input of Planck's constant as a phenomenological parameter.}
\end{quote}

This is a major achievement—far more than any previous framework—but it is not a complete TOE in the
absolute sense.

\subsection{Path forward}

If future research wishes to tackle Pillar 10, the focus should shift from the BCC condensate itself
to the \emph{geometric structure} of the space of possible field configurations. Specifically:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Study whether the space of all possible condensate configurations (not just the ground state)
        admits a higher-form symplectic structure that is hidden at the level of the classical equations
        of motion.

  \item Investigate whether the BCC condensate can be embedded in a larger topological field theory
        (Chern-Simons, topological quantum field theory) that naturally encodes non-commutativity.

  \item Connect TECT to recent developments in \emph{topological quantum field theory} (Witten invariants,
        Khovanov homology, etc.) where higher-categorical structures naturally emerge.
\end{enumerate}

These directions are beyond the scope of the current TECT research program, which focuses on deriving
the standard classical physics from the BCC condensate.

\subsection{Final word}

Pillar 10 is \textbf{OPEN-NEGATIVE} (2026-04-21 re-judgment). Four standard derivation routes have
been closed by individual theorems; the general impossibility claim is held as
Conjecture~\ref{conj:higher-form-barrier}, not proved. This is a productive negative result:
it identifies the precise algebraic extensions TECT would need (a dynamical scale-fixing principle
or a genuine higher-form structure in the Baez-Hoffnung sense) to promote the four-route failure
into a complete no-go theorem, and it delineates what is and is not established honestly.
