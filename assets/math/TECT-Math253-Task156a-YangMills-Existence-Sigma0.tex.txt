\section*{2. Donaldson-Uhlenbeck-Yau Existence Theorem: Statement and Hypotheses}

\subsection*{2.1 Textbook Theorem (Donaldson 1985, Uhlenbeck-Yau 1986)}

\begin{theorem}[Donaldson-Uhlenbeck-Yau]
Let $X$ be a compact Kähler manifold and $E$ a holomorphic vector bundle of rank $r$ over $X$. The following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item $E$ is polystable (a direct sum of stable bundles with equal slope $\mu(E_i) = \mu(E)$).
  \item There exists a unique Hermitian metric $h$ on $E$ such that the Chern connection $\nabla_h$ satisfies the Hermitian-Einstein equation:
  \begin{equation}
  \mathrm{i} \Lambda_{\omega} F_{\nabla_h} = \lambda(E) \cdot \mathbf{1}_r,
  \end{equation}
  where $\Lambda_{\omega}$ is the contraction by the Kähler form, $F_{\nabla_h}$ is the curvature, and $\lambda(E) = \frac{2\pi c_1(E)}{r \int_X \omega^{n-1} / (n-1)!}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Key property}: On a **compact Kähler base**, the HE solution is **smooth** (C$^\infty$) — this is Yamabe regularity.

\section*{3. Grothendieck Splitting on $\mathbb{P}^1$ and Polystability}

\subsection*{3.1 Grothendieck Splitting Theorem (Hartshorne Theorem 2.2.3)}

\begin{theorem}[Grothendieck]
Every holomorphic vector bundle $E$ on $\mathbb{P}^1$ is a direct sum of line bundles:
\begin{equation}
E \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^r \mathcal{O}(d_i),
\end{equation}
where $d_1 \geq d_2 \geq \ldots \geq d_r$ are integers (determined uniquely up to permutation).
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Consequence for SU(5) and U(1)$_\chi$ bundles}:

On $\mathbb{P}^1_1$, the SU(5) part of $A_1$ defines a bundle $E_1^{(SU(5))}$. By Grothendieck, $E_1^{(SU(5))} \cong \bigoplus_j \mathcal{O}(d_{1,j})$. Each summand $\mathcal{O}(d_{1,j})$ is a **line bundle**, hence automatically **stable** (a line bundle has no proper sub-bundles).

Similarly for the U(1)$_\chi$ part and the $\mathbb{P}^1_2$ factor.

\subsection*{3.2 Polystability Criterion}

A bundle $E = \bigoplus_i E_i$ is **polystable** iff each $E_i$ is stable with **equal slope**:
\begin{equation}
\mu(E_i) := \frac{c_1(E_i)}{\mathrm{rank}(E_i)} = \mu(E) \quad \forall i.
\end{equation}

For line bundles on $\mathbb{P}^1$:
- Each $\mathcal{O}(d_i)$ is rank-1, so $\mu(\mathcal{O}(d_i)) = c_1(\mathcal{O}(d_i)) = d_i$.
- **Polystability requires all $d_i$ equal**: $d_1 = d_2 = \ldots = d_r$.

By Grothendieck, if a bundle $E$ on $\mathbb{P}^1$ has $d_1 = d_2 = \ldots = d_r = d$ (balanced splitting), then $E$ is polystable.

\subsection*{3.3 Application to SU(5) and U(1)$_\chi$}

From Math162 (BCC defect-bundle structure) and Math250 (separation ansatz):

The SU(5) part on each $\mathbb{P}^1_i$ factor is inherited from the **flat connection structure** of the BCC cubic lattice. On $\mathbb{P}^1$, a flat SU(5) bundle is **trivial** (all holomorphic bundles with vanishing Chern classes split into copies of $\mathcal{O}(0)$).

Thus: $E_i^{(SU(5))} \cong \mathcal{O}(0)^{\oplus 5} = \text{trivial rank-5 bundle}$.

The U(1)$_\chi$ part is **also trivial** on $\mathbb{P}^1$ (U(1) connections have at most one independent Chern class $c_1$, which vanishes for flat connections).

**Polystability conclusion**: Both SU(5) and U(1)$_\chi$ bundles on each $\mathbb{P}^1_i$ are **trivial bundles** (balanced splitting with $d = 0$ for all summands). Trivial bundles are **polystable** (all summands are $\mathcal{O}(0)$, with $\mu = 0$). ✓
