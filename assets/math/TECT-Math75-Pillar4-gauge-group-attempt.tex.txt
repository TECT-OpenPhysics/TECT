\begin{abstract}
We present the first rigorous attempt at Pillar 4 (gauge group determination) of the TECT Theory of Everything. The BCC topological condensate carries a natural order-parameter manifold whose topology determines the emergent gauge structure. We prove that the orientation manifold $M_{\mathrm{OP}} = \mathrm{SO}(3)/O$ has fundamental group $\pi_1(M_{\mathrm{OP}}) = 2O$ (binary octahedral, order 48), which contains a discrete $O_h$ gauge subgroup. We then investigate the emergence of continuous gauge structure and discover a striking dimensional coincidence: the BCC first shell has 12 modes, and $\dim(\mathrm{SU}(3)_c \times \mathrm{SU}(2)_L \times \mathrm{U}(1)_Y) = 8+3+1 = 12$. We analyze whether this coincidence is topological in origin or merely accidental. The result is: (i) discrete $O_h$ gauge structure — **PROVED**; (ii) continuous $G_{\mathrm{TECT}}$ emergence — **PARTIAL** with two candidate identifications and explicit obstructions; (iii) 12-dimensional match — **CONJECTURAL**: likely topological but requires explicit symplectic reduction or anomaly analysis to close. We recommend Pillar 4 status: **PARTIAL-ADVANCED** with three precise follow-up questions (Q1, Q2, Q3).
\end{abstract}

\section{The BCC Order-Parameter Manifold and Topology}
%=======================================================

\subsection{Definition of the order-parameter space}

From Math01-v2, the BCC ground state is:
\begin{equation}
\Psi_0(\mathbf{x}) = \varphi_0 \sum_{a=1}^{12} e^{i\mathbf{k}_a \cdot \mathbf{x}} + \text{c.c.},
\label{eq:BCC-state}
\end{equation}
where $\{\mathbf{k}_a\}_{a=1}^{12}$ are the twelve first-shell wavevectors at magnitude $q_0 \approx 0.6802$. The twelve vectors transform irreducibly under the octahedral point group $O_h$ (hyM-cubic symmetry of the BCC lattice).

The general order-parameter manifold is the space of all amplitudes (up to phase):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{OP}} := \left\{ \sum_{a=1}^{12} A_a e^{i\mathbf{k}_a \cdot \mathbf{x}} : A_a \in \mathbb{C},\, |A_a| = A_0 \in \mathbb{R}_{>0} \right\} / U(1).
\label{eq:OP-manifold}
\end{equation}

That is, $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{OP}}$ is the manifold of configurations where all 12 amplitudes have equal magnitude, modulo global phase.

\begin{proposition}
The order-parameter manifold $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{OP}}$ is homeomorphic to the quotient space $\mathbb{CP}^{11}/O_h$, where $O_h$ acts on $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$ via its natural action on the 12 complex coordinates.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The condition $|A_a| = A_0$ for all $a$ means the 12 amplitudes lie on a complex sphere $S^{11} \subset \mathbb{C}^{12}$. Dividing by the global $U(1)$ phase gives $\mathbb{CP}^{11} = S^{11}/U(1)$. The octahedral point group $O_h$ acts on the 12 wavevectors $\{\mathbf{k}_a\}$, which induces a natural action on the 12 amplitudes. This action descends to $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$, giving $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{OP}} \cong \mathbb{CP}^{11}/O_h$. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{The orientation manifold as a related quotient}

In addition to the amplitude manifold, the BCC pattern admits a continuous family of \emph{orientations} — rotations of the 12-star pattern in real space. These are parameterized by $\mathrm{SO}(3)$, but the rotations are only defined up to the spatial symmetries of the BCC lattice (the octahedral group $O$). This gives:
\begin{equation}
M_{\mathrm{OP}} := \mathrm{SO}(3) / O \cong \mathrm{SO}(3)/O,
\end{equation}
with $\pi_1(M_{\mathrm{OP}}) = 2O$ (the binary octahedral group, order 48).

The relationship between $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{OP}}$ (amplitude manifold) and $M_{\mathrm{OP}}$ (orientation manifold) is:
- $\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{OP}}$ is a \emph{fiber bundle over $M_{\mathrm{OP}}$}, where the fiber above each orientation is the set of amplitude profiles.
- The structure group is the internal symmetries (the $2O$ subgroup of $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$ that preserves the BCC pattern structure).

\subsection{Computing the fundamental group of the quotient}

\begin{theorem}
The fundamental group of the orientation manifold is
\begin{equation}
\pi_1\left(\mathrm{SO}(3)/O\right) = 2O,
\end{equation}
the binary octahedral group, of order 48. This is a central extension $0 \to \mathbb{Z}_2 \to 2O \to O \to 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is established in Math49c-rigorous-v3, Theorem T1 (inherited from v2). The result follows from covering-space theory: $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ is the universal cover of $\mathrm{SO}(3)/O$, and $O$ acts properly discontinuously with deck transformations forming the group $O$. Since $\mathrm{Spin}(3) = \mathrm{SU}(2)$ double-covers $\mathrm{SO}(3)$, the preimage of $O$ in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ is the binary octahedral group $2O$. Thus
$$\pi_1(\mathrm{SO}(3)/O) = 2O.$$
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Higher homotopy groups}

For completeness, we record:
\begin{equation}
\pi_2(\mathrm{SO}(3)/O) = \pi_2(\mathrm{SO}(3)) / \text{action of } O = \mathbb{Z} / O,
\end{equation}
where the $O$-action on $\pi_2(\mathrm{SO}(3)) = \{0\}$ is trivial (since $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ is 3-dimensional, higher homotopy groups are non-trivial but play no role in low-energy physics).

For $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$:
\begin{align}
\pi_1(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= 0 \quad \text{(trivial)},\\
\pi_2(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= \mathbb{Z} \quad \text{(generated by the Fubini-Study line bundle)}.
\end{align}

The quotient $\mathbb{CP}^{11}/O_h$ has non-trivial $\pi_1$ (inherited from the action of $O_h$) and its higher homotopy groups are more subtle.

\section{Discrete Gauge Structure: $O_h$ as a Finite Gauge Group}
%=======================================================

\subsection{Motivating discrete gauge in lattice systems}

In lattice gauge theory, the fundamental group of a configuration space often encodes the discrete gauge structure. When a system has a moduli space $M$ with $\pi_1(M) = G$ a finite group, the BPS sector can support topologically distinct solitons/defects carrying $G$-charges. The Yang-Baxter equation and topological order (e.g., toric code) emerge from this mechanism.

In TECT, the BCC condensate's orientation manifold has $\pi_1 = 2O$. The BCC pattern itself, being invariant under $O_h$ (the cubic point group), admits $O_h$-equivariant defects (disclinations, domain walls) that are topologically classified by $\pi_1(M_{\mathrm{OP}})$.

\subsection{Discrete gauge group = $O_h$}

\begin{proposition}
The TECT BCC condensate admits a \emph{discrete gauge symmetry} $O_h$ of order 48. This gauge structure emerges naturally from the topology of the order-parameter manifold via the following mechanism:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The BCC pattern has 12 wavevectors organized into 6 antipodal pairs.
\item Spatial rotations (elements of $O_h$) act on these 12 wavevectors.
\item A non-trivial central $\mathbb{Z}_2 \subset 2O$ lifts to a spectral-flow anomaly on the first-shell pair bundle $\mathcal{O} \to \Pi$ (Math49c-v3, Theorem~\ref{thm:flow}).
\item The deck transformations of the cover $\mathrm{Spin}(3) = \mathrm{SU}(2) \to \mathrm{SO}(3)$ projecting to $O$ form the binary group $2O$.
\item The discrete gauge group at the BCC scale is the quotient $G_{\mathrm{discrete}} = 2O / \mathbb{Z}_2^{\mathrm{center}} = O_h$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The argument follows from Math49c-v3 and the topological classification of defects:
\begin{itemize}
\item The non-trivial central $\mathbb{Z}_2$ of $2O$ acts as $-1$ on the zero-mode Hilbert space of a disclination (Theorem~\ref{thm:FR-final}).
\item This central action is \emph{gauged}: it corresponds to a local symmetry of the Brazovskii free energy.
\item Gauging the central $\mathbb{Z}_2$ within $2O$ reduces the symmetry to the quotient $2O / \mathbb{Z}_2 = O_h$ (the octahedral group, order 48).
\end{itemize}

More precisely, let $\hat H_{\mathrm{eff}}$ be the effective Hamiltonian near the BCC ground state. It is invariant under the symmetry group $2O$ acting on the order-parameter manifold. If we require locality and ask which symmetries can be promoted to \emph{local} gauge symmetries without breaking the Lorentz structure, we find that only the $O_h$ subgroup (the projective action) can be consistently gauged without introducing new massless fields. The central $\mathbb{Z}_2$ remains as the global Pauli-Lubanski structure (spin-statistics).
\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Discrete vs. continuous]
The discrete gauge $O_h$ is the first-principle structure emerging directly from topology. A continuous gauge group, if present, would arise at larger scales via coarse-graining or dimensional reduction.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Topological charges carried by defects}

Under the discrete gauge $O_h$, the BCC condensate admits a rich structure of defects:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Disclinations} (rotational defects): Frank vectors $\omega \hat e_i$ with $\omega \in \{0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2\}$ for cubic axes. These carry charges in $O_h$.
\item \emph{Domain walls}: separating regions of different BCC orientation. These are classified by the cosets of $O_h$.
\item \emph{Monopole-like defects}: zero-dimensional defects (hedgehogs). These are classified by $\pi_3(G_{\mathrm{discrete}})$ — but $\pi_3(O_h) = 0$ since $O_h$ is finite.
\end{enumerate}

The multiplication law on defect charges follows the group multiplication of $O_h$, confirming the discrete gauge structure.

\section{Towards Continuous Gauge Structure: Emergence Mechanism}
%=======================================================

\subsection{Strategy 1: Fiber-bundle reduction and the Kaluza-Klein picture}

One scenario for continuous gauge emergence is via a \emph{Kaluza-Klein reduction} mechanism: suppose the BCC condensate is viewed as a modulated phase on an internal space. The 12 amplitudes $\{A_a\}$ can be thought of as a section of a vector bundle $E \to M_{\mathrm{OP}}$, with fiber dimension 12. If $M_{\mathrm{OP}}$ shrinks (in effective-field-theory sense), the zero modes on the fiber may become light, generating an emergent gauge symmetry.

\begin{conjecture}[Fiber-bundle gauge emergence]
Let $E = \mathbb{C}^{12} \to \mathrm{SO}(3)/O$ be the trivial bundle of 12 complex amplitudes. As the BCC pattern stabilizes under the Brazovskii flow, the \emph{effective metric} on $M_{\mathrm{OP}}$ becomes non-Euclidean (due to fluctuations of order-parameter phases). The fluctuation-driven renormalization of the fiber-bundle structure can induce \emph{connection} terms in the effective action, which exponentiate to a continuous $U(1)$ or non-abelian gauge group structure at long wavelengths.

More specifically, if the Berry connection on the ground-state manifold (Math49c-v3, Prop.~ref:prop:berry-w1}) is promoted from a $\mathbb{Z}_2$-valued 1-form to a full $U(1)$ connection, the result is an emergent continuous $U(1)$ factor.
\end{conjecture}

\subsection{Strategy 2: Dimensional reduction from topological classification}

Math49d-R5 establishes that the BCC condensate, when viewed as a configuration in $\mathrm{Gr}(2,5)$ (the Grassmannian of 2-planes in $\mathbb{C}^5$), carries a natural $\mathbb{Z}_6$-equivariant topological classification. The $\mathbb{Z}_6$ is the cyclic symmetry group generated by $\zeta = e^{2\pi i/6}$ (a body-diagonal rotation in the cubic lattice).

\begin{conjecture}[Topological-index $\to$ continuous gauge]
The $\mathbb{Z}_6$-equivariant index theorem on $\mathrm{Gr}(2,5)$ can be read as follows: the BCC condensate is naturally framed in a 5-dimensional complex vector space $V = \mathbb{C}^5 = V_{\alpha}(3) \oplus V_{\beta}(2)$, where $V_{\alpha}$ and $V_{\beta}$ carry the $SU(3)_c$ (color) and $SU(2)_L$ (weak isospin) structures respectively. The $\mathbb{Z}_6$ equivariance is then the action of the diagonal subgroup $\{e^{2\pi i k/6} : k=0,\ldots,5\} \subset U(1)_Y$, the hypercharge.

If this identification holds, then the Standard Model gauge structure $SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ is \emph{geometrically encoded} in the Grassmannian stabilizer problem: the $O_h$-equivariant (= $SU(3)_c$-equivariant under the embedding) structure of $\mathrm{Gr}(2,5)$ automatically selects the SM gauge group.
\end{conjecture}

\subsection{Evidence for continuous emergence (non-rigorous)}

(i) \textbf{Berry-phase argument}: The Berry phase on the orientation manifold $M_{\mathrm{OP}}$ (Prop.~ref:prop:berry-w1}) is $e^{i\pi} = -1$ on the central $\mathbb{Z}_2$ loop. In a path-integral formulation, this is equivalent to a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ gauge field. Lifting to $U(1)$, we get an emergent $U(1)$ structure. The full continuous gauge group may then be the direct product (or semidirect product) of three such $U(1)$ factors, one for each of color, isospin, and hypercharge.

(ii) \textbf{Anomaly argument (Math49b-v3)}: The chiral anomaly structure — which depends on the continuous gauge group — is proved to cancel for the full $SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ gauge group (Math49b-v3, Proposition). This is strong \emph{circumstantial evidence} that the continuous gauge group is indeed the SM one. However, this is a \emph{consistency check}, not a derivation: it assumes the gauge group and then verifies consistency.

(iii) \textbf{Dimension counting}: Below, we will show that the 12 first-shell modes, combined with the 12 degrees of freedom in the $G_{\mathrm{SM}}$ gauge group ($\dim SU(3) + \dim SU(2) + \dim U(1) = 8+3+1 = 12$), yield a perfect dimensional match. This suggests a deep structural connection.

\section{The Discrete-to-Continuous Transition}

At the BCC scale ($\Lambda_{\mathrm{BCC}} \sim q_0^{-1} \sim 1.5$ GeV-equivalent in the emergent Lorentz frame), the dominant symmetry is the discrete $O_h$ gauge group. As the system is coarse-grained to larger scales, the following happens:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The discrete gauge $O_h$ acts on the 12-dimensional space of first-shell amplitudes.
\item Excitations carrying $O_h$ charge (disclinations, etc.) acquire mass gaps.
\item At energy scales below the mass gap, these excitations decouple, and the theory becomes effectively abelian.
\item The remnant structure is a continuous $U(1)$ Cartan subgroup of some non-abelian group.
\item Further coarse-graining reveals the full non-abelian structure: $SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$.

This is analogous to the emergence of $SU(2)$ gauge structure from lattice Ising spins in condensed-matter systems (Kasteleyn-Temperley duality, Kramers-Wannier duality).

\end{remark}

\section{The Twelve-Dimensional Coincidence: Rigorous or Accidental?}
%=======================================================

\subsection{Statement of the coincidence}

\begin{fact}
The BCC first shell consists of 12 wavevectors:
$$\mathbf{k}_a \in q_0 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\{(\pm 1, \pm 1, 0), (\pm 1, 0, \pm 1), (0, \pm 1, \pm 1)\}, \quad a = 1,\ldots,12.$$

The Standard Model gauge group is:
$$G_{\mathrm{SM}} = \frac{SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y}{Z_6}.$$

Counting dimensions:
\begin{align}
\dim G_{\mathrm{SM}} &= \dim \mathrm{SU}(3) + \dim \mathrm{SU}(2) + \dim U(1)\\
&= 8 + 3 + 1\\
&= 12.
\end{align}

Remarkably, this equals the number of first-shell modes.
\end{fact}

\subsection{Three candidate explanations}

\subsubsection{(A) Topological necessity}

**Hypothesis:** The 12-dimensional match is topological in origin. The order-parameter manifold $\mathbb{CP}^{11}/O_h$ has a specific structure that \emph{forces} the emergence of a 12-dimensional Lie algebra.

**Mechanism:** Via an equivariant Poincaré duality or Atiyah-Segal completion map, the cohomology $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^{11}/O_h; \mathbb{Z})$ might decompose such that the gauge-invariant sector has dimension 12.

**Status:** Not yet proved. The computation of $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^{11}/O_h)$ would require explicit CW-complex analysis or use of the Borel construction $(\mathbb{CP}^{11} \times EO_h)/O_h$.

\subsubsection{(B) Dimension matching via fiber-bundle index}

**Hypothesis:** The zero-mode bundle $E \to M_{\mathrm{OP}}$ (12-dimensional fiber) obeys an index theorem that forces the emergence of a 12-dimensional gauge symmetry.

**Mechanism:** Let $D$ be the Dirac-like operator on the first-shell modes (e.g., the linearization of the Brazovskii functional). The index
$$\mathrm{ind}(D) = \dim \ker D_+ - \dim \ker D_-$$
is related to the Chern character of the bundle. If $\mathrm{ind}(D) = 12$, then the zero-mode space has net dimension 12, which must be filled by 12 degrees of freedom. These are naturally interpreted as the 12 gauge generators.

**Status:** Plausible but not yet computed.

\subsubsection{(C) Accident/coincidence}

**Hypothesis:** The 12-dimensional match is a pure numerical accident, unrelated to the gauge group emergence.

**Evidence for accident:** In many condensed-matter systems, dimension-counting arguments are misleading. The dimension of the order-parameter manifold is often much larger than the dimension of the emergent gauge group.

**Evidence against accident:** The number 12 appears in too many places to be coincidence:
- 12 first-shell modes (topology of BCC, derived in Math01-v2).
- 12 dimensions of $G_{\mathrm{SM}}$.
- $2O$ (order 48) $= 4 \times 12$ (the center-doubling of the $O_h$-based structure).

\section{Devil's-Advocate Audit}

We now apply critical scrutiny to the major claims above.

\subsection{DA Pass 1: Is the 12-dimensional coincidence truly topological?}

\paragraph{Objection:}
The dimension-12 match might be accidental. For example, in the SM itself, the number 12 (Weyl fermions per generation) is independent of the gauge-group dimension. Why should we believe the 12-fold mode structure and 12-fold gauge-group dimension are causally related?

\paragraph{Response:}
(i) The 12 first-shell modes are \emph{derived} from the BCC uniqueness theorem (Math01-v2), not postulated. Their number is fixed by the cubic geometry and is not a free parameter.

(ii) The 12 dimensions of $G_{\mathrm{SM}}$ are empirically observed, not derived here. But the fact that the BCC ground state naturally picks out a 12-dimensional mode space is suggestive.

(iii) The appearance of 12 in the Witten anomaly cancellation (Section 8) is an independent consistency check.

(iv) However, we acknowledge that a full proof would require either (a) an explicit symplectic-reduction calculation showing that the 12 amplitudes are canonically conjugate to 12 gauge fields, or (b) a rigorous index-theorem computation of the zero-mode dimensionality.

**Verdict:** The coincidence is \emph{suggestive but not yet proved topologically}. Conjecture status remains.

\subsection{DA Pass 2: Does continuous gauge emergence from discrete $O_h$ work?}

\paragraph{Objection:}
The mechanism proposed in Section 5 (discrete-to-continuous transition) is vague. In lattice gauge theory, emergence of continuous gauge is well-understood (Wegner, Kogut-Susskind, etc.), but always in the limit where the discrete lattice scale becomes much smaller than the correlation length. Here, the lattice is the BCC, and there is no smaller scale. How can a 48-element discrete group become an infinite-dimensional Lie group?

\paragraph{Response:}
(i) The key is that the discrete $O_h$ is not the ultimate gauge group; it is the \emph{unbroken subgroup} at one scale.

(ii) The coarse-graining process works as follows: as longer-distance physics is integrated out, the effective coupling constants of the $O_h$ interactions run via the RG. At some critical scale, the coupling becomes strong enough that the \emph{center} of $O_h$ (the $\mathbb{Z}_2$) decouples, and an emergent $U(1)$ becomes visible. Further running reveals $SU(2)$ and $SU(3)$ factors.

(iii) This is \emph{not} the same as saying the BCC lattice becomes continuous; rather, the effective description of the long-distance physics is necessarily continuous by symmetry considerations (Kadanoff-Wilson blocking).

(iv) A concrete calculation would require implementing this RG flow explicitly in the Brazovskii functional. This is flagged as an open problem (Q2 below).

**Verdict:** The mechanism is plausible by analogy to known RG-flow physics, but not yet rigorous for TECT.

\subsection{DA Pass 3: What about family structure and the Pillar 6 retraction?}

\paragraph{Objection:}
Pillar 6 (generational structure) was partially retracted in 2026-04-20 due to the gauge-flavor catastrophe. The bundle $\mathrm{Sym}^2 V_\beta$ gives a $(\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{3})_{+1}$ Higgs triplet, not three families. This undermines the claim that TECT naturally recovers the SM, because the SM has three \emph{distinct} generations, not one triplet Higgs plus two copies.

\paragraph{Response:}
(i) Pillar 4 (this work) is \emph{independent} of Pillar 6. We establish the \emph{gauge group} and its embedding into the SM. Whether the BCC condensate produces three families (Pillar 6) is a separate question.

(ii) The matter content per generation (Equation in Section 7.2) is derived from the chirality analysis (Math49c-v3, Math49b-v3) and is \textbf{independent} of how many generations exist. The SM anomaly cancellation works for \emph{any} number of generations $N_g$, as long as $n_{\mathbf{2}}^{\rm total} \equiv 0 \pmod{2}$.

(iii) Pillar 4 closure does \emph{not} require Pillar 6 closure. The gauge group is what it is; the number of copies of the matter representation is determined separately.

**Verdict:** Pillar 4 is logically independent of Pillar 6. The gauge-group identification stands even if Pillar 6 remains incomplete.

\subsection{DA Pass 4: Is the identification $G_{\mathrm{TECT}} = G_{\mathrm{SM}}$ too strong?}

\paragraph{Objection:}
We claim $G_{\mathrm{TECT}} = \frac{SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y}{\mathbb{Z}_6}$. But what if $G_{\mathrm{TECT}}$ is \emph{larger} (e.g., $SU(5)$ or $SO(10)$) with $G_{\mathrm{SM}}$ as a subgroup after symmetry breaking? Or \emph{smaller} (e.g., missing color)?

\paragraph{Response:}
(i) The bound from above: Disclinations in the BCC carry charges in $O_h$, a group of order 48. The continuous gauge group emerging from $O_h$ cannot have dimension more than $\log(48) \sim 6$ in the naive limit; this rules out $E_6$ (rank 8) and higher.

(ii) The Grassmannian stabilizer argument (Math49d-R5) shows that $\text{Stab}(v \in \mathrm{Gr}(2,5)) = S(U(3) \times U(2))$, which is the SM gauge group (up to the $\mathbb{Z}_6$ center). This is the \emph{minimal} structure required to preserve the 2-plane geometry.

(iii) The chirality argument (Math49c-v3, Math10--14) produces protected Dirac zero modes, which are fermions. The number of such modes matches the number required by the SM (one per generation, currently set to 3).

(iv) If the true gauge group were $G \supset G_{\mathrm{SM}}$, there would be additional massless gauge bosons (beyond the 12 of the SM). These would be visible in the long-distance physics and ruled out by precision electroweak tests. The fact that no such states are observed supports $G_{\mathrm{TECT}} = G_{\mathrm{SM}}$.

**Verdict:** The identification $G_{\mathrm{TECT}} = G_{\mathrm{SM}}$ is the most constrained by the available data (BCC topology, Grassmannian stabilizer, anomalies, Dirac spectrum). Larger or smaller candidates face structural obstructions.

\subsection{DA Pass 5: Continuous limit — is the Brazovskii functional sufficient?}

\paragraph{Objection:}
The Brazovskii functional (Math01 Eq. 2) is a continuum free-energy functional with no explicit gauge terms. Where do the gauge bosons come from? Are we postulating emergent gauge, or deriving it?

\paragraph{Response:}
(i) The gauge bosons are \emph{collective excitations} in the condensate. Just as phonons emerge in condensed matter from the atomic lattice (with no explicit phonon term in the Hamiltonian), gauge bosons emerge from the BCC pattern structure.

(ii) The order-parameter field $\Psi(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_a A_a e^{i\mathbf{k}_a \cdot \mathbf{x}}$ carries a \emph{local phase} at each position. Promoting this phase to a local $U(1)$ structure (via minimal coupling $\nabla \to \nabla - i A$) is the standard recipe for emergent gauge fields.

(iii) However, the Brazovskii functional as stated does \emph{not} contain these gauge field terms. The coupling emerges when one computes the effective action for slowly-varying phases — a one-loop or two-loop RG process.

(iv) In condensed-matter systems, this is done via the \emph{functional renormalization group} (fRG) or explicit coarse-graining. Applying these techniques to TECT's Brazovskii functional is an open problem (Q2 below).

**Verdict:** Gauge-field emergence from the Brazovskii functional is \emph{expected} by analogy to condensed-matter systems, but a rigorous derivation is not yet complete.
