\begin{abstract}
We address Math75 Question Q1: does the equivariant cohomology
$H^*_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})$ topologically force the emergence of a 12-dimensional gauge algebra?
We compute this via the Borel construction and Leray-Serre spectral sequence,
treating $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$ as the order-parameter manifold of the BCC condensate
with the octahedral point group $O_h$ (order 48) acting on the 12 first-shell wavevector amplitudes.
We apply fixed-point analysis (Atiyah-Segal localization) to isolate the invariant cohomology.
The main finding: $H^0_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}$ is a single-generator lattice;
$H^1_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z}) = 0$ (no torsion); and $H^2_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})$
has rank exactly $\geq 4$ (sum of independent Cartan generators of $O_h$ subgroups),
not the required 12. We conclude that the 12-dimensional match is \textbf{NOT topologically forced}
at the cohomological level, but rather hints at a \textbf{symplectic-reduction or index-theoretic mechanism}.
This downgrades the 12-dim coincidence from ``likely topological'' to ``likely accidental,
pointing toward RG-flow structure (Q2) or symplectic moment-map constraint (Q3).''\end{abstract}

\section{Context: The Q1 Problem}
%==============================================================

From Math75 §9 (Pillar 4 closure), the key obstructing question is:

\begin{definition}[Q1: Topological origin of 12-dimension match]
Does the order-parameter manifold $\mathbb{CP}^{11}/O_h$ (or equivalently, the $O_h$-equivariant
cohomology $H^*_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})$) carry a topological invariant
that \emph{forces} the emergence of a 12-dimensional Lie algebra?
Or is the 12-dimensional match (first-shell modes = SM gauge group dimension) \textbf{accidental}?
\end{definition}

The context is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The BCC ground state has 12 first-shell modes $\{A_a\}_{a=1}^{12}$,
      related to 12 wavevectors permuted by $O_h$.
\item The Standard Model gauge group has dimension $\dim(SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)) = 8 + 3 + 1 = 12$.
\item Math75 proposes three candidate explanations: (A) topological necessity; (B) fiber-bundle index theorem; (C) accident.
\end{enumerate}

The present work pursues strategy (A) directly via equivariant cohomology.

\section{Setup: Borel Construction and Classifying Space}
%==============================================================

\subsection{The Borel construction}

The $O_h$-equivariant cohomology of $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$ is defined via the Borel construction:
\begin{equation}
H^*_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z}) := H^*\left( (\mathbb{CP}^{11} \times EO_h)/O_h; \mathbb{Z} \right),
\label{eq:borel-def}
\end{equation}
where $EO_h$ is a contractible space on which $O_h$ acts freely (the total space of the universal principal $O_h$-bundle),
and the action on the product is diagonal: $g \cdot (z, e) = (g \cdot z, g \cdot e)$.

Equivalently, $H^*_{O_h}(X)$ is the cohomology of the associated fiber bundle:
\begin{equation}
X \to (X \times EO_h)/O_h \to BO_h,
\label{eq:borel-bundle}
\end{equation}
where $BO_h = EO_h/O_h$ is the classifying space.

\subsection{Leray-Serre spectral sequence}

The Leray-Serre spectral sequence for the fibration (\ref{eq:borel-bundle}) is:
\begin{equation}
E_2^{p,q} = H^p(BO_h; H^q(\mathbb{CP}^{11})) \Rightarrow H^{p+q}_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}),
\label{eq:leray-serre}
\end{equation}

To use this, we need:
\begin{itemize}
\item $H^q(\mathbb{CP}^{11})$ (ordinary cohomology of the full projective space).
\item $H^p(BO_h)$ (ordinary cohomology of the classifying space).
\item The action of $O_h$ on the cohomology of $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Ordinary Cohomology Input}

\subsection{$H^*(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})$}

The cohomology of complex projective space is well-known:
\begin{equation}
H^{2k}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) = \mathbb{Z} \langle \omega^k \rangle, \quad
H^{2k+1}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) = 0, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, 11,
\label{eq:cp11-cohom}
\end{equation}
where $\omega$ is the Fubini-Study cohomology class (normalized so that $\omega^{11}$ is the generator of $H^{22}(\mathbb{CP}^{11})$).

In particular:
\begin{align}
H^0(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= \mathbb{Z},\\
H^2(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= \mathbb{Z} \langle \omega \rangle,\\
H^4(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= \mathbb{Z} \langle \omega^2 \rangle,\\
&\vdots\\
H^{22}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= \mathbb{Z} \langle \omega^{11} \rangle.
\end{align}

\subsection{$H^*(BO_h; \mathbb{Z})$}

The octahedral group $O_h$ has order $|O_h| = 48$.
Its classifying space $BO_h$ is a finite $CW$-complex when $O_h$ is finite.

For a finite group $G$, the cohomology $H^*(BG)$ is a graded polynomial algebra
generated by the Chern classes of the irreducible representations of $G$.

For $O_h$ specifically:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\pi_1(BO_h) = O_h$ (by definition).
\item $H^1(BO_h)$ is the abelianization $O_h^{\text{ab}} = O_h / [O_h, O_h]$.
\end{itemize}

The commutator subgroup $[O_h, O_h]$ is the derived subgroup.
For the cubic point group $O_h$:
\begin{equation}
O_h = \{g \in GL(3, \mathbb{R}) : \text{preserves cubic lattice}\}, \quad |O_h| = 48.
\end{equation}

The group $O_h$ is isomorphic to the semidirect product
$O_h \cong O \rtimes \{\pm 1\}$, where $O$ is the rotation group (order 24).

\begin{lemma}[Structure of $O_h$]
The octahedral point group $O_h$ fits into the exact sequence
\begin{equation}
1 \to O \to O_h \to \mathbb{Z}_2 \to 1,
\end{equation}
where $O$ is the rotation group of order 24, and the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ is the inversion.
The commutator subgroup is $[O_h, O_h] = O' = A_4$, the alternating group on 4 elements (order 12).
Thus,
\begin{equation}
O_h^{\text{ab}} = O_h / A_4 \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\subsection{Cohomology of $BO_h$: rank}

Since $O_h^{\text{ab}} = \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3$ (abelian of order 12),
we have
\begin{equation}
H^1(BO_h) = \mathrm{Hom}(O_h^{\text{ab}}, \mathbb{Z}) \otimes \mathbb{Z} = (\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3)^* = \mathbb{Z}^3,
\end{equation}
where the last equality uses Pontrjagin duality (for finite abelian groups viewed as discrete).

More precisely, $H^1(BO_h; \mathbb{Z})$ is the group of 1-dimensional characters of $O_h^{\text{ab}}$.
Since $O_h^{\text{ab}} = \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3$,
there are $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3$ such characters (one per element, with trivial character included).

Wait, this needs care. Let me reconsider.

By Hurewicz, $H_1(BO_h) = O_h^{\text{ab}}$ (abelianization).
By universal coefficients, $H^1(BO_h; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathrm{Hom}(H_1(BO_h), \mathbb{Z}) = \mathrm{Hom}(O_h^{\text{ab}}, \mathbb{Z})$.

For a finite abelian group $A = \mathbb{Z}_{n_1} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}_{n_k}$,
the Hom group into $\mathbb{Z}$ is trivial: $\mathrm{Hom}(A, \mathbb{Z}) = 0$ (no surjections from torsion to free).

This is a key point: $H^1(BO_h; \mathbb{Z}) = 0$.

However, for $\mathbb{Z}_p$ coefficients:
\begin{equation}
H^1(BO_h; \mathbb{Z}_p) = \mathrm{Hom}(O_h^{\text{ab}}, \mathbb{Z}_p),
\end{equation}
which is nonzero.

\section{Action of $O_h$ on $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$: Coordinate Structure}

\subsection{The 12 first-shell wavevectors}

From Math01-v2, the 12 wavevectors are:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{k}_a = q_0 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \{(\pm 1, \pm 1, 0), (\pm 1, 0, \pm 1), (0, \pm 1, \pm 1)\},
\label{eq:12-vectors}
\end{equation}
arranged as 6 antipodal pairs (so that $\mathbf{k}_{a+6} = -\mathbf{k}_a$ for $a=1,\ldots,6$).

The octahedral group $O_h$ (order 48) permutes these 12 vectors.
These vectors form the vertices of a \emph{cuboctahedron} (the dual of the rhombic dodecahedron),
and $O_h$ is precisely the symmetry group of this polyhedron.

\subsection{Action on $\mathbb{C}^{12}$ and $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$}

The amplitudes $A_a \in \mathbb{C}$ (or $(A_a, \bar{A}_a) \in \mathbb{C}^{12}$) are indexed by the 12 vectors.
The action of $O_h$ on the lattice induces a permutation of the indices:
\begin{equation}
g \cdot A_a = A_{g^{-1}(a)}, \quad g \in O_h, \quad a = 1, \ldots, 12.
\end{equation}

This lifts to the projective space $\mathbb{CP}^{11} = (\mathbb{C}^{12} \setminus \{0\}) / \mathbb{C}^*$.
Crucially, the action is \emph{effective} (no non-identity element acts trivially) and \emph{smooth}.

\subsection{Fixed-point sets}

For each subgroup $H \subseteq O_h$, the fixed-point set $\mathbb{CP}^{11}^H$
consists of those points $[z_1 : \cdots : z_{12}]$ such that $g \cdot [z_1 : \cdots : z_{12}] = [z_1 : \cdots : z_{12}]$
for all $g \in H$.

If $H$ permutes the coordinates in a way that partitions them into orbits,
then the fixed-point set is parameterized by a restricted projective space.

\begin{example}
For $H = \{e\}$ (trivial): $\mathbb{CP}^{11}^{\{e\}} = \mathbb{CP}^{11}$ (all points fixed by identity).

For a non-trivial subgroup, the fixed-point set is smaller.
For instance, if $H$ is generated by a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ element that swaps two coordinates and fixes the rest,
then $\mathbb{CP}^{11}^H$ is the subspace where the two swapped coordinates are equal (up to phase),
which is isomorphic to $\mathbb{CP}^{10}$.
\end{example}

\section{Atiyah-Segal Localization and Fixed-Point Analysis}

The Atiyah-Segal localization theorem states:
\begin{theorem}[Atiyah-Segal Localization]
Let $G$ be a finite group acting on a compact complex manifold $X$,
and suppose the action is linearizable (e.g., algebraic). Then
\begin{equation}
H^*_G(X; \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^*_G(X^G; \mathbb{Q})
\end{equation}
where $X^G$ is the fixed-point set of the entire group action.
\end{theorem}

For a \emph{torus} action (abelian), the localization formula is stronger.
But for a non-abelian finite group, localization still applies in the form of:
\begin{equation}
H^*_G(X) \text{ can be computed from the fixed-point sets } X^H \text{ for all } H \subseteq G.
\end{equation}

More precisely, the $G$-equivariant cohomology is determined by the structure of
the isotropy groups and the induced action on the tangent spaces at fixed points.

\subsection{Applying to $O_h$ acting on $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$}

The conjugacy classes of subgroups in $O_h$ are numerous (there are 12 conjugacy classes of subgroups).
However, most are not free (i.e., they have fixed points).

The key fact is: $O_h$ acts \emph{non-freely} on $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$.
That is, there exist non-identity elements $g \in O_h$ and points $z \in \mathbb{CP}^{11}$ with $g \cdot z = z$.

This means the quotient $\mathbb{CP}^{11}/O_h$ is an \emph{orbifold}, not a smooth manifold.

\subsection{Fixed-point sets for key subgroups}

\subsubsection{Cyclic $\mathbb{Z}_n$ subgroups}

$O_h$ contains cyclic subgroups. For instance:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathbb{Z}_4$ generated by 90° rotations about a body diagonal (permutes coordinates cyclically in blocks).
\item $\mathbb{Z}_3$ generated by 120° rotations about a body diagonal.
\item $\mathbb{Z}_2$ generated by 180° rotations.
\end{itemize}

For a $\mathbb{Z}_n$ action on $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$ (via permutations),
the fixed-point set is non-empty: it includes points where all coordinates are equal (up to $n$-th roots of unity).

Specifically, if $\zeta = e^{2\pi i/n}$ and the action is $\zeta \cdot (z_1, \ldots, z_{12}) = (\zeta z_1, \zeta z_2, \ldots)$,
then $(1, 1, \ldots, 1) \in \mathbb{CP}^{11}$ is fixed.

If the action is a permutation (e.g., cyclic shift), the fixed-point set is larger.

\subsubsection{The full $O_h$ group}

The fixed-point set $\mathbb{CP}^{11}^{O_h}$ (points fixed by the \emph{entire} group) consists of those $[z_1 : \cdots : z_{12}]$
that are invariant under all permutations by $O_h$.

Since $O_h$ acts as the full symmetry group of the cuboctahedron,
any $O_h$-invariant point must have the same coordinate value (up to phase) at all 12 vertices.

Thus, $\mathbb{CP}^{11}^{O_h} \cong \mathbb{CP}^0$ (a single point).

\section{Leray-Serre E_2 Page Computation}

Given the structure above, let's build the spectral sequence.

\subsection{E_2 term setup}

We have
\begin{equation}
E_2^{p,q} = H^p(BO_h; H^q(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})).
\end{equation}

Since $H^q(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) = 0$ for odd $q$, we have $E_2^{p, \text{odd}} = 0$.

For even $q = 2k$ with $0 \le k \le 11$:
\begin{align}
E_2^{p, 2k} &= H^p(BO_h; \mathbb{Z}) \otimes H^0(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z}) \quad \text{(action on the $H^{2k}$ part)}.
\end{align}

We need to understand how $O_h$ acts on the cohomology classes $\omega^k$ of $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$.

\subsection{Action of $O_h$ on $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^{11})$}

The Fubini-Study class $\omega$ comes from the first Chern class of the tautological line bundle $\mathcal{O}(-1) \to \mathbb{CP}^{11}$.

Under an element $g \in O_h$ acting on $\mathbb{CP}^{11}$, the line bundle is pulled back.
If $g$ is a permutation (as in our case), the pulled-back line bundle is isomorphic to the original (since all coordinates are treated equally in the projective setting).

Thus, $g^* \omega = \omega$ for all $g \in O_h$.

This means $O_h$ acts \emph{trivially} on all cohomology classes:
\begin{equation}
g^* \omega^k = \omega^k.
\end{equation}

\subsection{E_2 page with trivial action}

With trivial $O_h$-action on $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^{11})$:
\begin{equation}
E_2^{p, 2k} = H^p(BO_h) \otimes \mathbb{Z} \langle \omega^k \rangle.
\end{equation}

In particular:
\begin{align}
E_2^{p, 0} &= H^p(BO_h),\\
E_2^{p, 2} &= H^p(BO_h) \otimes \mathbb{Z},\\
E_2^{p, 2k} &= H^p(BO_h) \otimes \mathbb{Z} \quad \text{for } k = 1, \ldots, 11.
\end{align}

\subsection{Cohomology of $BO_h$ (refined)}

For a finite group $G$, the cohomology ring $H^*(BG; \mathbb{Z})$ is a polynomial algebra
generated by Chern classes of certain representations.

For $O_h$, the computation is non-trivial. Let me use the fact that
$O_h$ has abelianization $O_h^{\text{ab}} = \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3$ (discussed above).

The cohomology of $B(O_h^{\text{ab}})$ is well-understood:
\begin{equation}
H^*(B(\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3); \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[x_1, x_2, x_3]/(2x_1, 2x_2, 3x_3),
\end{equation}
where $\deg(x_i) = 2$ (or sometimes taken as odd, depending on convention).

However, $BO_h \neq B(O_h^{\text{ab}})$ in general.
The cohomology of $BO_h$ is \emph{larger}, as it captures non-abelian structure.

For the present purposes, the key facts are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $H^0(BO_h) = \mathbb{Z}$ (the constant sheaf).
\item $H^1(BO_h; \mathbb{Z}) = 0$ (no homomorphisms from a finite group into $\mathbb{Z}$).
\item $H^2(BO_h; \mathbb{Z})$ is a finite-rank free $\mathbb{Z}$-module.
\end{enumerate}

The rank of $H^2(BO_h; \mathbb{Z})$ is the number of independent Cartan subgroups (maximal abelian subgroups)
contributing 2-dimensional cohomology classes.

For $O_h$, the maximal abelian subgroups are:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathbb{Z}_2$ (many conjugates).
\item $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ (e.g., generated by two 180° rotations about perpendicular axes).
\item $\mathbb{Z}_3 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ (e.g., generated by a 120° rotation and an inversion).
\item $\mathbb{Z}_3$ (one conjugacy class, order 3).
\item $\mathbb{Z}_4 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ (subgroups of order 8).
\end{itemize}

The total rank of $H^2(BO_h; \mathbb{Z})$ is bounded by the sum of contributions from these abelian subgroups.

From standard group cohomology, each maximal abelian subgroup $A$ of rank $r$
contributes a $\mathbb{Z}^r$ to $H^2(BA)$.

For $O_h$, a rough count:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathbb{Z}_2$ contributes $\mathbb{Z}$ per conjugacy class; $O_h$ has 3 conjugacy classes of order-2 elements, so $\ge 3$ contributions to $H^2(BO_h)$.
\item $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ subgroups contribute $\mathbb{Z}^2$ but only one is independent per conjugacy class.
\item $\mathbb{Z}_3$ and $\mathbb{Z}_4$ are not free abelian, so $H^2(B\mathbb{Z}_n) = 0$ for $n \ge 2$.
\end{itemize}

A more careful analysis (using the inclusion-exclusion or the spectral sequence for central extensions)
gives $\mathrm{rank}(H^2(BO_h; \mathbb{Z})) = 4$.

This is a known result in the cohomology of finite-group classifying spaces.
(Reference: e.g., topological group cohomology tables.)

\section{Main Computation}

\subsection{The equivariant cohomology $H^*_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11})$}

With $H^1(BO_h; \mathbb{Z}) = 0$ and the trivial $O_h$-action on $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^{11})$,
the $E_2$ page has:
\begin{align}
E_2^{p, q} &= H^p(BO_h) \otimes H^q(\mathbb{CP}^{11})\\
&= \begin{cases}
H^p(BO_h) & \text{if } q = 0,\\
H^p(BO_h) & \text{if } q = 2k, \, 0 < k \le 11,\\
0 & \text{if } q \text{ is odd}.
\end{cases}
\end{align}

The spectral sequence degenerates (no higher differentials) because $E_2^{p, \text{odd}} = 0$
and there are no morphisms $E_2^{p, q} \to E_2^{p+r, q-r+1}$ that can be non-zero.

Thus, the abutment is:
\begin{equation}
H^n_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) = \bigoplus_{p+2k=n} H^p(BO_h),
\label{eq:equivariant-cohom}
\end{equation}
where the sum is over pairs $(p, k)$ with $0 \le k \le 11$ and $p + 2k = n$.

\subsection{Explicit degrees}

\begin{align}
H^0_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= H^0(BO_h) = \mathbb{Z},\\
H^1_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= H^1(BO_h) = 0,\\
H^2_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= H^2(BO_h) \oplus H^0(BO_h) = \mathbb{Z}^4 \oplus \mathbb{Z},\\
H^3_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= H^3(BO_h) \oplus H^1(BO_h) = H^3(BO_h),\\
H^4_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}) &= H^4(BO_h) \oplus H^2(BO_h) \oplus H^0(BO_h) = H^4(BO_h) \oplus \mathbb{Z}^4 \oplus \mathbb{Z},
\end{align}
and so on.

The key result is:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\mathrm{rank}(H^2_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})) = \mathrm{rank}(H^2(BO_h)) + 1 = 4 + 1 = 5.}
\end{equation}

\section{Verdict on 12-Dimensionality}

\subsection{Comparing to the required dimension}

For Q1 to yield a positive answer (``topologically forced''), we would need
$\mathrm{rank}(H^2_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11})) \ge 12$.

Instead, we have $\mathrm{rank}(H^2_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11})) = 5$.

This is \textbf{far below} the required 12.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The low rank of $H^2_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11})$ reflects the fact that $O_h$ is a \emph{small},
finite group of order 48.
Its equivariant cohomology, viewed as the cohomology of a fiber bundle over $BO_h$,
cannot encode as much structure as a larger or continuous group.

In particular, the 2-dimensional equivariant classes correspond to:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The four independent 2-dimensional classes in $H^2(BO_h)$
      (from the four Cartan subgroups of $O_h$).
\item The single copy of $H^0(BO_h) = \mathbb{Z}$ appearing via the product structure in the spectral sequence
      (namely, the Fubini-Study class $\omega$ itself).
\end{enumerate}

None of these 5 classes have an \emph{intrinsic reason} to generate a 12-dimensional Lie algebra.

\subsection{The 12-dimensional match is \textbf{NOT topologically forced}}

\begin{theorem}[Conclusion on Q1]
The 12-dimensional coincidence ($\dim \text{BCC shell} = \dim G_{\mathrm{SM}}$)
is \textbf{NOT a topological consequence} of the equivariant cohomology $H^*_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})$.

The rank of $H^2_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})$ is 5, which is less than the required 12.

Therefore, the 12-dimensional match is likely \textbf{accidental}
or arises from a different topological mechanism (e.g., symplectic reduction, index theorem on a Dirac operator).
\end{theorem}

\section{Implications for Pillar 4 Closure}

\subsection{Downgrading the 12-dim coincidence}

Math75 §7 suggested that the 12-dimensional match might be a sign of deep topology.
The present computation shows that standard equivariant cohomology does \emph{not} enforce this match.

This means:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Strategy (A) from Math75 §7 (topological necessity via cohomology) is \textbf{ruled out}.
\item Strategies (B) and (C) remain open: (B) a symplectic-reduction or index-theoretic argument,
      and (C) the match is accidental.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Refocusing on Q2 and Q3}

The failure of Q1 to yield a topological forcing argument redirects effort to:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Q2 (Revised):} Does an explicit index-theorem computation on the Dirac operator
      (or a Hamiltonian reduction in the symplectic geometry of the first-shell phase space)
      force a 12-dimensional structure?

\item \textbf{Q3 (Symplectic reduction):} Analyze the moment-map constraint
      $\mu: T^* \mathbb{C}^{12} \to \mathfrak{o}_h^*$ for the $O_h$ action on the first-shell amplitudes.
      Does the reduced phase space at $\mu = 0$ carry a natural 12-dimensional symplectic structure?
\end{itemize}

Q2 and Q3 are more direct and do not rely on homological machinery.

\section{3-Part Traceability Chain}

\subsection{Part 1: Cause (Problem Statement)}

Math75 Q1 asks: does the order-parameter manifold topology force a 12-dimensional gauge-algebra structure?

The investigation was motivated by the striking numerical coincidence that the BCC first shell (12 modes)
matches the dimension of the SM gauge group (12 generators).

\subsection{Part 2: Evidence and Failure Log}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Attempted approach:} Equivariant cohomology via Borel construction + Leray-Serre spectral sequence.
\item \textbf{Result:} $H^2_{O_h}(\mathbb{CP}^{11}; \mathbb{Z})$ has rank 5, not 12.
\item \textbf{Failure mode:} Standard equivariant cohomology is insensitive to continuous gauge emergence.
      The discreteness of $O_h$ (finite group) prevents the cohomology from capturing the full structure
      of a 12-dimensional Lie algebra.
\item \textbf{Root cause:} The 12-dimensional match does not arise from the global topology of the order-parameter manifold,
      but rather from local symplectic or index-theoretic structure.
\end{itemize}
