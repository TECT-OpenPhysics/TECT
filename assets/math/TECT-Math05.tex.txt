\section{Multi-Shell Resonance Networks}

\subsection{Multi-shell wavevector sets}

Let the wavevectors be distributed across multiple shells
\begin{equation}
S = \bigcup_{a=1}^{K} S_a,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
S_a \subset S^2_{q_a},
\qquad
S^2_{q_a} = \{x\in\mathbb{R}^3 : |x|=q_a\}.
\end{equation}

Each shell satisfies the antipodal symmetry
\begin{equation}
S_a = -S_a,
\qquad
|S_a| = N_a.
\end{equation}

The total number of wavevectors is
\begin{equation}
N = \sum_{a=1}^{K} N_a .
\end{equation}

\subsection{Cross-shell pair sums}

For shells $S_a$ and $S_b$ define the cross-shell multiplicity
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)
=
\#\{(x,y)\in S_a\times S_b : x+y=v\}.
\end{equation}

The total pair-sum multiplicity becomes
\begin{equation}
r(v) = \sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Multi-shell resonance functional}

The natural generalization of the single-shell functional is
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S)
=
\sum_v r(v)^2.
\end{equation}

Expanding the square yields
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S)
=
\sum_{a,b,c,d}
\sum_v
r_{ab}(v)\, r_{cd}(v).
\end{equation}

Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S)
=
\left|
\{(a,b,c,d) : q_a+q_b=q_c+q_d\}
\right|,
\end{equation}
where $q_a,q_b,q_c,q_d$ are wavevectors drawn from the corresponding shells.

Thus $\Lambda_{\text{multi}}$ counts quartic resonance relations across shells.

\subsection{Geometric interpretation}

For fixed shells $S_a$ and $S_b$, the equation
\begin{equation}
x+y=v,
\qquad
x\in S_a,\ y\in S_b
\end{equation}
defines a circle on $S_a$ given by
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{ab}(v)
=
\{x\in S^2_{q_a} : |v-x|=q_b\}.
\end{equation}

Thus
\begin{equation}
x\in S_a\cap \mathcal F_{ab}(v).
\end{equation}

These circles generalize the fiber circles of the single-shell case.

\subsection{Generalized fiber occupancy}

Define
\begin{equation}
n_{ab}(v)
=
|S_a \cap \mathcal F_{ab}(v)|.
\end{equation}

Then
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)\le n_{ab}(v).
\end{equation}

Let
\begin{equation}
n_{\max}
=
\max_{a,b,v} n_{ab}(v).
\end{equation}

Using the same incidence arguments as in the single-shell case,
one obtains the bound
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S)
\le
n_{\max}\, N^2.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Multi-shell scaling law}

\begin{theorem}[Multi-shell resonance scaling]
Let $S=\bigcup_{a=1}^{K} S_a$ be a multi-shell antipodal set
with total size $N$.

If maximizers satisfy bounded generalized fiber occupancy
\begin{equation}
n_{\max}=O(1),
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi,max}}(N)
=
\Theta(N^2).
\end{equation}

\end{theorem}

\subsection{Structure selection principle (multi-shell)}

In a multi-shell Fourier expansion
\begin{equation}
\psi(x)
=
\sum_{a=1}^{K}
\sum_{q\in S_a}
A_q e^{i q\cdot x},
\end{equation}
quartic interactions contain resonance terms proportional to
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r(v)^2 .
\end{equation}

Thus the energetically preferred configuration of wavevectors is

\begin{equation}
S_{\text{selected}}
=
\arg\max_{S}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Examples}

Different resonance networks produce different spatial structures.

\begin{itemize}

\item Single-shell stars produce lamellar, hexagonal, or BCC patterns.

\item Two-shell interactions can generate quasicrystalline patterns.

\item Three or more shells may produce hierarchical multi-scale structures.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{General Structure Selection Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[General resonance selection principle]

Let $S$ be a finite union of antipodal shells in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Then the spatial pattern generated by shell-restricted Fourier modes
is determined by the maximizer of the resonance functional

\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S)=\sum_v r(v)^2.
\end{equation}

In particular, structure selection is governed by the maximization
of additive energy in wavevector space.

\end{theorem}

\section{Shell-Radius Resonance Conditions}

\subsection{Two-shell configuration}

Consider two shells
\begin{equation}
S_1 \subset S^2_{q_1},
\qquad
S_2 \subset S^2_{q_2},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
S^2_{q_a}=\{x\in\mathbb R^3:|x|=q_a\}.
\end{equation}

Each shell satisfies
\begin{equation}
S_a=-S_a,
\qquad
|S_a|=N_a.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Quartic resonance equation}

Quartic resonances arise from solutions of
\begin{equation}
q_i + q_j = q_k + q_l
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
q_i\in S_a,
\quad
q_j\in S_b,
\quad
q_k\in S_c,
\quad
q_l\in S_d.
\end{equation}

Taking norms yields the constraint
\begin{equation}
|q_i+q_j|^2 = |q_k+q_l|^2.
\end{equation}

Expanding,
\begin{equation}
q_a^2 + q_b^2 + 2 q_a q_b \cos\theta_{ij}
=
q_c^2 + q_d^2 + 2 q_c q_d \cos\theta_{kl}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Resonant radius ratios}

A necessary condition for abundant resonances is that the shell radii
satisfy algebraic relations allowing solutions for the angles.

For two shells this yields
\begin{equation}
q_1^2 + q_2^2 + 2 q_1 q_2 \cos\theta
=
2 q_1^2 (1+\cos\phi)
\end{equation}
for some angles $\theta,\phi$.

This implies
\begin{equation}
\cos\theta
=
\frac{q_1}{q_2}\cos\phi
+
\frac{q_1^2 - q_2^2}{2 q_1 q_2}.
\end{equation}

Thus resonances exist only when the ratio
\begin{equation}
\rho=\frac{q_2}{q_1}
\end{equation}
lies in a range where the right-hand side is in $[-1,1]$.

\subsection{Resonant ratio condition}

Define
\begin{equation}
f(\rho,\phi)
=
\frac{1}{\rho}\cos\phi
+
\frac{1-\rho^2}{2\rho}.
\end{equation}

Resonances occur when
\begin{equation}
|f(\rho,\phi)|\le 1.
\end{equation}

Thus the shell ratio $\rho$ controls the density of resonant quartets.

\subsection{Example: quasicrystalline ratios}

Certain ratios produce enhanced resonance networks.

For example, ratios related to the golden mean
\begin{equation}
\rho = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}
\end{equation}
produce dense resonance relations associated with
icosahedral and quasicrystalline symmetry.

Other ratios generate dodecagonal or decagonal
resonance networks.

\subsection{Multi-shell resonance functional}

For shells $S_a$, define
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)=\#\{(x,y)\in S_a\times S_b : x+y=v\}.
\end{equation}

The multi-shell resonance functional is
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S)
=
\sum_v
\left(\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)\right)^2.
\end{equation}

\subsection{General multi-shell selection theorem}

\begin{theorem}

Let $S=\bigcup_a S_a$ be a union of antipodal shells in $\mathbb R^3$.
The spatial pattern generated by shell-restricted Fourier modes
is determined by the maximizer of

\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S).
\end{equation}

Shell radius ratios that increase the density of resonant quartets
produce energetically preferred structures.

\end{theorem}

\subsection{Interpretation}

The shell radii determine the geometry of the resonance network.
Patterns such as lamellar, hexagonal, BCC, and quasicrystalline
structures correspond to configurations that maximize
the multi-shell resonance functional.


\section{Optimal Shell-Radius Ratios}

\subsection{Two-shell resonance geometry}

Consider two shells
\begin{equation}
S_1 \subset S^2_{q_1},
\qquad
S_2 \subset S^2_{q_2},
\end{equation}
with ratio
\begin{equation}
\rho=\frac{q_2}{q_1}.
\end{equation}

Let
\begin{equation}
a,c \in S_1,
\qquad
b,d \in S_2
\end{equation}
satisfy the resonance equation
\begin{equation}
a+b=c+d .
\end{equation}

\subsection{Geometric constraint}

Squaring both sides yields
\begin{equation}
|a|^2 + |b|^2 + 2a\cdot b
=
|c|^2 + |d|^2 + 2c\cdot d .
\end{equation}

Since
\begin{equation}
|a|=|c|=q_1,
\qquad
|b|=|d|=q_2,
\end{equation}
we obtain
\begin{equation}
a\cdot b = c\cdot d .
\end{equation}

Thus resonances correspond to equal dot products between
vectors from the two shells.

\subsection{Angle representation}

Let
\begin{equation}
a\cdot b = q_1 q_2 \cos\theta.
\end{equation}

Then
\begin{equation}
\cos\theta_{ab}=\cos\theta_{cd}.
\end{equation}

Hence resonances occur when many pairs of vectors
share the same relative angle.

\subsection{Density of resonances}

The number of resonant quartets is approximately proportional to
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}
\sim
\sum_{\theta}
n_1(\theta)^2 n_2(\theta)^2 ,
\end{equation}
where $n_a(\theta)$ counts the number of vectors on shell $a$
forming angle $\theta$ with vectors on the other shell.

Maximizing $\Lambda_{\text{multi}}$ requires that many angles coincide.

\subsection{Resonant polygon condition}

A particularly efficient resonance network occurs when
vectors from different shells can form closed polygons
\begin{equation}
q_1 e_i + q_2 e_j + q_1 e_k + q_2 e_l =0 .
\end{equation}

Such relations require
\begin{equation}
q_1^2 + q_2^2 + 2 q_1 q_2 \cos\phi
=
q_1^2 + q_2^2 + 2 q_1 q_2 \cos\psi .
\end{equation}

Thus large resonance networks arise when
angles $\phi$ and $\psi$ repeat frequently in the shell geometry.

\subsection{Optimal ratios from regular polygons}

Consider a regular $n$-gon in Fourier space.

If alternating edges correspond to shells $q_1$ and $q_2$,
geometry implies
\begin{equation}
\rho
=
\frac{q_2}{q_1}
=
2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Examples}

\begin{itemize}

\item $n=6$
\begin{equation}
\rho = \sqrt{3}
\end{equation}
(hexagonal interactions)

\item $n=8$
\begin{equation}
\rho = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{8}\right)
\end{equation}
(octagonal quasicrystals)

\item $n=10$
\begin{equation}
\rho = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{10}\right)
\end{equation}
(decagonal quasicrystals)

\item $n=12$
\begin{equation}
\rho = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{12}\right)
\end{equation}
(dodecagonal quasicrystals)

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Multi-shell structure selection principle}

Let
\begin{equation}
S = \bigcup_a S_a
\end{equation}
be a union of shells.

Then the preferred configuration of wavevectors is obtained by maximizing
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S)
=
\sum_v
\left(\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)\right)^2 .
\end{equation}

Shell radius ratios satisfying
\begin{equation}
\rho = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right)
\end{equation}
produce enhanced resonance networks and therefore correspond
to energetically favored multi-shell patterns.

\subsection{Generalized structure selection theorem}

\begin{theorem}

Let $S$ be a union of antipodal shells in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
The spatial pattern generated by shell-restricted Fourier modes
is determined by the maximizer of the resonance functional

\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\text{multi}}(S).
\end{equation}

In particular, shell radius ratios that generate dense resonance
networks lead to preferred spatial structures.

\end{theorem}

\section{Scaling Law for Multi-Shell Resonance Energy}

\subsection{Multi-shell configuration}

Let
\begin{equation}
S=\bigcup_{a=1}^{K} S_a
\end{equation}
be a union of shells
\begin{equation}
S_a \subset S^2_{q_a}, \qquad S_a=-S_a, \qquad |S_a|=N_a .
\end{equation}

Define the total size
\begin{equation}
N=\sum_{a=1}^{K} N_a .
\end{equation}

\subsection{Cross-shell pair sums}

For shells $S_a,S_b$ define
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)=\#\{(x,y)\in S_a\times S_b : x+y=v\}.
\end{equation}

The total multiplicity is
\begin{equation}
r(v)=\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Multi-shell resonance functional}

Define
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}(S)=\sum_v r(v)^2 .
\end{equation}

Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}(S)
=
\left|
\{(a,b,c,d)\in S^4 : a+b=c+d\}
\right|.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Generalized fiber circles}

For shells $S_a,S_b$ and vector $v$ define
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{ab}(v)
=
\{x\in S^2_{q_a} : |v-x|=q_b\}.
\end{equation}

Define occupancy
\begin{equation}
n_{ab}(v)=|S_a\cap \mathcal F_{ab}(v)|.
\end{equation}

Then
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)\le n_{ab}(v).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Incidence bound}

Let
\begin{equation}
n_{\max}=\max_{a,b,v} n_{ab}(v).
\end{equation}

Then
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r(v) \le N(N-2)
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}(S)
=
\sum_v r(v)^2
\le
n_{\max} N(N-2).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Multi-shell scaling theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Quadratic scaling]

Let $S=\bigcup_a S_a$ be a union of shells with total size $N$.

If maximizers satisfy bounded generalized fiber occupancy
\begin{equation}
n_{\max}=O(1),
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi,max}}(N)=\Theta(N^2).
\end{equation}

\end{theorem}

\subsection{Contribution decomposition}

Expanding
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}(S)
=
\sum_{a,b,c,d}
\sum_v r_{ab}(v) r_{cd}(v),
\end{equation}
reveals three types of contributions

\begin{itemize}

\item intra-shell
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r_{aa}(v)^2
\end{equation}

\item cross-shell
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r_{ab}(v)^2
\end{equation}

\item interference
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r_{ab}(v) r_{cd}(v)
\end{equation}

\end{itemize}

These terms encode the resonance network across shells.

\subsection{Structure selection principle}

For shell-restricted Fourier modes
\begin{equation}
\psi(x)=\sum_{a}\sum_{q\in S_a} A_q e^{iq\cdot x},
\end{equation}
quartic interactions produce energy contributions proportional to
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}(S).
\end{equation}

Hence the selected structure satisfies
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{selected}}
=
\arg\max_{S}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}(S).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Unified structure selection theorem}

\begin{theorem}

Let $S$ be a union of antipodal shells in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

The spatial structure generated by shell-restricted Fourier modes
is determined by maximizing the resonance functional

\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S)=\sum_v r(v)^2 .
\end{equation}

Single-shell and multi-shell patterns arise as extremizers of this
functional under different shell configurations.

\end{theorem}

\section{Quasicrystal Resonance Networks}

\subsection{Multi-shell wavevector sets}

Let
\begin{equation}
S = \bigcup_{a=1}^{K} S_a
\end{equation}
be a union of shells
\begin{equation}
S_a \subset S^2_{q_a}, \qquad S_a=-S_a .
\end{equation}

Define the shell ratio
\begin{equation}
\rho_{ab}=\frac{q_b}{q_a}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Resonant quartets}

Resonant quartets satisfy
\begin{equation}
a+b=c+d
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
a\in S_i,\quad b\in S_j,\quad c\in S_k,\quad d\in S_l .
\end{equation}

Taking norms yields
\begin{equation}
q_i^2+q_j^2+2q_iq_j\cos\theta_{ab}
=
q_k^2+q_l^2+2q_kq_l\cos\theta_{cd}.
\end{equation}

Thus resonances correspond to matching dot products across shells.

\subsection{Angle degeneracy}

Let
\begin{equation}
a\cdot b = q_i q_j \cos\theta .
\end{equation}

Resonance multiplicity increases when many vector pairs share the same
angle $\theta$.

This occurs naturally in sets of vectors arranged with rotational
symmetry.

\subsection{Regular polygon construction}

Consider vectors arranged on a circle with rotational symmetry $n$.

Let successive edges alternate between shells $q_1$ and $q_2$.
Closure of the polygon requires

\begin{equation}
q_1^2 + q_2^2 + 2 q_1 q_2 \cos\left(\frac{2\pi}{n}\right)
=
(q_1+q_2)^2 .
\end{equation}

This yields the shell ratio

\begin{equation}
\rho = \frac{q_2}{q_1} = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Quasicrystal ratios}

Important ratios include

\begin{itemize}

\item $n=8$
\begin{equation}
\rho = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{8}\right)
\end{equation}

\item $n=10$
\begin{equation}
\rho = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{10}\right)
\end{equation}

\item $n=12$
\begin{equation}
\rho = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{12}\right)
\end{equation}

\end{itemize}

These ratios correspond to octagonal, decagonal, and dodecagonal
quasicrystalline resonance networks.

\subsection{Resonance functional}

Define
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)=\#\{(x,y)\in S_a\times S_b : x+y=v\}.
\end{equation}

The resonance functional is

\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S)=
\sum_v
\left(\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)\right)^2 .
\end{equation}

\subsection{Quasicrystal selection theorem}

\begin{theorem}

Let $S=\bigcup_a S_a$ be a multi-shell configuration.

If the shell radii satisfy

\begin{equation}
\frac{q_2}{q_1}=2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right)
\end{equation}

for some integer $n$, then the resulting resonance network contains
enhanced quartic degeneracies.

Such configurations maximize the resonance functional

\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S)
\end{equation}

and therefore correspond to energetically preferred quasicrystalline
structures.

\end{theorem}

\subsection{Unified selection principle}

Combining the previous results, spatial structure formation is governed
by the maximization problem

\begin{equation}
S_{\text{selected}}
=
\arg\max_S \Lambda(S).
\end{equation}

Single-shell crystals, multi-shell crystals, and quasicrystals all arise
as extremizers of this resonance functional under different geometric
constraints.

\section{Classification of Extremizers of the Resonance Functional}

\subsection{Optimization domains}

We fix an ambient dimension $d=3$ and consider configurations of wavevectors
\begin{equation}
S \subset \mathbb{R}^3
\end{equation}
subject to geometric constraints.

\paragraph{Single-shell domain.}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_{1}(N;q_0)
:=
\{S\subset S^2_{q_0}:\ S=-S,\ |S|=N\}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Multi-shell domain.}
Let radii $q_1,\dots,q_K$ be fixed and write
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_{K}(\mathbf N;\mathbf q)
:=
\left\{
S=\bigcup_{a=1}^{K} S_a:\ 
S_a\subset S^2_{q_a},\
S_a=-S_a,\
|S_a|=N_a
\right\},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf N=(N_1,\dots,N_K)$ and $\mathbf q=(q_1,\dots,q_K)$.

\subsection{The resonance functional}

For any finite $S\subset\mathbb{R}^3$ define
\begin{equation}
r(v)=\#\{(x,y)\in S\times S:\ x+y=v\},
\qquad
\Lambda(S)=\sum_v r(v)^2.
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S)
=
\left|
\{(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)\in S^4:\ x_1+x_2=x_3+x_4\}
\right|.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Extremizers}

\begin{definition}[Extremizer]
An extremizer in a domain $\mathcal D$ is any $S^\ast\in\mathcal D$ such that
\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S^\ast)=\sup_{S\in\mathcal D}\Lambda(S).
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Structural parameters: fiber circles and occupancy}

For a single shell $S\subset S^2_{q_0}$, define the fiber circle at $v$ by
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F(v)=\{x\in S^2_{q_0}:\ |v-x|=q_0\}.
\end{equation}
For multi-shell pairs $(a,b)$ define the generalized fiber
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{ab}(v)=\{x\in S^2_{q_a}:\ |v-x|=q_b\}.
\end{equation}

Define occupancies
\begin{equation}
n(v)=|S\cap\mathcal F(v)|,
\qquad
n_{ab}(v)=|S_a\cap\mathcal F_{ab}(v)|,
\end{equation}
and the maximal occupancy
\begin{equation}
n_{\max}(S):=\max_v n(v),
\qquad
n_{\max}^{(K)}(S):=\max_{a,b,v} n_{ab}(v).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Finite candidate reduction: a conditional classification template}

The classification problem is to describe the set of extremizers (up to rotation)
and the extremal value as a function of $N$ (or $\mathbf N$).

A general template proceeds in two steps:

\begin{enumerate}
\item (Rigidity) show that extremizers obey a strong structural constraint
(e.g.\ bounded $n_{\max}$ or a rigid circle arrangement).
\item (Enumeration) reduce the admissible Gram matrices to a finite list,
then compute $\Lambda$ on that list.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{theorem}[Candidate reduction, conditional form]\label{thm:candidate_reduction_conditional}
Fix a domain $\mathcal D$ (single- or multi-shell) and suppose there exists
a structural constraint $\mathcal P(S)$ such that:

\begin{enumerate}
\item every extremizer $S^\ast$ satisfies $\mathcal P(S^\ast)$, and
\item the constraint $\mathcal P$ implies that $S$ belongs to a finite
rotation-equivalence class family $\mathcal S_{\mathrm{rigid}}(\mathcal D)$.
\end{enumerate}

Then
\begin{equation}
\sup_{S\in\mathcal D}\Lambda(S)
=
\max_{S\in\mathcal S_{\mathrm{rigid}}(\mathcal D)} \Lambda(S),
\end{equation}
and extremizers are exactly the maximizers within $\mathcal S_{\mathrm{rigid}}(\mathcal D)$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Single-shell classification conjecture}

\begin{conjecture}[Single-shell extremizers are rigid]\label{conj:single_shell_rigid}
For each $N$, every extremizer in $\mathcal{D}_1(N;q_0)$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
n_{\max}(S^\ast)=O(1)
\end{equation}
with an absolute constant independent of $N$.
Consequently extremizers belong to a finite rigid family
consisting of lattice-shell stars, polyhedral stars, and finitely many exceptions.
\end{conjecture}

\subsection{Multi-shell ratio-locking and quasicrystal families}

In multi-shell settings, the radii ratios $\rho_{ab}=q_b/q_a$ influence the density of
quartic resonances through angle-locking constraints.

\begin{definition}[Ratio-locking set]
A ratio $\rho$ is called \emph{locked} at order $n$ if
\begin{equation}
\rho = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right)
\end{equation}
and the induced cross-shell resonance graph admits a large family of closed
alternating polygons of length $2n$ in wavevector space.
\end{definition}

\begin{conjecture}[Quasicrystal extremizer families]\label{conj:qc_extremizers}
Fix $K=2$ shells with $q_2/q_1=\rho$.
If $\rho$ is locked at order $n\in\{8,10,12\}$, then there exist multi-shell extremizers
whose resonance networks realize $n$-fold (octagonal, decagonal, dodecagonal) quasicrystalline symmetry.
Moreover, for such locked ratios, extremizers belong to a finite rigid family determined by the
allowed polygonal resonance motifs.
\end{conjecture}

\subsection{What can be closed immediately vs.\ what remains open}

\paragraph{Closed for specific $N$.}
For small $N$ (e.g.\ $N=12$ in the single-shell domain), a complete classification may be achieved
by explicit rigidity plus finite enumeration.

\paragraph{Open in general.}
An unconditional proof of Conjecture~\ref{conj:single_shell_rigid} is an inverse-type statement
in the spirit of additive combinatorics on constrained manifolds (here, spheres and spherical shells).
Similarly, Conjecture~\ref{conj:qc_extremizers} requires a quantitative control of cross-shell
angle degeneracies induced by locked ratios.

\subsection{Roadmap for a full classification theorem}

A full classification theorem can be organized as:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Prove a \emph{rigidity lemma} for extremizers (bounded occupancy or rigid circle arrangements).
\item Prove a \emph{finite Gram list lemma} under the rigidity hypothesis.
\item Enumerate candidates and compute $\Lambda$ exactly on the finite list.
\item Identify symmetry classes (lattice, polyhedral, quasicrystal) that realize the maxima.
\end{enumerate}

\section{General Multi-Shell Structural Selection (K\,$\ge$\,3)}

\subsection{K-shell amplitude model and quartic form}

Let shells be indexed by $a=1,\dots,K$ with radii $q_a$ and stars $S_a\subset S^2_{q_a}$.
Assume antipodality $S_a=-S_a$ and let $N_a=|S_a|$.

Introduce shell amplitudes $A_a\ge 0$ (equal-amplitude within each shell, but shell-to-shell free).
The Landau functional takes the generic form
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F(\mathbf A)
=
\sum_{a=1}^K r_a A_a^2
+
\frac14\sum_{a,b=1}^K U_{ab} A_a^2 A_b^2
+
\frac16\sum_{a=1}^K W_a A_a^6,
\qquad
\mathbf A=(A_1,\dots,A_K).
\label{eq:Kshell_F}
\end{equation}
Here $U_{ab}=U_{ba}$ encodes quartic resonant degeneracy (intra- and inter-shell),
and $W_a>0$ stabilizes first-order transitions but is \emph{structure-neutral} if it depends only on $a$.

\subsection{Structure dependence via resonance invariants}

For each shell-star pair define the resonance multiplicities
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)=\#\{(x,y)\in S_a\times S_b:\ x+y=v\}.
\end{equation}
Define the multi-shell resonance functional
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}(S_1,\dots,S_K)
=
\sum_v\Big(\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)\Big)^2
=
\sum_{a,b,c,d}\sum_v r_{ab}(v)\,r_{cd}(v).
\label{eq:Lambda_multi}
\end{equation}
In equal-amplitude-per-shell reductions, the structure dependence enters $U_{ab}$ through
shell-resolved resonance densities (normalized by $N_a,N_b$), i.e.\ invariants of the form
\begin{equation}
C_{ab}:=\frac{1}{N_a N_b}\sum_v r_{ab}(v)^2,
\qquad
C_{abcd}:=\frac{1}{N_a N_b N_c N_d}\sum_v r_{ab}(v)r_{cd}(v),
\end{equation}
with $U_{ab}$ expressed as an explicit linear combination of these invariants
(depending on the microscopic vertex class).

\subsection{Reduction to a quadratic program in shell intensities}

Let $x_a:=A_a^2\ge 0$.
Then \eqref{eq:Kshell_F} becomes
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F(\mathbf x)
=
\sum_a r_a x_a
+
\frac14\,\mathbf x^\top U\,\mathbf x
+
\frac16\sum_a W_a x_a^3,
\qquad
\mathbf x=(x_1,\dots,x_K)\in\mathbb R_{\ge 0}^K.
\label{eq:Kshell_F_x}
\end{equation}
In the fluctuation-dominated quartic regime, the leading selection problem is governed by
\begin{equation}
\min_{\mathbf x\ge 0}\ \Big(\sum_a r_a x_a + \frac14\,\mathbf x^\top U\,\mathbf x\Big),
\end{equation}
with higher-order terms controlling metastability/first-order behavior but not the ordering of minimizers
when $W_a$ is structure-neutral.

\subsection{Mixed-phase existence: support characterization}

A \emph{phase} is characterized by its active shell set
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{supp}(\mathbf x)=\{a:\ x_a>0\}.
\end{equation}
For any subset $I\subset\{1,\dots,K\}$, define the restricted matrix $U_I$ and vector $r_I$.
Assuming $U_I$ is invertible, the stationary point in the interior of $I$ is
\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast = -2\,U_I^{-1} r_I,
\label{eq:interior_stationary}
\end{equation}
and it is feasible iff $x_I^\ast>0$ componentwise.
Therefore, the mixed-phase existence problem reduces to:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\exists\,I\ \text{with}\ |I|\ge 2\ \text{s.t.}\ U_I\succ 0\ \text{and}\ -U_I^{-1}r_I>0.
}
\label{eq:mixed_phase_exist}
\end{equation}

\subsection{No-inversion / inversion as a comparison of restricted minima}

For each feasible support $I$, define the restricted minimum value
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_I^\ast
=
\min_{\mathbf x_I\ge 0}\ \Big(r_I^\top x_I + \frac14 x_I^\top U_I x_I\Big)
=
-\ r_I^\top U_I^{-1} r_I
\qquad
(\text{for interior feasible }x_I^\ast).
\label{eq:F_I_star}
\end{equation}
Then global selection is the comparison problem
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{argmin}_I\ \mathcal F_I^\ast,
\end{equation}
and \emph{inversion} occurs when the minimizing support changes order relative to the single-shell ranking
as cross-couplings $U_{ab}$ (or resonance ratios) vary.

\subsection{Multi-shell strong resonance}

Let a set of resonance ratios make several cross-couplings simultaneously near their saturation bounds,
so that a block of $U$ approaches marginality.
Define a strong-resonance region by
\begin{equation}
\lambda_{\min}(U_I)\approx 0
\quad\text{for some}\quad |I|\ge 2,
\end{equation}
and characterize the transition surface by the condition that two supports tie:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{I_1}^\ast = \mathcal F_{I_2}^\ast,
\qquad
\mathcal F_{I_1}^\ast \le \mathcal F_{J}^\ast\ \forall J.
\end{equation}
This provides explicit codimension-one critical boundaries in the space of couplings and ratios.

\subsection{Deliverables}

A complete K-shell closure consists of:
(i) explicit formulas for $U_{ab}$ in terms of resonance invariants,
(ii) feasibility characterization \eqref{eq:mixed_phase_exist},
(iii) closed-form minima \eqref{eq:F_I_star} for all supports $I$,
(iv) explicit inversion/no-inversion boundaries from support comparisons,
(v) strong-resonance critical hypersurfaces from marginality and tie conditions.

\section{Multi-Shell Mixed-Phase Theorem (General $K$)}

\subsection{Quadratic (quartic-dominated) shell-intensity program}

Let $K\ge 2$. Consider shell intensities
\begin{equation}
x_a := A_a^2 \ge 0,
\qquad
a=1,\dots,K,
\end{equation}
and the quartic-dominated reduced objective
\begin{equation}
\mathcal Q(x)
:=
r^\top x + \frac14\, x^\top U x,
\qquad
x\in\mathbb R_{\ge 0}^K,
\label{eq:Q_def}
\end{equation}
where $r\in\mathbb R^K$ and $U\in\mathbb R^{K\times K}$ is symmetric.
(Structure dependence enters through $U$ via resonance invariants; this is suppressed here.)

We study the global minimization problem
\begin{equation}
\mathcal Q^\ast := \min_{x\ge 0} \mathcal Q(x).
\label{eq:global_min_problem}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Support-restricted stationary points}

For any nonempty index set $I\subset\{1,\dots,K\}$, let $x_I$ denote the restriction
and $U_I,r_I$ the principal submatrix / subvector.

Assume $U_I$ is invertible. The interior stationary point on the face
\begin{equation}
\{x\ge 0:\ \mathrm{supp}(x)\subset I\}
\end{equation}
is
\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast = -2\,U_I^{-1} r_I.
\label{eq:xI_star}
\end{equation}
It is \emph{feasible} (a mixed phase on $I$) iff
\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast>0
\quad\text{(componentwise)}.
\label{eq:feasible_condition}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Closed-form value on a feasible support}

If \eqref{eq:feasible_condition} holds, then the restricted minimum value is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal Q_I^\ast
:=
\min_{\substack{x\ge 0\\ \mathrm{supp}(x)\subset I}} \mathcal Q(x)
=
\mathcal Q(x^\ast)
=
-\, r_I^\top U_I^{-1} r_I.
\label{eq:QI_star}
\end{equation}

\subsection{KKT characterization (closed)}

Global minimizers of \eqref{eq:global_min_problem} are characterized by the KKT system:
there exists a Lagrange multiplier vector $\lambda\in\mathbb R_{\ge 0}^K$ such that
\begin{equation}
r + \frac12 U x - \lambda = 0,
\qquad
x\ge 0,
\qquad
\lambda\ge 0,
\qquad
x_a\lambda_a=0\ \ \forall a.
\label{eq:KKT}
\end{equation}

Let $I=\{a:\ x_a>0\}$ be the active support. Then \eqref{eq:KKT} implies
\begin{equation}
r_I + \frac12 U_I x_I = 0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
x_I = -2 U_I^{-1} r_I,
\label{eq:KKT_interior_solution}
\end{equation}
and for inactive indices $J=I^c$,
\begin{equation}
\lambda_J
=
r_J + \frac12 U_{J I} x_I
=
r_J - U_{J I} U_I^{-1} r_I
\ \ge\ 0.
\label{eq:KKT_dual_feasibility}
\end{equation}

Therefore, a given support $I$ is the support of a global minimizer iff:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\text{(primal feasibility)} && x_I^\ast=-2U_I^{-1}r_I>0,\\
&\text{(dual feasibility)} && r_J - U_{J I}U_I^{-1}r_I \ge 0\ \ \text{for }J=I^c.
\end{aligned}}
\label{eq:support_optimality_conditions}
\end{equation}

These conditions are \emph{closed} and require no enumeration beyond supports.

\subsection{Mixed-phase existence theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Mixed-phase existence and identification]\label{thm:mixed_phase_existence}
Assume $U_I$ is invertible for all nonempty $I$.
For any nonempty $I\subset\{1,\dots,K\}$ define
\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast=-2U_I^{-1}r_I.
\end{equation}
Then there exists a mixed phase with support $I$ (i.e.\ a local minimizer on that face) iff
\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast>0.
\end{equation}
Moreover, $I$ is the support of a \emph{global} minimizer of \eqref{eq:global_min_problem}
iff \eqref{eq:support_optimality_conditions} holds.
In that case the global minimum value is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal Q^\ast=\mathcal Q_I^\ast=-r_I^\top U_I^{-1}r_I.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Inversion / no-inversion boundaries}

Let $I_1,I_2$ be two feasible supports (both satisfying $x_{I_s}^\ast>0$).
A phase boundary between $I_1$ and $I_2$ occurs when their values tie:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal Q_{I_1}^\ast=\mathcal Q_{I_2}^\ast
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
r_{I_1}^\top U_{I_1}^{-1} r_{I_1}
=
r_{I_2}^\top U_{I_2}^{-1} r_{I_2}.
\label{eq:tie_surface}
\end{equation}
This provides an explicit codimension-one hypersurface in the parameter space of $(r,U)$.

Inversion relative to a baseline ordering (e.g.\ single-shell ranking)
is precisely the occurrence of \eqref{eq:tie_surface} as parameters are varied.

\subsection{Multi-shell strong resonance and marginality}

A strong-resonance regime corresponds to near-marginality of some block $U_I$:
\begin{equation}
\lambda_{\min}(U_I)\approx 0
\end{equation}
for some feasible $I$.
In that regime, the stationary point $x_I^\ast=-2U_I^{-1}r_I$ becomes large and highly sensitive
to parameter perturbations, generating sharp phase boundaries and possible cascades of tie events.

\subsection{Structure-neutrality of sixth-order stabilization}

Consider the stabilized functional
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F(x)
=
r^\top x + \frac14 x^\top U x + \frac16\sum_{a=1}^K W_a x_a^3,
\qquad
W_a>0.
\label{eq:stabilized_F}
\end{equation}
If $W_a$ depends only on the shell index $a$ (and not on the star geometry within a shell),
then $W$ is \emph{structure-neutral}:
for fixed $(r,U)$, the ordering of candidate supports determined by \eqref{eq:QI_star} and
\eqref{eq:tie_surface} persists for sufficiently small amplitudes (near the quartic-dominated regime),
with the sixth-order term only shifting transition locations (e.g.\ first-order vs.\ second-order)
without changing which support is selected.

\subsection{Summary}

The multi-shell selection problem in the quartic-dominated regime reduces to:
\begin{enumerate}
\item compute $(r,U)$ from microscopic coefficients and resonance invariants,
\item enumerate supports $I$ (finite, $2^K-1$ possibilities),
\item test feasibility and global optimality via \eqref{eq:support_optimality_conditions},
\item compare values via $\mathcal Q_I^\ast=-r_I^\top U_I^{-1}r_I$,
\item obtain phase boundaries from tie surfaces \eqref{eq:tie_surface}.
\end{enumerate}
This provides a closed algebraic characterization of mixed phases, inversion, and strong resonance
for arbitrary $K$.


\section{Appendix G. General Multi-Shell Structural Selection}

\subsection{K-shell amplitude representation}

Let shells be indexed by
\begin{equation}
a=1,\dots,K
\end{equation}

with wavevector stars
\begin{equation}
S_a \subset S^2_{q_a}, \qquad S_a=-S_a .
\end{equation}

Define shell amplitudes

\begin{equation}
A_a \ge 0 .
\end{equation}

Introduce shell intensities

\begin{equation}
x_a = A_a^2 .
\end{equation}

The quartic Landau functional takes the form

\begin{equation}
F(x)
=
\sum_{a=1}^K r_a x_a
+
\frac14
\sum_{a,b=1}^K
U_{ab} x_a x_b .
\label{G1}
\end{equation}

Here

\begin{itemize}

\item $r_a$ are quadratic coefficients

\item $U_{ab}=U_{ba}$ encode quartic resonance degeneracies

\end{itemize}

Structure dependence enters only through $U_{ab}$.

\subsection{Resonance representation of $U_{ab}$}

Let

\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)
=
\#\{(x,y)\in S_a \times S_b : x+y=v\}.
\end{equation}

Define normalized resonance invariants

\begin{equation}
C_{ab}
=
\frac{1}{N_a N_b}
\sum_v r_{ab}(v)^2 .
\end{equation}

Then

\begin{equation}
U_{ab}
=
\alpha C_{ab}
+
\beta \delta_{ab}
\label{G2}
\end{equation}

for model-dependent constants $\alpha,\beta$.

\subsection{Quadratic program}

Define

\begin{equation}
Q(x)
=
r^T x
+
\frac14 x^T U x .
\label{G3}
\end{equation}

The structural selection problem is

\begin{equation}
\min_{x \ge 0} Q(x).
\label{G4}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Support-restricted solution}

For any support

\begin{equation}
I \subset \{1,\dots,K\}
\end{equation}

let

\begin{equation}
U_I , r_I
\end{equation}

be the restricted matrices.

The stationary solution is

\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast
=
-2 U_I^{-1} r_I .
\label{G5}
\end{equation}

Feasibility requires

\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast > 0 .
\label{G6}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Closed-form free energy}

The minimum value on support $I$ is

\begin{equation}
F_I^\ast
=
- r_I^T U_I^{-1} r_I .
\label{G7}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Global optimality condition}

Let

\begin{equation}
J = I^c .
\end{equation}

Then $I$ is globally optimal if

\begin{equation}
r_J - U_{JI} U_I^{-1} r_I \ge 0 .
\label{G8}
\end{equation}

Equations (G5)-(G8) completely determine the selected shell set.

\subsection{Mixed-phase theorem}

\begin{theorem}

Let $U_I$ be invertible for all $I$.

Then a mixed phase with support $I$ exists iff

\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast > 0 .
\end{equation}

Moreover $I$ is the global minimum iff

\begin{equation}
r_J - U_{JI} U_I^{-1} r_I \ge 0 .
\end{equation}

\end{theorem}

\subsection{Phase boundary}

Two supports $I_1,I_2$ exchange stability when

\begin{equation}
F_{I_1}^\ast
=
F_{I_2}^\ast .
\label{G9}
\end{equation}

Thus phase boundaries correspond to

\begin{equation}
r_{I_1}^T U_{I_1}^{-1} r_{I_1}
=
r_{I_2}^T U_{I_2}^{-1} r_{I_2}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Strong resonance regime}

Strong resonance occurs when

\begin{equation}
\lambda_{\min}(U_I) \to 0 .
\end{equation}

In this regime

\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast = -2U_I^{-1} r_I
\end{equation}

becomes large and small perturbations in $(r,U)$ generate phase switching.

\subsection{Structure neutrality of sixth order terms}

Consider stabilization

\begin{equation}
F_6
=
F
+
\frac16
\sum_a W_a x_a^3 .
\end{equation}

If $W_a$ depends only on shell index $a$, then ordering of $F_I^\ast$ remains unchanged near the quartic regime.

\subsection{General multi-shell selection theorem}

\begin{theorem}

For any number of shells $K$, structural selection reduces to the finite comparison

\begin{equation}
F^\ast
=
\min_I
\left(
- r_I^T U_I^{-1} r_I
\right).
\end{equation}

Thus the globally selected pattern corresponds to the support $I$
that maximizes resonance degeneracy encoded in $U_{ab}$.

\end{theorem}

\section{Explicit Evaluation of $U_{ab}$ for Representative Structures}

\subsection{Resonance invariants}

Let $S_a$ denote the star on shell $q_a$ with $|S_a|=N_a$.
Define
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)=\#\{(x,y)\in S_a\times S_b:\ x+y=v\},
\end{equation}
and the normalized invariant
\begin{equation}
C_{ab}=\frac{1}{N_aN_b}\sum_v r_{ab}(v)^2 .
\end{equation}

In quartic amplitude reductions, the interaction matrix can be written as
\begin{equation}
U_{ab}=\alpha C_{ab}+\beta\delta_{ab},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha,\beta$ depend on the microscopic vertex but the geometry
enters only through $C_{ab}$.

\subsection{Lamellar structure}

The lamellar star consists of two opposite vectors
\begin{equation}
S=\{\pm q\},
\qquad N=2.
\end{equation}

Pair sums yield
\begin{equation}
q+q=2q,\qquad
q-q=0.
\end{equation}

The multiplicities are
\begin{equation}
r(2q)=1,\quad r(0)=2,\quad r(-2q)=1 .
\end{equation}

Thus
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r(v)^2 = 6,
\end{equation}
and the invariant becomes
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{lam}}=\frac{6}{4}=\frac{3}{2}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Hexagonal structure}

The hexagonal star contains six vectors forming a regular hexagon
\begin{equation}
N=6 .
\end{equation}

Explicit counting of pair sums gives
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r(v)^2 = 90 .
\end{equation}

Hence
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{hex}}=\frac{90}{36}=\frac{5}{2}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{BCC reciprocal star}

The BCC reciprocal shell corresponds to the cuboctahedral star
\begin{equation}
N=12 .
\end{equation}

Direct enumeration yields
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r(v)^2 = 540 .
\end{equation}

Thus
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{BCC}}=\frac{540}{144}=\frac{15}{4}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Relative ordering}

The normalized resonance densities satisfy
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{BCC}}
>
C_{\mathrm{hex}}
>
C_{\mathrm{lam}} .
\end{equation}

Therefore quartic resonance degeneracy increases with the connectivity
of the star.

\subsection{Two-shell interaction}

For two shells with stars $S_1,S_2$ define
\begin{equation}
C_{12}=\frac{1}{N_1N_2}\sum_v r_{12}(v)^2 .
\end{equation}

The quartic interaction matrix becomes
\begin{equation}
U=
\begin{pmatrix}
\alpha C_{11}+\beta & \alpha C_{12} \\
\alpha C_{12} & \alpha C_{22}+\beta
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Two-shell mixed phase}

Let
\begin{equation}
r=(r_1,r_2).
\end{equation}

The mixed solution is
\begin{equation}
x^\ast=-2U^{-1}r .
\end{equation}

Explicitly
\begin{equation}
x_1^\ast=
\frac{-2}{\det U}
\left(
U_{22} r_1 - U_{12} r_2
\right),
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
x_2^\ast=
\frac{-2}{\det U}
\left(
U_{11} r_2 - U_{12} r_1
\right).
\end{equation}

Feasibility requires
\begin{equation}
x_1^\ast>0,
\qquad
x_2^\ast>0.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Free energy comparison}

The mixed-phase free energy is

\begin{equation}
F_{12}^\ast
=
- r^T U^{-1} r.
\end{equation}

Single-shell energies are

\begin{equation}
F_1^\ast = -\frac{r_1^2}{U_{11}},
\qquad
F_2^\ast = -\frac{r_2^2}{U_{22}}.
\end{equation}

The selected phase is

\begin{equation}
F^\ast
=
\min(F_1^\ast,F_2^\ast,F_{12}^\ast).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Structure selection rule}

Structures with larger resonance invariant $C$ generate larger
quartic degeneracy and therefore lower free energy in the
quartic-dominated regime.

Consequently the ordering

\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{BCC}}
>
C_{\mathrm{hex}}
>
C_{\mathrm{lam}}
\end{equation}

explains the stability hierarchy observed in block-copolymer
and pattern-forming systems.

\section{Exact Optimality of the $N=12$ BCC Star}

\subsection{Problem formulation}

Let
\begin{equation}
S \subset S^2
\end{equation}
be an antipodal set

\begin{equation}
S=-S,
\qquad
|S|=12.
\end{equation}

Define the additive-energy functional

\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S)
=
\sum_v r(v)^2,
\qquad
r(v)=\#\{(a,b)\in S\times S:\ a+b=v\}.
\end{equation}

The goal is to determine

\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\max}(12)
=
\max_{|S|=12} \Lambda(S).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Fiber circle structure}

If

\begin{equation}

\section{Structural Design Theory: Inverse Resonance Engineering}

\subsection{Design objective as a constrained optimization}

Let the target structure be specified by a multi-shell star family
\begin{equation}
S^{\mathrm{tar}}=\bigcup_{a=1}^{K} S_a^{\mathrm{tar}},
\qquad
S_a^{\mathrm{tar}}\subset S^2_{q_a},
\qquad
S_a^{\mathrm{tar}}=-S_a^{\mathrm{tar}}.
\end{equation}
We seek physical control parameters $\Theta$ (shell radii, interaction kernel, symmetry-breaking knobs)
such that the target becomes the global minimizer of the effective free energy.

\subsection{Weighted resonance functional (designable network)}

Define cross-shell pair-sum multiplicities
\begin{equation}
r_{ab}(v)=\#\{(x,y)\in S_a\times S_b:\ x+y=v\}.
\end{equation}

Introduce a designable weight tensor $W_{ab,cd}(v)\ge 0$ encoding microscopic vertex weights,
angular selectivity, and shell-dependent coupling strengths. Define the weighted resonance energy
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_W(S)
=
\sum_v
\sum_{a,b,c,d}
W_{ab,cd}(v)\,
r_{ab}(v)\,r_{cd}(v).
\label{eq:Lambda_weighted}
\end{equation}

The unweighted geometry-only case corresponds to $W_{ab,cd}(v)\equiv 1$.

\subsection{Amplitude reduction and designable quartic form}

Assume equal amplitude within each shell and define intensities $x_a=A_a^2\ge 0$.
The quartic-dominated reduced functional takes the form
\begin{equation}
F(x;\Theta)
=
r(\Theta)^\top x
+
\frac14\,x^\top U(\Theta)\,x,
\qquad x\in\mathbb R_{\ge 0}^K,
\label{eq:design_quartic_form}
\end{equation}
where $U(\Theta)$ is determined by weighted resonance invariants derived from \eqref{eq:Lambda_weighted}.
Formally,
\begin{equation}
U_{ab}(\Theta)
=
\sum_{c,d}\sum_v
\Xi_{ab,cd}(v;\Theta)\,
\frac{r_{ac}(v)\,r_{bd}(v)}{N_aN_b},
\label{eq:U_from_weights}
\end{equation}
for a model-dependent tensor $\Xi$ determined by the microscopic vertex class and kernel.

\subsection{Closed-form phase selection (support theorem)}

For any support $I\subset\{1,\dots,K\}$, let $U_I,r_I$ denote restrictions.
If $U_I$ is invertible, the stationary solution is
\begin{equation}
x_I^\ast(\Theta)=-2\,U_I(\Theta)^{-1}\,r_I(\Theta),
\end{equation}
feasible iff $x_I^\ast>0$ componentwise. The restricted minimum value is
\begin{equation}
F_I^\ast(\Theta)
=
-\,r_I(\Theta)^\top U_I(\Theta)^{-1} r_I(\Theta).
\label{eq:F_I_star_design}
\end{equation}
The globally selected support is
\begin{equation}
I^\ast(\Theta)\in \arg\min_I F_I^\ast(\Theta).
\label{eq:support_global_design}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Inverse design condition for a target}

Let the target be associated with a desired active-support set $I_{\mathrm{tar}}$
and target stars $S_a^{\mathrm{tar}}$ on shells $a\in I_{\mathrm{tar}}$.
A sufficient (algorithmically checkable) inverse-design condition is:
\begin{equation}
F_{I_{\mathrm{tar}}}^\ast(\Theta) < F_I^\ast(\Theta)
\qquad
\forall I\neq I_{\mathrm{tar}}
\label{eq:inverse_design_inequality}
\end{equation}
together with feasibility $x_{I_{\mathrm{tar}}}^\ast(\Theta)>0$.

This reduces inverse design to an explicit inequality system over $\Theta$.

\subsection{Three design levers (engineering knobs)}

\paragraph{(1) Multi-shell engineering (length-scale design).}
Choose radii $\{q_a\}$ (or ratios $\rho_{ab}=q_b/q_a$) to increase target cross-shell degeneracies.
Locked ratios such as $\rho=2\cos(\pi/n)$ generate dense alternating-polygon motifs and enhance
specific $r_{ab}(v)$.

\paragraph{(2) Kernel weight tuning (interaction engineering).}
Select $\Theta$ to shape $W_{ab,cd}(v)$ in \eqref{eq:Lambda_weighted}, thereby modifying $U(\Theta)$.
This can privilege chosen cross-shell channels $(a,b)\leftrightarrow(c,d)$ and suppress competitors,
realizing \eqref{eq:inverse_design_inequality} for a desired target.

\paragraph{(3) Symmetry breaking (triad activation).}
If $\mathbb Z_2$ symmetry is intentionally broken, cubic terms appear:
\begin{equation}
F_3 \sim -\sum_{u} T(u)^2,
\qquad
T(u)=\#\{(a,b,c):a+b+c=u\},
\end{equation}
yielding a combined design objective
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{eff}}\sim
-\gamma_3 \Lambda_3(S)
-\gamma_4 \Lambda_4(S),
\end{equation}
enabling engineered competition between triad and quartet networks and admitting complex phases
(e.g.\ Frank--Kasper families) as inverse-designed solutions.

\subsection{Inverse design algorithm (closed mapping)}

Given a target $S^{\mathrm{tar}}$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute target resonance signatures:
\begin{equation}

=
\{r_{ab}^{\mathrm{tar}}(v)\}_{a,b,v},
\qquad

\section{Universal Additive Upper Bound and Rigidity}

\subsection{Additive energy on a spherical star}

Let $S\subset S^2_{q_0}\subset\mathbb R^3$ be a finite antipodal set:
\begin{equation}

\end{equation}
Define pair-sum multiplicities and additive energy
\begin{equation}
r(v)=\#\{(a,b)\in S\times S:\ a+b=v\},
\qquad
\Lambda(S)=\sum_v r(v)^2.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $\Lambda(S)$ counts additive quadruples
\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S)=\#\{(a,b,c,d)\in S^4:\ a+b=c+d\}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Trivial bounds and the extremal regime}

We always have
\begin{equation}
\sum_v r(v)=N^2,
\qquad
\Lambda(S)\ge \frac{(\sum_v r(v))^2}{|\{v\}|}=\frac{N^4}{|\{v\}|}.
\end{equation}
The extremal problem is
\begin{equation}
\Lambda_{\max}(N)=\sup_{|S|=N,\ S=-S}\Lambda(S),
\qquad
S^\ast\in \arg\max \Lambda(S).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Difference formulation}

Define difference multiplicities
\begin{equation}
d(u)=\#\{(a,c)\in S\times S:\ a-c=u\}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
\Lambda(S)=\sum_u d(u)^2.
\label{eq:Lambda_diff}
\end{equation}
Hence large $\Lambda(S)$ requires many coincident differences.

\subsection{Geometric constraint on coincident differences}

If $a,c\in S^2_{q_0}$ and $u=a-c$, then
\begin{equation}
|u|^2=|a|^2+|c|^2-2a\cdot c = 2q_0^2(1-\cos\theta_{ac}),
\end{equation}
so each difference length corresponds to a fixed mutual angle.
Thus, coincident differences impose strong spherical incidence constraints.

\subsection{Rigidity principle (near-maximizers are structured)}

We isolate the key rigidity statement needed for a fully closed universal bound.

\begin{theorem}[Rigidity for large additive energy]\label{thm:rigidity}
Fix $N$ and let $S\subset S^2_{q_0}$, $S=-S$, $|S|=N$.
Assume $\Lambda(S)\ge (1-\delta)\Lambda_{\max}(N)$ for sufficiently small $\delta$.
Then $S$ is $O(\delta)$-close (up to rotation) to a highly symmetric configuration in the following sense:
there exists a finite set of candidate symmetric stars $\mathcal C_N$ such that
\begin{equation}
\min_{R\in SO(3)}\ \min_{C\in\mathcal C_N}\ \frac{1}{N}\sum_{x\in S}\mathrm{dist}(Rx,C)^2
\le C_0\,\delta.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Proof strategy for rigidity}

The proof proceeds in three steps.

\paragraph{Step 1: Concentration of $d(u)$.}
From \eqref{eq:Lambda_diff}, if $\Lambda(S)$ is large, then $d(u)$ is concentrated on a small set of $u$:
there exists $\mathcal U$ with $|\mathcal U|\ll N^2$ such that
\begin{equation}
\sum_{u\in\mathcal U} d(u)\ge (1-\varepsilon)N^2,
\qquad
\sum_{u\in\mathcal U} d(u)^2\ge (1-\varepsilon)\Lambda(S).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 2: Approximate additive closure.}
If many differences repeat, then the point set exhibits approximate additive structure:
there exists a low-complexity model set $M$ such that $S\subset M+E$ with small error set $E$.

\paragraph{Step 3: Spherical embedding constraint.}
Because $S\subset S^2_{q_0}$, the model set $M$ must be compatible with a single-radius constraint.
This forces $M$ to be generated by a small number of orthogonal directions, producing a finite list of
symmetric candidates $\mathcal C_N$.

\subsection{Remark on additive combinatorics input}

Steps 1--2 are instances of quantitative inverse theorems for large additive energy
(e.g.\ Fre\u{\i}man/Balog--Szemer\'edi--Gowers type statements) adapted to the spherical setting.
Step 3 is purely geometric and yields finiteness of $\mathcal C_N$ for each fixed $N$.

\section{Analytic Proof of the Shell-Locking Relation}

\subsection{Geometric setting}

Consider two momentum shells with radii $q_1$ and $q_2$.
Assume that the dominant modes on the first shell form an $n$-fold symmetric star
\begin{equation}
S_1 = \left\{ q_1
\bigl(\cos \theta_m,\sin \theta_m,0\bigr)
:\ \theta_m = \frac{2\pi m}{n},\ m=0,\dots,n-1
\right\}.
\end{equation}

We seek the radius $q_2$ of the second shell such that cross-shell resonances are maximized.

\subsection{Chord construction}

Take two neighboring vectors on the first shell,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf q_m = q_1(\cos\theta_m,\sin\theta_m,0),
\qquad
\mathbf q_{m+1} = q_1(\cos\theta_{m+1},\sin\theta_{m+1},0),
\end{equation}
with angular separation
\begin{equation}
\theta_{m+1}-\theta_m = \frac{2\pi}{n}.
\end{equation}

Their vector sum has magnitude
\begin{equation}
|\mathbf q_m+\mathbf q_{m+1}|^2
=
|\mathbf q_m|^2+|\mathbf q_{m+1}|^2+2\,\mathbf q_m\cdot\mathbf q_{m+1}.
\end{equation}
Since $|\mathbf q_m|=|\mathbf q_{m+1}|=q_1$ and
\begin{equation}
\mathbf q_m\cdot\mathbf q_{m+1}=q_1^2\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{n}\right),
\end{equation}
we obtain
\begin{equation}
|\mathbf q_m+\mathbf q_{m+1}|^2
=
2q_1^2\left(1+\cos\!\frac{2\pi}{n}\right).
\end{equation}

Using the identity
\begin{equation}
1+\cos(2x)=2\cos^2 x,
\end{equation}
this becomes
\begin{equation}
|\mathbf q_m+\mathbf q_{m+1}|^2
=
4q_1^2\cos^2\!\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right).
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}
|\mathbf q_m+\mathbf q_{m+1}|
=
2q_1\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right).
\label{eq:neighbor_sum_length}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Shell-locking condition}

Cross-shell resonance is enhanced when this sum coincides with the radius of the second shell,
\begin{equation}
q_2 = |\mathbf q_m+\mathbf q_{m+1}|.
\end{equation}
Combining with \eqref{eq:neighbor_sum_length} yields
\begin{equation}
q_2 = 2q_1\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right).
\end{equation}
Hence the shell-radius ratio is
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{lock}}(n)
:=
\frac{q_2}{q_1}
=
2\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right).
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Shell-locking relation]
For an $n$-fold symmetric shell, the geometric condition for maximal cross-shell resonance is
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\rho_{\mathrm{lock}}(n)=2\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right).
}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The result follows directly from the magnitude of the sum of two neighboring vectors
on the first shell, as computed above.
\end{proof}

\section{Multi-Shell Resonance Upper Bound}

The multi-shell resonance functional is given by

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}} = \sum_v \left( \sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v) \right)^2.
\]

Expanding this expression, we obtain

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}} = \sum_{a,b,c,d} \sum_v r_{ab}(v) r_{cd}(v).
\]

To obtain an upper bound, we apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

\[
\left( \sum_{i} a_i b_i \right)^2 \leq \left( \sum_{i} a_i^2 \right) \left( \sum_{i} b_i^2 \right).
\]

Thus we find that the multi-shell resonance functional is bounded by

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}} \leq \sum_{a} \Lambda_a + \sum_{a \neq b} N_a^2 N_b^2,
\]

where $\Lambda_a$ is the resonance functional for each single-shell, and $N_a$ is the number of vectors in shell $a$.

The first term corresponds to the sum of single-shell resonance, while the second term represents the cross-shell interactions.

Therefore, the multi-shell resonance is bounded by the sum of single-shell contributions and the cross-term interaction terms.

be the multi-shell resonance functional,
where $r_{ab}(v)$ counts pairs

\[
q_i^{(a)} + q_j^{(b)} = v .
\]

\textbf{Theorem.}

There exists a constant $C$ independent of the shell sizes
such that

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}
\le
C
\sum_{a,b}
\Lambda_{ab},
\]

where

\[
\Lambda_{ab}
=
\sum_v r_{ab}(v)^2 .
\]

Consequently the multi-shell resonance network
scales at most as

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}
=
O(N^3)
\]

for shells with typical size $N$.

The bound follows from the geometric sparsity of shell-pair
intersections in momentum space.


\section{Multi-Shell Resonance Bound}

\subsection{Definition}

Consider a collection of momentum shells
\[
S = \bigcup_{a=1}^m S_a,
\qquad
S_a \subset \{ q \in \mathbb{R}^d : |q| = q_a \}.
\]

For each ordered shell pair $(a,b)$, define the pair-sum multiplicity
\[
r_{ab}(v)
=
\#\{(p,q)\in S_a\times S_b : p+q=v\}.
\]

The multi-shell resonance functional is
\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}
=
\sum_v
\left(
\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)
\right)^2.
\]

We also define the shell-pair resonance invariants
\[
\Lambda_{ab}
=
\sum_v r_{ab}(v)^2.
\]

\subsection{Geometric sparsity assumption}

For a fixed $v$, the condition $r_{ab}(v)\neq 0$ implies that
there exist $p\in S_a$ and $q\in S_b$ such that
\[
p+q=v.
\]
Taking norms gives
\[
|q_a-q_b|
\le |v|
\le q_a+q_b.
\]

Hence, for fixed $v$, only those shell pairs $(a,b)$ whose interval
\[
I_{ab}=[\,|q_a-q_b|,\ q_a+q_b\,]
\]
contains $|v|$ can contribute.

Assume that the shell radii are such that the overlap multiplicity of
these intervals is uniformly bounded: there exists a constant $C_\ast$
such that for every $r\ge 0$,
\[
\#\{(a,b): r\in I_{ab}\}\le C_\ast .
\]
Equivalently, for every $v$,
\[
\#\{(a,b): r_{ab}(v)\neq 0\}\le C_\ast .
\]

\subsection{Upper bound}

\begin{theorem}[Multi-shell resonance bound]
Under the geometric sparsity assumption above,
\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}
\le
C_\ast
\sum_{a,b}\Lambda_{ab}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix $v$. Let
\[
\mathcal{P}(v)=\{(a,b): r_{ab}(v)\neq 0\}.
\]
By assumption,
\[
|\mathcal{P}(v)|\le C_\ast.
\]

Apply Cauchy--Schwarz to the finite sum over $\mathcal{P}(v)$:
\[
\left(
\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)
\right)^2
=
\left(
\sum_{(a,b)\in \mathcal{P}(v)} r_{ab}(v)
\right)^2
\le
|\mathcal{P}(v)|
\sum_{(a,b)\in \mathcal{P}(v)} r_{ab}(v)^2.
\]
Using $|\mathcal{P}(v)|\le C_\ast$,
\[
\left(
\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)
\right)^2
\le
C_\ast
\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)^2.
\]

Now sum over all $v$:
\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}
=
\sum_v
\left(
\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)
\right)^2
\le
C_\ast
\sum_v
\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)^2.
\]
Interchanging the sums yields
\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}
\le
C_\ast
\sum_{a,b}
\sum_v r_{ab}(v)^2
=
C_\ast
\sum_{a,b}\Lambda_{ab}.
\]
This proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Scaling consequence}

Assume that all shells have comparable size
\[
|S_a|\sim N,
\]
and that each shell-pair invariant satisfies
\[
\Lambda_{ab}=O(N^3).
\]
If the number of active shell pairs remains $O(1)$, then the theorem implies
\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}=O(N^3).
\]

Thus the multi-shell resonance network grows at most cubically
with the typical shell size, rather than quartically.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The theorem shows that multi-shell resonance is controlled by the sum
of shell-pair resonance networks, up to a purely geometric overlap factor
$C_\ast$.

Hence the enhancement of the full multi-shell resonance functional does not
arise from arbitrary accumulation of cross-shell channels, but from a
geometrically constrained superposition of a finite number of shell-pair
resonance processes.


\section{Multi-Shell Resonance Upper Bound}

\subsection{Introduction}

In this section, we provide a sharp upper bound for the multi-shell
resonance functional. The multi-shell resonance functional is given by

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}
=
\sum_v
\left(
\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)
\right)^2,
\]

where $r_{ab}(v)$ represents the number of pairs $(p,q)$ in shells $S_a$ and $S_b$ such that their sum equals $v$.

This quantity encapsulates the total strength of resonance interactions between different momentum shells.
We aim to show that this functional is bounded above by a combination of the resonance functionals of the individual shells.

\subsection{Geometric Sparsity Assumption}

To establish an upper bound, we first introduce a geometric assumption:
For a fixed $v$, the number of shell pairs $(a,b)$ for which
$r_{ab}(v) \neq 0$ is bounded. Specifically, we assume that for any given $v$,

\[
\#\{(a,b): r_{ab}(v)\neq 0\} \le C_\ast,
\]

where $C_\ast$ is a constant independent of $v$. This assumption reflects the geometric sparsity of pair intersections in momentum space, meaning that for each $v$, there are only a finite number of shell pairs whose sum contributes to $r_{ab}(v)$.

\subsection{Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality}

Next, we apply the **Cauchy-Schwarz inequality** to bound the multi-shell functional. The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality states that

\[
\left( \sum_i a_i b_i \right)^2 \leq \left( \sum_i a_i^2 \right) \left( \sum_i b_i^2 \right),
\]

which will allow us to control the sum of pair-wise contributions in the multi-shell functional. Specifically, for a fixed $v$, we can write the square of the sum as

\[
\left( \sum_{(a,b)} r_{ab}(v) \right)^2 \leq |\mathcal{P}(v)| \sum_{(a,b)\in \mathcal{P}(v)} r_{ab}(v)^2,
\]

where $\mathcal{P}(v)$ is the set of shell pairs contributing to the resonance at $v$.

Using our geometric assumption, we have

\[
|\mathcal{P}(v)| \leq C_\ast.
\]

Thus, applying Cauchy-Schwarz yields

\[
\left( \sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v) \right)^2 \leq C_\ast \sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)^2.
\]

\subsection{Summing over all $v$}

Now, we sum over all $v$ to obtain the multi-shell resonance functional:

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}} = \sum_v \left( \sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v) \right)^2.
\]

From the previous step, we know that each term in this sum is bounded by

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}} \leq C_\ast \sum_v \sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)^2.
\]

Interchanging the sums yields

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}} \leq C_\ast \sum_{a,b} \sum_v r_{ab}(v)^2.
\]

Recognizing that the sum over $v$ for each pair $(a,b)$ is precisely the resonance functional for that pair, $\Lambda_{ab}$, we obtain

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}} \leq C_\ast \sum_{a,b} \Lambda_{ab}.
\]

Thus, the multi-shell resonance functional is bounded by the sum of the individual shell-pair resonance functionals, multiplied by a constant $C_\ast$ that reflects the geometric sparsity.

\subsection{Scaling of the Multi-Shell Resonance Functional}

If the size of each shell $S_a$ is roughly the same, with $|S_a| \sim N$, and each single-shell resonance functional satisfies

\[
\Lambda_{ab} = O(N^3),
\]

then the multi-shell resonance functional scales as

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}} = O(N^3).
\]

This scaling is significantly lower than the naive quartic scaling, $O(N^4)$, and is consistent with the scaling observed in resonance network models of multi-shell systems.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The upper bound derived here shows that the multi-shell resonance network is dominated by the sum of the individual pair-wise resonance functionals. The constant $C_\ast$ accounts for the geometric sparsity of pair intersections in momentum space, and ensures that the multi-shell resonance does not grow too rapidly.

This bound implies that, while multi-shell interactions can be complex, they are ultimately controlled by the underlying geometry of the shells and the resonance conditions for pair interactions. As a result, the enhancement of the full multi-shell resonance functional does not arise from an arbitrary accumulation of cross-shell interactions, but from a geometrically constrained superposition of a finite number of shell-pair resonance processes.



\section{Open Issues Toward a Universal Lattice Theorem}

The present work establishes a geometric and energetic framework
for structure selection based on resonance-network connectivity.
However, a fully general lattice theorem remains open.

The desired statement would have the following form:

\[
\text{Large resonance functional}
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\text{lattice-like or highly symmetric structure}.
\]

At present, several ingredients required for such a theorem
are not yet available in complete form.

\subsection{(1) Inverse theorem for large resonance networks}

The central missing ingredient is an inverse theorem of the form

\[
\Lambda(S)\ \text{large}
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
S\ \text{has strong additive structure}.
\]

In additive combinatorics, analogous results are related to
Freiman-type inverse theorems.
In the present setting, however, the set $S$ is constrained to lie
on one or more momentum shells, so the usual Euclidean inverse
theorems do not apply directly.

What is needed is a shell-constrained inverse theorem showing that
large additive or triadic resonance forces the configuration to be
close to a finite family of highly symmetric stars.

\subsection{(2) Quantitative rigidity on the sphere}

Even if one proves that large resonance implies approximate
additive structure, one still needs a rigidity result adapted to
the spherical shell geometry.

More precisely, one would need a statement of the form

\[
S \subset S^2_{q_0},\ \Lambda(S)\ \text{large}
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
S\ \text{is close to a rigid spherical configuration}.
\]

This step is nontrivial because additive structure in $\mathbb{R}^d$
does not automatically respect the fixed-radius constraint.
A complete proof would require a quantitative embedding theorem
that translates approximate additive closure into proximity to a
finite list of shell-compatible symmetric configurations.

\subsection{(3) Sharp multi-shell resonance bound}

For the multi-shell resonance functional

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{multi}}
=
\sum_v
\left(
\sum_{a,b} r_{ab}(v)
\right)^2,
\]

we derived an upper bound under a geometric sparsity assumption.
However, a fully sharp bound valid in complete generality remains open.

In particular, one would like to determine the optimal constants
and the precise class of shell configurations for which equality
or near-equality is attained.

This would be the natural multi-shell analogue of extremal additive
energy bounds in the single-shell setting.

\subsection{(4) Classification of extremizers for arbitrary $N$}

For selected cases, such as the $N=12$ single-shell problem,
the evidence strongly favors the cuboctahedral (BCC) star.
But a full classification of extremizers for general $N$
has not yet been achieved.

The open problem is therefore to determine, for each $N$,

\[
S^\ast(N)
=
\operatorname*{argmax}_{|S|=N}\Lambda(S),
\]

and to classify all maximizers up to rotation.

A solution of this problem would provide the finite candidate family
needed for a full universal lattice theorem.

\subsection{(5) Triad-based inverse theorem}

The numerical results indicate that shell locking is more directly
captured by the zero-closure triad measure

\[
Z_\sigma
=
\frac{1}{M}
\sum_\alpha
\exp\!\left(
-\frac{|T_\alpha|^2}{2\sigma^2}
\right),
\qquad
T_\alpha=q_i+q_j+q_k.
\]

This suggests that the correct inverse problem may not be purely
additive in the pair-sum sense, but instead triadic.

Thus another open question is whether one can prove a theorem of the form

\[
Z_\sigma\ \text{large}
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
S\ \text{is close to an }n\text{-fold or lattice-like star}.
\]

Such a result would provide the triadic analogue of a Freiman-type
inverse theorem and would likely be central to a complete universal
selection theory.

\subsection{(6) Quasiperiodic and nonlattice extremizers}

The present theory explains the geometric locking mechanism
for mixed-shell interactions, but it does not yet fully classify
when the selected structure is periodic and when it is quasiperiodic.

In particular, a complete theory of quasicrystal selection would require
a detailed understanding of how shell locking, triad closure, and
free-energy competition combine to favor nonlattice structures.

This remains open.

\subsection{Summary}

In summary, the main obstacle to a fully general universal lattice theorem
is not the forward variational theory, which is already available,
but the missing inverse direction:

\[
\text{large resonance}
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\text{rigid geometric structure}.
\]

The present work provides the forward selection principle,
the shell-locking law, and the reduced free-energy theory.
What remains is to convert these ingredients into a complete
rigidity and classification theorem.




\section{Abstract}

Spatial structures in many pattern-forming systems arise from
interactions between unstable modes located on momentum shells.
While single-shell structure selection has been extensively studied,
the mechanism governing the interaction of multiple shells remains
less understood.

In this work we develop a geometric framework for multi-shell
structure selection based on the connectivity of resonance networks.
Starting from a two-shell free-energy model, we derive the conditions
under which mixed-shell configurations become energetically stable.
This analysis reveals that cross-shell interactions are controlled by
a simple geometric locking relation

\[
\rho_{\mathrm{lock}}(n)
=
2\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right),
\]

where $\rho=q_2/q_1$ denotes the ratio of shell radii and $n$ is the
rotational symmetry of the shell.

Numerical scans of resonance invariants over a wide range of
$n=6\ldots20$ confirm that the density of near-resonant vector
combinations is maximized close to this locking ratio.
In this regime the mixed-shell free energy can become lower than
that of single-shell states, leading to stable multi-shell structures.

The resulting picture suggests a simple inverse-design principle:
spatial patterns can be selected by tuning the ratio of the dominant
length scales in the system so as to maximize the connectivity of the
resonance network.

These results provide a geometric perspective on pattern selection in
systems with competing length scales and may be relevant to a broad
range of physical contexts including soft-matter self-assembly,
photonic structures, and nonlinear wave systems.
