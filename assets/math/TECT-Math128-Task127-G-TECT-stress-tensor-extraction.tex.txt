\section*{Statement}

The gravitational coupling strength $G^{\rm TECT}$ (Newton's constant in TECT) is extracted from the conserved stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu\nu}(x)$ via its two-point correlator at zero spatial separation. This task requires two independent calculations: (i) exact stress-tensor operator construction from the Brazovskii elastic Lagrangian (trace and traceless components); (ii) numerical evaluation of $\langle T_{\mu\nu}(x) T_{\rho\sigma}(0) \rangle$ on the converged BCC ground state from Task #115, leading to extraction of $G^{\rm TECT}$ via low-$k$ residue matching with linearized Einstein-Hilbert gravity.

The task is classified as **independent verification** (Math45/46c vs. Math110-AddA pathways; see §3.2 below).

\section*{§1. Stress-Tensor Operator from Brazovskii Lagrangian}

\subsection*{§1.1 Elastic Free Energy}

The Brazovskii field-theory Hamiltonian (continuum, zero-temperature limit) is
\[
  H = \int d^3 x \left\{ \left[ (q_0^2 - k^2)^2 + \mu^2 \right] |\Psi|^2 + \lambda |\Psi|^4 + \ldots \right\}.
\]

On a 3D BCC lattice (characteristic spacing $a_{\text{BCC}}$), the elastic strain tensor $u_{\alpha\beta}(x)$ represents deformations from the ideal BCC configuration. The elastic Lagrangian density is
\[
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{elastic}} = \frac{1}{2} C_{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta} u_{\alpha\beta} u_{\gamma\delta} + \text{cubic/higher anisotropy terms}.
\]

For a cubic (BCC) crystal, the elastic tensor has 3 independent components (isotropic approximation with small anisotropy correction from Math57-AddA):
\[
  C_{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta}^{\text{cubic}} = \lambda_c \delta_{\alpha\beta} \delta_{\gamma\delta} + 2\mu_c P_{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta},
\]
where $\lambda_c, \mu_c$ are the Lamé parameters, and $P$ is the deviatoric projector.

\subsection*{§1.2 Canonical Stress-Tensor Definition}

The canonical stress-energy tensor is defined via the Noether prescription:
\[
  T_{\mu\nu} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial_\mu \Psi)} \partial_\nu \Psi - \mathcal{L} \, \delta_{\mu\nu}.
\]

For the Brazovskii Lagrangian density
\[
  \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2} (\nabla^2 + \mu^2) |\Psi|^2 + \frac{\lambda}{4} |\Psi|^4,
\]

the stress-energy tensor components are:

\textbf{Kinetic part}:
\[
  T_{\mu\nu}^{(\text{kin})} = \partial_\mu \Psi^* \partial_\nu \Psi + \partial_\nu \Psi^* \partial_\mu \Psi - \delta_{\mu\nu} (\nabla^2 |\Psi|^2 + \mu^2 |\Psi|^2).
\]

\textbf{Quartic part}:
\[
  T_{\mu\nu}^{(\text{quar})} = - \frac{\lambda}{4} \delta_{\mu\nu} |\Psi|^4.
\]

\textbf{Total}:
\[
  T_{\mu\nu} = T_{\mu\nu}^{(\text{kin})} + T_{\mu\nu}^{(\text{quar})}.
\]

\subsection*{§1.3 Decomposition into Trace and Traceless Parts}

The stress tensor decomposes as
\[
  T_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{3} \delta_{\mu\nu} T_\rho^\rho + T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}},
\]
where $T_\rho^\rho = \mathrm{tr}(T)$ is the trace (scalar) and $T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}}$ is the traceless (transverse-traceless) part.

\textbf{Trace} (energy density):
\[
  T_\rho^\rho = \nabla^2 |\Psi|^2 + \mu^2 |\Psi|^2 + \frac{\lambda}{4} |\Psi|^4.
\]

\textbf{Traceless part} (spatial deformations):
\[
  T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}} = \partial_\mu \Psi^* \partial_\nu \Psi + \partial_\nu \Psi^* \partial_\mu \Psi - \frac{2}{3} \delta_{\mu\nu} (\nabla^2 |\Psi|^2 + \mu^2 |\Psi|^2) - \frac{\lambda}{3} \delta_{\mu\nu} |\Psi|^4.
\]

\textbf{Gravity coupling}: Only the traceless (TT) part couples to the transverse-traceless (graviton) modes. Thus, for gravitational coupling extraction, focus on
\[
  \langle T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}}(x) T_{\rho\sigma}^{\text{(TT)}}(0) \rangle \quad \text{(2-point function)}.
\]

\section*{§2. Two-Point Correlator in Fourier Space}

\subsection*{§2.1 Lattice Definition}

On the discrete cubic lattice with spacing $a = h$ (from Task #115), the stress-tensor correlator is
\[
  G_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(x_1, x_2) = \langle T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}}(x_1) T_{\rho\sigma}^{\text{(TT)}}(x_2) \rangle - \langle T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}} \rangle \langle T_{\rho\sigma}^{\text{(TT)}} \rangle.
\]

In the BCC ground state ($\Psi = \Psi^*(x)$ from Newton-Krylov), the one-point function $\langle T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}} \rangle$ is nonzero (condensate stress). Thus
\[
  G_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(x_1, x_2) = \langle T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}}(x_1) T_{\rho\sigma}^{\text{(TT)}}(x_2) \rangle.
\]

\subsection*{§2.2 Fourier Transform}

Define
\[
  \widetilde{G}_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(\veck) = \sum_{x_1, x_2} e^{i \veck \cdot (x_1 - x_2)} G_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(x_1, x_2).
\]

For a translation-invariant system (away from boundaries), the correlator depends only on $\veck = x_1 - x_2$.

\subsection*{§2.3 Low-$k$ Asymptotics and Gravity Coupling}

In the infrared limit $k \to 0$, the stress-tensor correlator in a gravitational theory takes the form
\[
  \widetilde{G}_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(k) = \frac{C_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}}{k^2} + \text{regular terms},
\]
where the residue $C_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}$ encodes the strength of gravitational coupling.

For a 3D linearized gravity theory (Einstein-Hilbert + cosmological constant), the universality relation is
\[
  C_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma} = \frac{1}{16\pi G} \mathcal{P}_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}^{\text{(TT)}},
\]
where $\mathcal{P}^{\text{(TT)}}$ is the TT projector and $G$ is Newton's constant.

In TECT, we extract $G^{\rm TECT}$ by measuring the low-$k$ behavior of the stress-tensor correlator and inverting the matching relation.

\section*{§3. Numerical Extraction Algorithm (Test D3)}

\subsection*{§3.1 Step-by-Step Procedure}

\textbf{Input}: Converged BCC ground state $\Psi^*(x, y, z)$ on $N \times N \times N$ lattice (from Task #115, math note Math82-H).

\textbf{Step 1}: Compute the stress-tensor $T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}}(x, y, z)$ at each lattice point, using finite-difference approximations for $\partial_\mu \Psi$ and $\nabla^2 \Psi$.

\textbf{Step 2}: Compute the correlator $G_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(r)$ by averaging over all pairs of lattice points at distance $|\mathbf{r}| = r$:
\[
  G_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(r) = \frac{1}{|\{(x_1, x_2) : |x_1 - x_2| = r\}|} \sum_{|x_1-x_2|=r} T_{\mu\nu}^{\text{(TT)}}(x_1) T_{\rho\sigma}^{\text{(TT)}}(x_2).
\]

\textbf{Step 3}: Fourier transform to $\widetilde{G}(\veck)$:
\[
  \widetilde{G}_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(k_i) = \sum_{\mathbf{r}} e^{i \veck \cdot \mathbf{r}} G_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(r), \quad k_i = \frac{2\pi n_i}{N h}, \, n_i \in \{0, 1, \ldots, N/2\}.
\]

\textbf{Step 4}: Extract low-$k$ regime ($k \lesssim 0.1 \, q_0 = 0.068$). Fit the correlator to
\[
  \widetilde{G}(\veck) = \frac{A_0}{k^2} + A_1 + A_2 k^2 + O(k^4).
\]

\textbf{Step 5}: Solve for Newton's constant using
\[
  G^{\rm TECT} = \frac{1}{16\pi A_0} \quad (\text{inverted from matching relation}).
\]

\section*{§4. Gauge Fixing and Lattice Issues}

\subsection*{§4.1 Gauge Dependence}

The stress-tensor operator is defined in a particular frame/gauge. In continuum gravity, different gauges (e.g., harmonic gauge vs. synchronous gauge) yield different stress-tensor operators. On the lattice, the choice of finite-difference scheme (forward, backward, centered) affects the stress-tensor expression.

\textbf{Strategy}: Use \textbf{centered finite differences} (most symmetric on the cubic lattice):
\[
  \partial_\mu \Psi(x) \approx \frac{\Psi(x+a\hat{\mu}) - \Psi(x-a\hat{\mu})}{2a}, \quad \nabla^2 \Psi(x) \approx \frac{1}{a^2} \sum_\mu [\Psi(x+a\hat{\mu}) - 2\Psi(x) + \Psi(x-a\hat{\mu})].
\]

\subsection*{§4.2 Continuum Limit and Lattice Artifacts}

The stress-tensor computation involves products of derivatives. On a discrete lattice, these products can introduce $O(a)$ or $O(a^2)$ artifacts. To extract the continuum limit:

\textbf{Procedure}:
- Compute $G_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(r)$ at three lattice spacings $h \in \{0.1, 0.05, 0.025\}$ (same as Task #115).
- At each $h$, perform the low-$k$ fit and extract $A_0(h)$.
- Use Richardson extrapolation (as in Math127 §4.2) to obtain continuum limit $A_0(\infty)$.
- Convert to $G^{\rm TECT}_{\infty}$.

\section*{§7. Devil's-Advocate Self-Test (CLAUDE.md §6.3.1)}

\subsection*{Objection α: Two-point function of stress tensor includes disconnected diagrams; Wick contraction is ambiguous on finite lattice}

\textbf{Assessment}: VALID (subtle point). In field theory, the 2-point function includes both connected and disconnected pieces. On a lattice at finite volume, the disconnected piece is subleading in $1/V$ and vanishes as $V \to \infty$. \textbf{Remediation}: compute the correlator as $\langle T_{\mu\nu} T_{\rho\sigma} \rangle - \langle T_{\mu\nu} \rangle \langle T_{\rho\sigma} \rangle$ (connected piece only); report both raw correlator and connected correlator to quantify the contribution.

\subsection*{Objection β: Low-$k$ fitting is sensitive to numerical noise in Fourier space; $A_0$ extraction is unstable}

\textbf{Assessment}: VALID (practical challenge). For $k \lesssim 0.068$, the signal can be small. Noise in the stress-tensor computation (finite-difference errors, lattice discreteness) can dominate. \textbf{Remediation}: use data-driven regularization (e.g., Tikhonov regularization on the Fourier coefficients); report the condition number of the fit matrix; if $\text{cond}(M) > 100$, flag as unstable.

\subsection*{Objection γ: Stress-tensor operator is not uniquely defined on the lattice; different finite-difference schemes yield different results}

\textbf{Assessment}: VALID (gauge-dependence artifact). The stress tensor's definition depends on the choice of kinetic-term discretization. \textbf{Remediation}: compute the correlator using two independent finite-difference schemes (centered vs. one-sided) and verify that results agree at the $O(10\%)$ level; if they don't, the extraction is ambiguous and should be flagged as suspect.
