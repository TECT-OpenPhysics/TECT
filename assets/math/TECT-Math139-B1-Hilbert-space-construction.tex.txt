\section*{Math139 (Round B1): Explicit Hilbert Space Construction for TECT Quantum Field}

\subsection*{0. Executive Summary}

This note constructs the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}$ underlying the canonical quantization of
the TECT BCC condensate. The construction follows from the Brazovskii free-energy functional and the
Hartree ground state (Math82-H / Math98 ground state). All quantum operators ($\hat{\Psi}$, $\hat{\Pi}_\Psi$,
creation/annihilation) will be built on this Hilbert space in Round B2 (Math140).

\subsection*{1. Fock Space Over Brazovskii Shell Modes}

\subsubsection*{§1.1 Shell-mode basis}

The TECT BCC condensate is localized to a \emph{spherical shell} in Fourier space (Brazovskii
modulation, Math02–Math09). Denote:
\begin{align}
  q_0 &= \text{BCC shell wavenumber} = 0.6801747616 \quad [\text{lattice units}], \\
  \Delta q &= \text{shell width} \approx 0.05 \quad [\text{lattice units}],
\end{align}
confirmed by numerical BCC solution (Math55, continuation endpoint).

The \emph{shell modes} are orthonormal basis functions:
$$\phi_{\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{for} \quad |\mathbf{k}| \in [q_0 - \Delta q / 2, q_0 + \Delta q / 2].$$

Each shell mode has an associated \emph{polarization tensor} structure (longitudinal + transverse,
from the cubic BCC symmetry). The exact eigenfunctions are obtained as solutions to the
\emph{Hartree equations}:
$$[-\nabla^2 + V_{\rm eff}(\mathbf{x})] \phi_{\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{x}) = \lambda_{\mathbf{k}} \phi_{\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{x}),$$
where $V_{\rm eff}$ includes the Hartree self-consistency condition from the background condensate density $|\Psi^*|^2$.

\subsubsection*{§1.2 Hilbert space definition}

Define the \emph{one-particle Hilbert space} over the shell:
$$\mathcal{H}_{\rm 1} := \bigoplus_{n=1}^{N_{\rm modes}} \mathbb{C},$$
where $N_{\rm modes}$ is the number of distinct shell modes (discrete sum over $\mathbf{k}$ within the shell,
plus degeneracies from polarization).

The \emph{Fock space} is the second-quantized Hilbert space:
$$\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT} := \bigoplus_{N=0}^{\infty} \mathcal{H}_1^{\otimes^{\text{sym}} N},$$
where $\otimes^{\text{sym}}$ denotes the symmetric tensor product (Bose statistics).

Concretely:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT} &= \{|0\rangle, |1_\mathbf{k}\rangle, |2_{\mathbf{k}_1, \mathbf{k}_2}\rangle, \ldots\}, \\
  \text{where} \quad |n_{\mathbf{k}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{k}_n}\rangle &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{n_{\mathbf{k}_1}! \cdots n_{\mathbf{k}_M}!}} \,
    (a_{\mathbf{k}_1}^\dagger)^{n_{\mathbf{k}_1}} \cdots (a_{\mathbf{k}_M}^\dagger)^{n_{\mathbf{k}_M}} |0\rangle,
\end{align}
with $\sum_j n_{\mathbf{k}_j} = N$ (occupation number of each mode).

\subsubsection*{§1.3 Inner product and completeness}

The Fock-space inner product is:
$$\langle \psi | \phi \rangle_{\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}} := \text{standard Fock inner product}.$$

For one-particle states:
$$\langle \phi_\mathbf{k} | \phi_{\mathbf{k}'} \rangle = \delta_{\mathbf{k} \mathbf{k}'},$$
where $\delta_{\mathbf{k} \mathbf{k}'}$ is the Kronecker delta (finite system) or $\delta^3(\mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}')$
in the continuum limit.

For many-particle states:
$$\langle n_1, n_2, \ldots | m_1, m_2, \ldots \rangle = \prod_j \delta_{n_j m_j}.$$

\subsubsection*{§1.4 Vacuum specification}

The \emph{Hartree ground state} (from Math82-H) specifies:
$$|0\rangle_{\rm TECT} := \text{lowest-energy BCC condensate state}.$$

Key properties:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $a_\mathbf{k} |0\rangle = 0$ for all $\mathbf{k}$ (annihilation kills the vacuum).
\item $\langle 0 | 0 \rangle = 1$ (normalized).
\item The expectation value $\langle 0 | \hat{\Psi} | 0 \rangle = \phi_0$ is the background condensate amplitude (order parameter).
\item The gap (ground state to first excited state) is the Hartree spectral gap $\Delta E_{\rm gap} = \hbar \omega_{\mathbf{k}_{\min}}$,
  where $\omega_{\mathbf{k}_{\min}}$ is the minimum shell-mode frequency.
\end{enumerate}

From Math82-H, we have (analytically):
$$m^{*2} := \omega_{\mathbf{k}_{\min}}^2 \approx 9.005 \quad [\text{dimensionless TECT units}],$$
confirming the theoretical prediction from Math37 (one-loop linear-response calculation).

\subsection*{2. Mode Decomposition and Dispersion Relation}

\subsubsection*{§2.1 Shell-mode frequency structure}

Each shell mode $\mathbf{k}$ has a quantum frequency (energy per photon-like excitation):
$$\omega_\mathbf{k}^2 = c_T^2 [Y (k^2 - q_0^2)^2 / q_0^2 + \varepsilon^2 q_0^2],$$
where:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $c_T$ = transverse sound speed $\approx 1$ (in normalized TECT units; see Math39, Math115).
\item $Y$ = stiffness constant from Brazovskii elastic Lagrangian (explicit formula in Math82, Math98).
\item $\varepsilon$ = small Hartree mass parameter (encodes the spectral gap).
\item The term $(k^2 - q_0^2)^2 / q_0^2$ captures the sharp shell modulation.
\end{enumerate}

The \emph{minimum frequency} is:
$$\omega_{\min} = c_T \sqrt{\varepsilon^2} q_0 \equiv m^*,$$
which is precisely the Hartree-computed mass gap.

\subsubsection*{§2.2 Polarization structure}

The shell modes carry cubic-lattice BCC polarization tensors. For each $\mathbf{k}$, define:
\begin{align}
  \text{Longitudinal}: \quad &\hat{\mathbf{e}}_L(\mathbf{k}) := \frac{\mathbf{k}}{|\mathbf{k}|}, \\
  \text{Transverse}: \quad &\hat{\mathbf{e}}_T^{(1,2)}(\mathbf{k}) \quad \text{(two orthogonanal directions)}.
\end{align}

The shell-mode polarization is predominantly transverse (since the BCC modulation is at the shell boundary).
The exact admixture is determined by the Hartree equations.

\subsubsection*{§2.3 Completeness and over-completeness}

The shell modes form an \emph{orthonormal basis} of $\mathcal{H}_1$:
$$\sum_\mathbf{k} |\phi_\mathbf{k}\rangle \langle \phi_\mathbf{k}| = \mathbb{I}_{\mathcal{H}_1}.$$

For Fock space, the corresponding completeness relation is:
$$\sum_{N=0}^\infty \sum_{\mathbf{k}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{k}_N} |n_{\mathbf{k}_1}, \ldots, n_{\mathbf{k}_N}\rangle \langle n_{\mathbf{k}_1}, \ldots, n_{\mathbf{k}_N}| = \mathbb{I}_{\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}}.$$

\subsection*{3. Spectral Properties and Mass Gap}

\subsubsection*{§3.1 Spectrum of the one-particle Hamiltonian}

Define the \emph{one-particle Hamiltonian} restricted to shell modes:
$$\hat{h}_{\rm shell} := \sum_\mathbf{k} \omega_\mathbf{k} a_\mathbf{k}^\dagger a_\mathbf{k},$$
where $a_\mathbf{k}$ is the lowering operator for mode $\mathbf{k}$ (to be defined precisely in Math140).

The spectrum of $\hat{h}_{\rm shell}$ is:
$$\text{spec}(\hat{h}_{\rm shell}) = \{0, \omega_1, \omega_2, \ldots\},$$
where $\omega_1 \le \omega_2 \le \cdots$ are the shell-mode frequencies, sorted in increasing order.

\subsubsection*{§3.2 Mass gap existence theorem (statement)}

\begin{boxed}
\textbf{Theorem 1 (Mass Gap in TECT Quantum Theory)}
Let $\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}$ be the Fock space over BCC shell modes. Let $E_0$ be the energy eigenvalue of
the Hartree ground state $|0\rangle_{\rm TECT}$, and let $E_1$ be the lowest excited-state energy.

Then:
$$\Delta E_{\rm gap} := E_1 - E_0 = \hbar \omega_{\min} = m^* \hbar c_T > 0,$$
where $m^* = \sqrt{\varepsilon} q_0 c_T$ (Hartree mass parameter).

From Math82-H, $m^{*2} \approx 9.005$ (dimensionless TECT units).
\end{boxed}

\textbf{Proof sketch (full rigorous proof deferred to Math140–141 analysis of spectral properties)}:
The Hartree equations solve the ground-state variational problem, yielding the lowest energy state
$|0\rangle$. By definition, the next state is a one-particle excitation: $a_{\mathbf{k}_{\min}}^\dagger |0\rangle$,
which has energy $E_0 + \omega_{\min}$. Thus $\Delta E_{\rm gap} = \hbar \omega_{\min} > 0$.

\subsection*{4. Regularity and Continuity Properties}

\subsubsection*{§4.1 Finite-size effects and continuum limit}

The shell modes depend on the lattice spacing $h$ (or equivalently, the box size $L = N h$):
$$\phi_{\mathbf{k}}^{(h)}(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{on a lattice with spacing } h.$$

As $h \to 0$ (continuum limit), the discrete set of shell modes becomes dense and approaches
the continuum shell modes $\phi_\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{x})$ (Fourier-like basis).

The mass gap $m^*(h)$ exhibits finite-size corrections:
$$m^*(h) = m^*_{\infty} + O(h^2),$$
consistent with the Richardson extrapolation framework (Math127, Math131).

\subsubsection*{§4.2 Ultraviolet regularity}

The TECT Hilbert space is naturally regularized by:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The \emph{shell cutoff}: modes with $|\mathbf{k}| > q_0 + \Delta q/2$ are absent (UV-irrelevant by Brazovskii)
  (see Math02–09, Math82 shell-spectrum analysis).
\item The \emph{lattice spacing} $h$: the continuum limit $h \to 0$ must be taken carefully to avoid spurious UV modes.
\item The \emph{Hartree mass} $m^*$: acts as a natural IR regulator (the lowest-frequency mode has $\omega_\min = m^*$).
\end{enumerate}

These regularity properties ensure that all quantum observables (to be defined in Math140) are well-defined
and not plagued by divergences.

\subsection*{5. Comparison with Standard Bosonic Quantum Field Theory}

TECT's Hilbert space differs subtly from a standard scalar field in several ways:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Shell localization}: Standard QFT has all wavenumbers $\mathbf{k} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ (or on a lattice, $\mathbf{k} \in [-\pi/h, \pi/h]^3$).
  TECT restricts to the shell $|\mathbf{k}| \approx q_0$, reducing the Hilbert space dimension.

\item \textbf{Hartree ground state}: In standard scalar QFT, the vacuum is empty (no condensate). In TECT,
  $|0\rangle$ has a background amplitude $\phi_0 \approx 1$ (in TECT units), reflecting the BCC condensate.

\item \textbf{Anomalous dimensions}: The Brazovskii modulation induces anomalous scaling dimensions
  (Math97, Math120). Standard scalar fields have trivial scaling dimensions at tree level.

\item \textbf{Lorentz symmetry}: TECT's shell modes emerge Lorentz-invariant only as a consequence of
  universality-class membership (Math97). This is a structural necessity, not an assumption.
\end{enumerate}

The upshot: TECT is \emph{not} a standard scalar QFT, but a \emph{constrained effective theory} whose
Hilbert space and symmetries emerge from the Brazovskii condensate structure.

\subsection*{6. Devil's-Advocate Self-Test (CLAUDE.md §6.3.1)}

I now apply the three-objection devil's-advocate test:

\subsubsection*{Objection α: Wick rotation validity — is the imaginary-time formalism rigorous?}

\textbf{Claim}: The Hartree ground state $|0\rangle$ is obtained via Onsager-Machlup path-integral variational
principle in \emph{real time}. The transition to imaginary-time Euclidean formalism (needed for Wick rotation
in Math142 Feynman path integral) may introduce artifacts or require careful analytic continuation.

\textbf{Counter-argument (DISMISSED)}:
The Hartree equations are derived from the real-time Brazovskii free energy $\mathcal{F}[\Psi]$ (Math82-H).
Once the ground state $|0\rangle$ is identified as a solution to the Hartree variational problem,
the Fock-space quantization around $|0\rangle$ is independent of whether we use real or imaginary time.
The Wick rotation in Math142 is a technical step to compute correlation functions via path integrals;
it does not alter the \emph{quantum Hilbert space} structure defined here. Analytic continuation of
correlation functions is a well-understood rigorous procedure (Osterwalder-Schrader axioms in constructive QFT).

\textbf{Closure}: DISMISSED. No modification to Hilbert space definition required.

\subsubsection*{Objection β: Lattice cutoff and regulator dependence — how sensitive is the spectrum to lattice spacing?}

\textbf{Claim}: The shell-mode frequencies depend on the lattice-spacing parameter $h$ (via the Hartree equations).
Different choices of $h$ (or different regularization schemes) might yield different shell-mode spectra,
making the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}$ regulator-dependent. This would undermine the claim that
$\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}$ is a universal structure.

\textbf{Counter-argument (VALID, with mitigation)}:
Yes, $\omega_\mathbf{k}(h)$ depends on $h$. This is the standard finite-size effect in lattice field theory.
However, \emph{physical observables} (like the continuum mass gap $m^*_\infty$) are independent of $h$ in the limit $h \to 0$.
Math127 and Math131 address this explicitly through the Richardson extrapolation procedure:
$$m^*(h) = m^*_\infty + O(h^2) \quad \Rightarrow \quad m^*_\infty = \lim_{h \to 0} m^*(h).$$

The continuum Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}^{\rm continuum}$ is then constructed by taking the limit $h \to 0$.

\textbf{Mitigation strategy}:
In Math140–141 (field operator and CCR consistency), we will explicitly work with the continuum limit
$\mathcal{H}_{\rm TECT}^{\rm continuum}$, and all quantum observables will be defined in that limit.
Thus, regulator dependence is \emph{removed} by the Richardson extrapolation framework.

\textbf{Closure}: VALID objection, mitigation documented. No change to Hilbert space definition, but
full quantum analysis in Math140–141 must verify continuum-limit robustness.

\subsubsection*{Objection γ: Fock-space over-completeness — is the shell-mode basis sufficiently dense?}

\textbf{Claim}: The TECT Hilbert space includes only modes with $|\mathbf{k}| \approx q_0$ (the BCC shell).
All other modes (IR modes, UV modes) are absent. This is a very \emph{small} fraction of the full momentum space.
How can the Fock space over \emph{only shell modes} capture all relevant quantum fluctuations? Might there be
important contributions from modes outside the shell?

\textbf{Counter-argument (DISMISSED, with caveat)}:
The Brazovskii theory predicts that at the operating point $\mu^2 = 0.26$, the condensate energy
is strongly localized to the shell, and all IR (long-wavelength) and UV (short-wavelength) modes
decouple. This is a \emph{consequence of symmetry breaking and dimensional reduction}.

Math02–09 and Math82-H establish the shell-spectrum decomposition:
$$\hat{H} = H_{\rm IR} \oplus H_{\rm shell} \oplus H_{\rm UV}.$$

The IR and UV Hilbert spaces $H_{\rm IR}$ and $H_{\rm UV}$ contribute negligibly to the ground-state
energy and low-lying excitations (verified numerically in Math56 and analytically in Math82-H).

Thus, for the purpose of \emph{low-energy effective quantum field theory}, the Fock space over shell
modes is the correct and complete description.

\textbf{Caveat}: At very high energies (far above $m^*$), IR and UV modes may become relevant. However,
the programme (Math139–144) focuses on \emph{low-energy quantum fluctuations} around the Hartree ground state.
For Stage 3 (cosmological extension, Branch B), a fuller inclusion of IR modes may be necessary.

\textbf{Closure}: DISMISSED for low-energy quantum field theory (the scope of Branch A, Math139–144).
The caveat is documented; higher-energy extensions flagged for future work (Stage 4, Branch C).
