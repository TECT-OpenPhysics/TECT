\section{Why the Minimal Microscopic TECT Model Was Chosen}

\subsection{Statement of the problem}

Before continuing with further consequences, we must justify the microscopic model choice itself.

The correct question is not:
\[
\text{``Which equation looks plausible?''}
\]
but rather:
\[
\text{``Which minimal microscopic equation can simultaneously support all already-established TECT requirements?''}
\]

At the current stage of the program, the following structures are already fixed at the theorem level:

\begin{enumerate}
\item a finite-\(q\) shell instability is required for shell condensation and BCC-type pattern formation;
\item the internal geometry must reduce to \(\mathbb{CP}^2\);
\item the shell-level Dirac mechanism requires an enriched internal doublet and gradient-linear microscopic tensor couplings;
\item the low-energy limit should admit a relativistic-type wave/elastic sector.
\end{enumerate}

Therefore the microscopic model must be selected by \emph{constraint satisfaction}, not by aesthetic preference.

\subsection{Constraint I: finite-\(q\) shell instability}

TECT requires a condensed background built from a nonzero wavevector shell.
Thus the microscopic linear dispersion must have a minimum at
\[
|k|=q_*>0.
\]

A purely Klein--Gordon-type quadratic spatial operator
\[
\partial_t^2\Psi = -m^2\Psi + c^2\nabla^2\Psi
\]
cannot do this, because its dispersion
\[
\omega^2(k)=m^2+c^2|k|^2
\]
has its minimum at \(k=0\).

Therefore a finite-shell model necessarily requires a quartic spatial derivative term.
The lowest-order rotationally invariant possibility is
\[
Z\nabla^2\Psi - Y\nabla^4\Psi.
\]

The corresponding linear dispersion is
\begin{equation}
\omega^2(k)=\mu^2+Z|k|^2+Y|k|^4.
\label{eq:disp_general}
\end{equation}
If
\begin{equation}
Z<0,
\qquad
Y>0,
\label{eq:ZY_cond}
\end{equation}
then \(\omega^2(k)\) has a nonzero shell minimum at
\begin{equation}
q_*^2=-\frac{Z}{2Y}.
\label{eq:qstar_choice}
\end{equation}

Hence the finite-shell requirement by itself already forces the microscopic equation into the Brazovskii class.

\subsection{Constraint II: quartic stabilization}

A shell instability alone is not enough.
The condensed phase must be bounded and stable.

For a local microscopic model with a \(U(3)\)-invariant amplitude sector, the lowest stabilizing nonlinearity is
\[
(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2.
\]
At the equation-of-motion level this produces the cubic field term
\[
-g(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)\Psi,
\qquad g>0.
\]

Thus the minimal stable nonlinear completion is
\[
\partial_t^2\Psi
=
-\mu^2\Psi
+Z\nabla^2\Psi
-Y\nabla^4\Psi
-g(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)\Psi.
\]

Any lower-order local nonlinearity either violates the assumed internal symmetry or does not generically stabilize the amplitude.

\subsection{Constraint III: internal \texorpdfstring{$\mathbb{CP}^2$}{CP2} geometry}

TECT does not merely require shell condensation.
It also requires an internal projective geometry whose defect sector leads to the already-established \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) structure.

Let \(\Psi\neq0\).
Write
\begin{equation}
\Psi(x,t)=\rho(x,t)\,z(x,t),
\qquad
\rho\ge0,
\qquad
z^\dagger z=1.
\label{eq:amp_dir_decomp}
\end{equation}
Then \(z\) is defined only up to a phase,
\[
z\sim e^{i\chi}z.
\]
Therefore the internal direction lives on
\begin{equation}
[z]\in \mathbb{CP}^2.
\label{eq:cp2_direction}
\end{equation}

This is the decisive reason for choosing a complex triplet field:
a single complex scalar would give only \(S^1\) phase;
a complex doublet would give \(\mathbb{CP}^1\simeq S^2\);
the minimal field producing \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) is precisely
\[
\Psi\in\mathbb C^3.
\]

Thus the complex triplet is not optional.
It is the minimal microscopic carrier of the required TECT internal geometry.

\subsection{Constraint IV: enriched shell-Dirac mechanism}

The shell-level Dirac mechanism already proved in the previous stages does not arise from a plain scalar shell theory alone.
What was proved is that the decisive microscopic structure is a gradient-linear enriched term of the form
\begin{equation}
\delta \mathcal L_{\mathrm{enr}}
=
\mathsf N_{ij}^{a}\,
(\partial_i\phi_0)\,

\label{eq:enriched_tensor_needed}
\end{equation}
with an internal Pauli sector \(s^a\), \(a=1,2,3\), coming from a complex internal doublet.

Therefore the microscopic theory must support, after condensation and reduction, at least:

\begin{enumerate}
\item a nontrivial internal projective sector;
\item a reduced complex doublet inside that sector;
\item gradient-linear couplings between the shell background and the internal doublet.
\end{enumerate}

A plain real scalar Brazovskii field cannot provide this.
A complex triplet field can, because after amplitude-direction splitting it naturally gives a projective orientation field \(z\in\mathbb{CP}^2\), whose low-energy internal subspace may reduce to the required shell-relevant complex doublet.

So the complex triplet is the minimal field content compatible with the already-closed shell-Dirac framework.

\subsection{Constraint V: second-order time dynamics}

The microscopic TECT equation should serve as a true condensate field equation, not merely as a static free-energy minimization condition.
Moreover, the long-wavelength sector should admit a relativistic-type elastic/wave limit.

Therefore the most natural minimal time structure is second order:

A first-order-in-time Schr\"odinger/Gross--Pitaevskii form is possible as an effective nonrelativistic reduction, but it is less natural as the fundamental pre-condensation microscopic law if one wants an emergent Lorentz-type sector in the infrared.

Hence the minimal microscopic TECT equation should be of wave type rather than Schr\"odinger type.

\subsection{Minimal microscopic assumptions}

We impose the following assumptions.

\paragraph{A1. Locality.}
The microscopic action is local in space and time.

\paragraph{A2. Translation invariance.}
No explicit \(x\)-dependence appears.

\paragraph{A3. Pre-condensation isotropy.}
Before shell selection, the microscopic action is \(SO(3)\)-invariant in space.

\paragraph{A4. Internal unitary symmetry.}
At the microscopic level, the condensate field transforms as
\[
\Psi \mapsto U\Psi,
\qquad
U\in U(3),
\]
or at least under the subgroup sufficient to preserve \(\Psi^\dagger\Psi\).

\paragraph{A5. Time-reversal/parity-even lowest-order dynamics.}
The fundamental dynamics is second order in time and even under \(t\mapsto -t\), \(x\mapsto -x\).

\paragraph{A6. Lowest-order derivative truncation.}
We keep terms up to:
\begin{itemize}
\item quadratic order in time derivatives,
\item fourth order in spatial derivatives,
\item quartic order in the field amplitude.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{A7. Stable shell-forming spectrum.}
The spatial derivative terms must permit a minimum at a nonzero shell \(q_*\).

These are the weakest assumptions under which one can hope to reconstruct a usable microscopic TECT equation.

\subsection{Reconstruction theorem for the minimal action}

We now determine the most general action compatible with A1--A6.

\begin{theorem}[Minimal microscopic TECT action under the truncation assumptions]
Let \(\Psi:\mathbb R^{3,1}\to \mathbb C^3\) satisfy assumptions A1--A6.
Then, up to total derivatives, field rescalings, and an overall normalization, the most general local \(SO(3)\)-invariant, \(U(3)\)-invariant action with at most two time derivatives, four spatial derivatives, and quartic field self-interaction is
\begin{equation}
S[\Psi]
=
\int dt\, d^3x\,
\left[
\frac{1}{2}\,\partial_t\Psi^\dagger \partial_t\Psi
-\frac{\mu^2}{2}\,\Psi^\dagger\Psi
+\frac{Z}{2}\,\partial_i\Psi^\dagger \partial_i\Psi
-\frac{Y}{2}\,(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
-\frac{g}{4}\,(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2
\right].
\label{eq:minimal_action}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By locality and translation invariance, the Lagrangian density may depend only on \(\Psi\), \(\Psi^\dagger\), and their derivatives.

By \(U(3)\)-invariance, scalar terms must be built from contractions of the form
\[
\Psi^\dagger \Psi,\qquad
\partial_\mu\Psi^\dagger \partial_\nu\Psi,\qquad
(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi),
\]
and products thereof.

By \(SO(3)\)-invariance and parity evenness, the only scalar terms involving up to four spatial derivatives are, up to integrations by parts:
\[
\Psi^\dagger\Psi,
\qquad
\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi,
\qquad
(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi).
\]
Mixed terms such as
\[
\Psi^\dagger \nabla^2 \Psi
\]
are equivalent, up to total derivatives, to \(-\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi\), hence are not independent.

By time-reversal evenness and the assumption of second-order-in-time dynamics, the only independent quadratic time term is
\[
\partial_t\Psi^\dagger \partial_t\Psi.
\]
Terms linear in \(\partial_t\Psi\) are odd under time reversal and are excluded by A5.

By quartic truncation and \(U(3)\)-invariance, the only independent local quartic potential term is
\[
(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2.
\]
Therefore the action must be of the form \eqref{eq:minimal_action}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Microscopic Euler--Lagrange equation}

Varying \eqref{eq:minimal_action} with respect to \(\Psi^\dagger\), we obtain

-Y\nabla^4\Psi
-g(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)\Psi.

\frac{Z}{2}\,\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi
+
\frac{Y}{2}\,(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
+
\frac{g}{4}\,(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2
\right],
\label{eq:triplet_static_free}
\end{equation}
with
\[
Z<0,\qquad Y>0,\qquad g>0.
\]
The quadratic kernel is
\begin{equation}
\omega(k)=r+Z|k|^2+Y|k|^4,
\label{eq:triplet_quadratic_kernel}
\end{equation}
whose shell minimum occurs at
\begin{equation}
q_*^2=-\frac{Z}{2Y}.
\label{eq:qstar_triplet}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Polarization-locked sector}

Let
\begin{equation}
\Psi(x)=z\,\phi(x),
\qquad
z\in \mathbb C^3,
\qquad
z^\dagger z=1,
\qquad
z=\mathrm{const}.
\label{eq:locked_ansatz_general}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
\Psi^\dagger\Psi
=
(z^\dagger z)\,|\phi|^2
=
|\phi|^2,
\label{eq:locked_norm}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi
=
(z^\dagger z)\,\partial_i\phi^*\,\partial_i\phi
=
|\nabla\phi|^2,
\label{eq:locked_grad}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
=
(z^\dagger z)\,|\nabla^2\phi|^2
=
|\nabla^2\phi|^2.
\label{eq:locked_lap}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:locked_norm}--\eqref{eq:locked_lap} into \eqref{eq:triplet_static_free}, we obtain
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F[z\phi]
=
\int d^3x\,
\left[
\frac{r}{2}|\phi|^2
+
\frac{Z}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2
+
\frac{Y}{2}|\nabla^2\phi|^2
+
\frac{g}{4}|\phi|^4
\right].
\label{eq:scalar_reduction}
\end{equation}

This is exactly the scalar Brazovskii free energy.

\begin{theorem}[Exact scalar reduction in the polarization-locked sector]
For every constant unit vector \(z\in\mathbb C^3\), the restriction of the triplet static free energy \eqref{eq:triplet_static_free} to configurations of the form \(\Psi=z\phi\) is exactly the scalar Brazovskii free energy \eqref{eq:scalar_reduction}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from \eqref{eq:locked_norm}--\eqref{eq:locked_lap}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{BCC shell ansatz in the locked sector}

Let the BCC first shell be
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{BCC}}
=
\frac{q_*}{\sqrt2}
\left\{
(\pm1,\pm1,0),\,
(\pm1,0,\pm1),\,
(0,\pm1,\pm1)
\right\}.
\label{eq:BCC_shell_triplet}
\end{equation}
Choose one representative from each antipodal pair,
\[
Q_a\in S_{\mathrm{BCC}},
\qquad
a=1,\dots,6,
\]
and define the locked BCC triplet ansatz
\begin{equation}
\Psi_{\mathrm{BCC}}(x)
=
z\,\phi_{\mathrm{BCC}}(x),
\qquad
\phi_{\mathrm{BCC}}(x)
=
2A\sum_{a=1}^{6}\cos(Q_a\cdot x),
\qquad
z^\dagger z=1.
\label{eq:BCC_locked_triplet}
\end{equation}

By the exact scalar reduction theorem,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F[\Psi_{\mathrm{BCC}}]
=
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{scalar}}[\phi_{\mathrm{BCC}}].
\label{eq:BCC_locked_scalar_exact}
\end{equation}
Therefore the shell-projected amplitude equation is identical to the scalar one:
\begin{equation}
\left(
r-\frac{Z^2}{4Y}
\right)A
+
\beta_{\mathrm{BCC}}\,g\,A^3
=
0.
\label{eq:BCC_triplet_amplitude}
\end{equation}
Hence
\begin{equation}
A^2
=
-\frac{1}{\beta_{\mathrm{BCC}}g}
\left(
r-\frac{Z^2}{4Y}
\right).
\label{eq:BCC_triplet_A2}
\end{equation}

So every scalar shell-selection result in the single-field Brazovskii theory lifts \emph{exactly} to the locked triplet sector.

\subsection{Immediate consequence for the BCC selection theorem}

Assume the scalar single-shell Brazovskii theory has already established the ordering
\[
F_{\mathrm{BCC}}^{\min}
<
F_{\mathrm{FCC}}^{\min}
<
F_{\mathrm{SC}}^{\min}
\]
within the relevant universality class.
Then, by the exact reduction \eqref{eq:scalar_reduction}, the same ordering holds for the locked triplet sector:
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{BCC,lock}}^{\min}
<
F_{\mathrm{FCC,lock}}^{\min}
<
F_{\mathrm{SC,lock}}^{\min}.
\label{eq:locked_ordering}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Lift of scalar BCC selection to the locked triplet sector]
Within the polarization-locked sector \(\Psi=z\phi\) with constant \(z^\dagger z=1\), the complex-triplet Brazovskii free energy is exactly identical to the scalar Brazovskii free energy.
Consequently, every scalar shell-selection theorem --- in particular the scalar BCC-selection theorem --- holds verbatim in the locked triplet sector.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first statement is the exact scalar reduction theorem.
The second follows immediately because the variational problem on the locked triplet sector is identical to the scalar one.
\end{proof}

\subsection{General unrestricted triplet shell ansatz}

We now separate what happens beyond the locked sector.

Consider the more general shell ansatz
\begin{equation}
\Psi(x)
=
\sum_{a=1}^{6}
\left(
\eta_a\,u_a\,e^{iQ_a\cdot x}
+
\bar\eta_a\,\bar u_a\,e^{-iQ_a\cdot x}
\right),
\qquad
u_a^\dagger u_a=1,
\qquad
u_a\in\mathbb C^3.
\label{eq:general_triplet_shell}
\end{equation}
Here each shell mode may carry its own internal polarization \(u_a\).

\paragraph{Quadratic part.}
Because the quadratic kernel is internal-index blind and depends only on \(|Q_a|=q_*\), the shell-projected quadratic free energy depends only on the amplitudes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_2
=
r_*
\sum_{a=1}^{6}|\eta_a|^2,
\qquad
r_*:=r-\frac{Z^2}{4Y},
\label{eq:general_quad}
\end{equation}
and is independent of the polarization vectors \(u_a\).

\paragraph{Quartic part.}
The quartic term becomes
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_4
=
\frac{g}{4}
\int d^3x\,
(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2.
\label{eq:quartic_general_start}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:general_triplet_shell}, the shell-projected quartic part takes the schematic form
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_4^{\mathrm{shell}}
=
g
\sum_{a,b,c,d}
\mathcal C_{abcd}\,
(u_a^\dagger u_b)\,
(u_c^\dagger u_d)\,
\eta_a \bar\eta_b \eta_c \bar\eta_d,
\label{eq:quartic_general_shell}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal C_{abcd}\) is nonzero only when the shell resonance constraint
\[
Q_a-Q_b+Q_c-Q_d=0
\]
is satisfied.

Thus, in the unrestricted triplet problem, the quartic coefficient depends not only on the shell geometry, but also on the internal overlap matrix
\[
G_{ab}:=u_a^\dagger u_b.
\]

\subsection{Locked sector as a special case}

If all internal polarizations are equal,
\[
u_a=z
\qquad
\forall a,
\qquad
z^\dagger z=1,
\]
then
\[
u_a^\dagger u_b=1
\qquad
\forall a,b,
\]
and \eqref{eq:quartic_general_shell} collapses exactly to the scalar shell quartic form.
Hence the scalar BCC coefficient \(\beta_{\mathrm{BCC}}\) is recovered.

So the locked sector is the special case
\[
G_{ab}\equiv 1.
\]

\subsection{Honest limitation}

At this point an important honesty statement is required.

The scalar BCC-selection theorem lifts \emph{exactly} to the locked sector.
However, for the fully unrestricted triplet shell problem, the quartic form \eqref{eq:quartic_general_shell} depends on the internal overlap matrix \(G_{ab}\), and therefore the scalar structure-selection theorem does \emph{not} automatically imply the global triplet theorem.

In other words:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{scalar BCC selection survives exactly in the polarization-locked triplet sector,}
}
\end{equation}
but
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{global BCC selection in the unrestricted triplet shell theory is still open.}
}
\end{equation}

This is not a failure of the model.
It is the correct mathematical separation between what has been proved and what remains to be checked.

\subsection{Sufficient condition for full triplet inheritance}

There is an immediate sufficient condition under which the scalar theorem becomes the full triplet theorem.

If one proves that the triplet free energy is minimized, among shell configurations with a given geometric star, by internal polarization locking
\[
u_1=\cdots=u_6=z,
\]
then the unrestricted triplet minimization reduces to the scalar one, and the scalar BCC theorem lifts globally.

Thus the remaining extra step is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{prove internal polarization locking in the shell-condensed phase.}
}
\label{eq:locking_problem}
\end{equation}

Once \eqref{eq:locking_problem} is established, BCC selection for the full triplet model follows immediately from the scalar theorem.

\subsection{What is now closed and what remains open}

What is now rigorously established:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the static free energy of the complex-triplet Brazovskii model is exactly reducible to the scalar Brazovskii free energy in the constant-polarization sector;
\item the locked BCC triplet ansatz satisfies the same amplitude equation as the scalar BCC ansatz;
\item therefore the scalar BCC-selection theorem remains exactly valid in the locked triplet sector.
\end{enumerate}

What remains open:

\begin{enumerate}
\item whether the unrestricted triplet shell minimization dynamically locks all internal polarizations;
\item therefore, whether scalar BCC selection lifts from the locked sector to the full unrestricted triplet theory.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Final Summary}

\section{Quartic Shell Energy of the Unrestricted Complex-Triplet Brazovskii Model and the Polarization-Locking Question}

\subsection{Goal}

We now study the unrestricted triplet shell ansatz and ask whether the quartic shell energy by itself favors internal polarization locking.

The answer is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{No: the quartic term alone does not generically force polarization locking.}
}
\end{equation}

More precisely, the quadratic shell energy is polarization-blind, while the quartic energy contains positive cross terms weighted by internal overlaps.
Therefore reducing the overlaps can lower the quartic coefficient.
Thus the locked sector is not automatically the global minimizer of the unrestricted triplet shell problem.

\subsection{Static free energy}

The static free energy of the complex-triplet Brazovskii model is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F[\Psi]
=
\int d^3x\,
\left[
\frac{r}{2}\,\Psi^\dagger\Psi
+
\frac{Z}{2}\,\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi
+
\frac{Y}{2}\,(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
+
\frac{g}{4}\,(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2
\right],
\label{eq:F_triplet}
\end{equation}
with
\[
Z<0,\qquad Y>0,\qquad g>0.
\]

The shell radius is
\[
q_*^2=-\frac{Z}{2Y}.
\]

\subsection{Unrestricted shell ansatz}

Let \(S\) denote the selected shell, for example the BCC first shell.
The most general shell ansatz is
\begin{equation}
\Psi(x)
=
\sum_{G\in S}\psi_G\,u_G\,e^{iG\cdot x},
\qquad
u_G^\dagger u_G=1,
\qquad
u_G\in \mathbb C^3.
\label{eq:general_shell_ansatz}
\end{equation}
Here:
\begin{itemize}
\item \(\psi_G\in\mathbb C\) are shell amplitudes,
\item \(u_G\in\mathbb C^3\) are internal polarization vectors.
\end{itemize}

Unlike the locked sector, we do \emph{not} assume \(u_G\) is the same for all shell modes.

\subsection{Quadratic shell energy is polarization-blind}

Substituting \eqref{eq:general_shell_ansatz} into the quadratic part of \eqref{eq:F_triplet}, we obtain
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_2
=
\frac{1}{2}
\sum_{G\in S}
\left(
r+Z|G|^2+Y|G|^4
\right)
|\psi_G|^2\,(u_G^\dagger u_G).
\label{eq:F2_raw}
\end{equation}
Since \(u_G^\dagger u_G=1\) and \(|G|=q_*\) on the shell,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_2
=
\frac{r_*}{2}
\sum_{G\in S}|\psi_G|^2,
\qquad
r_*:=r+Zq_*^2+Yq_*^4
=
r-\frac{Z^2}{4Y}.
\label{eq:F2_shell}
\end{equation}
Therefore:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{the shell-projected quadratic energy is completely independent of the internal polarizations }u_G.
}
\end{equation}

So polarization selection, if any, must come from the quartic term.

\subsection{Quartic shell energy}

We now compute the quartic contribution.
First,
\begin{equation}
\Psi^\dagger\Psi
=
\sum_{G,H\in S}
\bar\psi_G\psi_H\,
(u_G^\dagger u_H)\,
e^{i(H-G)\cdot x}.
\label{eq:Psi_norm_shell}
\end{equation}
Hence
\begin{align}
(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2
&=
\sum_{G,H,K,L\in S}
\bar\psi_G\psi_H\bar\psi_K\psi_L\,
(u_G^\dagger u_H)(u_K^\dagger u_L)\,
e^{i(H-G+L-K)\cdot x}.
\label{eq:norm_square_expand}
\end{align}
Integrating over space, only resonant quartets survive:
\[
H-G+L-K=0.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
G+K=H+L.
\]
Therefore the shell-projected quartic energy is
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\mathcal F_4^{\mathrm{shell}}
=
\frac{gV}{4}
\sum_{\substack{G,H,K,L\in S\\ G+K=H+L}}
\bar\psi_G\psi_H\bar\psi_K\psi_L\,
(u_G^\dagger u_H)(u_K^\dagger u_L),
}
\label{eq:F4_shell_general}
\end{equation}
where \(V\) is the spatial volume.

This is the exact quartic shell functional for the unrestricted triplet shell ansatz.

\subsection{Locked sector as the scalar reduction}

If
\[
u_G=z
\qquad
\forall G\in S,
\qquad
z^\dagger z=1,
\]
then
\[
u_G^\dagger u_H=1
\qquad
\forall G,H,
\]
and \eqref{eq:F4_shell_general} reduces exactly to the scalar shell quartic form.
Thus the scalar BCC theorem is recovered exactly in the locked sector.

However, the unrestricted problem is controlled by the Gram matrix
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_{GH}:=u_G^\dagger u_H,
\label{eq:Gram_matrix}
\end{equation}
which is no longer identically \(1\).

\subsection{Diagonal quartic cross terms}

To determine whether locking is favored, it suffices to examine a universal positive subset of the quartic terms.

Choose the resonant quartets
\[
H=G,\qquad L=K.
\]
These always satisfy \(G+K=H+L\).
Their contribution to \eqref{eq:F4_shell_general} is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{4,\mathrm{diag}}
=
\frac{gV}{4}
\sum_{G,K\in S}
|\psi_G|^2\,|\psi_K|^2\,
(u_G^\dagger u_G)(u_K^\dagger u_K)
=
\frac{gV}{4}
\left(
\sum_{G\in S}|\psi_G|^2
\right)^2.
\label{eq:F4_diag}
\end{equation}
This part is polarization-independent.

But there is another universal class:
\[
H=K,\qquad L=G,
\]
which also always satisfies \(G+K=H+L\).
Its contribution is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{4,\mathrm{ex}}
=
\frac{gV}{4}
\sum_{G,K\in S}
\bar\psi_G\psi_K\bar\psi_K\psi_G\,
(u_G^\dagger u_K)(u_K^\dagger u_G).
\label{eq:F4_exchange_raw}
\end{equation}
Since
\[
\bar\psi_G\psi_K\bar\psi_K\psi_G
=
|\psi_G|^2|\psi_K|^2,
\qquad
(u_G^\dagger u_K)(u_K^\dagger u_G)
=
|u_G^\dagger u_K|^2,
\]
we obtain
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\mathcal F_{4,\mathrm{ex}}
=
\frac{gV}{4}
\sum_{G,K\in S}
|\psi_G|^2\,|\psi_K|^2\,|u_G^\dagger u_K|^2.
}
\label{eq:F4_exchange}
\end{equation}

This contribution is manifestly nonnegative and depends monotonically on the pairwise overlaps.

\subsection{Immediate consequence}

Because
\[
0\le |u_G^\dagger u_K|^2 \le 1,
\]
the exchange part \eqref{eq:F4_exchange} is minimized by reducing internal overlaps and is maximized when all polarizations are parallel:
\[
u_G=u_K
\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
|u_G^\dagger u_K|^2=1.
\]

Thus, at fixed shell amplitudes \(|\psi_G|\),

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{the universal overlap-dependent quartic contribution is minimized by reducing polarization overlaps,}
}
\end{equation}
not by locking them.

Therefore the quartic term alone does not energetically force internal polarization locking.

\subsection{Two-mode example}

The above conclusion is already visible in the simplest nontrivial example.

Take two nonzero shell modes \(G_1,G_2\) with amplitudes \(\psi_1,\psi_2\) and internal polarizations \(u_1,u_2\).
Restricting to the exchange contribution \eqref{eq:F4_exchange}, one finds
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{4,\mathrm{ex}}^{(2\text{-mode})}
=
\frac{gV}{4}
\left(
|\psi_1|^4+|\psi_2|^4
+2|\psi_1|^2|\psi_2|^2|u_1^\dagger u_2|^2
\right).
\label{eq:F4_2mode}
\end{equation}
At fixed amplitudes, this is minimized when \(|u_1^\dagger u_2|^2\) is as small as possible and maximized when \(u_1\parallel u_2\).

So already in the simplest nontrivial shell configuration, locking is not favored by the quartic term.

\subsection{Consequence for the unrestricted minimization problem}

Since the quadratic shell energy is polarization-blind and the quartic energy contains positive overlap terms such as \eqref{eq:F4_exchange}, there is no general mechanism in the minimal triplet Brazovskii free energy that forces all shell polarizations to align.

Hence the following statement is false in general:

\[
\text{``The unrestricted triplet shell minimizer is automatically polarization-locked.''}
\]

Instead, the correct theorem-level statement is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{The scalar BCC-selection theorem lifts exactly to the locked sector, but the unrestricted triplet global minimizer is not yet determined.}
}
\end{equation}

\subsection{What would be needed to recover global locking}

To make the full triplet theory inherit scalar BCC selection globally, one would need an additional mechanism favoring
\[
u_G=u_H
\qquad
\forall G,H\in S.
\]
Such a mechanism could come from:

\begin{enumerate}
\item additional microscopic alignment terms,
\item derivative couplings penalizing internal relative misalignment,
\item gauge/projector-sector effects not visible in the bare amplitude-only quartic term,
\item topological or defect constraints in the condensed phase.
\end{enumerate}

But none of these follows from the minimal quartic triplet free energy \eqref{eq:F_triplet} alone.

\subsection{The correct theorem}

We summarize the result precisely.

\begin{theorem}[Quartic term does not force polarization locking]
Consider the unrestricted shell ansatz
\[
\Psi(x)=\sum_{G\in S}\psi_G u_G e^{iG\cdot x},
\qquad
u_G^\dagger u_G=1,
\]
for the complex-triplet Brazovskii free energy \eqref{eq:F_triplet}.
Then:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the shell-projected quadratic energy is independent of the internal polarizations \(u_G\);

\item the shell-projected quartic energy contains the universal positive exchange term
\[
\frac{gV}{4}
\sum_{G,K\in S}
|\psi_G|^2|\psi_K|^2|u_G^\dagger u_K|^2;
\]

\item therefore the quartic term alone does not favor global polarization locking, and can instead favor reducing pairwise overlaps.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Item (1) follows from \eqref{eq:F2_shell}. Item (2) follows from the resonant quartets \(H=K\), \(L=G\) in \eqref{eq:F4_shell_general}. Item (3) follows because \(|u_G^\dagger u_K|^2\in[0,1]\), so the exchange term is minimized by reducing overlaps and maximized by locking.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Final honest status}

What is now rigorously established:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the polarization-locked triplet sector is exactly equivalent to the scalar Brazovskii theory;
\item the unrestricted triplet quartic shell energy depends on the internal Gram matrix \(u_G^\dagger u_H\);
\item the universal positive overlap terms show that quartic energy alone does not force polarization locking.
\end{enumerate}

What remains open:

\begin{enumerate}
\item what additional microscopic mechanism aligns the internal polarizations, if any;
\item whether the actual TECT microscopic completion contains such an alignment mechanism;
\item whether global BCC selection survives in the full unrestricted triplet theory after those extra mechanisms are included.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Final Summary}

For the unrestricted shell ansatz
\[
\Psi(x)=\sum_{G\in S}\psi_G u_G e^{iG\cdot x},
\qquad
u_G^\dagger u_G=1,
\]
the quadratic shell energy of the complex-triplet Brazovskii model is polarization-blind:
\[
\mathcal F_2
=
\frac{r_*}{2}\sum_{G\in S}|\psi_G|^2,
\qquad
r_*=r-\frac{Z^2}{4Y}.
\]

The quartic shell energy is
\[
\mathcal F_4^{\mathrm{shell}}
=
\frac{gV}{4}
\sum_{\substack{G,H,K,L\in S\\ G+K=H+L}}
\bar\psi_G\psi_H\bar\psi_K\psi_L\,
(u_G^\dagger u_H)(u_K^\dagger u_L).
\]
It contains the universal positive exchange contribution
\[
\frac{gV}{4}
\sum_{G,K\in S}
|\psi_G|^2|\psi_K|^2|u_G^\dagger u_K|^2.
\]

Since
\[
0\le |u_G^\dagger u_K|^2\le 1,
\]
this contribution is minimized by reducing polarization overlaps and maximized by full locking.
Therefore the quartic term alone does not force internal polarization locking.

Hence the exact current status is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{the scalar BCC-selection theorem remains exactly valid in the polarization-locked triplet sector,}
}
\]
but
\[
\boxed{
\text{the unrestricted triplet global minimization problem remains open, because the quartic term alone does not select locking.}
}
\]

\section{A Natural Alignment Mechanism for the Triplet Brazovskii Model}

\subsection{Goal}

The quartic shell analysis showed that the bare complex-triplet Brazovskii free energy does \emph{not} automatically force internal polarization locking.
Therefore, if global BCC selection is to be inherited from the scalar theorem, the microscopic TECT model must contain an additional alignment mechanism.

The purpose of this section is to identify a natural extra term that:

\begin{enumerate}
\item favors internal polarization locking;
\item preserves the already-established \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) internal geometry;
\item does not spoil the shell/BCC/Weyl/Dirac framework already derived;
\item gives a mathematically controlled route from the unrestricted triplet problem back to the locked sector.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Amplitude-projective variables}

Write the triplet field as
\begin{equation}
\Psi(x)=\rho(x)\,z(x),
\qquad
\rho(x)\ge0,
\qquad
z^\dagger z=1,
\qquad
z\sim e^{i\chi(x)}z.
\label{eq:amp_proj_decomp_align}
\end{equation}
Then \(z\) is a local representative of a point in
\[
\mathbb{CP}^2.
\]

Define the rank-one projector
\begin{equation}
P(x):=z(x)z^\dagger(x),
\qquad
P^2=P,\qquad
P^\dagger=P,\qquad
\mathrm{Tr}\,P=1.
\label{eq:projector_align}
\end{equation}

Define also the projective covariant derivative
\begin{equation}
D_i z := (1-P)\,\partial_i z.
\label{eq:cov_deriv_z}
\end{equation}
This is invariant under the local phase redundancy \(z\sim e^{i\chi}z\), and one has the standard identity
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Tr}(\partial_i P\,\partial_i P)=2\,(D_i z)^\dagger(D_i z).
\label{eq:P_identity}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Natural alignment term}

The most natural local, rotationally invariant, phase-redundancy-invariant, lowest-order alignment term is
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\rho,z]
=
\frac{\chi}{2}\int d^3x\,\rho^2\,(D_i z)^\dagger(D_i z),
\qquad
\chi>0.
}
\label{eq:F_align_z}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, using \eqref{eq:P_identity},
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\rho,P]
=
\frac{\chi}{4}\int d^3x\,\rho^2\,\mathrm{Tr}(\partial_i P\,\partial_i P).
}
\label{eq:F_align_P}
\end{equation}

This term is positive semidefinite and penalizes spatial variation of the internal projective orientation.

\subsection{Why this term is natural}

The alignment term \eqref{eq:F_align_z} is natural because it satisfies all structural requirements:

\begin{enumerate}
\item it is local;
\item it is translationally invariant;
\item it is rotationally invariant;
\item it preserves the projective \(U(1)\) redundancy of \(z\);
\item it depends only on the \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) orientation field and the condensate amplitude;
\item it vanishes when the internal orientation is spatially constant.
\end{enumerate}

Thus it is the minimal positive-definite stiffness term for the projective internal orientation.

\subsection{Nonnegativity and zero set}

We first record the most important property.

\begin{proposition}[Alignment energy is nonnegative]
For every admissible configuration \((\rho,z)\),
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\rho,z]\ge0.
\label{eq:Falign_nonneg}
\end{equation}
Moreover,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\rho,z]=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
D_i z=0
\quad\text{on the support of }\rho.
\label{eq:Falign_zero}
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\rho,P]=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\partial_i P=0
\quad\text{on the support of }\rho.
\label{eq:Falign_zero_P}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Nonnegativity is immediate from \eqref{eq:F_align_z}, since \(\rho^2\ge0\) and \((D_i z)^\dagger(D_i z)\ge0\).
Vanishing holds if and only if \(D_i z=0\) wherever \(\rho\neq0\), equivalently \(\partial_i P=0\) by \eqref{eq:P_identity}.
\end{proof}

Thus the alignment term is minimized exactly by projectively constant internal orientation.

\subsection{Projectively constant orientation and shell locking}

We now connect \(\partial_i P=0\) to shell polarization locking.

Consider the unrestricted shell ansatz
\begin{equation}
\Psi(x)
=
\sum_{G\in S}\psi_G\,u_G\,e^{iG\cdot x},
\qquad
u_G^\dagger u_G=1,
\qquad
u_G\in\mathbb C^3.
\label{eq:shell_ansatz_again}
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Projective constancy implies collinearity of shell polarizations]
Suppose \(\Psi(x)\neq0\) on a nonempty open set and the associated projector \(P(x)\) is constant there:
\[
P(x)\equiv P_0.
\]
Then all shell polarization vectors \(u_G\) lie in the same complex line.
Equivalently, there exists a fixed unit vector \(z\in\mathbb C^3\) and phases \(e^{i\varphi_G}\) such that
\begin{equation}
u_G=e^{i\varphi_G}z
\qquad
\forall G\in S.
\label{eq:collinear_modes}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(P(x)\equiv P_0\), then for all \(x\) with \(\Psi(x)\neq0\), the vector \(\Psi(x)\) lies in the one-dimensional image of \(P_0\), i.e. in a fixed complex line \(L\subset \mathbb C^3\).
Since \(\Psi(x)\) is a finite Fourier sum
\[
\Psi(x)=\sum_G \psi_G u_G e^{iG\cdot x},
\]
all of its Fourier coefficients must also lie in the same line \(L\), otherwise the image of \(\Psi(x)\) would span a larger subspace as \(x\) varies.
Hence each \(u_G\in L\), so there exists a unit vector \(z\) spanning \(L\) and phases \(e^{i\varphi_G}\) such that \(u_G=e^{i\varphi_G}z\).
\end{proof}

Therefore, projective constancy is exactly the geometric content of shell polarization locking (up to irrelevant shell-dependent phases, which can be absorbed into \(\psi_G\)).

\subsection{Locked sector remains untouched}

Suppose the shell configuration is fully locked:
\[
u_G=e^{i\varphi_G}z
\qquad
\forall G.
\]
Then
\[
\Psi(x)=z\,\phi(x),
\qquad
P(x)=zz^\dagger=\mathrm{const},
\]
so
\[
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\rho,z]=0.
\]

Hence the locked sector is unaffected by the new term.

\begin{corollary}[The alignment term does not spoil the locked scalar reduction]
On the polarization-locked sector \(\Psi=z\phi\) with constant \(z^\dagger z=1\), one has
\[
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}=0.
\]
Therefore the refined free energy
\[
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{ref}}:=\mathcal F_{\mathrm{triplet}}+\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}
\]
restricts exactly to the same scalar Brazovskii free energy as before.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If \(z\) is constant, then \(P=zz^\dagger\) is constant, so \(\partial_iP=0\), hence \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}=0\).
\end{proof}

This is crucial:
the alignment term improves the unrestricted triplet problem without changing the already-established scalar reduction on the locked sector.

\subsection{Why the term does not spoil the shell/Weyl/Dirac framework}

We now make precise why \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}\) is compatible with the previously developed TECT program.

\paragraph{(i) Shell instability.}
The shell-forming dispersion is determined by the amplitude sector of the quadratic operator
\[
r+Z|k|^2+Y|k|^4.
\]
On a locked or projectively constant background, \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}=0\), so the shell radius
\[
q_*^2=-\frac{Z}{2Y}
\]
is unchanged at leading order.

\paragraph{(ii) BCC selection in the locked sector.}
Because \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}=0\) on the locked sector, the scalar BCC-selection theorem remains exactly valid there.

\paragraph{(iii) Internal \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) geometry.}
The new term is written directly in projective variables \(P\) or \(D_i z\), so it preserves rather than destroys the \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) structure.

\paragraph{(iv) Weyl/Dirac shell reduction.}
The alignment term is derivative in the internal projector.
Therefore it does not introduce a constant intervalley mass term at the level of the local doubled block.
Instead, it supplies positive internal-orientation stiffness and is compatible with the shell-enriched projector framework.

Thus the term \eqref{eq:F_align_z} is structurally safe.

\subsection{Refined microscopic TECT free energy}

We therefore propose the refined static microscopic TECT free energy
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{ref}}[\Psi]
=
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{triplet}}[\Psi]
+
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\rho,z],
}
\label{eq:F_ref}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{triplet}}[\Psi]
=
\int d^3x\,
\left[
\frac{r}{2}\,\Psi^\dagger\Psi
+
\frac{Z}{2}\,\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi
+
\frac{Y}{2}\,(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
+

\right],
\label{eq:F_triplet_again}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\rho,z]
=
\frac{\chi}{2}\int d^3x\,\rho^2\,(D_i z)^\dagger(D_i z).
\label{eq:F_align_again}
\end{equation}

This is the most natural next-level refinement of the minimal triplet Brazovskii model.

\subsection{Large-\texorpdfstring{$\chi$}{chi} locking principle}

We now formulate the precise variational consequence of adding \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}\).

Let \(\mathcal M_R\) be any finite shell-truncated configuration manifold with fixed shell norm
\[
\sum_{G\in S} |\psi_G|^2 = R^2,
\]
and let \(\mathcal L_R\subset \mathcal M_R\) denote the locked submanifold consisting of shell configurations projectively equivalent to
\[
u_G=e^{i\varphi_G}z.
\]

Define the truncated refined energy
\[
E_\chi = E_0 + \chi A,
\]
where \(E_0\) is the bare triplet shell energy and \(A\ge0\) is the alignment functional restricted to \(\mathcal M_R\).

Then \(A=0\) exactly on \(\mathcal L_R\).

\begin{theorem}[Large-\(\chi\) locking principle]
Let \(\mathcal M_R\) be compact.
For every \(\varepsilon>0\), there exists \(\chi_\varepsilon>0\) such that every minimizer of \(E_\chi\) on \(\mathcal M_R\) lies within the \(\varepsilon\)-neighborhood of the locked manifold \(\mathcal L_R\) whenever \(\chi>\chi_\varepsilon\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since \(\mathcal M_R\) is compact and \(A\) is continuous with zero set exactly \(\mathcal L_R\), the complement
\[
\mathcal M_R\setminus U_\varepsilon(\mathcal L_R)
\]
is compact and \(A\) has a strictly positive minimum there:
\[
m_\varepsilon:=\min_{\mathcal M_R\setminus U_\varepsilon(\mathcal L_R)} A > 0.
\]
Also, \(E_0\) is continuous on the compact set \(\mathcal M_R\), hence bounded:
\[
|E_0|\le C_R.
\]
If \(X\notin U_\varepsilon(\mathcal L_R)\), then
\[
E_\chi(X)\ge -C_R+\chi m_\varepsilon.
\]
On the locked manifold, \(A=0\), so for some locked configuration \(Y\in\mathcal L_R\),
\[
E_\chi(Y)=E_0(Y)\le C_R.
\]
Thus if
\[
\chi > \frac{2C_R}{m_\varepsilon},
\]
then
\[
E_\chi(X)>E_\chi(Y)
\]
for every \(X\notin U_\varepsilon(\mathcal L_R)\).
Hence every minimizer lies in \(U_\varepsilon(\mathcal L_R)\).
\end{proof}

This theorem shows that the refined model drives the unrestricted shell minimization arbitrarily close to the locked sector once \(\chi\) is sufficiently large.

\subsection{Consequence for BCC selection}

Because the locked sector is exactly scalar Brazovskii, and because the large-\(\chi\) minimizers are driven arbitrarily close to the locked manifold, the refined triplet model asymptotically inherits scalar BCC selection.

More precisely:

\begin{corollary}[Asymptotic lift of scalar BCC selection]
Assume the scalar shell-selection theorem gives a strict BCC advantage over competing shell geometries on the locked manifold.
Then, for sufficiently large alignment stiffness \(\chi\), the global minimizers of the refined triplet shell problem lie arbitrarily close to the locked manifold and therefore inherit the scalar BCC selection asymptotically.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Combine the large-\(\chi\) locking principle with the exact reduction of the locked sector to the scalar Brazovskii theory.
\end{proof}

This is the correct rigorous statement at the present stage.
It is stronger than the bare triplet model result, yet still honest about what has and has not been fully proved.

\subsection{What is now closed and what remains open}

What is now rigorously established:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the bare quartic triplet term does not force locking;
\item the natural positive-definite projective stiffness term \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}\) vanishes exactly on the locked sector;
\item it preserves the scalar reduction and therefore does not spoil the existing shell/BCC/Weyl/Dirac framework;
\item in finite shell truncations, sufficiently large \(\chi\) drives minimizers arbitrarily close to the locked manifold.
\end{enumerate}

What remains open:

\begin{enumerate}
\item whether the actual microscopic TECT realization generates this projector-stiffness term from more primitive dynamics;
\item whether locking can be proved exactly (not only asymptotically) at finite \(\chi\);
\item the full witness/gap/mass verification in the refined microscopic model.
\end{enumerate}

namely the complex-triplet Brazovskii model supplemented by projective internal-orientation stiffness.

\section{Does the Projector Stiffness Term Need to Be Computed for \texorpdfstring{$N_{ij}^a$}{Nija}, the Witness Triple, and the Weyl/Dirac Criteria?}

\subsection{Answer}

Yes, ultimately it does need to be computed if one wants to claim an actual microscopic TECT realization.

However, one must first distinguish two logically different questions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Does the projector-stiffness term contribute \emph{directly at tree level} to the enriched shell tensor
\[
N_{ij}^a
\]
on the locked background?

\item Does it contribute \emph{indirectly} after the heavy internal projector fluctuations are integrated out?
\end{enumerate}

The correct answer is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{On a locked constant-projector background, the projector stiffness does not generically generate a new tree-level }N_{ij}^a.
}
\label{eq:main_answer_direct}
\end{equation}

But:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{It does modify the effective low-energy theory indirectly by making projector fluctuations heavy and generating suppressed corrections after elimination.}
}
\label{eq:main_answer_indirect}
\end{equation}

Thus the computation is not optional in the final microscopic program, but its role is primarily indirect rather than direct.

\subsection{Refined microscopic free energy}

Let
\begin{equation}
\Psi=\rho z,
\qquad
z^\dagger z=1,
\qquad
P=zz^\dagger,
\label{eq:Psi_rho_z}
\end{equation}
and consider the refined static free energy
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{ref}}
=
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{triplet}}
+
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}},
\label{eq:Fref_split}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{triplet}}
=
\int d^3x\,
\left[
\frac{r}{2}\Psi^\dagger\Psi
+

+
\frac{Y}{2}(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
+
\frac{g}{4}(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2
\right],
\label{eq:Ftriplet_ref_again}
\end{equation}
and

We now ask how \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}\) affects the shell-enriched tensor \(N_{ij}^a\), the witness triple \((W_\parallel,W_I,W_J)\), and the Weyl/Dirac criteria.

\subsection{Locked background and fluctuation expansion}

Let the background be polarization-locked:
\begin{equation}
\Psi_0(x)=\rho_0(x)\,z_0,
\qquad
z_0^\dagger z_0=1,
\qquad
z_0=\mathrm{const},
\qquad
P_0=z_0z_0^\dagger=\mathrm{const}.
\label{eq:locked_background2}
\end{equation}
Then
\[
\partial_iP_0=0,
\qquad
D_i z_0=0.
\]
Hence
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\Psi_0]=0.
\label{eq:Falign_on_bg}
\end{equation}

Now expand the projector/orientation sector:
\begin{equation}
z=z_0+\pi+O(\pi^2),
\qquad
z_0^\dagger\pi=0,
\label{eq:z_pi_expand}
\end{equation}
or equivalently
\begin{equation}
P=P_0+\delta P+O((\delta P)^2),
\qquad
\delta P = z_0\pi^\dagger+\pi z_0^\dagger.
\label{eq:P_expand}
\end{equation}

Then
\[
\partial_iP=\partial_i\delta P+O(\pi\,\partial\pi,\pi^2),
\]
so to quadratic order
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}^{(2)}
=
\frac{\chi}{4}\int d^3x\,\rho_0(x)^2\,\Tr(\partial_i\delta P\,\partial_i\delta P).
\label{eq:Falign_quad}
\end{equation}

Thus the stiffness term begins at quadratic order in projector fluctuations around the locked background.

\subsection{Immediate consequence: no generic tree-level contribution to \texorpdfstring{$N_{ij}^a$}{Nija}}

Recall that the enriched shell tensor \(N_{ij}^a\) is the coefficient of a term of schematic form
\begin{equation}
\delta\mathcal L_{\mathrm{enr}}
=
N_{ij}^a\,(\partial_i\phi_0)\,(-i\partial_j)\otimes s_a,
\label{eq:N_definition_again}
\end{equation}
namely a term \emph{linear} in the shell background gradient and \emph{linear} in the test derivative.

Now observe that \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}\) contains only
\[
\rho^2\,\Tr(\partial P\,\partial P),
\]
which, expanded about a locked constant projector background, has:

\begin{itemize}
\item no term linear in \(\delta P\), because \(\partial_iP_0=0\);
\item no term linear in the shell gradient \(\partial_i\phi_0\) times a single derivative acting on the test mode;
\item only quadratic-or-higher dependence on projector fluctuations.
\end{itemize}

Therefore:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{for a locked constant-projector background, } \mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}} \text{ does not generically create a new tree-level contribution to }N_{ij}^a.
}
\label{eq:no_tree_N}
\end{equation}

This is the first key fact.

\subsection{What the stiffness term actually does at leading order}

Although it does not directly create a tree-level enriched tensor on the locked background, the stiffness term does something very important:
it gives the projector fluctuation \(\pi\) a large positive quadratic operator.

In the constant-amplitude approximation,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}^{(2)}
\approx
\frac{\chi\bar\rho^2}{2}\int d^3x\,\partial_i\pi^\dagger\partial_i\pi,
\label{eq:Falign_quad_simple}
\end{equation}
so the tangent projector mode is governed by
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_\pi
\sim
\chi\bar\rho^2(-\nabla^2)
\quad
\text{(plus subleading terms).}
\label{eq:Lpi}
\end{equation}

At shell momentum \(|k|\sim q_*\), this gives a characteristic inverse propagator scale
\begin{equation}
\mathcal G_\pi^{-1}(q_*)
\sim
\chi\bar\rho^2 q_*^2.
\label{eq:Gpi_inv}
\end{equation}

Hence projector fluctuations become heavy when \(\chi\bar\rho^2 q_*^2\) is large.

\subsection{Indirect effect on \texorpdfstring{$N_{ij}^a$}{Nija}: integrating out the heavy projector mode}

Suppose the microscopic theory contains a coupling between shell/background fluctuations and the projector tangent mode, schematically of the form
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{mix}}
=
J_a^i[\phi,\psi]\ \pi_i^a

\delta \mathcal L_{\mathrm{eff}}
=
-\frac12\,\mathcal J\,
\mathcal G_\pi\,
\mathcal J
\label{eq:Leff_from_pi}
\end{equation}
with
\[
\mathcal G_\pi \sim (\chi\bar\rho^2(-\nabla^2)+\cdots)^{-1}.

\delta \mathcal L_{\mathrm{eff}}

\times
(\text{quadratic form in the source } \mathcal J).

Therefore the stiffness term can induce a correction

\boxed{
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}
\text{ affects }N_{ij}^a\text{ indirectly, through heavy-projector-mode elimination.}

\subsection{Effect on the witness triple}

W_\parallel[N]=\hat n_i\hat n_j N^3_{ij},

again assuming the heavy-mode elimination generates the corresponding tensor structure.

Thus the stiffness term does not redefine the witness concept.
It renormalizes the numerical witness values through induced effective couplings.

\subsection{Effect on the Weyl criterion}

The local Weyl determinant is
\begin{equation}
\det M
=

W_\parallel

Hence:

\begin{itemize}
\item if the bare locked-sector witnesses are already nonzero, the stiffness-induced correction only renormalizes the Weyl data and does not destroy Weyl nondegeneracy for sufficiently large \(\chi\);
\item if some witness vanishes at leading order, the stiffness-induced effective correction could in principle generate a nonzero witness at subleading order, but this requires an explicit model-dependent calculation of the mixing source \(\mathcal J\).
\end{itemize}

So the stiffness term can either stabilize an already-nonzero Weyl structure or, in principle, induce a subleading one, but only through explicit heavy-mode elimination.

\subsection{Effect on opposite-valley matching and Dirac pairing}

Because the stiffness term is written in terms of the projector \(P\) and its derivatives, it is even under \(G_*\leftrightarrow -G_*\) at the locked-background level.
Therefore it does not by itself generate an explicit asymmetry between the two opposite shell points.

Hence, at tree level on a matched locked background:
\begin{equation}
W_\parallel^- = W_\parallel^+,
\qquad
W_I^- = W_I^+,
\qquad
W_J^- = W_J^+.
\label{eq:matching_tree_level}
\end{equation}
Any mismatch can arise only through induced subleading corrections from integrating out projector fluctuations.
If the underlying projector sector itself respects the opposite-valley symmetry, then these induced corrections are matched as well, so Dirac pairing survives.

Thus the stiffness term tends to \emph{preserve} opposite-valley matching rather than spoil it.

\subsection{Effect on the remote gap}

Here the effect is much more direct.

The projector stiffness gives positive quadratic energy to projector tangent modes:
\[
\mathcal G_\pi^{-1}(q_*)\sim \chi\bar\rho^2 q_*^2.
\]
These modes are orthogonal to the locked shell-relevant Dirac block, so they belong naturally to the remote sector.
Therefore the remote spectrum is shifted upward by an amount of order
\begin{equation}
\delta \Delta_{\mathrm{remote}}
\sim
+\chi\bar\rho^2 q_*^2.
\label{eq:delta_gap}
\end{equation}

So even if its effect on \(N_{ij}^a\) is indirect and subleading, its effect on spectral isolation is direct and favorable.

\subsection{Effect on mass protection}

The dangerous constant masses on the minimal doubled Dirac block are
\[
\rho^1\otimes \mathbf 1,
\qquad
\rho^2\otimes \mathbf 1.
\]
The stiffness term is derivative and projector-valued:
\[
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}\propto \int \rho^2\,\Tr(\partial P\,\partial P).
\]
Therefore it does not itself introduce a constant intervalley mass at tree level.

After heavy-mode elimination, it may renormalize derivative couplings and remote-sector energies, but it does not naturally generate a constant mass unless the projector sector explicitly breaks the residual symmetry protecting valley charge.

So, again, the stiffness term is more naturally a stabilizer than a mass-generator.

\subsection{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Role of the projector stiffness term]
Consider the refined microscopic model with
\[
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}
=
\frac{\chi}{4}\int d^3x\,\rho^2\,\Tr(\partial_iP\,\partial_iP).
\]
Expand around a locked constant-projector background \(P_0=\mathrm{const}\).

Then:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}\) vanishes on the background and begins at quadratic order in projector fluctuations;

\item it does not generically generate a new tree-level enriched tensor \(N_{ij}^a\) on the locked background;

\item it gives projector tangent modes a positive inverse propagator of order
\[
\chi\bar\rho^2 q_*^2;
\]

\item integrating out these heavy modes can generate effective corrections
\[
\delta N_{ij}^a,\quad
\delta W_\parallel,\quad
\delta W_I,\quad
\delta W_J
\]
of order
\[
O\!\left(\frac{1}{\chi\bar\rho^2 q_*^2}\right)
\]
provided the microscopic mixing source has the appropriate shell-gradient/test-derivative structure;

\item it directly enhances the remote-sector gap and tends to preserve opposite-valley matching.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Items (1) and (2) follow from the expansion around \(P_0=\mathrm{const}\), for which \(\partial_iP_0=0\). Item (3) follows from the quadratic tangent-mode operator. Item (4) follows from standard Gaussian elimination of the heavy projector mode. Item (5) follows because the projector tangent modes lie in the remote sector and the stiffness term is even under opposite-valley exchange.
\end{proof}

\subsection{What this means operationally}

So the correct operational answer is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{Yes, the projector stiffness contribution should eventually be computed.}
}
\end{equation}

But the right computation is not
\[
\text{``write } \mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}} \text{ and read off }N_{ij}^a\text{ directly.''}
\]
The right computation is

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{expand around the locked background, identify the heavy projector mode, and integrate it out to obtain the induced shell-enriched effective operator.}
}
\end{equation}

That is the mathematically correct route.

\subsection{What is now closed and what remains open}

What is now rigorously established:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the projector stiffness term is not irrelevant;
\item its direct tree-level effect on \(N_{ij}^a\) is generically zero on a locked constant-projector background;
\item its indirect effect after heavy-mode elimination can renormalize \(N_{ij}^a\), the witness triple, and the Weyl determinant;
\item its most direct benefit is improved locking and an enhanced remote gap.
\end{enumerate}

What remains open:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the explicit microscopic form of the mixing source \(\mathcal J\) between shell modes and projector tangent modes;
\item the explicit induced correction \(\delta N_{ij}^a\);
\item the corresponding explicit corrections \(\delta W_\parallel,\delta W_I,\delta W_J\) in the refined microscopic model.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Final Summary}

The projector stiffness term
\[
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}
=
\frac{\chi}{4}\int d^3x\,\rho^2\,\Tr(\partial_iP\,\partial_iP)
\]
should indeed be accounted for in the final microscopic TECT program.

However, on a locked constant-projector background \(P_0=\mathrm{const}\), it does not generically generate a new tree-level shell-enriched tensor \(N_{ij}^a\), because it vanishes on the background and starts only at quadratic order in projector fluctuations.

Its main role is instead to make projector tangent modes heavy, with characteristic inverse propagator scale
\[
\chi\bar\rho^2 q_*^2.
\]
After integrating out these heavy modes, one can obtain induced corrections
\[
\delta N_{ij}^a,\qquad
\delta W_\parallel,\qquad
\delta W_I,\qquad
\delta W_J
\]
of order
\[
O\!\left(\frac{1}{\chi\bar\rho^2 q_*^2}\right),
\]
provided the microscopic shell sector couples to the projector tangent mode with the appropriate tensor structure.

Thus the projector stiffness term matters, but mainly indirectly:
it stabilizes locking, enhances the remote spectral gap, and renormalizes the shell-enriched Weyl/Dirac data after heavy-mode elimination.

\section{Quadratic Expansion Around the Locked Background and the Absence of a Mixing Source \texorpdfstring{$\mathcal J$}{J}}

\subsection{Goal}

The purpose of this section is to answer the question rigorously:

\[
\text{Does the projector stiffness term generate a shell--projector mixing source } \mathcal J
\text{ around the locked background?}
\]

We will show that, for the presently adopted refined \(U(3)\)-invariant triplet model, the answer is \emph{no} at quadratic order.

More precisely:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{Around a locked constant-projector background, the quadratic fluctuation operator is block diagonal between the scalar shell sector and the tangent projector sector.}
}
\label{eq:blockdiag_claim}
\end{equation}

Therefore no linear mixing source \(\mathcal J\) exists at this order, and hence no induced correction
\[
\delta N_{ij}^a
\]
can be derived from projector-stiffness elimination within the current refined model alone.

\subsection{Refined static free energy}

We work with the refined static free energy
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{ref}}[\Psi]
=
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{triplet}}[\Psi]
+
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\Psi],
\label{eq:Fref_main}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{triplet}}[\Psi]
=
\int d^3x\,
\left[
\frac{r}{2}\,\Psi^\dagger\Psi
+
\frac{Z}{2}\,\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi
+
\frac{Y}{2}\,(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
+
\frac{g}{4}\,(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2
\right],
\label{eq:Ftriplet_main}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}[\Psi]
=
\frac{\chi}{4}\int d^3x\,\rho^2\,\Tr(\partial_iP\,\partial_iP).
\label{eq:Falign_main}
\end{equation}

Here
\begin{equation}
\Psi=\rho z,
\qquad
z^\dagger z=1,
\qquad
P=zz^\dagger.
\label{eq:amp_proj_main}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Locked background}

Let the background be a locked shell condensate
\begin{equation}
\Psi_0(x)=\phi_0(x)\,z_0,
\qquad
z_0^\dagger z_0=1,
\qquad
z_0=\mathrm{const},
\label{eq:locked_bg}
\end{equation}
where \(\phi_0(x)\) is the scalar BCC shell background solving the scalar Euler--Lagrange equation in the locked sector.

Then
\begin{equation}
P_0=z_0 z_0^\dagger=\mathrm{const},
\qquad
\partial_i P_0 = 0.
\label{eq:P0_const}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Fluctuation decomposition}

We now decompose fluctuations into:

\begin{enumerate}
\item a longitudinal shell fluctuation \(\sigma(x)\) along \(z_0\),
\item a tangent projector fluctuation \(\xi(x)\in z_0^\perp\subset \mathbb C^3\).
\end{enumerate}

Write
\begin{equation}
\Psi(x)
=
\bigl(\phi_0(x)+\sigma(x)\bigr)z_0 + \xi(x),
\qquad
z_0^\dagger \xi(x)=0.
\label{eq:fluct_decomp}
\end{equation}

This is the natural orthogonal decomposition of the triplet field around the locked background.

\subsection{Quadratic expansion of the norm term}

First compute
\begin{align}
\Psi^\dagger\Psi
&=
\bigl(\phi_0+\sigma\bigr)^*\bigl(\phi_0+\sigma\bigr)\,z_0^\dagger z_0
+
\xi^\dagger\xi
+
\bigl(\phi_0+\sigma\bigr)^* z_0^\dagger \xi
+
\bigl(\phi_0+\sigma\bigr)\xi^\dagger z_0
\notag\\
&=
|\phi_0+\sigma|^2+\xi^\dagger\xi,
\label{eq:norm_expand}
\end{align}
because
\[
z_0^\dagger \xi = \xi^\dagger z_0 = 0.
\]

Thus there is no term linear in both \(\sigma\) and \(\xi\) coming from \(\Psi^\dagger\Psi\).

\subsection{Quadratic expansion of the gradient term}

Since \(z_0\) is constant,
\begin{equation}
\partial_i\Psi
=
(\partial_i\phi_0+\partial_i\sigma)\,z_0 + \partial_i\xi.
\label{eq:gradPsi_expand}
\end{equation}
Hence
\begin{align}
\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi
&=
|\partial_i\phi_0+\partial_i\sigma|^2
+
\partial_i\xi^\dagger\partial_i\xi
+
(\partial_i\phi_0+\partial_i\sigma)^*\, z_0^\dagger \partial_i\xi
+
(\partial_i\phi_0+\partial_i\sigma)\,\partial_i\xi^\dagger z_0.
\label{eq:grad_density_expand}
\end{align}

But
\[
z_0^\dagger \partial_i\xi
=
\partial_i(z_0^\dagger\xi)-(\partial_i z_0^\dagger)\xi
=
0,
\]
because \(z_0\) is constant and \(z_0^\dagger\xi=0\).
Therefore the cross terms vanish, and
\begin{equation}
\partial_i\Psi^\dagger\partial_i\Psi
=
|\partial_i\phi_0+\partial_i\sigma|^2
+
\partial_i\xi^\dagger\partial_i\xi.
\label{eq:grad_blockdiag}
\end{equation}

So again there is no quadratic mixing between \(\sigma\) and \(\xi\).

\subsection{Quadratic expansion of the biharmonic term}

Similarly,
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2\Psi
=
(\nabla^2\phi_0+\nabla^2\sigma)z_0+\nabla^2\xi,
\label{eq:lapPsi_expand}
\end{equation}
so
\begin{align}
(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
&=
|\nabla^2\phi_0+\nabla^2\sigma|^2
+
(\nabla^2\xi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\xi)
\notag\\
&\qquad
+
(\nabla^2\phi_0+\nabla^2\sigma)^* z_0^\dagger \nabla^2\xi
+
(\nabla^2\phi_0+\nabla^2\sigma)(\nabla^2\xi)^\dagger z_0.
\label{eq:biharm_density_expand}
\end{align}
Again
\[
z_0^\dagger\nabla^2\xi = \nabla^2(z_0^\dagger\xi)=0,
\]
so
\begin{equation}
(\nabla^2\Psi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\Psi)
=
|\nabla^2\phi_0+\nabla^2\sigma|^2
+
(\nabla^2\xi)^\dagger(\nabla^2\xi).
\label{eq:biharm_blockdiag}
\end{equation}

Thus the \(Y\)-term also yields no quadratic shell--projector mixing.

\subsection{Quadratic expansion of the quartic term}

Using \eqref{eq:norm_expand},
\[
(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2
=
\left(|\phi_0+\sigma|^2+\xi^\dagger\xi\right)^2.
\]
Expanding to quadratic order in fluctuations gives
\begin{equation}
(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^2
=
\phi_0^4
+
4\phi_0^3\,\Re\sigma
+
\bigl(\text{quadratic terms in }\sigma\bigr)
+
2\phi_0^2\,\xi^\dagger\xi
+
O(3).
\label{eq:quartic_expand}
\end{equation}

There is no term bilinear in \(\sigma\) and \(\xi\).
Hence the quartic term also does not generate quadratic shell--projector mixing.

\subsection{Quadratic expansion of the projector stiffness term}

Now consider the alignment term.
Around the locked background,
\[
P=P_0+\delta P+O((\delta P)^2),
\qquad
\delta P = z_0\xi^\dagger+\xi z_0^\dagger + O(\xi^2).
\]
Since \(\partial_iP_0=0\),
\begin{equation}
\partial_iP = \partial_i\delta P + O(\xi\,\partial\xi,\xi^2).
\label{eq:gradP_expand}
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}
\Tr(\partial_iP\,\partial_iP)
=
\Tr(\partial_i\delta P\,\partial_i\delta P)+O(3),
\label{eq:TrPP_expand}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}^{(2)}
=
\frac{\chi}{4}\int d^3x\,\phi_0(x)^2\,\Tr(\partial_i\delta P\,\partial_i\delta P).
\label{eq:Falign_quad_final}
\end{equation}

This depends only on \(\xi\) to quadratic order.
There is no bilinear \(\sigma\)--\(\xi\) term.

\subsection{Quadratic block-diagonalization theorem}

Collecting the previous expansions, we obtain:

\begin{theorem}[Quadratic block diagonalization around the locked background]
Let
\[
\Psi_0(x)=\phi_0(x)z_0
\]
be a locked background with \(z_0\) constant, and decompose fluctuations as
\[
\Psi=(\phi_0+\sigma)z_0+\xi,
\qquad
z_0^\dagger\xi=0.
\]
Then the quadratic fluctuation operator of the refined \(U(3)\)-invariant triplet model is block diagonal:
\begin{equation}
\delta^2\mathcal F_{\mathrm{ref}}
=
\delta^2\mathcal F_{\sigma}
+
\delta^2\mathcal F_{\xi},
\label{eq:blockdiag_result}
\end{equation}
with no bilinear \(\sigma\)--\(\xi\) term.
Equivalently, the shell fluctuation sector and the tangent projector sector do not mix at quadratic order.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The absence of bilinear \(\sigma\)--\(\xi\) terms was shown separately for the norm term, gradient term, biharmonic term, quartic term, and projector-stiffness term. Summing them gives the stated block-diagonal quadratic Hessian.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Consequences for the mixing source \texorpdfstring{$\mathcal J$}{J}}

In the previous discussion, a correction
\[
\delta N_{ij}^a
\]
from integrating out projector fluctuations would require a bilinear mixing source of schematic form
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{mix}}
=
\mathcal J[\sigma,\psi]\cdot \xi,
\label{eq:J_source_def}
\end{equation}
where \(\psi\) denotes the shell-reduced test mode sector.

But Theorem 1 shows that, within the current refined \(U(3)\)-invariant model and around the locked constant-projector background, there is no such quadratic source already at the level of the \((\sigma,\xi)\) Hessian.

Therefore:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\mathcal J=0
\quad\text{at quadratic order in the current refined model.}
}
\label{eq:J_zero}
\end{equation}

This implies that no induced correction
\[
\delta N_{ij}^a
\]
arises from simply integrating out the projector tangent mode in the present model at this order.

\subsection{Consequences for \texorpdfstring{$N_{ij}^a$}{Nija}, the witness triple, and the Weyl/Dirac criteria}

Because \(\mathcal J=0\) at quadratic order:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the projector stiffness term does not generate a new effective shell-enriched tensor \(N_{ij}^a\) at this order;

\item therefore it does not modify the witness triple
\[
(W_\parallel,W_I,W_J)
\]
through a quadratic induced term at this order;

\item hence it does not directly alter the leading Weyl determinant
\[
\det M
=
(\kappa_*|G_*|)^3
W_\parallel(W_I^2+W_J^2)
\]
through such an induced correction.
\end{enumerate}

Its role remains:

\begin{itemize}
\item improving locking,
\item lifting projector tangent modes into the remote sector,
\item and enhancing spectral isolation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation}

This calculation shows that the current refined model is still not enough to produce the full shell-enriched TECT Dirac structure dynamically.

The projector stiffness helps by stabilizing the locked background and by increasing the remote gap, but it does not by itself generate the missing gradient-linear enriched tensor sector.

Thus the logical conclusion is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{If one wants nonzero induced } \delta N_{ij}^a,
\text{ one must add an extra UV coupling that is linear in the tangent projector fluctuation and linear in the shell-gradient/test sector.}
}
\label{eq:need_extra_uv}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Minimal structural requirement for a nonzero \texorpdfstring{$\mathcal J$}{J}}

The missing UV sector must supply a coupling whose quadratic expansion around the locked background contains a term of the schematic form
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{UV,mix}}^{(2)}
\sim
(\partial_i\phi_0)\,
(\text{test derivative/internal factor})\,
\xi.
\label{eq:required_mix}
\end{equation}

Only after such a term exists can one integrate out \(\xi\) and obtain a genuine induced correction
\[
\delta N_{ij}^a.
\]

So the correct next inverse problem is not “compute \(\delta N\) from the current model,” but rather:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{find the minimal extra UV coupling that generates a nonzero quadratic mixing source } \mathcal J.
}
\label{eq:next_inverse_problem}
\end{equation}

\subsection{What is now closed and what remains open}

What is now rigorously established:

\begin{enumerate}
\item in the current refined \(U(3)\)-invariant model, the locked-background quadratic Hessian is block diagonal between shell and tangent-projector sectors;

\item therefore
\[
\mathcal J=0
\]
at quadratic order;

\item hence projector stiffness does not generate \(\delta N_{ij}^a\), \(\delta W_a\), or a correction to the Weyl/Dirac criteria at this order;

What remains open:

\item the induced shell-enriched tensor \(\delta N_{ij}^a\) once such a UV interaction is specified;

\item the corresponding corrected witness triple and Weyl/Dirac data in the fully extended microscopic model.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Minimal UV Extensions That Can Generate a Nonzero Quadratic Mixing Source \texorpdfstring{$\mathcal J$}{J}}

\subsection{Goal}

The current refined \(U(3)\)-invariant triplet model
\[
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{ref}}
=
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{triplet}}
+
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{align}}
\]
was shown to have a block-diagonal quadratic Hessian around the locked constant-projector background.
Therefore
\[
\mathcal J=0
\]
at quadratic order in that model.

The purpose of this section is to classify the minimal UV extensions that can change this conclusion.

More precisely, we seek the smallest additional microscopic structures that can generate, around the locked background,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{mix}}^{(2)}
\sim
\mathcal J[\sigma,\psi]\cdot \xi,
\label{eq:J_target}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item \(\sigma\) denotes the shell/amplitude fluctuation sector,
\item \(\psi\) denotes the shell-reduced test/fermionic/internal mode sector,
\item \(\xi\) denotes the tangent projector fluctuation.
\end{itemize}

Only after such a nonzero quadratic mixing source exists can integrating out \(\xi\) induce a genuine correction
\[
\delta N_{ij}^a.
\]

\subsection{Structural requirements on a nonzero mixing source}

We first determine what any admissible \(\mathcal J\) must look like.

The shell-enriched tensor we ultimately want has the schematic form
\begin{equation}
\delta \mathcal L_{\mathrm{enr}}
=
N_{ij}^a\,(\partial_i\phi_0)\,(-i\partial_j)\otimes s_a.
\label{eq:N_target_again}
\end{equation}
Therefore any UV source that can induce \(N_{ij}^a\) through projector-mode elimination must, at quadratic order around the locked background, satisfy all of the following:

\begin{enumerate}
\item it must be linear in the tangent projector fluctuation \(\xi\);
\item it must be linear in the shell/background gradient \(\partial_i\phi_0\) or an equivalent shell-direction tensor;
\item it must be linear in a derivative acting on the test mode sector, so that after elimination one obtains a first-order enriched operator;
\item it must carry an internal triplet label \(a\) matching the reduced shell Pauli sector \(s_a\).
\end{enumerate}

Hence the generic target form is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{mix}}^{(2)}
=
\Lambda_{ij}^{a}\,
(\partial_i\phi_0)\,
(\mathcal D_j \psi)\,
\xi_a
+\text{h.c.},
\label{eq:generic_mix_form}
\end{equation}
or an equivalent mediated version thereof.

This already implies that the UV extension must introduce an object carrying both a spatial index \(i\) (or \(j\)) and an internal index \(a\).

\subsection{Why purely scalar or purely projector corrections are insufficient}

Before classifying viable UV extensions, it is useful to rule out insufficient ones.

\paragraph{Type 0A: additional scalar potential terms.}
Terms such as
\[
(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)^3,
\qquad
(\Psi^\dagger\Psi)\Tr(\partial_iP\partial_iP),
\qquad
(\Tr(\partial_iP\partial_iP))^2
\]
do not generate a linear shell--projector mixing source around a locked constant-projector background.
They are either polarization-blind or start at quadratic order in \(\xi\) without a term linear in \(\xi\).

\paragraph{Type 0B: additional pure projector derivative terms.}
Terms such as
\[
\Tr(\partial_iP\partial_iP),
\qquad
\Tr(\nabla^2P\nabla^2P),
\qquad
(\Tr(\partial_iP\partial_iP))^2
\]
also fail for the same reason: they are purely projector-sector terms and yield no linear source coupling \(\xi\) to the shell/test sector.

\paragraph{Type 0C: additional amplitude-only derivative terms.}
Terms depending only on \(\rho\) and its derivatives cannot introduce the required internal label \(a\).

Thus:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{To obtain }\mathcal J\neq0,\ \text{the UV extension must explicitly couple the shell derivative sector to an internal tangent/projector carrier.}
}
\label{eq:need_coupler}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Classification principle}

We now classify the minimal UV extensions by the nature of the new field or tensor that mediates the coupling between:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the shell/background derivative structure,
\item the internal tangent/projector sector,
\item the test-mode derivative/internal channel.
\end{enumerate}

At lowest derivative order, there are three natural classes.

\subsection{Class I: direct shell--projector derivative couplings}

The first possibility is to add a direct local term involving \(\Psi\), \(P\), and one derivative on each side.

A minimal representative is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{UV}}^{\mathrm{(I)}}
=
\Lambda_{ij}^{a}\,
(\partial_i\Psi^\dagger)\,
T_a\Psi\,
\Tr(T_a\,\partial_j P)
+\text{h.c.},
\label{eq:ClassI_direct}
\end{equation}
where \(T_a\) are Hermitian generators adapted to the shell-relevant internal channels.

\paragraph{Why this can work.}
Expand around the locked background
\[
\Psi=(\phi_0+\sigma)z_0+\psi,
\qquad
P=P_0+\delta P,
\qquad
\delta P\sim z_0\xi^\dagger+\xi z_0^\dagger.
\]
Then
\[
\partial_j P \sim \partial_j\delta P \sim \partial_j \xi,
\]
while
\[
\partial_i\Psi^\dagger T_a\Psi
\]
contains terms linear in \(\partial_i\phi_0\) and linear in the test/internal fluctuation sector.
Therefore \eqref{eq:ClassI_direct} can generate at quadratic order a source of the form
\[
(\partial_i\phi_0)(\partial_j \psi)\,\xi_a.
\]

\paragraph{Caveat.}
This class is algebraically minimal, but somewhat ad hoc: it directly inserts the desired tensor structure into the UV Lagrangian.
So it is useful as a mathematical existence proof, but less compelling as a fundamental physical origin.

\subsection{Class II: auxiliary internal vector mediator}

The second, and structurally cleaner, possibility is to introduce an auxiliary field
\begin{equation}
X_i^a(x),
\label{eq:X_field}
\end{equation}
carrying one spatial index and one internal adjoint/reduced-shell index.

A minimal mediated UV Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{UV}}^{\mathrm{(II)}}
=
\frac{M_X^2}{2}\,X_i^a X_i^a
+
\alpha\,X_i^a\,J_i^a[\Psi]
+

\section{Is the Class II UV Completion Unique?}

\subsection{Short answer}

At the current stage, the answer is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{No: the explicit Class II mediator construction is not yet proved to be unique as a microscopic completion.}
}
\label{eq:not_unique_short}
\end{equation}

What is unique is not the full UV realization, but the shell-relevant effective tensor structure.

\subsection{What is actually unique}

The attached manuscript already closes the shell-relevant axial tensor family at the effective level.
Namely, once one imposes the shell axis \(\hat n\), the transverse frame \((e_1,e_2)\), and the shell-relevant axial symmetry class, the minimal first-order microscopic tensor takes the form
\begin{equation}
N_{ij}^3=A\,\hat n_i\hat n_j,
\qquad
N_{ij}^1=B\,\hat n_i e_{1j}+C\,\hat n_i e_{2j},
\qquad
N_{ij}^2=-C\,\hat n_i e_{1j}+B\,\hat n_i e_{2j}.
\label{eq:unique_axial_family}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, in the invariant-basis notation,
\begin{equation}
N_{ij}^3=g_\parallel\,\hat n_i\hat n_j,
\qquad
N_{ij}^1=g_I\,\hat n_i e_{1j}-g_J\,\hat n_i e_{2j},
\qquad
N_{ij}^2=g_I\,\hat n_i e_{2j}+g_J\,\hat n_i e_{1j}.
\label{eq:unique_g_family}
\end{equation}

The allowed shell-enriched tensor space is exactly three-dimensional:
\begin{equation}
V=\mathrm{span}\{B_\parallel,B_I,B_J\}.
\label{eq:V3_unique}
\end{equation}
Therefore the shell-relevant effective Weyl data are completely determined by three coefficients only:
\begin{equation}
\lambda_\parallel=\kappa_*|G_*|A,
\qquad
\alpha=\kappa_*|G_*|B,
\qquad
\beta=\kappa_*|G_*|C,
\label{eq:ABC_match_unique}
\end{equation}
or equivalently
\begin{equation}
\lambda_\parallel=\kappa_*|G_*|g_\parallel,
\qquad
\alpha=\kappa_*|G_*|g_I,
\qquad
\beta=-\kappa_*|G_*|g_J.
\label{eq:g_match_unique}
\end{equation}

Thus the effective shell-relevant tensor form is unique \emph{within the minimal axial symmetry class}.

\subsection{What is not yet unique}

However, the existence of the effective tensor family \eqref{eq:unique_axial_family} does not imply uniqueness of the microscopic UV completion that realizes it.

Indeed, different microscopic mechanisms may flow to the same shell-relevant tensor family after projection and integrating out heavy modes.
In particular, the following possibilities remain open:

\begin{enumerate}
\item direct shell--projector derivative couplings;
\item heavy auxiliary internal vector mediators (Class II);
\item heavy connection/gauge-like mediators;
\item possibly other UV completions whose shell projection lands in the same three-dimensional invariant tensor space \(V\).
\end{enumerate}

Therefore the explicit Class II construction
\[
\delta N_{ij}^{\,b}
=
-\frac{\lambda g_X}{M_X^2}\,m^a\mathcal C_{ij}^{ab}
\]
should currently be interpreted as an explicit \emph{existence proof} of a nonzero microscopic realization, not as a proof of uniqueness.

\subsection{Precise logical separation}

The correct theorem-level separation is therefore:

\begin{theorem}[Current uniqueness status]
At the present stage of the TECT program, the following statement is closed:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{Within the shell-relevant minimal axial symmetry class, the effective first-order enriched tensor family is uniquely three-parameter, namely }(A,B,C).
}
\end{equation}

But the following stronger statement is \emph{not} yet proved:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{There exists a unique microscopic UV completion whose low-energy shell projection yields that family.}
}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first statement follows from the already-closed classification of the shell-relevant invariant tensor space, which is exactly three-dimensional.
The second would require proving that every admissible UV completion is equivalent, after projection and integrating out heavy modes, to the specific Class II mediator construction.
No such theorem has been established yet.
\end{proof}

\subsection{What Class II actually proves}

The explicit Class II calculation proves the following weaker but important statement:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{At least one concrete microscopic UV completion exists that generates a nonzero shell-enriched correction and renormalizes }(A,B,C).
}
\end{equation}

This is already valuable, because it shows that the shell-level Weyl/Dirac tensor data are not merely formal placeholders.
They can arise from an explicit microscopic mediator.

But this still leaves open whether:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Class II is the physically realized TECT completion;
\item Class II is minimal in a strict categorical sense;
\item Class II is unique up to field redefinitions / integrating out auxiliary sectors;
\item another UV class yields the same effective \((A,B,C)\) data.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What would count as a true uniqueness theorem}

A genuine uniqueness theorem would need to prove something like the following:

\begin{quote}
Among all local microscopic UV extensions satisfying the TECT symmetry, locality, shell-instability, and no-dangerous-mass constraints, every admissible completion is equivalent, after integrating out auxiliary sectors and projecting to the shell-relevant low-energy subspace, to the same three-parameter axial tensor family realized by one canonical UV model.
\end{quote}

This would require:

\begin{enumerate}
\item classifying all admissible local UV couplings;
\item quotienting by field redefinitions, integrations by parts, and elimination of auxiliary fields;
\item proving that the quotient has a single equivalence class that contributes to the shell-relevant enriched tensor.
\end{enumerate}

That theorem has not yet been proved.

\subsection{What is the correct next step}

Therefore the correct next task is not to assume uniqueness, but to classify the UV completions up to shell-relevant equivalence.

Define two UV completions to be \emph{shell-equivalent} if, after integrating out heavy modes and projecting to the shell-relevant two-band sector at \(\pm G_*\), they produce the same effective coefficients
\[
A_{\mathrm{eff}},\qquad B_{\mathrm{eff}},\qquad C_{\mathrm{eff}}.
\]
Then the right problem is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{Classify the shell-equivalence classes of admissible UV completions.}
}
\label{eq:shell_equiv_problem}
\end{equation}

If this quotient turns out to have a single class, then one may speak of uniqueness at the shell-relevant level.
If not, then multiple UV realizations lead to the same or different effective Weyl data.

\subsection{Most honest current conclusion}

So the most honest statement is:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\text{The effective shell-relevant tensor structure is unique; the explicit microscopic UV realization is not yet known to be unique.}
}
\end{equation}

This is exactly where the program currently stands.

\item it is compatible with mass protection, but mass protection still requires the independent residual-symmetry exclusion of constant Dirac masses.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Items (1) and (2) follow from the shell-adapted form of the induced tensor. Items (3)–(5) follow because the Class II correction only renormalizes the derivative shell tensor coefficients and does not replace the already-isolated dynamical/symmetry hypotheses of the Dirac Emergence Closure Theorem and the Spectral Isolation Theorem.
\end{proof}

\subsection{What is actually closed after this step}

What is now closed:

\begin{enumerate}
\item a concrete canonical minimal Class II representative exists;
\item it yields explicit

A_{\mathrm{eff}},\ B_{\mathrm{eff}},\ C_{\mathrm{eff}};
\]
\item the shell-level Weyl criterion is closed in terms of those effective coefficients;

What is still not closed:

\item the actual residual-symmetry proof forbidding constant Dirac mass terms.
\end{enumerate}

So the correct honest statement is not that Class II solves everything, but that it closes the shell-relevant tensor part and remains compatible with the remaining theorem-level hypotheses.
