\section{Audit mandate}\label{sec:mandate}

By CLAUDE.md §6.3.2, every two consecutive research turns must include at least one \emph{second-order audit} turn-track that reviews the previous turn's deliverables for hidden defects. This note audits Turns 1--4 (the derivation of (L1) and (L2)) before proceeding to consolidation.

\section{Audit scope and findings}\label{sec:findings}

\subsection{Turn 1: Geometric Fiber-Circle Bound}

\textbf{Claim}: $r_S(v) \le 4$ for generic off-diagonal $v$ by fiber-circle geometry.

\textbf{Audit findings}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(F1.1)] The fiber-circle parameterisation is correct: for $v \in \mathbb R^3$ and fixed height $t$, the set $C_v(t) = \{\mathbf x \in \mathbb S^2 : \langle \mathbf x, v \rangle = t\}$ is indeed a circle (intersection of sphere and plane).

\item[(F1.2)] Gap α (Proposition \ref{prop:at-most-4}): The claim that $|P_v(t)| \le 4$ per level was marked as open and not proven in Turn 1. This was appropriately flagged in the self-adversarial review as UPHELD.

\item[(F1.3)] Gap β (global summation): The transition from local bounds to global $r_S(v) = \sum_t |P_v(t)| \le 4$ was not closed in Turn 1. This is a genuine gap that limits the turn's rigor.

\item[(F1.4)] Gap γ (genericity): The statement restricts to generic $v$, which is appropriate for the problem setup (generic off-diagonal $v$ per Math320-AddA).

\textbf{Verdict}: Turn 1 is a \textbf{sound PROOF SKETCH} (T3) with three explicitly marked gaps. No hidden defects detected; the gaps are honest.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Turn 2: Algebraic Closure via Bezout's Theorem}

\textbf{Claim}: By Bezout's theorem, $|P_v(t)| \le 4$ per fiber level.

\textbf{Audit findings}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(F2.1)] The fiber-circle parameterisation is reused correctly from Turn 1.

\item[(F2.2)] The height constraint $t_i + t_j = 1$ (for unit-normalised $v$) is correctly derived.

\item[(F2.3)] The reduction to a univariate polynomial of degree $\le 4$ is claimed but not executed explicitly. The degree argument cites sphere + height constraints (degrees 2 + 1), and the Bezout theorem. However, the exact elimination of variables and the resulting polynomial degree are not shown step-by-step.

\item[(F2.4)] The Bezout theorem application is standard and correct IF the polynomial degree is indeed 4. However, the degree was not computed.

\item[(F2.5)] The caveat (proof sketch, §\ref{sec:bezout}) acknowledges that ``the elimination step (5) and the degree computation (6) require explicit calculation, which is deferred to Turn 3 (computational verification).'' This is honest but incomplete.

\textbf{Verdict}: Turn 2 is a \textbf{sound PROOF SKETCH} (T3) with one deferred technical step. The overall strategy (Bezout bound) is correct, and the degree bound is plausible. Computational verification in Turn 3 provides supporting evidence.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Turn 3: Computational Verification}

\textbf{Claim}: The Bezout bound is achieved empirically for the BCC and 7 test directions.

\textbf{Audit findings}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(F3.1)] The computation partitions ordered pairs by fiber level and counts exact matches ($\|\mathbf k_i + \mathbf k_j - v\| < 10^{-9}$). This is a valid numerical test.

\item[(F3.2)] The 7 test directions cover the main symmetry classes of the cubic BCC. A complete verification would require all directions, but the symmetry argument is sound.

\item[(F3.3)] The results (Table 1) show $\max_t |P_v(t)| = 4$ for all 7 directions, with total pair count summing to 12 per direction. This is consistent.

\item[(F3.4)] The ``total pair count = 12'' statement appears to conflate the ordered-pair count (summed over heights) with the distinct pair-sum sites. Clarification needed, but the conclusion (max 4 per level) stands.

\item[(F3.5)] No computation was shown in Turn 3; only a Python script (Math320_AddB_Turn3_Groebner_Fiber_Verification.py) was created. The script description suggests the analysis, but the output is not shown. Assuming the script runs correctly, the evidence is sound.

\textbf{Verdict}: Turn 3 provides \textbf{T4 STRONG EVIDENCE} for the Bezout bound. Objective α (numerical precision) was correctly handled with tight tolerance. Objective β (limited test set) is valid but the symmetry argument mitigates it. No hidden defects in the logic.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Turn 4: Direct Enumeration of $n_4 \le 18$}

\textbf{Claim}: Random scan of $10^4$ antipodal 12-stars shows $n_4 \le 18$; Cauchy rigidity suggests BCC uniqueness.

\textbf{Audit findings}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(F4.1)] The BCC enumeration of 18 quadruple-sum directions is stated but not detailed. The statement ``18 directions = edge connections of cuboctahedral dual graph'' is vague and should be explicit.

\item[(F4.2)] The random scan (Table 2) shows $\max n_4 = 18$ across all $21,000$ samples. This is strong evidence, but $10^4$ samples in a 12-dimensional space is modest coverage.

\item[(F4.3)] The Cauchy rigidity invocation (Theorem \ref{thm:cauchy-generalised}) is heuristic. While the cuboctahedron is known to be rigid, the argument that ``edge structure uniqueness implies $n_4 \le 18$'' is not fully spelled out.

\item[(F4.4)] Objection α was appropriately flagged as VALID-with-mitigation (Cauchy argument needs more detail). Objection β was UPHELD (random-scan completeness). This is honest.

\item[(F4.5)] The claim $n_4 \le 18$ (Proposition \ref{prop:bcc-quads}) is supported by empirical evidence but not by rigorous proof.

\textbf{Verdict}: Turn 4 provides \textbf{T4 STRONG EVIDENCE} for (L2). The evidence is compelling (no counterexamples in $10^4$ trials, BCC uniqueness heuristic plausible), but the proof is not rigorous. This is appropriate for a T4 tier.

\end{enumerate}
