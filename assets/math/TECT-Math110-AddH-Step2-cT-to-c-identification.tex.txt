\subsection*{1. Transverse-phonon dispersion and speed definition}

\subsubsection*{1.1 Phonon modes in the BCC phase}

At the BCC operating point, the excitation spectrum exhibits three phonon
families (from the three plane families $\{xy, xz, yz\}$):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Acoustic (longitudinal)}: $\omega_L(\mathbf{k}) = c_L|\mathbf{k}|$ (to leading order).
\item \textbf{Acoustic (transverse, mode 1)}: $\omega_T^{(1)}(\mathbf{k}) = c_T |\mathbf{k}|$.
\item \textbf{Acoustic (transverse, mode 2)}: $\omega_T^{(2)}(\mathbf{k}) = c_T |\mathbf{k}|$.
\end{itemize}

By symmetry, the two transverse branches are degenerate at leading order.
The transverse-phonon speed is:
\begin{equation}
c_T = \left.\frac{\partial\omega_T(\mathbf{k})}{\partial|\mathbf{k}|}\right|_{k\to 0}.
\label{eq:ct-def}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{1.2 Relation to elastic moduli}

From classical elasticity theory, the transverse-phonon speed is related
to the shear modulus $\mu$ and the mass density $\rho_{\rm cond}$ by:
\begin{equation}
c_T = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\rho_{\rm cond}}}.
\label{eq:ct-elastic}
\end{equation}

This relation holds in any elastic medium (solids, fluids, condensates)
and is independent of the microscopic origin of $\mu$ and $\rho_{\rm cond}$.

\subsection*{3. Quantification of residual deviations}

\subsubsection*{3.1 Sources of deviation from $c_T = c$}

Even at the continuum level, residual deviations from $c_T = c$ can arise
from three sources:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Anisotropy of the BCC order parameter}: The three plane
families are not exactly degenerate; their energy differences are
$O(\epsilon_{\rm aniso})$. This causes small differences in the
transverse-phonon speeds from different families.

\item \textbf{Matter-coupling hierarchy}: Once matter (SM fermions) couples
to the TECT background, higher-order operators (e.g., Lorentz-violating
dimension-5 operators) induce small corrections to the phonon dispersion.
These are suppressed by powers of $(M_{\rm Planck}/M_{\rm phys})$.

\item \textbf{Higher-loop corrections in the RG}: Beyond one-loop, the
RG flow may generate small anomalous dimensions that shift the phonon
velocity at the next-to-leading order.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{3.2 Anisotropy-bound quantification}

From Math57-AddA (shell-isotropy analysis at $N=256$), the residual

\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{\rm aniso}^{\rm shell} = \max_{ij\ne kl}\left|\frac{c_T^{(ij)} - c_T^{(kl)}}{c_T}\right| \le 2.408 \times 10^{-4}.
\label{eq:aniso-shell}
\end{equation}

This bound comes from interval arithmetic on the Hessian eigenvalues
of $\mathcal{F}$ in the three transverse directions. Correspondingly, the

\begin{equation}
\left|\frac{\overline{c_T} - c}{c}\right| \le \epsilon_{\rm aniso}^{\rm shell} \le 2.4 \times 10^{-4}.
\label{eq:ct-deviation}
\end{equation}
