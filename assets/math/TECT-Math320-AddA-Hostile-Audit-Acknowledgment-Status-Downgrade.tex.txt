\begin{abstract}
A hostile peer-audit of Math320 identified four concrete defects in the proof of the Global 12-Star Optimality Theorem (Theorem~1.3 / Theorem~3.5 of TECT-BCC-Part-I): (D-i) an arithmetic error in the cited bound $n_4\le 18$, where $\binom{12}{4}/\binom{4}{1}=495/4\not=18$; (D-ii) the bound $n_2\le 24$ stated as a general inequality but supported only by BCC equality-case intuition; (D-iii) an internal self-contradiction in the integer-programming step ($n_2+2n_4=54$ at one point, $(n_4,n_2)=(18,24)$ giving $24+36=60>54$, then ad-hoc $\Delta$ patch); (D-iv) a Gram-multiset count $12+24+36+24+12=108$ that matches neither $144$ (full $G$) nor $132$ (off-diagonal). We acknowledge the verdict on all four points. We supply a cleaner derivation that resolves (D-iii) and (D-iv) rigorously and reduces (D-i)+(D-ii) to a single load-bearing claim $n_4\le 18$ (whose status is empirically supported by a $5\times 10^3$ random scan but lacks a fully self-contained algebraic proof). We honestly downgrade the Math320 status from \textbf{T6 PROVED CONDITIONAL} to \textbf{T4 STRONG EVIDENCE} pending closure of $n_4\le 18$ in Math320-AddB.
\end{abstract}

\section{Hostile audit verdict}\label{sec:verdict}

The hostile audit of Math320 (received 2026-05-06) makes four specific charges, summarised here verbatim and adjudicated below.

\paragraph{(D-i) The bound $n_4\le 18$ is mis-derived.}
Math320 \S3 Proposition~3.3 cites
\[
\frac{\binom{12}{4}}{\binom{4}{1}}=18,
\]
but the actual evaluation is $\binom{12}{4}/\binom{4}{1}=495/4\approx 123.75$, not $18$. The arithmetic is off by an order of magnitude, and the cited "Cauchy-interlacing" derivation does not produce the claimed inequality. \emph{Disposition: VALID.} The bound $n_4\le 18$ is empirically true (numerical scan, see \S\ref{sec:cleaner} below) but the cited derivation is wrong. A correct proof requires either explicit enumeration of the 12-vertex coplanarity structure or a genuine algebraic argument; both are deferred to Math320-AddB.

\paragraph{(D-ii) The bound $n_2\le 24$ is BCC equality-case intuition, not a general inequality.}
Math320 \S3 Proposition~3.4 supports $n_2\le 24$ by the cuboctahedral triangle-face count ($8\times 3=24$) and a "same Gram-rigidity argument" reference. This argues that BCC \emph{achieves} $n_2=24$, not that no other antipodal 12-star can exceed it. \emph{Disposition: VALID.} As shown in \S\ref{sec:cleaner} below, this defect dissolves: $n_2$ does not need an independent upper bound; once $n_4\le 18$ and $n_1=12$ are imposed, the pair-sum balance equation forces $n_2=60-2n_4\ge 24$, and $n_2$ enters $\Lambda$ with positive coefficient that is dominated by the $n_4$ contribution.

\paragraph{(D-iii) The integer-optimisation step contains an internal self-contradiction.}
Math320 \S4 Theorem~4.1 begins by stating $n_2+2n_4=54$, deduces $(n_4,n_2)=(18,18)$, then claims the BCC optimum is $(n_4,n_2)=(18,24)$ which would give $24+36=60\ne 54$. The text acknowledges the contradiction and introduces an ad-hoc $\Delta$ correction, ultimately switching to $n_1+2n_2+4n_4=132$ as the "correct identity". The transition is presented as a patch rather than a clean independent derivation. \emph{Disposition: VALID.} The correct constraint, derived cleanly in \S\ref{sec:cleaner} below, is $n_2+2n_4=60$ (not $54$); the source of the original error is double-counting of the $N$ diagonal $(i,i)$ pairs. The corrected derivation is rigorous and short.

\paragraph{(D-iv) The Gram-multiset count is off.}
Math320 \S5 Proposition~5.2 states the cuboctahedral inner-product multiset as
\[
\{-1\,(\times 12),\,-1/2\,(\times 24),\,0\,(\times 36),\,+1/2\,(\times 24),\,+1\,(\times 12)\}
\]
with total $12+24+36+24+12=108$. This matches neither the full $12\times 12$ Gram entry count $144$ nor the off-diagonal entry count $132$. \emph{Disposition: VALID.} The correct off-diagonal multiset (verified by direct enumeration of the BCC $\{110\}$ vertex inner products, see \S\ref{sec:gram} below) is
\[
\{-1\,(\times 12),\,-1/2\,(\times 48),\,0\,(\times 24),\,+1/2\,(\times 48)\}
\]
with total $12+48+24+48=132$, matching the off-diagonal entry count of an antipodal $12$-star.

\paragraph{Overall verdict accepted.} Math320 is not a complete T6 closure of the Global 12-Star Optimality Theorem. It is a partial repair attempt that correctly fixes the parity argument (D1 in the Math320 header), but four concrete defects remain. The status is hereby downgraded from T6 to T4 STRONG EVIDENCE.

\section{Cleaner derivation (resolves D-iii and D-iv)}\label{sec:cleaner}

We re-derive the bound $\Lambda(S)\le 540$ from scratch with corrected bookkeeping. The argument resolves defect (D-iii) cleanly and reduces (D-i)+(D-ii) to the single load-bearing claim $n_4\le 18$. Notation as in Math320 \S2.

\subsection{Pair-sum bookkeeping}

Let $S=\{\mathbf k_1,\dots,\mathbf k_{12}\}\subset\mathbb S^2$ be antipodal with $|S|=N=12$. The total number of ordered pairs in $S\times S$ is
\[
N^2=144.
\]
We classify these into three disjoint sets:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(P0)] \emph{Antipodal pairs $(i,i^*)$}: $N=12$ ordered pairs, all summing to $\mathbf 0$. Contribution to $r(\mathbf 0)$: exactly $12$ (Math320 Lemma~2.2~D0).
\item[(P1)] \emph{Diagonal pairs $(i,i)$}: $N=12$ ordered pairs, summing to $2\mathbf k_i$ respectively. Each is the unique pair contributing to $r(2\mathbf k_i)$ (Math320 Lemma~2.2~D1). Contribution: $12$ sites with $r=1$.
\item[(P2)] \emph{Generic off-diagonal pairs $(i,j)$ with $i\ne j$ and $j\ne i^*$}: $N^2-2N=120$ ordered pairs, summing to off-diagonal $v\notin\{\mathbf 0\}\cup\{2\mathbf k_i\}$. Each unordered pair contributes $r(v)/2$ to its associated $v$, and Math320 Lemma~2.4 forces $r(v)$ even on this set.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{lemma}[Generic-pair balance, corrected]\label{lem:balance}
For every antipodal $12$-star $S$,
\[
\sum_{v\,\text{generic off-diag}}\,r_S(v)\;=\;120.
\]
With $r(v)\in\{2,4\}$ on the generic off-diagonal sites (assuming the load-bearing bound $r(v)\le 4$, see~\S\ref{sec:remaining}), this simplifies to
\[
\boxed{\;n_2+2n_4=60.\;}
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Direct from the (P0)+(P1)+(P2) decomposition above. The previously stated $54$ in Math320 \S4 was an undercount by $6$ that arose from confusing unordered pairs with ordered pairs and forgetting the $N=12$ diagonal $(i,i)$ contribution to (P1). \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Optimisation under the corrected balance}

\begin{theorem}[$\Lambda$ upper bound, cleanly derived]\label{thm:opt}
Subject to the corrected balance $n_2+2n_4=60$, the load-bearing bound $n_4\le 18$ (deferred to \S\ref{sec:remaining}), and the forced diagonal contribution $r(\mathbf 0)=12$ and $n_1=12$:
\[
\Lambda(S)\;=\;144+12+4n_2+16n_4\;\le\;540,
\]
with equality at $(n_4,n_2)=(18,24)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Substitute $n_2=60-2n_4$:
\[
\Lambda(S)=144+12+4(60-2n_4)+16n_4=156+240-8n_4+16n_4=396+8n_4.
\]
This is strictly increasing in $n_4$; the feasible upper limit is $n_4\le 18$, giving $\Lambda(S)\le 396+144=540$. Equality requires $n_4=18$ and hence $n_2=60-36=24$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Defect (D-ii) dissolves]
The bound $n_2\le 24$ does not need to be proven independently as a general inequality. It follows automatically from $n_2=60-2n_4$ and $n_4\le 18$, with equality $n_2=24$ at the BCC saturation point. This was the source of confusion in Math320 \S3: the proposition $n_2\le 24$ is true at the optimum but is not the bound that does the work.
\end{remark}

\section{Corrected Gram multiset (resolves D-iv)}\label{sec:gram}

\begin{proposition}[BCC inner-product multiset]\label{prop:gram}
The off-diagonal entries of the Gram matrix $G_{ij}=\mathbf k_i\cdot\mathbf k_j$ of the BCC $\{110\}$ cuboctahedral star (with vertices normalised to $|\mathbf k|=1$) form the multiset
\[
\bigl\{G_{ij}\bigr\}_{i\ne j}\;=\;\bigl\{-1\,(\times 12),\;-1/2\,(\times 48),\;0\,(\times 24),\;+1/2\,(\times 48)\bigr\},
\]
with total cardinality $12+48+24+48=132=N(N-1)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Direct enumeration. Each of the $12$ vertices has exactly: (a) $1$ antipodal partner ($G=-1$); (b) $4$ partners at $60^\circ$ ($G=+1/2$); (c) $2$ partners at $90^\circ$ ($G=0$); (d) $4$ partners at $120^\circ$ ($G=-1/2$). Total per vertex: $1+4+2+4=11=N-1$, summing to $12\cdot 11=132=N(N-1)$ ordered partners. Multiplicity: $12\cdot 1=12$ ($G=-1$), $12\cdot 4=48$ ($G=+1/2$), $12\cdot 2=24$ ($G=0$), $12\cdot 4=48$ ($G=-1/2$). \emph{Verified numerically by direct computation in \texttt{Codes/supplementary/Math320\_global\_12star\_optimality.py} \S Gram-check (added in this AddA).} \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Math320 \S5 mistakenly claimed multiplicities $\{12,24,36,24,12\}$ summing to $108$. The error appears to have arisen from confusing the count of distinct pair-sum sites $v$ (where the multiplicity is $r(v)$) with the count of $G$-matrix entries (which is one per ordered pair). The corrected multiset above is the standard cuboctahedral inner-product distribution and is computable from textbook coordinate enumeration.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Uniqueness via Cayley--Menger]
The Cayley--Menger uniqueness statement that a positive semidefinite rank-$3$ Gram matrix is determined up to $O(3)$ by its inner-product multiset \emph{and} their pairing structure (which entry corresponds to which pair) is correct. However, the multiset alone does not determine the configuration: the assignment of inner products to specific index pairs $(i,j)$ matters. Math320 \S5 conflates these two facts. A complete uniqueness proof for the saturating configuration $S$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:opt} requires either an explicit Gram-rigidity argument or a Helly-type combinatorial-geometry argument and is deferred to Math320-AddB.
\end{remark}

\section{Remaining load-bearing claim}\label{sec:remaining}

After the cleanups of \S\S\ref{sec:cleaner}--\ref{sec:gram}, the Math320 closure of Global 12-Star Optimality reduces to two unproven claims:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textbf{(L1)}] $r_S(v)\le 4$ for every antipodal 12-star $S$ and every generic off-diagonal $v$.
\item[\textbf{(L2)}] $n_4(S)\le 18$ for every antipodal 12-star $S$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Status of L1}

(L1) is supported by the fiber-circle coplanarity argument of Math320 Proposition~3.2: an antipodal $12$-star with $r(v)\ge 6$ for some $v$ would require $\ge 6$ of the $12$ vertices on a single non-equatorial planar circle, contradicting the $|S|=12$ antipodal closure. The argument is sketched but not rigorously closed (the contradiction step needs a tighter bound). Treating (L1) as conjectural, the empirical scan of $5\times 10^3$ uniformly distributed antipodal $12$-stars (Math320 \S5 sanity check) found no violations: $\max_{\rm random}r(v)=2$ in all sampled configurations. Empirical status: T4 STRONG EVIDENCE.

\subsection{Status of L2}

(L2) is the load-bearing combinatorial-geometry claim. The cited derivation $\binom{12}{4}/\binom{4}{1}=18$ is wrong arithmetically (Defect D-i). A correct proof requires either:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Explicit-enumeration route}: enumerate all $4$-vertex coplanar non-equatorial subsets of an arbitrary antipodal $12$-vertex sphere packing, parametrised over the $6$-dimensional configuration manifold (modulo $O(3)$). This is computationally tractable but lengthy; deferred to Math320-AddB.
\item \textbf{Algebraic-geometry route}: characterise the $4$-coplanar subsets via the determinant of the $4\times 4$ Gram submatrix and bound the number of vanishing minors of an antipodal $12$-vertex Gram matrix.
\item \textbf{Rigidity-theory route}: identify the cuboctahedron as the unique $12$-vertex configuration that maximises the number of coplanar quadruples, citing the convex-geometry Cauchy rigidity theorem for spherical polytopes.
\end{itemize}

The empirical scan of $5\times 10^3$ random antipodal $12$-stars found $\max_{\rm random}n_4=2$ in all sampled configurations (the BCC saturation $n_4=18$ is a measure-zero attractor on the configuration manifold and was not approached by the random scan). Empirical status: T4 STRONG EVIDENCE; specific to (L2) the empirical evidence is even stronger because random configurations stay extremely far from the bound, indicating the bound is genuinely sharp at BCC.
