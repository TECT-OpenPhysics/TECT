## 2. Task #156 Mathematical Specification

### 2.1 The target geometric object

We seek a holomorphic vector bundle $E$ on the product base manifold
$$\Sigma_0 = \mathbb{P}^1_a \times \mathbb{P}^1_b$$
with the following properties:

1. **Structure group**: $G(E) = SO(10)$ (or an extension $E_8 \supset SO(10) \times SU(4)_F$ with SO(10) as a subbundle).
2. **Chern classes**:

   - $c_2(E) = 16$ (the defect-sector topological charge, unified via Math270 topological certificate from the original ℂℚ² calculation; but now realizable on Σ₀ by product-structure geometry).
3. **Kähler metric**: Standard Fubini–Study metric on $\mathbb{P}^1_a \times \mathbb{P}^1_b$, with form
   $$\omega = \omega_a + \omega_b,$$
   where $\omega_a$ and $\omega_b$ are the normalized Fubini–Study 2-forms on each $\mathbb{P}^1$ factor.
4. **Stability condition**: The bundle $E$ is $\mu$-polystable with respect to $\omega$ (Donaldson-Uhlenbeck-Yau criterion).

### 2.2 Hermitian-Yang-Mills (HYM) Equation

The HYM equation is the coupled system:
$$\bar{\partial}_A^* F_A^{(1,1)} = 0, \quad F_A^{(2,0)} = 0, \quad F_A^{(0,2)} = 0.$$

**Interpretation**:
- $F_A^{(1,1)}$ is the $(1,1)$-part of the curvature of the connection $A$ on $E$.
- The first equation states that the $(1,1)$-part is $\bar\partial$-closed (holomorphic structure is preserved).
- The second and third state that the bundle is holomorphic (no $(2,0)$ or $(0,2)$ curvature components).

In local coordinates, writing $A = A_i dz^i + A_{\bar j} d\bar z^{\bar j}$ and decomposing the curvature:
$$F_A = dA + A \wedge A = F_A^{(2,0)} + F_A^{(1,1)} + F_A^{(0,2)},$$
the HYM conditions become:
$$\bar\partial A_{\bar j} - \partial A_j = \mu(x) \cdot \omega^{(1,1)}$$
plus holomorphicity constraints on the curvature components. Here $\mu(x)$ is the scalar function encoding the curvature's trace (proportional to the slope $\mu(E) := c_1(E) \cdot \mathbf{v} / \text{rank}(E)$ where $\mathbf{v}$ is a polarization class).

### 2.3 Donaldson-Uhlenbeck-Yau (DUY) Theorem

**Theorem (DUY, Donaldson 1985, Uhlenbeck-Yau 1986)**: A holomorphic vector bundle $E$ on a compact Kähler manifold $(X, \omega)$ admits an HYM connection if and only if $E$ is $\mu$-polystable with respect to $\omega$. Moreover, if $E$ is $\mu$-stable (and not merely polystable), the HYM connection is unique up to gauge transformation.

**Application to our task**: Math253 (Turn 24) establishes that DUY applies to $\Sigma_0 = \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$. Existence of a solution to HYM is **structurally guaranteed** if we can construct or verify that our candidate bundle $E$ is $\mu$-polystable. The proof of stability is **algebraic** (depends on the splitting type of $E$ as a direct sum of line bundles or analysis of slopes of subbundles). The numerical construction of the actual connection $A$ is a **PDE problem** requiring Newton-Krylov solve.

## 3. Numerical-Driver Architecture Proposal

### 3.1 Discretization strategy

**Domain**: $\Sigma_0 = \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ is discretized as an $N \times N$ tensor-product grid, where each factor $\mathbb{P}^1$ is covered by $N$ overlapping patches (using an atlas of affine charts on the blowup or a direct covering of ℝℙ¹ ≅ $S^1$ quotient of $\mathbb{P}^1$). Standard implementations use:
- $N \in \{32, 64, 128\}$ (scan over three resolutions, analogous to Math236 continuum-limit protocol).
- Spectral methods or finite-difference on tensor-product grids (Fourier in periodic directions, Chebytshev in bounded Chebyshev-domain approximations of $\mathbb{P}^1$ patches).

**Bundle fields**: The connection $A$ and its curvature $F_A$ are represented as matrix-valued differential forms on each patch, with overlap maps ensuring holomorphic transition functions consistent with $c_2(E) = 16$.

### 3.2 Solver architecture (Newton-Krylov HYM residual)

**Objective function**:
$$\mathcal{R}(A) := \left\| \bar\partial_A^* F_A^{(1,1)} \right\|^2_{L^2(\Sigma_0)}$$

**Newton-Krylov algorithm**:
1. **Initial guess**: Decompose $E = \bigoplus_{i=1}^n \mathcal{O}(p_i)$ where $p_i \in \mathbb{Z}^2$ are degree vectors and $\sum p_i = 16$ (enforcing $c_2(E) = 16$). Construct the trivial connection on each summand and sum them via a partition-of-unity (crude but numerically stable initial data).

2. **Jacobian**: Compute the Fréchet derivative
   $$J_{A} := \frac{d\mathcal{R}}{dA}\bigg|_A$$
   via adjoint-Jacobian-vector-product (AJP) using spectral-Laplacian representation (analogous to Math66 v0.2 Path-A architecture for continuum-limit solves; see Docs/manual/CODE_MANUAL.md Math66 entry).

3. **Krylov iteration** (GMRES or MINRES): Solve
   $$J_A \cdot \Delta A = -\nabla_A \mathcal{R}(A)$$
   iteratively within a Newton loop:
   $$A_{k+1} = A_k + \lambda_k \cdot \Delta A_k,$$
   where $\lambda_k$ is a line-search parameter (backtracking, step halving, or more sophisticated).

4. **Convergence criterion**: Iterate until $\|\nabla_A \mathcal{R}\|_{L^2} < \epsilon_{\rm tol}$ (typical: $\epsilon_{\rm tol} \sim 10^{-8}$ relative to $\|\mathcal{R}\|_{\rm initial}$).

5. **Output**: Converged connection $A^*$ on $\Sigma_0$.

### 3.3 Initial guess / ansatz refinement

**Split-bundle ansatz**: For a degree vector $(p_a, p_b)$ on each $\mathbb{P}^1$ factor, the line bundle $\mathcal{O}(p_a, p_b) = \mathcal{O}(p_a) \boxtimes \mathcal{O}(p_b)$ is **automatically** holomorphic (no HYM solve needed). We build $E$ as a direct sum:
$$E = \bigoplus_{i=1}^n \mathcal{O}(p_{a,i}, p_{b,i}), \quad \sum_{i=1}^n (p_{a,i}^2 + p_{b,i}^2) = 16 \text{ (Chern-class constraint)}.$$

**Stability verification**: Check the Chern slopes of sub-bundles to verify $\mu$-stability. If the result is only polystable (some subsheaf has equal slope), the split bundle already solves HYM trivially (all components are flat Hermitian with respect to the product metric).

**Perturbative refinement**: If a non-trivial HYM perturbation is expected, use the split bundle as the initial condition and apply the Newton-Krylov solve to lower $\mathcal{R}(A)$ further (seeking a "true" stable bundle that minimizes HYM defect).

### 3.4 Output extraction and defect-mass computation

**Outputs from converged solution $A^*$**:

1. **Holomorphic bundle moduli** $\{p_{a,i}, p_{b,i}\}_{i=1}^n$ (the Chern roots or splitting type).
2. **Numerical curvature tensor** $F_{A^*}^{(1,1)}(z, \bar z)$ at grid points.
3. **Eigenvalue spectrum of the Laplacian operator** $\Box_E := D^*_A D_A$ acting on sections $\Gamma(E)$, where $D_A$ is the covariant derivative.

**Defect-mass formula** (derived §4 below):
$$\mu_{\rm defect}^2 := \lambda_{\min}(\Box_E) = \min \mathrm{spec}(D^*_A D_A)$$
where $\lambda_{\min}$ is extracted numerically from the Krylov eigensolver (e.g., LOBPCG or Lanczos) applied to the spectral-Laplacian matrix representation.

## 4. Defect-Mass Formula Derivation (NEW ANALYTICAL CONTENT)

### 4.1 The BCC condensate spectrum and mass tower

From Math110-AddI (lattice action), the BCC condensate supports a tower of excitation energies:
$$m_n = n \cdot m_0 + O(g^2) \text{ corrections}, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$
where $m_0$ is the ground-state defect mass (the condensate gap) and $m_0 \sim a_{\rm BCC}^{-1}$ with $a_{\rm BCC} \sim 10^{-19}$ m (Planck-scale lattice spacing).

**Physical interpretation**: Defects in the BCC lattice correspond to topologically non-trivial sectors (e.g., domain walls, vortex lines, or gauge-bundle instantons). Their masses are determined by the **quantum effective action**, which couples to the gauge-bundle curvature.

### 4.2 Defect mass as eigenvalue of bundle Laplacian

**Key observation**: The lowest defect mass is the **zero-mode eigenvalue** of the covariant Laplacian operator $\Box_E$ acting on bundle-valued zero-form fields (sections of $E$):
$$\Box_E = D_A^* D_A = -\nabla_A^2,$$
where $\nabla_A = \partial + A$ is the covariant derivative and $D_A^*$ is its formal adjoint.

For a holomorphic bundle with an HYM connection:
$$\box_{\bar\partial} = \bar\partial^* \bar\partial + \bar\partial \bar\partial^* \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda_{\min}(\Box_E) \geq 0.$$
The smallest positive eigenvalue encodes the defect mass.

**Dimensional analysis**:
- $[\Box_E] = [\text{operator}] = L^{-2}$ (mass dimension 2 in 4D).
- $[\lambda_{\min}] = L^{-2}$ → $[\mu_{\rm defect}] = L^{-1}$ (mass dimension 1).
- $[\mu_{\rm defect}] = [\hbar c / a]$ where $a$ is a length scale (BCC lattice spacing).

### 4.3 Eigenvalue-bound theorem (Bogomolov-type inequality)

**Proposition 4.3.1** (Bogomolov Inequality for Chern Classes on Kähler Surfaces):
Let $E$ be a holomorphic vector bundle on a Kähler surface $(X, \omega)$ with $c_1(E) = 0$. Then:
$$c_2(E) \geq \frac{(\text{rk}(E) - 1)}{2 \cdot \text{rk}(E)} \cdot c_1(\text{ad}(E))^2 / \omega^2.$$

**Application to bundle Laplacian**: The spectrum of $\Box_E$ is bounded below by a quantity involving $c_2(E)$, the volume of the base, and the rank:
$$\lambda_{\min}(\Box_E) \geq C_{\rm geo} \cdot \frac{c_2(E)}{\text{vol}(\Sigma_0)} \cdot \min_{x \in \Sigma_0} |\det F_{A^*}(x)|.$$

Here $C_{\rm geo}$ is a universal geometric constant ($O(1)$ in natural units) and $\det F_{A^*}$ is the determinant of the curvature matrix restricted to the base metric.

**Remark**: The precise form of Proposition 4.3.1 depends on the metric and the bundle structure. For product bundles ($E = \bigoplus_i \mathcal{O}(d_i)$), the bound simplifies and the eigenvalue can often be computed explicitly.
