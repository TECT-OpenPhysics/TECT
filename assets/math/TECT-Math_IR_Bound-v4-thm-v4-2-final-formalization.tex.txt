\section{Physical motivation for (H-suppression)}
% =====================================================================

The hypothesis (H-suppression) is not arbitrary. It is grounded in three
complementary physical arguments:

\subsection{RG argument: Loop-order hierarchy}

In renormalization-group theory, loop-integral coefficients scale as powers
of the coupling constant. At the Brazovskii critical point with $\lambda = -0.43$,
the dimensional analysis gives:

\begin{align}
  c_4 &\propto \lambda^2 \approx 0.185,\\
  c_6 &\propto \lambda^{2k_6} \quad \text{for some } k_6 \ge 1,\\
  c_8 &\propto \lambda^{2k_8} \quad \text{for some } k_8 \ge k_6.
\end{align}

The most conservative estimate from the loop structure (treating $c_6$ as a
two-vertex 2-loop diagram) gives $c_6 / c_4 \sim \lambda^2 \approx 0.18$.

Thus, the suppression factor between $c_6$ and $c_4$ is $\sim 20\%$ — non-asymptotic
but still significant. Higher terms decay faster.

\subsection{Smooth-integrand convergence on convex domains}

A classical result in harmonic analysis states that the cubic-harmonic expansion
of a smooth function on a convex domain (like the BCC truncated octahedron) converges
exponentially fast. For $r_{\mathrm{BZ}}(\hat{\mathbf{n}})$, which is piecewise-smooth
on the domain boundary, the coefficients $J_1^{(L)}$ decay as $L^{-\alpha}$ for some $\alpha > 0$.

Combined with the loop-coefficient suppression $c_L \propto \lambda^{2\lfloor L/2 \rfloor}$,
the product $c_L J_1^{(L)}$ benefits from two sources of decay: both the coefficient
and the harmonic content shrink with $L$.

PC-3A numerical evidence (Math\_IR\_Bound-v4-PC-3A-L6-closure-attempt.tex.txt)
shows that $|c_6 J_1^{(L=6)}| \approx 1.57 \times 10^{-5}$ and
$|c_8 J_1^{(L=8)}| \approx 8.86 \times 10^{-7}$, confirming the two-pronged decay.

\subsection{Physical interpretation: Effective-field-theory standard}

In effective-field-theory literature (condensed matter, nuclear physics, particle physics),
it is standard practice to assume that higher-order corrections beyond the leading
order are suppressed and to formulate theorems **conditionally** on this assumption.
Examples include:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Chiral perturbation theory} (Weinberg 1990s): Loop integrals are expanded
  in powers of the Goldstone-boson energy; contributions from loops with more vertices
  are assumed suppressed relative to lower-loop terms.

\item \textbf{Emergent Lorentz invariance} (Zee, Wen 2010s): The hypothesis that the
  long-distance effective Hamiltonian is Lorentz-invariant is stated explicitly and
  verified numerically (often to next-order in the scaling parameter).

\item \textbf{Landau-Fermi liquid theory}: The assumption that higher-frequency contributions
  to the quasiparticle self-energy are suppressed is a foundational hypothesis, not
  a limitation.
\end{enumerate}

The present work follows this precedent. Hypothesis (H-suppression) is an effective-field-theory
assumption, transparently stated, physically justified, and testable by higher-order calculations.

\begin{enumerate}

In each case, the hypothesis is \textit{explicitly stated and justified}, not hidden.

By reformulating Thm v4-2 with (H-suppression) as an explicit hypothesis, we make the
proof \textit{more rigorous}, not less. The refereeing standard is: (i) Is the hypothesis
